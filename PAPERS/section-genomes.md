### OUTPUT_B — RESEARCH_ECHO

The old ekphrastic question — can words make readers see? — has been answered by generative AI in the affirmative, and that answer is already obsolete. The decisive theoretical intervention is not to celebrate the word-to-image collapse (Bajohr 2024) but to show that image generation is a way-station toward world generation, where description stops pointing at a world and starts constituting its operating conditions. Recover Webb's ancient operative definition, track the Spitzer narrowing that concealed it, and then show that Ha and Schmidhuber's world models — compressed spatiotemporal prediction engines — are the computational form of what Homer's shield already staged: not an image but a world-specification. The thesis must make one clean move: worldtext is not a rival term for world model; worldtext names the moment when description becomes an interface to a world model. Frame Mitchell's imagetext as the first-order achievement and worldtext as the second-order pressure that renders imagetext insufficient.

`#operative-reclassification #word-to-world #shield-as-specification #imagetext-exceeded`

### OUTPUT_B — RESEARCH_ECHO

Prompting is not a single act of description but an iterative performance against a model already saturated with cultural codes — captions, genres, styles, platform defaults, visual clichés. The operative unit is the loop, not the first prompt: write, generate, read the system's misinterpretation, revise, regenerate. This returns ekphrasis to its ancient performative condition (the *progymnasmata*) while exceeding it: the prompt is now performed before a generative system, not an audience. Geertz's "thick description" names the structural condition — the model is already thick, so the prompt must thicken to negotiate its layers — but the analogy must stay functional, not decorative. A thin prompt ("Make Achilles' shield") treats the model as a vending machine; a thick prompt ("Make the shield as a worked object, scene by scene, with sky, city, field, dance, war, law, harvest, and ocean as living order") gives the system enough situated pressure to emerge from a specific world rather than generic visual habit. The image is not the product. The image is feedback. Thickness is not verbosity; it is selected context that changes what the system can operationalize. There is no final perfect prompt.

`#thick-prompting #loop-as-unit #image-as-feedback #cultivation-not-command`

### OUTPUT_B — RESEARCH_ECHO

Homer's Shield of Achilles should not enter the argument as a famous example. It should enter as the model. The shield does not depict a scene — it specifies a world: cosmology at center, governance and labor in the rings, Ocean at the rim as the boundary beyond which the world-model has no data. This structural analysis maps onto Ha and Schmidhuber's (2018) concept of world models — compressed spatiotemporal prediction engines — and onto the emergent platforms (DeepMind's Genie, World Labs' Marble) that generate navigable environments from prompts. The critical distinction is not image vs. bigger-image but image vs. world: image generation asked "what can a sentence make visible?" while world generation asks "what can a sentence make habitable?" The shield already stages this: its scenes imply movement, duration, social procedure, conflict, and cosmic boundary — not a frozen ornament but a compressed environment. Worldtext names this shift, and it must not become a graceful theoretical upgrade; it should sound like a door opening in the floor. The prompt is not sovereign. The prompt is one operator among many — collaborating with system prompts, training data, physics engines, and procedural rules. The world model is cumulative, stateful, and layered. The shield was a world carried by a warrior. The world model is a shield the user can enter.

`#shield-as-world-model #habitable-not-visible #constitution-not-caption #world-model-not-bigger-image`

### OUTPUT_B — RESEARCH_ECHO

Ekphrasis once asked whether words could make an audience see. Operative ekphrasis asks what happens when words make images. Worldtext asks the next question: what happens when words help generate worlds? The paper should not resolve this question — it should name the threshold at which the question becomes operational. The shield of Achilles shows that the ambition was always present: a world compressed into an artifact and unfolded through performance. Generative AI does not fulfill that ambition; it operationalizes one of its oldest fantasies — and the fantasy remains unstable, platformed, cumulative, and made from the sediment of other descriptions. Worldtext is therefore not a finished theory but a threshold concept: it marks the point at which operative ekphrasis opens beyond the image and toward the world model. The concepts introduced here — operative ekphrasis, thick prompting, imagetext, worldtext — will likely diverge as the technologies develop. The strongest ending is not "worldtext is the future" but that the image is no longer where ekphrasis ends. Human beings once asked language to preserve the world; now they ask language to generate one. The wish has changed less than we think.

`#threshold-concept #door-not-destination #wish-unchanged #ekphrasis-beyond-image`
