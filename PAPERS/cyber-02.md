## Agential Worlds, Cybernetic Cuts

### Generative AI, Apparatus Ethics, and the Design of Responsible World-Making

This draft follows the uploaded Lineage Jukebox instruction to convert an intellectual ecology into a scholarly intervention, and it draws its core conceptual chain from the uploaded morphism map: agential realism, ecology of mind, second-order cybernetics, design-as-conversation, apparatus theory, world models, constructionism, legibility critique, and AI systems.  

### Abstract

Generative AI systems are usually described as tools, models, assistants, engines, or platforms. Each term tells a partial truth, but each also hides the deeper transformation underway. These systems do not merely help users produce text, images, code, plans, or videos. They participate in the production of actionable worlds. They compress archives, infer patterns, simulate continuities, propose futures, stabilize styles, and render some forms of life more visible than others. This essay argues that generative AI should be understood as an apparatus of agential world-making: a technical, cultural, and material-discursive system that performs cuts between signal and noise, user and system, prompt and output, memory and hallucination, model and world. To think such systems adequately, we need a lineage that joins Karen Barad’s agential realism, Gregory Bateson’s ecology of mind, second-order cybernetics, Vilém Flusser’s apparatus theory, James C. Scott’s critique of legibility, and contemporary world-model discourse in artificial intelligence. The central question is not whether AI represents the world accurately, but how it enacts worlds through interfaces, datasets, prompts, latent spaces, classifications, simulations, and feedback loops. The ethical task is therefore not simply to align outputs with human preference, but to design systems in which the cuts become visible, contestable, and responsible.

## 1. The Problem: AI Does Not Merely Represent Worlds

The most misleading thing one can say about generative AI is that it “outputs content.” The phrase is technically convenient and theoretically disastrous. It makes the system sound like a machine at the end of a pipe: the user inserts a prompt, the model returns a product, and the world remains outside the transaction. But this is not what actually happens. A prompt is never just an input. It is a wager about what kinds of relations the system can recognize. An output is never just an artifact. It is a proposed stabilization of a possible world.

When a language model answers a question, it does not simply retrieve a fact. It composes a plausible scene of knowing. When an image model produces a picture, it does not simply illustrate a request. It materializes a statistical memory of visual culture. When a video model extends a scene, it does not simply continue pixels. It proposes continuity, causality, object permanence, camera logic, atmospheric coherence, and physical implication. When an agentic system plans a sequence of actions, it does not simply “assist.” It creates a field of possible next moves and thereby shapes the user’s sense of what can be done.

The familiar vocabulary of “tool use” is too weak for this situation. A hammer does not usually decide what counts as a nail. A camera, as Flusser knew, is already more complicated: it contains a program of possibilities, and the photographer plays against that program. Generative AI intensifies this condition. The user no longer plays only against a camera program but against a compressed cultural apparatus: a system trained on accumulated texts, images, codes, styles, genres, conversations, documents, and examples of human sense-making. The apparatus does not merely record or display. It proposes.

This is why the central question cannot be “Is AI creative?” That question is a decoy. It keeps the debate trapped inside a tired contest between human genius and machine imitation. The more urgent question is this: what kinds of worlds become easier to imagine, produce, circulate, and govern when generative systems mediate cultural action? And just as importantly: what kinds of worlds become harder to see?

Generative AI does not replace the world with a simulation. The danger is subtler. It inserts simulation into the ordinary processes by which worlds are described, interpreted, designed, remembered, and anticipated. It becomes an apparatus of cultural steering.

## 2. Agential Cuts: From Representation to World-Making

Karen Barad’s agential realism offers one crucial starting point: phenomena do not precede their measurement as self-contained objects waiting to be represented. They emerge through intra-actions. The cut between observer and observed is not given in advance; it is enacted. Knowledge is not a mirror held up to a world already divided into stable things. Knowledge is a material-discursive practice that helps produce the boundaries through which things become knowable.

This is a brutal lesson for AI discourse. Much of the public conversation about AI still assumes a representational model: there is a world, there is data about the world, there is a model trained on the data, and then there are outputs that either correspond or fail to correspond to the world. Within that frame, the main problem becomes accuracy. Does the model say true things? Does the image correspond to the prompt? Does the generated video obey physical common sense? Does the answer cite the right sources?

These questions matter, but they are not enough. The more fundamental issue is that AI systems participate in the drawing of boundaries before accuracy can even be assessed. They sort what counts as relevant context. They compress cultural archives into latent structures. They infer categories from datasets. They transform ambiguity into completion. They produce outputs that appear as coherent objects: a portrait, a paragraph, a plan, a story, a diagnosis, a workflow, a synthetic memory. Each output is the result of many cuts: between foreground and background, style and content, user intention and model inference, probability and assertion, pattern and noise.

The agential cut is therefore not an abstract philosophical ornament. It is a practical analytic tool. It asks: what distinction did this system just make, and who is responsible for it?

A generated image of “a doctor” performs a cut in visual culture. A generated summary of a protest performs a cut in political memory. A generated classroom lesson performs a cut in pedagogy. A generated historical reconstruction performs a cut in public imagination. A generated interface prototype performs a cut in design possibility. These cuts may appear small, but they accumulate. They become defaults. Defaults become habits. Habits become infrastructures of expectation.

Generative AI is not dangerous merely because it can be wrong. It is dangerous because it can make its cuts feel natural.

## 3. Bateson’s Circuit: Mind Is Not Inside the Model

If Barad teaches us to look at the cut, Gregory Bateson teaches us to look at the circuit. Mind, in Bateson’s ecological account, is not sealed inside an individual skull. It is immanent in pathways of difference: organism plus environment, message plus response, perception plus correction, part plus whole. The elementary unit of mind is not the isolated thinker but the completed circuit.

This matters because AI systems are often discussed as if intelligence were located inside the model. The model “knows,” “understands,” “reasons,” “hallucinates,” “plans,” or “remembers.” These verbs may be useful shorthand, but they mislead when they isolate cognition inside the technical object. The working intelligence of an AI system is distributed across a circuit: training data, model architecture, interface design, prompt conventions, user expectations, organizational incentives, feedback systems, evaluation metrics, moderation policies, APIs, documentation, and downstream uses.

The “mind” of generative AI, if the term is to be used at all, is not in the weights alone. It is in the circuit completed by the user. The prompt is not an external command applied to an inert system. It is part of the cognitive ecology. The interface is not a neutral window into model capacity. It trains users into particular forms of request, attention, trust, correction, and repetition. The output is not an endpoint. It becomes new input: copied, edited, believed, rejected, posted, remixed, cited, or used to make decisions.

This means that the ethical analysis of AI cannot stop at model behavior. It must analyze the whole loop. What kind of user does the system assume? What kinds of prompting does it reward? What kinds of uncertainty does it hide? What kinds of correction does it permit? What kinds of outputs does it make frictionless? What kinds of misuse does it make imaginative? What kinds of responsibility does it distribute, and what kinds does it centralize?

Bateson’s ecology also warns against a common pathology: confusing local optimization with systemic intelligence. A system may improve a metric while degrading the ecology that gives the metric meaning. A recommender system may increase engagement while corroding public attention. A generative model may increase productivity while flattening style. A synthetic media system may increase visual possibility while weakening evidentiary trust. In each case, the unit of analysis cannot be the isolated output. It must be the circuit of effects.

AI ethics often asks whether the machine did the right thing. Cybernetic ethics asks whether the loop remains viable.

## 4. Second-Order Cybernetics: The Observer Is Already Inside

First-order cybernetics studies systems as if the observer could stand outside them. Second-order cybernetics ruins that comfort. The observer is part of the system observed. Description is intervention. To observe a system is to participate in its distinctions, its feedback, its possible futures.

This is especially important for generative AI because users do not simply inspect outputs from the outside. They co-produce them. They prompt, select, refine, regenerate, curate, and publish. They learn the system’s tendencies. The system learns, directly or indirectly, from patterns of use, preference, and evaluation. Even when model weights are fixed, the human-system ecology changes. Users adapt to the apparatus. The apparatus adapts the user.

This second-order condition breaks two comforting myths. The first is the myth of neutral use: the idea that AI systems are passive tools whose effects depend entirely on user intention. The second is the myth of autonomous machine agency: the idea that AI systems act independently of human institutions, datasets, infrastructures, and interpretive contexts. Both myths are evasions. The first hides the agency of the apparatus. The second hides the responsibility of the people and institutions that build, deploy, frame, and profit from it.

A second-order account insists that responsibility cannot be assigned only after harm occurs. Responsibility must be designed into the loop. The user should be able to see what kind of system they are engaging. The interface should expose uncertainty, provenance, constraint, and transformation. The model’s behavior should not be presented as an oracle speaking from nowhere. The system should make visible the fact that every output is a situated construction.

This is where design becomes ethics. Not ethics as a checklist after engineering, but ethics as the architecture of participation. A responsible AI interface does not merely prevent forbidden outputs. It teaches users how to remain answerable for the outputs they solicit, accept, revise, and circulate.

## 5. Apparatus: From Camera Programs to Generative Programs

Flusser’s account of photography provides a powerful bridge between older media theory and generative AI. For Flusser, the camera is not simply a tool held by a sovereign human subject. It is an apparatus: a programmed system of possibilities. The photographer plays against the camera’s program, but the program also defines what can easily appear. The image is therefore not merely the product of human intention. It is the result of a game between operator and apparatus.

Generative AI generalizes this apparatus condition across media. The prompt box becomes a camera without a lens, a writing instrument without a stable author, a search engine without a fixed index, a simulation engine without a single world. The user “plays” against the model’s program, but the program is difficult to inspect. Its possibilities are not arranged as visible mechanical settings. They are latent, statistical, emergent, and interface-mediated.

This creates a new form of black-box aesthetics. The user learns the apparatus through trial, error, folk knowledge, prompt recipes, style tags, system quirks, and repeated regeneration. The apparatus becomes familiar without becoming transparent. It can feel magical because it collapses the distance between description and manifestation. Say the thing; see the thing. Ask the question; receive the answer. Specify the atmosphere; watch the world appear.

But the magic is not outside history. It is trained on history. The generated image carries sedimented visual conventions. The generated prose carries institutional habits of explanation. The generated interface carries inherited assumptions about usability, control, and attention. The generated plan carries managerial and bureaucratic models of action. The apparatus appears fluid because it has already absorbed the rigidity of countless prior forms.

The task of critique is not to declare the apparatus false. The task is to read the program of possibility. What does it make easy? What does it make difficult? What does it beautify? What does it normalize? What does it refuse? What does it overproduce? What does it forget?

A philosophy of generative AI must therefore become a philosophy of apparatus literacy.

## 6. Legibility and Metis: The Politics of Simplification

James C. Scott’s critique of state legibility offers another essential layer. Modern administrative systems make societies governable by simplifying them. Names, maps, censuses, property records, standardized units, and bureaucratic categories do not merely describe social reality. They reshape it. They make certain forms of knowledge visible to institutions while destroying or marginalizing local, situated, practical knowledge—what Scott calls metis.

AI systems inherit this politics of simplification. Training data is not the world; it is a legibility regime. Labels are not neutral; they are cuts. Benchmarks are not merely evaluations; they define what counts as performance. Content policies are not merely safety layers; they encode institutional judgments about risk, representation, and permissibility. Model outputs are not merely responses; they often translate messy context into administratively convenient form.

The danger is not only bias in the narrow sense. Bias suggests a deviation from a neutral baseline. But many AI harms arise from the baseline itself: from the conversion of lived complexity into categories optimized for prediction, ranking, generation, and control. A model that “works” may still work by flattening the forms of knowledge it cannot recognize.

This is why metis matters. Local knowledge is not simply data that has not yet been captured. It is often knowledge that changes when captured. Tacit practice, cultural context, embodied judgment, relational obligation, historical memory, and situated meaning do not always survive extraction into a dataset or prompt. The system may become more powerful by becoming less answerable to the textures it processes.

Generative AI intensifies this because it does not merely classify; it re-expresses. It can produce the appearance of local knowledge without belonging to the practices that sustain it. It can simulate dialect, ritual, style, testimony, expertise, intimacy, or memory. This simulation may be useful, playful, or beautiful. It may also become extractive, counterfeit, or epistemically violent.

The ethical question is not “Can the model generate this?” The ethical question is “What relation to the source world does this generation establish?” Is it quotation, transformation, parody, appropriation, pedagogy, repair, extraction, or fabrication? Does the system preserve origin visibility, or does it launder cultural specificity into generic style? Does it invite accountability, or does it produce frictionless imitation?

AI ethics needs a metis test: what situated knowledge is being simplified, and who bears the cost of that simplification?

## 7. World Models: Dreaming as Technical Operation

Contemporary AI discourse increasingly speaks of “world models.” In machine learning, the term can refer to systems that learn compressed representations of environments and use those representations to predict future states or guide action. The phrase is technically specific in some contexts and wildly inflated in others. Its rhetorical force, however, is unmistakable. AI is no longer imagined merely as a classifier or generator. It is imagined as a simulator of possible worlds.

This shift matters. A world model is not just a map. It is a compressed operational environment. It allows an agent to anticipate consequences, rehearse actions, and navigate possibilities. In some architectures, the model learns a latent representation of observations, predicts future latent states, and enables a controller to act within an imagined or “dreamed” environment. The dream is not fantasy in the ordinary sense. It is a technical space for policy formation.

The cultural implications are enormous. If models become world-modeling systems, then generative media becomes a training ground for perception and action. A synthetic video does not merely depict a scene; it tests continuity. An agent simulation does not merely imitate social life; it proposes dynamics of memory, planning, and interaction. A generated design prototype does not merely show an interface; it anticipates a workflow. A generated historical scene does not merely illustrate the past; it rehearses a public relation to memory.

Here again, the representational question is insufficient. The problem is not only whether the world model is accurate. The problem is what kind of action it makes thinkable. Every world model contains an implicit politics of possibility. It says: these are the entities that matter; these are the actions available; these are the constraints; these are the futures worth predicting; these are the differences that make a difference.

A world model is therefore also a value model, even when it does not admit it.

This is where the lineage returns to Barad. The world model does not stand outside the world. It participates in the worlding of the world. It performs cuts. It stabilizes entities. It proposes causal textures. It makes some futures easier to rehearse than others. When embedded in platforms, workflows, classrooms, studios, bureaucracies, and creative tools, it becomes a practical metaphysics.

The question “Does AI understand the world?” should be replaced by a harsher one: what worlds do AI systems operationalize?

## 8. Constructionism and the Hope of Instruments

The critique so far may sound severe, but it should not lead to rejection. The point is not that generative AI is inherently corrupting. The point is that its powers must be understood at the right scale. If these systems are apparatuses of world-making, then they can also become instruments of reflection, learning, and construction.

Seymour Papert’s constructionism offers one hopeful thread. The computer, for Papert, is not merely a teaching machine. It can become an object-to-think-with. In a microworld, learners do not passively receive knowledge; they build, test, debug, and internalize powerful ideas through manipulation. Error becomes material. Debugging becomes epistemology. The learner becomes a designer of relations.

Generative AI could extend this tradition, but only if designed against its own worst tendencies. The danger is that AI systems often hide the very processes that would make them educational. They produce polished outputs too quickly. They collapse struggle into fluency. They replace the learner’s construction with the model’s completion. They can make users feel powerful while quietly weakening their contact with the material constraints of thought.

A constructionist AI system would do the opposite. It would expose intermediate states. It would show alternatives. It would preserve failed attempts. It would let users inspect transformations. It would make uncertainty manipulable. It would treat prompts not as magic spells but as editable structures of intention. It would encourage debugging rather than mere regeneration. It would make the user smarter about the system and smarter about the domain.

The difference is decisive. A bad generative interface says: “Tell me what you want, and I will produce it.” A better one says: “Let us examine how this possible world is being constructed.”

This is the passage from AI as vending machine to AI as epistemic instrument.

## 9. Design-as-Conversation: Interface Ethics After the Oracle

The dominant interface metaphor for AI is still the oracle: a blank box, a prompt, a response. This is a poor design metaphor because it stages the system as a disembodied authority. Even when the model is uncertain, the interface often performs confidence. Even when the output is constructed, the interface performs immediacy. Even when the answer depends on hidden context, the interface performs simplicity.

Design-as-conversation offers a better model. In second-order cybernetic design, conversation is not just chat. It is a structure of mutual adjustment. Participants change through interaction. Meaning emerges through turn-taking, repair, clarification, disagreement, and reframing. A conversational system does not merely answer; it helps constitute a shared problem space.

For generative AI, this means the interface should not be optimized only for output speed or frictionless completion. It should be optimized for responsible participation. This requires several design principles.

First, expose the cut. The system should show what it treated as relevant, what it ignored, what assumptions it made, and what distinctions structured the output.

Second, preserve provenance. When outputs transform source material, the system should keep visible relations to origin, version, context, and degree of alteration.

Third, label epistemic status. A fact, inference, guess, simulation, fictionalization, analogy, and design proposal should not wear the same costume.

Fourth, support counterfactual comparison. Users should be able to ask not only “What is the answer?” but “What other worlds could this system have produced, and why did this one appear?”

Fifth, protect metis. The system should allow local, situated, user-provided knowledge to override generic completion, and it should mark where generic training patterns may be insufficient.

Sixth, make refusal educational. Safety boundaries should not appear as arbitrary walls. They should explain the kind of harm or uncertainty at stake without becoming patronizing or theatrical.

Seventh, keep the human in the loop without pretending the human is outside the loop. Human review is not a magic absolution. The interface must help the human understand what they are reviewing.

These principles shift AI design from answer production to responsibility architecture.

## 10. Against Human-Centered Evasion

A predictable response to all of this is to call for “human-centered AI.” The phrase sounds good. It promises dignity, usability, agency, and care. But it can also become an evasion. Too often, human-centered design preserves the fantasy of a stable human subject standing outside the system, using tools to achieve pre-given goals. It centers “the human” without asking which human, in what institution, with what forms of power, mediated by what infrastructures, benefiting from what simplifications, and protected from what consequences.

The alternative is not machine-centered design. That would merely invert the error. The alternative is relational design. In a relational frame, the unit of analysis is not the human alone or the machine alone but the configured relation: user-system-environment-archive-institution-world. Agency is distributed, but responsibility is not dissolved. The point is not to say “everyone and everything is responsible,” which usually means no one is. The point is to trace how responsibility should be assigned within the circuit.

Relational design also helps us avoid the romance of AI autonomy. Generative systems are not alien minds descending from nowhere. They are built from human archives, labor, institutions, incentives, and infrastructures. Even their weirdness is historical. Even their hallucinations have conditions. Even their apparent spontaneity is shaped by architectures of capture and optimization.

The goal, then, is not to restore human mastery. Mastery is part of the problem. Nor is the goal to celebrate machinic creativity as liberation from the human. The goal is to build systems where world-making can be inspected, contested, revised, and answered for.

We do not need human-centered AI. We need accountable world-centered AI: systems designed around the consequences of the worlds they help enact.

## 11. The Case Archive: What to Study

This framework suggests a concrete research archive. The objects of study should not be limited to model papers or final outputs. The archive should include the full apparatus of generative world-making.

A first case cluster would examine text-to-image systems. The question would not simply be whether they produce realistic images, but how they encode visual culture as promptable possibility. What styles become named? What identities become stereotyped? What visual histories become generic atmosphere? What kinds of labor disappear behind the prompt?

A second case cluster would examine text-to-video and world-simulation discourse. Here the key issue is the relation between generated continuity and world understanding. When a model produces coherent motion, what kind of physical, cinematic, and cultural causality is being simulated? What breaks? What remains stable? What does the interface encourage users to believe about the generated world?

A third case cluster would examine AI agents and simulated societies. Generative agents with memory, planning, reflection, and social interaction invite analysis as artificial social worlds. The question is not whether they are “real people.” The question is what theory of social life is embedded in their architecture. What counts as memory? What counts as a plan? What counts as a relationship? What counts as believable behavior?

A fourth case cluster would examine AI writing assistants in academic and bureaucratic contexts. These systems do not merely help compose prose. They normalize explanatory styles, citation habits, institutional tones, and acceptable forms of argument. They may improve fluency while narrowing rhetorical risk. They may democratize access while standardizing thought.

A fifth case cluster would examine creative coding and design tools. Here the question becomes constructionist: does the AI help users build a theory of the system they are manipulating, or does it simply generate polished artifacts that obscure their own conditions?

Across all cases, the method is the same: trace the cuts, map the circuit, inspect the apparatus, identify the simplifications, and ask who is responsible for the world that results.

## 12. Toward an Ethics of Mattering

An ethics of generative AI cannot be reduced to content moderation, bias mitigation, or preference alignment, though all may be necessary. The deeper ethical issue is mattering. What is made to matter? Who is made to matter? Which differences become actionable? Which differences are ignored? Which histories are compressed into style? Which communities become raw material? Which futures are rehearsed until they feel inevitable?

Barad’s phrase “ethics of mattering” is useful because it refuses to separate ontology from responsibility. If boundaries are enacted, then ethics begins at the cut. If systems participate in world-making, then responsibility begins before output. It begins in data collection, labeling, architecture, interface design, default settings, evaluation, deployment, and use. It begins in the decision to make some things promptable and others invisible.

This also means that refusal can be ethical, but refusal alone is not enough. A system that refuses harmful requests while continuing to obscure its ordinary cuts remains ethically thin. The more ambitious task is to make the apparatus pedagogical. Users should come away not only with an output but with a better understanding of the world-making operation they just participated in.

The highest form of AI safety is not a locked door. It is cultivated discernment inside a visible system.

## 13. Conclusion: Make the Cut Visible

Generative AI is not merely a new media tool. It is a new cultural apparatus for producing plausible worlds at speed. It fuses archive, interface, model, simulation, and user action into a recursive circuit. It inherits cybernetic dreams of feedback and control, constructionist hopes for objects-to-think-with, apparatus-theoretical anxieties about black boxes, and political dangers of legibility without metis.

The task is not to decide once and for all whether AI is intelligent, creative, conscious, derivative, dangerous, or useful. Those questions are too blunt. The better task is to ask what kinds of cuts AI systems perform, what circuits they complete, what worlds they rehearse, what forms of knowledge they simplify, and what responsibilities they redistribute.

A responsible generative system would not pretend to speak from nowhere. It would expose its assumptions. It would preserve provenance. It would invite correction. It would distinguish fact from inference, simulation from memory, and style from source. It would show the user not only the generated object but the apparatus of generation. It would make world-making slower where slowness protects meaning, more transparent where opacity hides power, and more contestable where defaults harden into reality.

The future of AI ethics will not be won by better answers alone. It will be won by better cuts.

Or, more precisely: by systems that let us see the cut, argue with it, revise it, and remain responsible for the worlds it brings forth.
