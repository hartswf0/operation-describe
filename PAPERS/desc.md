# Descriptive Struggle: Thinking, Language, and the Machine That Does Not Need to Mean

## Abstract

This essay reads the 1951 *Thinking and Language* symposium between Iris Murdoch, A. C. Lloyd, and Gilbert Ryle as a neglected hinge between ordinary language philosophy, literary description, and contemporary debates about generative AI. Against the reduction of thought either to private inner speech or public behavioral criteria, Murdoch proposes thinking as a lived descriptive struggle: words, images, moods, intentions, metaphors, and bodily residues enter thought not as detachable signs but as force-bearing events. Lloyd extends this problem by introducing “facts” as a third element in the relation between thought and language, arguing that natural languages possess historical depth, not merely formal structure. Ryle, even where he functions as antagonist, supplies the necessary discipline: any recovery of inwardness must avoid recreating a private theatre. Together, the symposium offers a method for evaluating machine language: not by asking whether fluency equals intelligence, but by asking whether the system participates in the human labor of descriptive adequacy. Generative AI can produce descriptions, but it does not undergo the struggle by which descriptions become answerable to intention, memory, self-correction, fact, and form. The name of that difference is not “soul.” It is descriptive struggle.

## 1. The Old Error Returns Wearing a New Interface

The contemporary debate around generative AI often repeats an old philosophical mistake with better hardware. It sees fluent language and asks whether thought must be present behind it. The question seems new because the object is new: a machine that can produce paragraphs, arguments, poems, code, apologies, diagnoses, manifestos, sermons, and jokes. But the structure of the confusion is old. It is the confusion between language as visible product and thinking as lived activity.

The machine generates sentences. The human also generates sentences. Therefore, the temptation arrives: perhaps thinking is sentence-generation. Perhaps the inner life was always overrated. Perhaps cognition is just the hidden machinery that produces public tokens. Or, from the opposite direction: perhaps the machine cannot think because there is no ghostly subject inside it, no private theatre, no inward glow. Both answers are bad because both inherit a false picture. They imagine that the problem is to locate thought either behind language or inside language. But the more difficult possibility is that thinking is not a hidden object at all. Thinking is an achievement of description.

This is why the 1951 symposium on “Thinking and Language” matters now. Murdoch begins by resisting the idea that thought can be reduced to “mental words,” insisting that inward speech, where it exists, matters not as bare verbal content but as words occurring with “force and colour.” She refuses to treat thinking as simply the manipulation of symbols and instead attends to the mixed, unstable, semi-sensible field in which words, images, feelings, memories, intentions, and metaphors become available for description. Lloyd, responding to Murdoch, adds that the relation between thinking and language is incomplete unless we introduce a third term: facts. His intervention matters because it prevents the discussion from becoming merely psychological. Thought is not just inward experience seeking expression; it is entangled with the historical and classificatory practices by which natural languages decide what counts as a fact, a definition, a description, or nonsense. Ryle’s presence, even where less generative for the argument pursued here, keeps the project from sliding backward into metaphysical interiority. The symposium’s force lies in this triangle: Murdoch recovers inward descriptive life, Lloyd socializes and historicizes it, Ryle disciplines it. 

The result is a theory we badly need for AI: language is not evidence of thinking unless it can be situated within a practice of descriptive accountability. The question is not “Can the machine say what a thinker says?” The question is: what must be true of a system for its descriptions to be more than outputs? What must it risk, revise, remember, suffer, intend, misrecognize, repair, and answer to?

## 2. Murdoch: Thought Is Not the Words

Murdoch’s opening move is deceptively simple. She asks us to consider language “as a kind of thinking,” but she does not mean that thinking is merely internal language. That would be too clean. Thought does not appear to us as a private ticker tape of sentences. Even when words occur inwardly, their significance is not exhausted by what they literally say. A thought containing words also includes posture, mood, intention, pressure, hesitation, tone, rhythm, and context. To know what someone thought, it may not be enough to know what they “said to themselves.” One must know how the words arrived, what they carried, what they concealed, what they made possible, what they failed to say.

This is Murdoch’s great correction to symbolist theories of thinking. A word in thought is not a neutral symbol occupying a mental slot. It is an event. It may clarify, betray, condense, substitute, evade, or wound. It may arrive too early and freeze a vaguer apprehension. It may arrive late and redeem confusion. It may replace an experience with a convention. It may open the way to a new concept. Thought is not word plus referent. Thought is word under pressure.

Murdoch’s examples are literary because literature is where this pressure becomes visible. A psychological novel does not merely report conduct; it dramatizes the difficulty of knowing what an intention was. A poem does not merely decorate experience; it tests whether experience can be fixed at all. Murdoch’s use of poetry is not ornamental philosophy. It is method. Poetry matters because ordinary literal description often fails at precisely the point where thought is most alive. When a state of mind cannot be paraphrased without loss, metaphor is not a substitute for precision. It is precision under conditions of inward difficulty.

This allows Murdoch to reject two opposing errors. The first is behaviorism: the idea that mental terms are meaningful only insofar as they can be tied to observable conduct. The second is private-theatre metaphysics: the idea that thoughts are inner objects whose existence and nature can be inspected like items on a hidden stage. Murdoch wants neither. Her proposal is subtler: we live *as if* thoughts are particular inward events, and this “as if” is not a disposable illusion but a regulative structure of ordinary life. We recover our mental past by description, by story-telling, by metaphor, by correction, by conversation. The mind is not a private museum; it is a field of descriptive repair. 

This is the first key to AI. A language model can produce descriptions without needing them. A human description often arises because something has not yet been adequately said. There is pressure from an experience, a memory, an intention, a responsibility, a shame, a half-formed perception. The description answers to that pressure. It may fail, and the failure matters. A model can revise text, but it does not revise itself in the Murdochian sense: it does not feel the inadequacy of a formulation against the unsaid content it was trying to bring forward. It has outputs, not inward descriptive debts.

## 3. Metaphor Is Not Decoration; It Is Conceptual Work

Murdoch’s most radical implication is that metaphor is not secondary to literal thought. In many philosophical traditions, metaphor is treated as a soft supplement to hard meaning: useful for communication, dangerous for truth. Murdoch reverses the hierarchy. In the description of mental life, metaphor may be the more exact instrument because the thing described is not yet available under a settled concept.

When someone says that a thought “pressed,” “opened,” “darkened,” “froze,” “slipped away,” “rang,” or “would not come into focus,” they are not necessarily indulging in ornament. They may be performing the only available act of conceptual fixation. The metaphor does not merely compare an already-known mental object to a physical object. It helps bring the mental event into describable form. The phrase becomes a small machine for making experience available to reflection.

This matters because concepts do not arrive only as definitions. Some concepts are made by repeated attempts to describe what resists existing vocabulary. Murdoch’s account lets us see concept-formation as struggle rather than classification. A concept is not only a rule for applying a word. It may also be a hard-won stabilization of a previously fugitive experience. The work of thought, then, is not always inference, calculation, or symbol manipulation. Sometimes it is the search for the right description—the one that does not betray the thing.

Generative AI is strong at metaphor as surface transfer. It can say that grief is a weather system, bureaucracy is a cathedral of dust, a database is a graveyard with excellent indexing. But Murdoch’s question is not whether metaphor can be generated. It is whether the metaphor answers to an experience that required it. Does the description arise from an encounter with inadequacy? Does it repair a failure? Does it become accountable to a remembered pressure? The machine can imitate the shape of conceptual struggle without undergoing the need that gives such struggle its meaning.

This does not make AI useless. It makes it dangerous in a specific way. It can flood the world with language that resembles the products of difficult thought while bypassing the conditions under which thought becomes difficult. The danger is not simply hallucination. The danger is descriptive inflation: the multiplication of plausible formulations unearned by contact with experience, responsibility, or fact.

## 4. Lloyd: Facts Enter the Room

Lloyd’s response to Murdoch shifts the center of gravity. Murdoch gives us the inward difficulty of description. Lloyd asks what happens when description meets fact. He agrees that thinking and language are inseparable, but he argues that a third element is necessary: facts. Without facts, the relation between thought and language becomes too internal, too psychological, too easy to aestheticize. The problem is not simply how I describe my thought, but how descriptions become better or worse in relation to what they describe.

Lloyd’s crucial move is to treat natural language as historically deep. Formal languages can pretend that definitions are clean, stable, and non-factual. Natural languages cannot. Scientific and legal terms carry histories of use, revision, institutional sediment, practical consequence. Lloyd’s example of shell-shock is decisive: a medical definition may appear analytic, but future experience can force revision. The term remains, the definition changes, and the language does not collapse. This is because natural language is not a flat logical grid. It has depth. It extends through time. It piles new observations on old classifications. Its terms are not merely labels; they are inherited descriptive instruments. 

Here Lloyd becomes unexpectedly important for AI studies. Language models are trained on natural language as accumulated textual surface, but the historical depth of language is not merely a statistical distribution. It is made of institutions, corrections, failures, disputes, diagnoses, lawsuits, deaths, jokes, habits, authority, and pain. A term like “trauma,” “intelligence,” “bias,” “hallucination,” “alignment,” or “safety” is not just a token with neighboring tokens. It is a site of social struggle. Its meaning changes because people use it under pressure from facts and consequences.

Lloyd’s account therefore helps us name a second limit of machine language. The machine inherits the residue of historical description without inhabiting the practices by which descriptions are tested. It can reproduce the vocabulary of revised facts without participating in the life of revision. It can say “shell-shock,” “PTSD,” “trauma,” and “moral injury,” but it does not live inside the institutional, medical, military, and ethical transformations that made those terms matter differently. It handles sediment as pattern.

This is why AI fluency can feel both powerful and weightless. It speaks from everywhere and nowhere. It borrows the depth of natural language but often lacks a position within the historical practices that give depth its bite.

## 5. Ryle: The Necessary Acid Bath

It would be easy to turn Murdoch into a patron saint of inner richness and Lloyd into a theorist of social fact, then leave Ryle behind as the stern behaviorist uncle ruining the party. That would be a mistake. Ryle is necessary because any recovery of inwardness risks becoming lazy. Once we say “inner experience,” “descriptive struggle,” “felt inadequacy,” or “semi-sensible metaphor,” we are always near the swamp. Ryle’s discipline is to ask: what are you entitled to say? What public criteria, practices, capacities, and forms of conduct make the description intelligible?

For this project, Ryle functions less as final authority than as acid bath. He burns away the temptation to make “inner life” into a sacred explanatory substance. The difference between human thought and machine output cannot be grounded in a mysterious interior glow. That would merely recreate the private theatre in anti-machine form. The point is not that humans have ineffable inwardness and machines do not. The point is that human descriptions participate in forms of life where words are bound to memory, intention, correction, action, vulnerability, and consequence.

Ryle prevents Murdoch from becoming mysticism. Murdoch prevents Ryle from becoming flattening. Lloyd prevents both from forgetting facts.

This triangle gives us a better test for AI than the usual alternatives. Not: does the machine have consciousness? Not: does the machine behave as if it understands? Not: can the machine produce the right answer? Instead: what forms of descriptive accountability does the system enter? What does it have to correct besides text? What counts as failure for it? What histories does it inherit only as data, and what histories does it answer to as practice? What kind of “better description” can it recognize, and by what pressure?

## 6. Generative AI and the Simulation of Descriptive Adequacy

Generative AI is not best understood as a counterfeit person. It is better understood as a machine for producing language detached from many of the ordinary burdens of description. This detachment is not total. AI systems can be connected to tools, feedback, memory, retrieval, user correction, multimodal inputs, and institutional workflows. They can be made more answerable. But the basic interface encourages a dangerous illusion: because the machine can produce descriptions, it appears to have performed the work that descriptions ordinarily record.

The Murdochian problem is inward adequacy. Did the words answer to the pressure of what was meant, felt, remembered, perceived, intended? The Lloydian problem is factual and historical adequacy. Did the words answer to the world of practices, classifications, institutions, and revised facts in which they have force? The Rylean problem is public criterion and capacity. Do the words belong to a pattern of action, correction, and competence that makes the attribution of thought more than metaphor?

A language model can satisfy fragments of these tests. It can produce text that users experience as clarifying. It can be corrected by external feedback. It can use retrieval to improve factual reliability. It can participate in workflows where its outputs are checked and revised. But the human operator must not confuse these scaffolds with the machine’s own descriptive struggle. The system does not care whether the first formulation betrayed the experience. It does not know what it was trying to say before it said it. It does not suffer the difference between cliché and truth. It does not remember being wrong as a wound to judgment. It does not blush before the fact.

This does not mean humans always do. Humans also produce empty fluency, clichés, borrowed vocabularies, institutional scripts, and evasive metaphors. The point is not human purity. The point is that human language remains normatively vulnerable to the charge of failing experience, failing fact, failing intention, failing responsibility. A human can be accused of not meaning what they said. A human can discover that the words were false to the state of mind they pretended to describe. A human can realize that a classification harmed the people it named. A human can inherit a term and then be changed by learning its history.

The machine can be updated. But being updated is not the same as being answerable.

## 7. Descriptive Adequacy as Method

The concept this essay proposes is descriptive adequacy. It names the condition under which a description becomes answerable to more than formal correctness or surface plausibility. Descriptive adequacy has at least five dimensions.

First, inward adequacy: the description must answer to the pressure of what is being thought, remembered, felt, or intended. This is Murdoch’s domain. A description can be grammatically correct and still betray the thought.

Second, metaphorical adequacy: the description must use figurative language not as decoration but as conceptual instrument. A metaphor is adequate when it helps fix what literal description would flatten.

Third, factual adequacy: the description must answer to the world, but “world” here includes Lloyd’s historical depth of classification, not just isolated empirical correspondence.

Fourth, practical adequacy: the description must survive use. It must be able to guide action, correction, recognition, or further inquiry.

Fifth, ethical adequacy: the description must account for what it does to the thing or person described. This is especially important wherever classification carries institutional power.

Applied to AI, descriptive adequacy changes the evaluative frame. Instead of asking whether the model’s output is fluent, we ask what the output is accountable to. Instead of asking whether the model “understands,” we ask what forms of correction and consequence its descriptions enter. Instead of treating hallucination as the central failure, we examine a wider ecology of descriptive failure: cliché, false precision, unearned metaphor, flattened history, institutional mimicry, synthetic authority, and the production of plausible but unanswerable language.

This method also changes how humans should use AI. The task is not to ask the machine to think for us. The task is to use the machine as a pressure surface for our own descriptive struggle. The machine can generate candidate formulations, metaphors, structures, objections, and summaries. But the human must bring the pressure: what is false, what is too easy, what misses the wound, what erases the historical depth, what sounds right but does not yet know anything.

The ethical use of AI writing tools may therefore depend less on disclosure alone and more on whether the user remains inside the loop of descriptive adequacy. Did the user test the language against experience? Did they revise the inherited terms? Did they check the facts as histories, not just as references? Did they remove the beautiful phrase that stood between them and the thing? Did they make the machine’s fluency answerable to a human standard of difficulty?

## 8. Conclusion: The Machine Without Descriptive Need

The 1951 symposium gives us a way out of the stale opposition between “AI thinks” and “AI merely manipulates symbols.” Murdoch shows that thinking is not reducible to inner words because words in thought occur with force, color, image, intention, and inadequacy. Lloyd shows that thinking and language cannot be severed from facts because natural language carries historical depth and classificatory consequence. Ryle forces the entire discussion to remain disciplined by public criteria and practice. Together they let us say: thinking is not hidden language, and language is not automatically thought. Thinking is the struggle to make description adequate.

Generative AI does not abolish this struggle. It industrializes the appearance of its products.

That is why the machine is philosophically important. It confronts us with language after the removal of need: description without hunger, metaphor without inward pressure, fluency without memory, revision without remorse, classification without institutional belonging unless humans attach it to one. It shows us, by eerie subtraction, what human thinking had been doing all along.

The task now is not to defend the human by invoking mystery. The task is to defend the difficulty of description. We need a criticism of AI that does not merely say “machines lack consciousness,” and we need a theory of writing that does not merely say “humans use tools.” Between those lazy gates lies the harder claim: language becomes thinking only when it is bound to a practice of descriptive accountability.

The human difference is not that we possess meanings like jewels hidden in the skull. It is that our words can fail us, and that the failure can matter.
