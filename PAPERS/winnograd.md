# Design After Cognitivism: Winograd and Flores, Breakdown, and the New Foundation for AI Design

## Abstract

*Terry Winograd and Fernando Flores’s* **Understanding Computers and Cognition: A New Foundation for Design** is often remembered as a landmark text in human-computer interaction, design theory, and the critique of artificial intelligence. But its deeper importance lies elsewhere: it is a philosophical intervention against the metaphysics of cognitivism. Against the fantasy that intelligence consists in the manipulation of internal representations, Winograd and Flores argue that human understanding is situated, linguistic, embodied, historical, and practical. Computers do not “understand” in the human sense because understanding is not merely the processing of symbols. It is participation in a world of concern, action, breakdown, interpretation, and commitment. The book therefore does not simply ask how computers should be designed; it asks what kind of beings humans are such that computers become meaningful tools for them at all. This chapter reads Winograd and Flores through Heidegger, Austin, Maturana, Varela, Wittgenstein, Dreyfus, Suchman, and Dourish in order to recover its still-radical claim: good computational systems are not artificial minds but accountable media for coordinating human action.

---

## 1. The old dream: computation as mind

The dominant mythology of artificial intelligence begins with a seduction: that intelligence is a kind of inner procedure, and that if the procedure can be made explicit, it can be reproduced in a machine. In this mythology, thought is a symbolic engine. The mind receives inputs, constructs representations, applies rules, searches a space of possible solutions, and produces outputs. Human cognition becomes an information-processing system; the computer becomes its technical mirror.

This model was not merely a technical hypothesis. It was an ontology. It decided in advance what counted as intelligence, what counted as action, and what counted as a problem. To think was to manipulate symbols. To know was to possess an adequate representation. To act was to execute a plan. To communicate was to transfer information. To design was to build formal systems that could model, optimize, automate, or replace these procedures.

Winograd and Flores enter precisely here, at the point where a technical research program has silently become a theory of the human. *Understanding Computers and Cognition* is not a rejection of computers. It is a rejection of the mistaken anthropology built into many computational dreams. The error is not that machines compute. The error is that human beings are redescribed as if they primarily compute.

Their question is therefore not, “Can computers think?” The more dangerous question is: what has to be forgotten about human life in order for that question to appear natural?

The answer is severe. One must forget embodiment. One must forget history. One must forget social obligation. One must forget that language does things before it describes things. One must forget that tools are meaningful because they are woven into practices. One must forget that action is rarely the execution of a complete prior plan. One must forget breakdown: the moment when ordinary practical involvement fails and the world becomes visible as a problem.

Winograd and Flores’s “new foundation for design” begins by refusing this forgetting.

## 2. Heidegger’s broken hammer and the visibility of design

The most important design object in this lineage is not the computer. It is Heidegger’s hammer.

In *Being and Time*, Heidegger distinguishes between things encountered as present-at-hand and things encountered as ready-to-hand. A tool in ordinary use does not appear as a detached object with properties. The hammer is not first experienced as a wooden handle attached to a metal head. It withdraws into the activity of hammering. It is absorbed into a practical world: nails, boards, shelter, work, skill, purpose. The tool is most fully itself when it disappears into use.

It becomes visible when something goes wrong.

The hammer breaks. It is too heavy, missing, awkward, misplaced. Suddenly the smooth activity is interrupted. The tool appears as an object of attention. The world that supported the action becomes visible as a network of dependencies. Breakdown is not an accident outside use; it is the moment that reveals the structure of use.

For Winograd and Flores, this is not merely a philosophical curiosity. It is a design principle. Computers are not meaningful because they instantiate formal procedures. They are meaningful because they enter human practices. They become part of writing, filing, requesting, promising, remembering, scheduling, repairing, negotiating, teaching, persuading, and coordinating. Their design must therefore be evaluated not only by what they can calculate, but by how they participate in human worlds of action.

This reframes the interface. An interface is not a neutral surface between user and machine. It is a practical field where certain actions become available, certain distinctions are made visible, certain commitments are recorded, and certain failures become possible. A system always carries a theory of the user. If the user is modeled as an information processor, the system will privilege inputs, outputs, menus, commands, and optimization. If the user is understood as a situated actor, the system must attend to timing, interpretation, recovery, context, social meaning, and responsibility.

Design, then, is not decoration laid over computation. Design is applied ontology. It decides what kind of world the user is assumed to inhabit.

The broken hammer teaches that the designer’s real archive is not the ideal workflow but the breakdown log: the missed handoff, the failed interpretation, the ambiguous request, the unkept promise, the hidden dependency, the moment when the tool stops disappearing and becomes a problem.

## 3. Language is not information; language is action

The second great displacement in Winograd and Flores comes from speech-act theory. If Heidegger breaks the fantasy of cognition as detached representation, Austin breaks the fantasy of language as mere description.

Language does not simply report the world. It acts within the world. To promise, request, declare, apologize, authorize, refuse, invite, command, or accept is not merely to transmit information. It is to alter a social situation. A promise creates an obligation. A request establishes a possible future. A declaration changes institutional reality. A refusal reorganizes the field of action.

This matters enormously for computational design.

A large portion of organizational life consists not in the exchange of neutral facts but in the management of commitments. Someone asks for something. Someone else agrees, delays, delegates, rejects, reinterprets, forgets, or completes it. Work is not simply a sequence of tasks; it is a web of commitments held together by language.

Winograd and Flores call attention to “conversations for action.” These are structured interactions through which people coordinate future activity. Their point is not that every conversation should be rigidly formalized. The point is that computation becomes powerful when it supports the pragmatic structure of action: who asked, who committed, what conditions count as fulfillment, what remains unresolved, what breakdowns require repair.

This is where the book’s promise and danger sit close together. On the one hand, the language-action perspective provides a profound alternative to information-processing design. It shifts attention from data to commitment, from representation to coordination, from messages to obligations. On the other hand, when translated too literally into workflow software, it risks flattening the ambiguity and richness of human communication into bureaucratic templates. Not every utterance wants to become a ticket. Not every promise wants to be rendered as a status field. Not every human relation survives procedural capture.

This tension is not a flaw to be ignored. It is the central design wound of the book.

Winograd and Flores help us see that computers should not pretend to understand human life from the outside. But the act of designing systems for commitments can itself become a new formalism if it forgets the interpretive flexibility of the practices it seeks to support. The lesson is therefore double: language is action, but action cannot be exhausted by a schema.

The best design does not merely encode speech acts. It keeps open the conditions for repair.

## 4. Autopoiesis and the rejection of representation

The third lineage running through the book comes from Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela. Their theory of autopoiesis challenges the idea that cognition is primarily the construction of representations of an independent external world. Living systems do not simply receive information from outside and process it internally. They maintain themselves through patterns of organization, structural coupling, and adaptive interaction.

For Winograd and Flores, this provides another way out of cognitivism. The organism is not a passive receiver of inputs. It is an active, self-organizing system whose world emerges through its mode of living. Cognition is not a mirror of reality; it is effective action within a domain of existence.

This anti-representational move matters for design because it attacks the assumption that better systems are always systems with better internal models. Much of artificial intelligence, old and new, assumes that the path to intelligence is the construction of more complete representations: larger knowledge bases, richer ontologies, more parameters, more context windows, more accurate predictive models. But Winograd and Flores ask us to be suspicious of this instinct. The issue is not only whether the system has enough information. The issue is whether the system participates meaningfully in a practice.

A calendar application does not need to “understand time” in the human sense to be useful. It needs to support the practices through which people coordinate time. A collaborative document editor does not need to “understand authorship” metaphysically. It needs to make revisions, conflicts, histories, and responsibilities available for human interpretation. A generative AI system does not need to be treated as a mind because it produces language. It needs to be situated within accountable practices of authorship, verification, delegation, and repair.

The autopoietic inheritance therefore helps distinguish between model and world. A model may be useful, but it is not the world. A representation may coordinate action, but it does not replace situated judgment. A system may generate plausible language, but plausibility is not commitment.

This distinction has become newly urgent in the age of generative AI. Contemporary systems are often described as if scale had solved the old philosophical problem. Because they produce fluent language, summarize documents, write code, generate images, and simulate conversation, they invite the revival of the very fantasy Winograd and Flores opposed: that language-like output implies understanding-like interiority.

But the old critique still cuts. The production of a sentence is not the same as participation in a world of obligation. The generation of a plan is not the same as situated action. The simulation of confidence is not the same as responsibility. The system may predict the next word; it does not thereby inhabit the consequences of the promise that word may make.

## 5. Dreyfus, Suchman, and the situated action critique

Winograd and Flores belong to a broader anti-cognitivist turn. Hubert Dreyfus attacked symbolic AI for misunderstanding human expertise. Human skilled action, he argued, is not primarily the application of explicit rules. It is embodied, contextual, and absorbed in practice. Experts do not always reason by consulting representations; they often perceive situations directly in terms of meaningful possibilities for action.

Lucy Suchman extended this critique into the analysis of plans and situated action. Plans do not determine action in advance. They are resources used within action. A plan may guide, justify, narrate, or coordinate, but real activity unfolds in response to local contingencies. People improvise. They notice. They repair. They reinterpret. They act from within situations, not above them.

This critique lands heavily on software design. Systems often presume that work is cleaner than it is. They imagine that users know their goals, that categories are stable, that procedures are explicit, that exceptions are rare, that plans precede action, and that breakdowns are edge cases. But in actual practice, the so-called edge case is often where the work lives.

Suchman’s contribution sharpens the political stakes of Winograd and Flores. If systems are designed around abstract plans rather than situated practices, they do not merely fail technically. They redistribute responsibility. They blame users for not conforming to the system’s model. They treat local judgment as noise. They make invisible work disappear. They punish the very improvisation that keeps organizations alive.

This is one of the most important lessons for contemporary AI design. Automated systems frequently encode managerial fantasies about work. They promise efficiency by formalizing tasks, classifying cases, recommending actions, and producing outputs. But if they do not understand the situated nature of work, they become engines of misrecognition. They classify what should be interpreted. They automate what should be negotiated. They optimize what should be questioned. They escalate the authority of the model while diminishing the legitimacy of the practitioner.

A post-cognitivist design theory therefore must place situated action at the center. It must ask not only what the system can do, but what kinds of local judgment it supports or suppresses. It must ask what becomes visible, what becomes invisible, who can repair errors, who bears the cost of misclassification, and whose world is being formalized.

## 6. Computers as media of commitment

The deepest design proposition in *Understanding Computers and Cognition* is that computers are not best understood as artificial minds. They are media for human coordination.

This sounds simple, but it reverses a great deal. If the computer is an artificial mind, the design goal is autonomy: make the system smarter, more capable, more human-like, more agentic. If the computer is a medium of coordination, the design goal is accountability: make commitments visible, make interpretation possible, make repair available, make responsibility traceable, make human action better supported.

The distinction matters because contemporary AI systems increasingly occupy ambiguous space between tool, assistant, agent, infrastructure, and institution. They write, summarize, recommend, classify, schedule, decide, and speak. The temptation is to understand them through the fantasy of delegation: the more capable the system becomes, the more human work it can absorb.

Winograd and Flores suggest a different frame. The question is not how much work the system can absorb. The question is what happens to the structure of commitment when work passes through the system.

Who is speaking when an AI drafts an email? Who is responsible when an AI summarizes a medical note? What is the status of a recommendation generated by a model but accepted by an employee under institutional pressure? What kind of promise is made when an automated agent schedules, cancels, purchases, flags, or refuses? Where does breakdown appear? Who can contest it? What counts as repair?

These questions move AI ethics away from abstract principles and toward the pragmatics of action. They ask how systems reorganize obligation. They treat design as the distribution of responsibility.

A commitment-centered system would not hide behind automation. It would show the chain of action. It would distinguish suggestion from decision, draft from declaration, prediction from verification, simulation from record, assistance from authorization. It would make uncertainty visible without making the user carry all interpretive burden. It would provide mechanisms for correction, refusal, escalation, and explanation. It would treat breakdown not as embarrassment but as a first-class design condition.

Such a system would be less magical. That is precisely its virtue.

The fantasy of magic is one of the most dangerous forces in AI design. Magic hides labor, hides uncertainty, hides judgment, hides dependency, hides politics. Winograd and Flores give us a way to resist magic without rejecting computation. They ask us to design systems that are powerful because their limits are intelligible.

## 7. The return of rationalist AI in generative form

Generative AI has revived the old dream in a new aesthetic. The system speaks. It produces fluent prose. It performs competence across domains. It generates code, images, arguments, summaries, plans, and conversations. The surface is no longer the brittle symbolic logic of early AI. It is fluid, probabilistic, adaptive, and stylistically rich.

But beneath the new surface, an old confusion returns: the confusion of linguistic performance with understanding.

This does not mean generative AI is trivial. It is not. These systems are extraordinarily powerful cultural and technical artifacts. They reorganize writing, search, programming, education, design, administration, and imagination. But their power makes the Winograd-Flores critique more necessary, not less. The danger is not simply that people will mistakenly believe machines are conscious. The deeper danger is that institutions will redesign human practices around systems that simulate participation without bearing responsibility.

A generative model can produce a plausible policy summary without understanding the institutional consequences of that summary. It can generate a therapeutic-sounding response without inhabiting a therapeutic relationship. It can draft legal language without being accountable to a client. It can write code without sharing the programmer’s theory of the program. It can simulate care, authority, expertise, humility, urgency, and confidence.

The problem is not that the machine is “fake” and the human is “real.” That opposition is too crude. The problem is that generative systems operate within human commitment structures while being ontologically exempt from commitment. They can trigger obligations without holding them. They can influence decisions without being answerable. They can produce language acts without being social actors in the full sense.

This is why “AI alignment” remains incomplete if it focuses only on model behavior. Alignment must also be a design problem about conversations, institutions, interfaces, and commitments. A model may produce a safe answer, but the surrounding system may still misallocate responsibility. A model may express uncertainty, but the workflow may pressure users to accept its output. A model may include a disclaimer, but the interface may frame its response as authoritative. A model may be corrigible in theory, but the institution may have no practice for correction.

Winograd and Flores would push us to ask: what kind of conversation is this system participating in? What commitments does it create, transform, obscure, or discharge? What breakdowns does it anticipate? What repairs does it permit? What human practices does it strengthen, and which does it quietly deform?

## 8. Design as ontological politics

One of the most radical implications of Winograd and Flores is that design is not merely functional. It is ontological. Technologies disclose worlds. They make some actions obvious and others obscure. They create categories, roles, paths, defaults, permissions, and impossibilities. They train users in what the world is supposed to be like.

A task management system teaches that work is decomposable into tasks. A customer relationship platform teaches that relationships are pipelines. A learning management system teaches that education is submission, grading, and compliance. A social media platform teaches that communication is engagement. A generative AI assistant may teach that thinking is prompting, that writing is output selection, that judgment is preference ranking, that style is detachable from responsibility.

These are not neutral metaphors. They are political forms.

Power enters design through the classification of action. It enters through defaults. It enters through what cannot be said, undone, appealed, repaired, or remembered. It enters through the conversion of ambiguous human situations into machine-readable states. It enters when a system decides what counts as completed, late, relevant, risky, authentic, toxic, productive, or true.

The Winograd-Flores inheritance allows us to criticize these systems without falling into nostalgia for a pretechnical humanism. The point is not that human action should remain untouched by tools. Human action has always been mediated. The point is that tools should be designed with a richer account of human being.

Design after cognitivism must therefore be humble and severe. Humble, because it accepts that formal systems cannot exhaust situated human worlds. Severe, because it refuses the laziness of pretending that all human ambiguity is sacred and therefore beyond design. The task is not to avoid formalization altogether. The task is to formalize carefully, visibly, provisionally, and repairably.

A good system should know where its ontology ends.

## 9. Toward post-cognitivist AI design

A post-cognitivist AI design theory would begin from several principles.

First, it would distinguish prediction from understanding. Predictive fluency may be useful, but it should not be mistaken for situated comprehension. Interfaces should not overstate the epistemic status of generated outputs.

Second, it would distinguish output from commitment. A generated statement becomes socially meaningful only when someone takes responsibility for its use. Systems should help users understand when they are drafting, deciding, declaring, delegating, or merely exploring.

Third, it would design for breakdown. Every AI system should assume failure, ambiguity, misuse, uncertainty, and contestation. Repair should not be an afterthought hidden in support channels. It should be part of the interaction grammar.

Fourth, it would preserve situated judgment. The system should not simply automate decisions; it should support the human practices through which decisions become accountable. This requires attention to context, expertise, local knowledge, and institutional pressure.

Fifth, it would make provenance and transformation visible. Users should be able to see what sources, prompts, assumptions, constraints, and edits shaped an output. Not because transparency solves everything, but because invisible transformation destroys responsibility.

Sixth, it would treat language as action. AI-generated language is not inert content. It can authorize, persuade, soothe, threaten, erase, include, exclude, and obligate. The design of language systems must therefore be a design of social force.

Finally, it would reject artificial personhood as the master metaphor. The goal is not to build systems that appear more human. The goal is to build systems that better support human worlds.

This does not mean AI systems must be weak, dull, or bureaucratic. On the contrary, systems designed around accountability can be more powerful because their power is usable without mystification. They can support creativity, analysis, coordination, and exploration while keeping the human structure of action intact.

The future of AI design should not be organized around the question, “How do we make machines think like humans?” It should be organized around a better question: “How do we design computational media that help humans think, act, commit, repair, and live together more responsibly?”

That is the question Winograd and Flores still hand us.

---

## 10. Conclusion: the new foundation was never merely about computers

*Understanding Computers and Cognition* remains powerful because it understood that the debate about computers was never only about computers. It was about the human image smuggled into computation. It was about whether cognition would be imagined as representation or situated action, whether language would be treated as information or commitment, whether design would serve formal models or living practices.

The book’s enduring lesson is not that computers cannot matter because they do not understand. It is that they matter precisely because they reorganize the conditions under which humans understand, coordinate, and act.

This is why Winograd and Flores are newly relevant in the age of generative AI. The old rationalist fantasy has returned in probabilistic dress. The machine speaks more fluidly now. It imitates expertise more convincingly. It enters more intimate and consequential domains. But the fundamental question remains unchanged: does the system participate responsibly in the human world it affects, or does it merely simulate the signs of participation?

A new foundation for design begins when we stop asking computers to become minds and start asking what kinds of worlds they help bring forth.

The task is not to make machines more magical.
The task is to make their role in human action more truthful.
