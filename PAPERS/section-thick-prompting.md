# Prompting as Performance and Thick Description

**From: "From Imagetext to Worldtext: Generative AI as Operative Ekphrasis"**
**Hartsoe & Bolter — Section Draft**

---

Prompting is a process, even if it involves only a single cycle of word to image. In fact, the process is so simple that users seldom stop after one cycle. They either refine the first prompt or create a different image with a new prompt. While prompting may have the practical goal of producing usable images for a variety of commercial or casual purposes, the simplicity of the process vastly expands the number of those who can produce such images. The emphasis shifts to process and participation. In this way, operative ekphrasis recalls the ancient rhetorical tradition of the *progymnasmata*. That tradition emphasized performance. Ekphrases were designed to be part of an oration, to be delivered before an audience and appreciated in the moment. The theory of ekphrasis in the twentieth century emphasized the finished product — the poem, not the process of its making — a well-wrought urn, as critic Cleanth Brooks put it, in a reference to perhaps the most famous English-language ekphrasis, Keats's "Ode on a Grecian Urn."

As ekphrastic prompting has developed into a community of practice, the way prompts are crafted has become more elaborate, for image generation as well as for text and coding. A skilled practitioner does not stop with a single short phrase. As we noted, they prompt iteratively to refine the result. In multimodal systems, prompters may deploy images to generate other images. Prompts can be data files (in JSON format), scripts, or even code. Even before generative AI, digital media theorists were arguing for the multimodality or intermediality of digital ekphrasis (Brosch 2018b) as well as for ekphrasis as performance (Brosch 2018a). Metaprompting is already an established term for the practice of writing prompts that prompt prompts. The term and much of the research have been applied to LLMs that output verbal responses to verbal prompts (Zhang, Yuan, and Yao 2025). But metaprompting can also apply for making and prompting images in two or three dimensions (Zhao et al. 2024; Ceurstemont 2025).

We suggest the term "thick prompting" to characterize all of these practices, appealing to the anthropologist Clifford Geertz's concept of "thick description" from the introductory essay to his book *The Interpretation of Cultures* (Geertz 1973). Geertz rejected anthropological theories that reduced cultural complexities to functional formulas. He argued for a process of description that acknowledges the complex structures of meaning behind any cultural practice. That complexity means that descriptions need to be "thick": there is always more to say. The process is provisional and potentially endless. Good prompting is also thick. Both thick description and thick prompting are seeking to engage with layers of coding. In the case of thick description, the layers of cultural coding that overdetermine any practice. In the case of thick prompting, the coding consists of all the semantic layers that have been absorbed into the weights of the model. To prompt is to probe those layers in order to tease out the artifact. Because the models are probabilistic (not the symbolically coded knowledge structures of earlier AI), the process of interrogating them is always approximate, incomplete, and potentially endless. There is no final perfect prompt.

The analogy between thick description and thick prompting is more than suggestive. Geertz's method demands that the ethnographer move beyond what he calls "thin description" — the report of behavior ("he contracted his eyelid") — to an account that maps the structures of significance that make a twitch into a wink, a wink into a parody, a parody into a rehearsal of a parody (Geertz 1973, 6–7). The move from thin to thick is a move from surface to situated meaning. The same structure distinguishes thin from thick prompting. A thin prompt ("a golden shield with battle scenes") reports the desired surface. A thick prompt situates the desired output within the layers of convention, audience expectation, and evaluative context that will determine whether the output succeeds. Thickness is not verbosity. It is selected context that changes the act's identity across frames. There is no formula for a thick prompt, just as there is no formula for thick description. What there is, in both cases, is a practice — iterative, provisional, attentive to layers of meaning — that develops through experience with the medium and its repertoire.

## Working Notes

> **What this section does**: Establishes prompting as performance (ancient) and process (modern), then introduces thick prompting as the framework for understanding what skilled practitioners actually do. Does NOT elaborate the full six-layer architecture here — that is available for a longer version or a separate paper on thick prompting specifically. The section stays functional: it gives the reader the concept ("thick prompting"), grounds it in Geertz, and moves on.

> **What this section does NOT do**: It does not explain how descriptions operate in general (that's a book). It does not relitigate operative ekphrasis. It does not over-elaborate Geertz. It introduces the concept and trusts the reader to follow the analogy.
