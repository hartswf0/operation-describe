# Conclusion

**From: "From Imagetext to Worldtext: Generative AI as Operative Ekphrasis"**
**Hartsoe & Bolter — Section Draft**

---

This paper has traced a single operation across twenty-seven centuries: the use of language to produce visual and spatial experience. We began with the ancient rhetoricians, for whom ekphrasis was already operative — a technology for turning hearing into sight, not by representing pre-existing images but by generating new ones in the listener's mind. We showed that the twentieth-century narrowing of ekphrasis from production to representation concealed this operative core, and that Bajohr's "operative ekphrasis" names not a new capacity of generative AI but the recovery of an ancient one. We introduced Mitchell's imagetext as the theoretical name for what the latent space of a multimodal model actually is: a manifold in which word and image are no longer distinct data types but co-constitutive elements of a single representational substrate. And we proposed thick prompting, after Geertz, as the framework for understanding what skilled practitioners do when they engage iteratively, provisionally, and attentively with the layers of semantic coding that the model has absorbed.

But the paper's central claim concerns a shift that is only now becoming visible: the shift from imagetext to worldtext, from image model to world model. An image model takes a prompt and generates a scene — a frozen intersection of language and vision. A world model takes a prompt and generates an *environment*: spatiotemporal, interactive, cumulative, governed. The image model produces an artifact. The world model produces a reality — or at least a simulation of one, with its own physics (learned or legislated), its own history (accumulated through interaction), and its own boundaries (specified by system prompts that function as constitutional documents). The distinction between imagetext and worldtext is not a difference of scale. It is a difference of kind. The imagetext is a picture made from words. The worldtext is a world made from words — a world that persists, responds, and constrains.

Homer's Shield of Achilles is our benchmark because it already embodies this distinction. The shield does not depict a scene. It specifies a world: cosmology at the center, governance and ecology in the middle rings, the Ocean at the rim. Hephaestus does not describe the shield; he fabricates it, element by element, as Homer narrates the process. The shield is not an image of the Homeric world. It is a *constitution* of the Homeric world — a compressed specification of everything that world contains. What Google DeepMind's Genie and World Labs are now building — generative systems that produce navigable, interactive, spatiotemporal environments from text prompts — is, in this light, the technological realization of an ambition that Homer articulated in bronze, tin, and gold.

We do not claim that this realization is complete. The world models now emerging are early-stage systems: inconsistent, approximate, limited in their interactivity and coherence. The genie is out of the bottle, but it is still learning to grant wishes. What we claim is that the conceptual trajectory is clear — from image generation to world generation, from the prompt as description to the prompt as constitution, from the imagetext as artifact to the worldtext as environment — and that this trajectory has profound implications for how we understand the relationship between language and visual experience, between description and creation, between the word and the world.

The concepts introduced here — operative ekphrasis, thick prompting, imagetext, worldtext — are at an early stage themselves. They are likely to grow and to diverge as scholars from different disciplines bring their own frameworks to bear. We do not expect these concepts to remain stable. We expect them to be productive — to open doors for work that we cannot foresee. Operative ekphrasis has named the moment when the word became the picture. Worldtext names the next: when the word becomes the world. How that world will be governed, who will have the authority to prompt it into being, and what thick prompting will mean when the stakes are not images but environments — these are questions for the work that follows.

The shield has been read and reread for twenty-seven centuries, and each reading has found in it something the previous reading missed. We offer ours — the shield as worldtext, as the first operative specification of a world compressed into language — not as a final interpretation but as an operative one: a reading that generates new questions and invites others to continue.
