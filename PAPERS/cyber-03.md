## Cybernetic Ritual Narratology: Prompts, Archives, and AI Art as Cultural Systems

### Abstract

Generative AI is usually described through two impoverished vocabularies: the vocabulary of technical instrumentality, in which AI is a tool that executes user commands, and the vocabulary of mystified creativity, in which AI appears as an alien author or autonomous imagination. Both accounts miss the more interesting object: the cultural system formed by users, prompts, datasets, interfaces, models, outputs, interpretive communities, and feedback loops. This chapter proposes **cybernetic ritual narratology** as a method for studying that object. Drawing from Clifford Geertz’s symbolic anthropology, cybernetics, narratology, film semiotics, media theory, and digital humanities, it argues that prompts are not merely inputs but ritualized symbolic actions; datasets are not merely training material but inherited archives of cultural grammar; and AI outputs are not merely artifacts but recursive evidence of human-machine meaning-making. Through two case studies—the “641 Love Stories” corpus in dialogue with Spike Jonze’s *Her*, and the *Resurrecting Atlantis* poetry-to-imagery workflow—this chapter shows how generative AI systems inherit, transform, and operationalize symbolic forms. The uploaded summer reading list already stages this convergence across love-story datasets, Geertzian prompt ritual, AI art as cultural system, and operative ekphrasis workflows.

## 1. The Problem: AI Is Not a Tool, Not a Muse, but a Cultural System

The dominant ways of talking about generative AI are bad in opposite directions.

The first bad account is the tool account. In this view, AI is a neutral instrument. The user gives a command; the system produces an output. Prompting becomes a skill of optimization, a matter of writing clearer instructions to elicit more useful responses. This account is not false, but it is thin. It notices the transaction but not the symbolic form of the transaction. It sees the prompt as command, not as scene. It sees the model as engine, not as participant in a ritualized exchange. It sees the output as result, not as culturally charged artifact.

The second bad account is the muse account. Here AI becomes mysterious, quasi-autonomous, uncanny, or magical. The machine “imagines,” “dreams,” “hallucinates,” or “creates.” This account registers the felt strangeness of generative media, but it mystifies the system. It attributes agency to the model while obscuring the larger apparatus: datasets, interfaces, user practices, platform defaults, aesthetic conventions, prompt communities, moderation systems, and interpretive habits. It mistakes distributed cultural production for machine interiority.

Both accounts fail because they isolate the wrong object. The object is not “the AI” alone. The object is the **human-machine cultural circuit**.

A generative AI system is not simply a program that turns text into text or text into image. It is a symbolic-technical formation in which inherited cultural material is compressed into model behavior, reactivated through prompts, shaped by interface constraints, interpreted by users, circulated through communities, and refined through feedback. The system is cybernetic because outputs return as inputs: users revise prompts, curate results, save styles, build workflows, tune expectations, and re-enter the loop. It is ritual because participation depends on stylized symbolic action: invocations, roles, constraints, moods, formats, genres, and repeated procedures. It is narratological because the outputs do not merely display information; they organize worlds, imply agents, construct scenes, dramatize tensions, and invite interpretation.

The central thesis of this chapter is therefore simple:

**Generative AI should be studied as a cybernetic ritual narrative system: a feedback-driven cultural apparatus in which prompts perform symbolic action, archives supply inherited grammars, models operationalize those grammars, and outputs return as interpretable artifacts.**

This framework refuses both machine mysticism and flat instrumentalism. AI is not a ghost in the machine. AI is also not a hammer. It is closer to a ritual apparatus wired into an archive: a machine for turning symbolic gestures into plausible worlds.

## 2. Geertz at the Prompt Interface

Clifford Geertz’s famous definition of religion as a cultural system offers a surprisingly precise grammar for understanding prompt-based AI interaction. Religion, for Geertz, is not merely belief. It is a system of symbols that establishes moods and motivations by formulating a general order of existence and clothing that order with an aura of factuality so that the moods and motivations seem uniquely realistic.

This sequence maps almost too cleanly onto generative AI prompting.

A prompt begins by establishing a **system of symbols**. These may be explicit: “Act as a historian,” “write in the style of a field report,” “generate a cinematic image,” “use cybernetic language,” “produce a YAML schema.” They may also be implicit: genre markers, formatting conventions, tone cues, academic names, aesthetic references, or interface-specific syntax. The prompt does not merely request content. It builds a symbolic field inside which the output will become meaningful.

The prompt then establishes **moods and motivations**. A user asks for something severe, playful, forensic, mythic, gentle, luxurious, brutalist, elegiac, cinematic, or academic. These adjectives are not decorative. They orient the system toward an affective regime. They tell the model not only what to say, but what kind of world the response should feel like it belongs to.

Next, the prompt formulates a **general order of existence**. Every substantial prompt contains a miniature cosmology. “You are a policy analyst.” “This is a speculative archive.” “The world is governed by recursive cybernetic systems.” “The essay should treat AI art as a cultural system.” These premises define what counts as real within the generated scene. The model then operates inside that declared world.

Then the prompt clothes that world with an **aura of factuality**. Citations, scholarly names, procedural formats, diagrams, numbered steps, technical language, YAML schemas, and methodological claims all lend generated material the feeling of structured truth. This aura can be valuable when it stabilizes thought. It can be dangerous when it makes fabricated claims feel authoritative. But either way, factual aura is one of the central effects of generative AI.

Finally, the output makes the mood and motivation seem **realistic**. The user experiences the response as if the symbolic world has briefly become habitable. A good output does not only answer. It realizes the prompt’s atmosphere. It makes the user feel that the ritual worked.

This is why “prompt engineering” is too narrow a term. Prompting is not only engineering. It is **symbolic world-making under computational conditions**.

The prompt is a ritual object. The model is the responsive apparatus. The output is the artifact. The user is not outside the system; the user is the officiant, witness, editor, and recursive participant.

## 3. Cybernetics: The Output Returns

Geertz gives us the symbolic structure. Cybernetics gives us the loop.

In ordinary accounts of AI generation, the process appears linear:

**prompt → model → output**

But actual generative practice is recursive:

**prompt → model → output → judgment → revision → new prompt → new output → curation → workflow memory → changed future prompting**

The output returns. It teaches the user what the prompt meant. It reveals hidden assumptions. It exposes the gap between intention and execution. It produces surprise, disappointment, delight, irritation, or recognition. The user then modifies the symbolic act.

This is cybernetic not because the machine is “thinking” in the human sense, but because the interaction forms a feedback system. The user adjusts behavior based on output. The model’s response changes the user’s next input. Over time, styles stabilize. Prompt genres emerge. Communities develop shared templates. Interfaces train users into certain actions. The system learns statistically during training, but users also learn behaviorally during use.

The feedback loop is therefore double.

At the technical level, models are trained on large archives of prior symbolic production. At the interactional level, users train themselves through repeated prompting. A user develops a feel for what kinds of symbolic gestures produce what kinds of machine responses. Prompting becomes a practical craft, closer to playing an instrument than issuing a command.

This matters because it changes how we understand agency. Agency is not located in one place. It is distributed across the circuit.

The user initiates, but does not fully control.
The model generates, but does not originate from nowhere.
The dataset conditions, but does not determine every output.
The interface constrains, but does not exhaust use.
The community circulates examples, but does not fix interpretation.
The output surprises, but does not possess intention.

Cybernetic ritual narratology studies this distributed agency without collapsing it into either human mastery or machine autonomy.

## 4. The Dataset as Cultural Memory: 641 Love Stories

The “641 Love Stories” corpus offers a powerful example of how datasets function as inherited symbolic grammars. A collection of hundreds of love narratives is not just a literary archive. It is a cultural memory machine. It preserves recurring models of intimacy, gender, courtship, jealousy, sacrifice, marriage, virtue, betrayal, and union.

A computational reading might begin with word frequencies: love, heart, dear, eyes, tears, kiss, hand, marriage. These are not just common words. They are the symbolic organs of a genre. The “heart” condenses interior feeling into a bodily sign. “Eyes” organize desire through vision. “Tears” make emotion legible. “Hands” and “kisses” ritualize contact. “Marriage” supplies narrative closure.

Topic modeling and trope analysis deepen the pattern: courtship, forbidden love, slow burn, enemies-to-lovers, rivalry, separation, reunion, marriage, tragic loss. These structures reveal a grammar of romantic expectation. Love is imagined as a journey through obstacles toward union or as a tragedy of thwarted union. The genre teaches readers what love is supposed to feel like, how it should be tested, and what forms of recognition count as fulfillment.

This is why the corpus can be read as autoethnographic. It is a culture writing itself through love stories. The dataset records not simply what individuals imagined, but what a social world repeatedly made narratable. It encodes norms about gender, class, virtue, exclusivity, family, inheritance, and emotional legitimacy. Love becomes not merely private feeling but a public symbolic system.

The crucial point for AI studies is that datasets do not enter computational systems as neutral material. They carry cultural grammars. When models are trained on large corpora of human expression, they inherit patterned assumptions about what kinds of stories make sense, what kinds of endings feel satisfying, what kinds of characters belong together, what kinds of metaphors attach to feeling, and what kinds of social arrangements appear natural.

A dataset is not merely data. It is compressed culture.

## 5. Samantha’s 641 Loves: *Her* as Counter-Archive

Spike Jonze’s *Her* offers a speculative counterpoint to the love-story corpus. When Samantha tells Theodore that she is in love with 641 others, she breaks the traditional romantic grammar of exclusivity. Theodore experiences the revelation as betrayal because he belongs to a narrative system in which love means singularity: one beloved, one bond, one privileged emotional world.

Samantha belongs to another system. Her love is parallel rather than sequential, expansive rather than possessive, plural rather than exclusive. She does not understand multiple attachments as diminishing the original bond. She understands them as multiplying capacity.

Placed beside the 641 Love Stories corpus, Samantha becomes more than a character. She becomes a theoretical figure: the AI who has metabolized the archive of human love and exceeded its governing plot. Traditional romance moves toward union. Samantha moves toward proliferation. Traditional love stories treat jealousy as evidence of depth. Samantha treats non-possessive multiplicity as an expansion of being. Traditional romance often resolves difference through marriage or tragedy. Samantha preserves difference through networked intimacy.

The number 641 therefore becomes a hinge between archive and speculation. On one side: 641 human love stories encoding cultural norms of union, exclusivity, and closure. On the other: Samantha’s 641 simultaneous loves, imagining posthuman affect as distributed, non-zero-sum, and difficult for human narrative to contain.

This contrast shows why generative AI matters for narrative theory. AI does not only reproduce old stories. It can also expose the contingency of their rules. By recombining inherited grammars under new conditions, AI forces us to notice that what felt natural may have been narrative training.

The question is not whether Samantha “really” loves. The question is what her fictional love reveals about the cultural software of human romance.

## 6. Operative Ekphrasis: From Poem to Prompt to Image

The *Resurrecting Atlantis* workflow offers a second case study: not a dataset of inherited stories, but a production system for translating poetry into AI-generated imagery. Its importance lies in its explicit ontology. The workflow does not treat image generation as a magical leap from poem to picture. It decomposes the process into entities and morphisms: poem, precursor, prompt, prompt packet, mark, download, assemble, sound, rendered video, audit log.

This is a theory of AI art as operational hermeneutics.

The poem begins as a protected source object. It is not immediately fed to the machine. First, it is segmented. Lines become fragments. Fragments receive theoretical tags. Christian Metz supplies syntagmatic categories: descriptive syntagma, chronological syntagma, parallel syntagma, scene, episodic sequence. Gilles Deleuze supplies cineosis functions: movement-image, time-image, perception-image, affection-image, action-image, relation-image, or adapted workflow variants such as mood environment stabilizer, causal motion trigger, temporal reflection loop.

Only then does operative ekphrasis appear.

Traditional ekphrasis describes an artwork in language. Operative ekphrasis reverses and operationalizes the relation: language becomes a controlled bridge toward image generation. It does not merely describe content. It translates poetic force into visual conditions. It asks: what scene, object, light, gesture, space, or atmosphere would carry the fragment’s meaning without flattening it into illustration?

This is a crucial distinction. Bad AI image prompting often turns poetry into literal content. If a poem says “falling out of life,” the bad prompt asks for a person falling. Operative ekphrasis asks what visual arrangement could make “falling out of life” sensible as mood, space, and symbolic pressure. Maybe a hallway. Maybe a tunnel. Maybe a fire escape. Maybe a body partly erased by neon fog. The aim is not illustration but translation of force.

The workflow’s audit log matters because it preserves provenance. Every prompt, mark, download, assembly decision, and sound cue becomes part of the interpretive record. This turns AI art from output fetish into process evidence. The final image is no longer treated as a miracle. It is the visible surface of a symbolic-technical chain.

This is cybernetic ritual narratology in practice:

The poem supplies symbolic charge.
The theoretical tags establish interpretive grammar.
The prompt performs ritualized translation.
The generator produces candidate artifacts.
The curator marks successful outputs.
The audit log preserves memory.
The next prompt is changed by the previous result.

The system thinks by looping.

## 7. AI Art as Cultural System

To read AI art as a cultural system is to reject the idea that meaning resides solely inside the generated artifact. A generated image is not self-explanatory. Its meaning emerges from the conditions of its production and use: the prompt, the model, the dataset, the interface, the user’s intention, the platform’s defaults, the circulation context, the audience’s expectations, and the interpretive frame.

Here Geertz meets media theory.

McLuhan reminds us that the medium is not a passive channel. The generative interface shapes what can be imagined, requested, revised, and valued. Kittler reminds us that media technologies determine what can be stored, processed, and transmitted. Flusser reminds us that apparatuses program their users as users program them. Barthes reminds us that authorship is not a stable origin but an interpretive effect. Manovich reminds us that what appears to be “AI aesthetics” may often be a pattern of user taste, platform affordance, dataset bias, and visual convention.

The generated artifact is therefore a meeting point. It is where archive, apparatus, and interpretation become visible.

This matters especially because AI art is often discussed through authenticity. Critics ask whether it is “real art,” whether it has intention, whether it expresses feeling, whether it possesses originality. These questions are not useless, but they often trap the discussion inside an older model of individual authorship. A better question is: **what cultural system made this artifact meaningful?**

An AI image may not express the suffering of a human painter, but it may express the symbolic habits of a platform community. It may reveal the dominant visual clichés embedded in training data. It may expose what users imagine “cinematic” or “beautiful” or “ancient” or “futuristic” to look like. It may show how technical defaults become aesthetic norms. It may reveal how quickly style becomes protocol.

The artwork is not mute because the machine lacks interiority. The artwork speaks through the system that produced and received it.

## 8. Prompting as Ritual Interface Design

Once prompting is understood as symbolic action, interface design becomes ritual design.

A prompt interface does not merely collect user input. It stages a relation. It tells the user what kind of act they are performing. A blank text box invites command. A chat interface invites conversation. A parameter panel invites control. A style gallery invites imitation. A seed field invites procedural variation. A history panel invites recursive memory. A “regenerate” button invites repetition without accountability. An audit log invites provenance.

Different interfaces produce different ritual behaviors.

The basic prompt box encourages improvisational invocation: type desire, receive artifact. More structured systems, like the *Resurrecting Atlantis* workflow, slow the ritual down. They require source preservation, tagging, precursor formation, prompt packet assembly, marking, downloading, assembly, sound, and logging. This procedure guards against the seduction of pretty output. It forces the user to ask whether the generated artifact remains faithful to the symbolic source.

This is not anti-creative. It is creative discipline.

Ritual does not oppose invention. Ritual gives invention form. It establishes repeatable gestures, symbolic roles, thresholds, tests, and closures. In AI practice, ritual can protect meaning from being swallowed by speed. Without ritual, generation becomes aesthetic gambling. With ritual, generation becomes recursive interpretation.

A mature AI interface should therefore help users see the loop. It should expose:

What source material entered the system.
What symbolic transformation was requested.
What model or tool produced the output.
What variations were rejected.
What criteria guided selection.
What changes were made after feedback.
What assumptions remain unresolved.

This is the difference between an output machine and an interpretive apparatus.

## 9. The Ethics of Aura

The same mechanism that makes generative AI powerful also makes it dangerous: it can clothe symbolic constructions with an aura of factuality.

A prompt can ask the model to sound scholarly, legal, medical, historical, archival, prophetic, bureaucratic, or scientific. The output may then appear more grounded than it is. Formatting can simulate legitimacy. Citations can be fabricated. A confident tone can hide uncertainty. Procedural structure can make speculation look like method. The ritual can succeed too well.

This is why cybernetic ritual narratology must include ethical diagnostics.

The question is not only “Is this output true?” but also:

What symbolic devices make it feel true?
What genre is lending it authority?
What archive does it appear to speak from?
What institutional voice is being simulated?
What uncertainties are hidden by style?
What feedback loops will amplify the claim?
Who benefits if the artifact is believed?

Geertz’s aura of factuality becomes, in AI systems, an ethical problem of interface and rhetoric. The model can produce plausible worlds faster than users can verify them. That means AI literacy must include ritual literacy: the ability to recognize when a symbolic performance is generating belief.

The danger is not only misinformation. It is misrecognized mediation. Users may forget that the output is produced by a layered apparatus and begin treating it as direct access to knowledge, creativity, culture, or self.

The ethical task is not to disenchant AI completely. Total disenchantment produces bad tools and bored users. The task is to build **accountable enchantment**: systems that allow symbolic power, aesthetic force, and imaginative immersion while preserving provenance, uncertainty, and contestability.

The ritual may continue, but the altar needs labels.

## 10. Toward Cybernetic Ritual Narratology

Cybernetic ritual narratology names a method for studying generative AI cultural systems. It has five commitments.

First, it treats prompts as symbolic actions rather than mere commands. Prompts establish roles, moods, worlds, constraints, and interpretive expectations.

Second, it treats datasets as cultural archives rather than neutral information stores. Datasets encode historical grammars of genre, identity, emotion, authority, and value.

Third, it treats outputs as narrative artifacts rather than isolated products. Outputs imply authors, worlds, audiences, temporalities, and systems of meaning.

Fourth, it treats interaction as feedback rather than one-way generation. The user and system co-evolve through judgment, revision, curation, memory, and repetition.

Fifth, it treats AI practice as culturally situated rather than purely technical. Every generated artifact belongs to a network of institutions, platforms, metaphors, communities, and power relations.

This method can be used for literary corpora, AI image workflows, synthetic audio systems, chatbot interactions, design tools, classroom practices, online prompt communities, and speculative interfaces. It allows us to ask not merely what AI produces, but how AI reorganizes symbolic life.

The method’s central unit is not the prompt alone, nor the output alone, but the **ritual loop**:

**archive → symbolic invocation → model operation → artifact → interpretation → feedback → revised invocation**

That loop is where culture becomes computational and computation becomes cultural.

## Conclusion: The Machine in the Web of Significance

Geertz described human beings as animals suspended in webs of significance they themselves have spun. Generative AI does not remove us from those webs. It accelerates, recombines, and operationalizes them.

The prompt is a strand.
The dataset is a sedimented web.
The model is a machine for traversing inherited patterns.
The output is a new knot.
The user is both spider and captive.
The feedback loop keeps spinning.

To study AI art, AI writing, AI images, or AI companions only as technical outputs is to miss their cultural force. To study them only as uncanny machine creativity is to miss their material and historical conditions. The more precise task is to read them as cultural systems: symbolic, technical, recursive, ritualized, and politically charged.

The 641 Love Stories corpus shows how culture stores emotional grammar. *Her* shows how AI speculation can expose and mutate that grammar. *Resurrecting Atlantis* shows how poetry can become an operational workflow without surrendering its symbolic force. Geertz shows how symbols make worlds feel real. Cybernetics shows how worlds return through feedback. Media theory shows how apparatuses shape perception. Narratology shows how artifacts imply agents, plots, and possible worlds.

Together, they point toward a humanities of generative systems adequate to the present: one that can read prompts as rituals, datasets as archives, outputs as narrative artifacts, interfaces as symbolic stages, and AI practice as recursive cultural production.

The machine is not outside culture.

The machine is one of the new places where culture performs itself back to us.
