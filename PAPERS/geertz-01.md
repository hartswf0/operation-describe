# Thick Description After the Event

## Public Meaning, Inscription, and the Interpretive Machine

### Abstract

Clifford Geertz’s “Thick Description” is often reduced to a call for richer contextual detail, as though the phrase meant little more than adding background, atmosphere, or cultural nuance to a thin empirical account. This chapter argues instead that thick description is a theory of how public meaning survives the death of the event. For Geertz, culture is not a total way of life, a mental program, a behavioral pattern, or a hidden structure beneath conduct. Culture is the public web of significance within which action becomes intelligible. The ethnographer’s task is therefore not to collect brute facts and later decorate them with interpretation, but to inscribe social discourse: to rescue the “said” of action from its passing occasion and render it available for reconsultation, debate, and theoretical pressure. Reading the wink, the sheep raid, and the field note as three archives of symbolic action, this chapter reconstructs thick description as a ground-near interpretive science: anti-totalizing, anti-behaviorist, anti-mentalist, anti-formalist, and hostile to any theory that understands human life without knowing it. The chapter then extends Geertz’s framework toward contemporary media and AI studies, asking what it would mean to thickly describe prompts, interfaces, datasets, platform rituals, and machine outputs as public symbolic actions rather than private intentions or neutral technical traces.

---

## I. The Event Is Already Gone

The first mistake is to think that thick description begins with detail.

It does not. It begins with loss.

The social event is already disappearing. The wink has passed. The sheep have been driven across the plain. The insult has landed, misfired, or been misunderstood. The joke has depended on a code no camera can see. The informant’s story has already converted action into narration before the anthropologist ever begins to convert narration into analysis. By the time culture becomes available to scholarship, it is no longer present as life. It is present as trace, account, residue, recollection, inscription.

This is why Geertz’s “Thick Description” is not merely a methodological recommendation. It is not a plea for richer field notes. It is not the anthropological version of “add context.” It is a theory of the afterlife of meaning.

Human action occurs in time, but cultural analysis occurs after the fact. What the ethnographer studies is not raw conduct but conduct already caught in webs of significance: already interpreted by actors, reinterpreted by observers, stabilized through writing, and made available as an object of argument. Thick description names this layered condition. It is the discipline of refusing to bleach action into behavior before analysis begins. It is the art of preserving the density that makes an act an act.

For Geertz, the problem of culture is therefore not that human beings behave in complex ways. Animals behave. Machines behave. Bodies twitch. The problem is that human beings act meaningfully in public worlds where conduct is saturated by socially established distinctions. A contracted eyelid may be a twitch, a wink, a parody of a wink, a rehearsal of a parody, a deception, a failed deception, or a signal taken wrongly by those who do not know the code. What matters is not the movement alone but the world in which the movement counts.

The object of anthropology lies in that gap: between the physical motion and the socially intelligible act.

The gap is not empty. It is filled with codes, histories, rival interpretations, institutional pressures, political asymmetries, habits of perception, genres of performance, and memories of prior use. It is filled, in short, with culture. But “culture” only becomes useful when it is cut down to this scale. If culture explains everything, it explains nothing. Geertz begins by shrinking the concept, not to weaken it, but to save it. He wants culture to stop behaving like a master-key and start functioning like an analytic instrument.

This is the first major violence of “Thick Description”: it cuts the grand idea down to size.

Geertz inherits a discipline burdened by the excess of its central concept. Culture had meant a total way of life, social inheritance, mental pattern, behavioral abstraction, storehouse of learning, normative mechanism, adaptation system, historical precipitate, map, sieve, and matrix. Such abundance looked generous but produced conceptual rot. A concept that can absorb everything cannot discriminate. It cannot tell the analyst what to look for, what to ignore, where to stop, or what kind of claim is being made.

Geertz’s answer is not to abandon culture but to narrow it. Culture becomes semiotic. Human beings are suspended in webs of significance they themselves have spun; analysis is not an experimental science in search of law but an interpretive one in search of meaning. The sentence is famous enough to have become decorative, but its force is severe. It rejects the dream that anthropology can become physics by discovering laws beneath conduct. It also rejects the romantic fantasy that meaning floats free from worldly pressure. Interpretation is not an escape from reality. It is a way of entering the reality of symbolic action.

Culture is not behind behavior. It is not inside the head. It is not above the world. It is the public condition under which conduct becomes readable at all.

## II. Against the Empire of Thin Description

Thin description is not false. It is worse than false: it is insufficient in a way that can masquerade as precision.

The camera records an eyelid contraction. The behavioral transcript says that a boy rapidly moved part of his face. The operationalist, comforted by visible evidence, imagines the matter secured. Yet the difference between twitching and winking is not visible as a difference in movement. It is visible only within a social world.

This is why Geertz’s use of Ryle is so decisive. The wink example destroys the fantasy that observation can be innocent. The same observable movement can belong to different orders of meaning. The empirical surface is real, but it is not self-interpreting. If we pretend otherwise, we do not become more scientific. We become less adequate to the object.

Thin description strips action of the very properties that make it humanly interesting. It records the motion while losing the act. It sees the eyelid and misses the conspiracy. It sees sheep and misses indemnity, honor, colonial authority, legal contradiction, and social farce. It sees words and misses the situation in which they count.

Geertz does not reject empirical attention. Quite the opposite: he demands a more exact attention to what is actually happening. The target is not observation but impoverished observation. Behavior must be attended to because symbolic forms are articulated through the flow of social action. Meaning does not hover in a separate realm. It happens in gestures, exchanges, rituals, refusals, jokes, insults, payments, confiscations, and misunderstandings.

The danger is not that anthropology will become too concrete. The danger is that it will become concrete in the wrong way.

One can reduce culture to behavior, but this produces an account that cannot tell a wink from a twitch. One can reduce culture to mental rules, but this mistakes knowing how to wink for winking. One can reduce culture to formal symbolic systems, but this risks producing elegant diagrams that no actual life can inhabit. One can inflate culture into a superorganic force, but this turns meaning into a ghostly power acting through people rather than something people do with one another.

Geertz’s interpretive anthropology moves between these failures. It refuses behaviorism because meaning cannot be read from motion alone. It refuses mentalism because meaning is public, not private. It refuses formalism because coherence is not validity. It refuses totalism because culture cannot be analytically useful if it has no edge.

The result is a difficult middle position: culture is public, symbolic, worldly, and interpretable, but never finally exhausted by interpretation. This position is easy to caricature because it does not offer the satisfactions of reduction. It does not say: culture is behavior. It does not say: culture is cognition. It does not say: culture is structure. It says: culture is the context within which social action can be thickly described.

That “context” is not background. It is not scenery. It is not the introductory paragraph before the real analysis begins. Context is the field of intelligibility in which an act becomes this act and not another.

A wink without context is just an eyelid. A sheep raid without context is just livestock movement. A platform ritual without context is just clicking, posting, liking, prompting, ranking, flagging, sharing, or generating. Thick description begins when the analyst refuses the comfort of the “just.”

## III. The Wink as First Machine

The wink is a machine for destroying bad theories of culture.

It is small, almost comic, philosophically staged rather than ethnographically discovered. Yet it contains the whole architecture of Geertz’s argument. It shows that human action is stratified. It shows that meaning depends on public codes. It shows that identical motions can belong to different social realities. It shows that interpretation is not optional but constitutive. It shows that mistakes in reading are not peripheral; they are part of the phenomenon.

A twitch is not a failed wink. It belongs to a different order. A wink is not an eyelid contraction plus intention. It is an eyelid contraction that counts, under a public code, as a deliberate signal to someone in particular. A parody of a wink is not merely another wink performed badly. It is an act that depends upon the recognizability of the wink as a prior genre. A rehearsal of a parody is still another layer: an act oriented toward a future act that itself imitates an act.

This regress is not ornamental. It is Geertz’s ontology of culture in miniature.

Culture is layered action under description. Social life is not a flat sequence of behaviors but a stratified hierarchy of meaningful structures. Each layer depends on distinctions that cannot be read from physical movement alone. Thick description is therefore not verbosity. It is stratigraphic interpretation.

The wink also shows why culture is public. If winking depended solely on private intention, no one could misread it, parody it, fake it, rehearse it, or be embarrassed by its failure. A wink can misfire because it exists in a shared world of possible uptake. It is not locked inside the winker’s mind. It must be legible enough to work and ambiguous enough to fail.

Here Geertz’s Wittgensteinian inheritance matters. Meaning is not an occult object hidden behind behavior. It is use within a form of life. To know what a wink means is not to peer into a private mental chamber but to understand what people are doing with that gesture in a world where such a gesture has consequences.

This is also why the wink is political before it appears political. Every public code sorts insiders from outsiders. It establishes who can read, who can misread, who is being signaled to, who is excluded, who is deceived, who is mocked, and who gets to decide which interpretation counts. Even the small example contains hierarchy. The social world is not merely a symbolic web; it is a contested space of uptake.

The wink teaches the analyst to ask not “What movement occurred?” but “What did this movement count as, for whom, under what code, with what stakes, and against what rival interpretations?”

That question travels. It can be asked of rituals, trials, ceremonies, interfaces, prompts, memes, dashboards, classification systems, bureaucratic forms, content moderation decisions, machine outputs, and datasets. It can be asked wherever a visible act is mistaken for its meaning.

The wink is therefore not just Geertz’s example. It is his method compressed into a gesture.

## IV. The Sheep Raid as Political Semiotics

If the wink gives Geertz his analytic grammar, the sheep raid gives him his world.

The Moroccan story is not simply an ethnographic anecdote. It is a collision of interpretive frames under colonial pressure. Cohen, the Jewish trader, acts within one system of trade, indemnity, and protection. The Berber groups act within another system of honor, responsibility, and negotiated retaliation. The French authorities act within the legal fiction of colonial sovereignty, where older arrangements are officially abolished but practically still operating. The event becomes farce because these frames are not merely different; they are simultaneously active.

The story is not about sheep. It is about what sheep can say.

For Cohen, the sheep are compensation, the material form of a claim. For the sheikh, they are part of a negotiated assertion of responsibility and alliance. For the offending tribe, they are a payment that prevents escalation. For the French, they become evidence of disorder, illegality, perhaps espionage, and certainly a threat to imperial authority. The same herd moves across several symbolic economies.

This is thick description at full force: the analyst must describe not only what happened but what happening meant within each frame, and what happened when the frames collided.

The drama is structured by a “confusion of tongues.” This does not mean simple linguistic misunderstanding. It means that several systems of social intelligibility are operating at once, each capable of making sense of the event in its own terms, none able to govern the whole situation completely. Cohen’s claim makes sense under the trade-pact system. It does not make sense under French legal abolition. Yet French authority has not actually replaced the older system on the ground. Thus the event reveals not the absence of order but the coexistence of rival orders.

This point is central. Social conflict does not happen only when cultural forms break down. It can happen when cultural forms continue to operate under altered conditions. The old code persists, but the environment in which it functions has changed. A trade pact that once stabilized relations becomes, under colonial sovereignty, administratively illegible. An indemnity becomes theft. A legitimate claim becomes suspicious movement. A settlement becomes evidence.

The sheep raid is therefore a theory of historical transition. It shows how power enters symbolic action not as an external force but as a pressure that changes what actions can count as. Colonial rule does not merely dominate bodies. It reorganizes interpretive conditions. It changes which codes are authorized, which are tolerated, which are criminalized, and which are forced to continue unofficially in the shadow of formal abolition.

The French do not simply take Cohen’s sheep. They seize the authority to decide what the sheep mean.

This is why Geertz’s interpretive anthropology cannot honestly be dismissed as apolitical. Its politics are not always announced in the vocabulary of domination, but they are embedded in the analysis of meaning under conditions of unequal power. Thick description asks how symbolic action is situated inside law, violence, sovereignty, honor, livelihood, and institutional force. It does not turn away from politics toward symbols. It shows that politics itself is symbolically organized action.

The sheep raid also clarifies the relation between small facts and large issues. Geertz does not claim that Cohen’s story is Morocco in miniature. The village is not the nation. The case is not a sample in a statistical series. The local scene is not a natural laboratory. The point is not representativeness. The point is diagnostic density.

A small event can speak to a large issue when it concentrates relations otherwise dispersed across a social field. The sheep story gathers colonial law, trade, religious minority status, tribal authority, property, violence, trust, and misunderstanding into one inspectable scene. Its scale is small, but its implications are not. It allows large concepts to be handled without becoming empty. Power loses its capital letter and becomes a commandant refusing responsibility. Sovereignty becomes the confiscation of sheep. Legitimacy becomes the difference between an indemnity and a crime.

This is how thick description thinks: not by inflating the local into the universal, but by using the local to discipline the universal.

## V. Inscription, or What the Ethnographer Actually Does

Geertz’s most radical answer to the question “What does the ethnographer do?” is brutally simple: the ethnographer writes.

Not merely observes. Not merely records. Not merely analyzes. Writes.

The simplicity is deceptive. To say that the ethnographer writes is to shift anthropology from the fantasy of raw data capture to the problem of representation. Social discourse occurs and vanishes. The ethnographer inscribes it, turning a passing event into an account that can be reconsulted. What is preserved is not the event itself but the “said” of the event: its meaning as grasped, guessed, narrated, translated, and written.

This does not make ethnography false. It makes it made.

Geertz’s use of “fiction” must be understood in this older sense: something fashioned. Anthropological accounts are constructed, but construction is not fabrication. The field note, the monograph, the lecture, the museum display, and the film are not transparent windows onto culture. They are representational forms through which culture becomes available to analysis.

This threatens naive objectivity, but only naive objectivity. The serious question is not whether the account is unmediated. It is whether the account clarifies what is going on. Does it sort winks from twitches? Does it identify the codes in play? Does it bring us into contact with lives otherwise opaque? Does it preserve the difficulty of the case without surrendering to confusion? Does it make better debate possible?

Geertz’s answer to verification is appraisal. Interpretations are not proven like chemical reactions. They are judged by their power to make social action intelligible. This does not mean “anything goes.” In fact, Geertz is harsher than many positivists on bad interpretation. Coherence alone is not enough. A paranoid delusion can be coherent. A swindler’s story can be elegant. A formal model can be immaculate and still vacant.

The force of an interpretation depends on its contact with the heart of the matter.

This is why theory must hover low over evidence. In interpretive anthropology, theoretical claims cannot float too far from the descriptions they organize. They become empty when abstracted from cases. Yet without theory, description remains mute. The task is not to choose between detail and concept but to bind them so tightly that each disciplines the other.

Geertz names this relation through the idea of clinical inference. The interpreter does not begin with a law and subsume observations beneath it. The interpreter begins with signifiers and tries to place them within an intelligible frame. The goal is not prediction but diagnosis. The theory must help reveal the unapparent import of symbolic acts.

This diagnostic model also explains why interpretive theory develops in spurts rather than cumulative laws. Cultural analysis advances when later studies plunge more deeply into related problems, refine distinctions, and make sharper claims. They do not stand neatly on the shoulders of previous studies; they run beside them, challenging and challenged. Progress is not consensus. It is the refinement of debate.

Here Geertz’s final image of interpretive anthropology is unusually severe. Cultural analysis is intrinsically incomplete. The deeper it goes, the less complete it feels. It produces not final answers but more precise ways of vexing one another. This is not a weakness to be overcome. It is the condition of studying meaning without reducing it.

A science of culture, if it is to exist, must be a strange science: one whose strongest assertions remain contestable, whose evidence is already interpreted, whose objects are public but not simple, and whose theories survive by continuing to make difficult cases more intelligible.

## VI. The Field Note as Archive

The field note is not raw data. It is already an interpretive artifact.

This matters because anthropology often hides its own mediation. Finished ethnographic prose tends to present the event as if the necessary context were simply there, naturally surrounding the case. But Geertz reminds us that what we call data are constructions of constructions. The people being studied are already interpreting their own lives. Informants interpret events for the ethnographer. The ethnographer interprets those interpretations. Later readers interpret the ethnographer’s account. Meaning is not a single layer waiting to be uncovered; it is a chain of interpretive acts.

The field note is therefore an archive of mediation.

It preserves not only an event but the conditions under which that event became tellable. The sheep raid enters anthropology because Cohen recounts it, because Geertz writes it, because the finished essay repositions it as theoretical evidence. Each stage alters the event’s form. The point is not to lament this mediation but to become accountable to it.

This accountability is what separates thick description from both positivist capture and literary indulgence. Geertz does not claim that ethnographic writing is neutral. But neither does he allow the impossibility of neutrality to become permission for sentiment. The fact that complete objectivity is impossible does not mean one should conduct interpretive surgery in a sewer. The impossibility of purity increases the demand for discipline.

The field note, then, is not a lower form of theory. It is where theory first touches the world. Every decision about what to record, what to omit, what to foreground, what to translate, and what to leave strange is already theoretical. The ethnographer does not first collect and then interpret. The distinction between observation, recording, and analysis is itself unstable.

This is one of Geertz’s most important contributions to any contemporary theory of media: inscription is not secondary to reality. Inscription is one of the ways reality becomes available to collective thought.

A prompt log, a chat transcript, a moderation queue, a dataset label, a model card, an interface screenshot, a version history, a bug report, a meme thread, an annotation dispute—these are not inert records of digital culture. They are field notes produced inside the culture they document. They are already shaped by institutional constraints, actor vocabularies, technical affordances, and rival theories of what is happening.

To thickly describe such objects is not to decorate them with context. It is to ask what social discourse they have fixed, what has vanished, what interpretive frames are colliding, and what kinds of action they make consultable.

The field note is the ancestor of the platform trace.

## VII. Thick Description After AI

The extension of Geertz to AI and media studies should not begin with analogy for its own sake. It should begin with a shared problem: the danger of mistaking technical traces for cultural meaning.

A prompt is not just text entered into a system. It is a public-facing symbolic action within a sociotechnical scene. It may be a command, prayer, wager, joke, test, exploit, confession, performance, labor instruction, aesthetic gesture, or bureaucratic request. It may be written for the model, for oneself, for an imagined audience, for a screenshot, for a platform, for a classroom, for a client, for a policy boundary, or for a future archive. Thin description says: the user typed a prompt. Thick description asks: what did that prompt count as?

The same applies to machine outputs. An image generated by AI is not merely an output file. It may be evidence, fantasy, prototype, forgery, mood board, threat, memory prosthesis, legal risk, ritual object, commodity, insult, joke, or proof of technical capacity. Its meaning depends on the codes under which it is produced, circulated, recognized, challenged, and used.

The dataset, too, is not merely a technical substrate. It is an inscription of prior social discourse under conditions of extraction, classification, compression, omission, and institutional desire. To describe a dataset thickly is to ask what forms of life have been made available to computation, under what categories, with what losses, and for whose purposes.

The interface is not a neutral surface. It is a grammar of possible action. Buttons, sliders, warnings, autocomplete suggestions, refusal messages, ranking systems, and share affordances organize what users can do and what they think they are doing. An interface teaches a theory of agency. It distributes responsibility. It makes some interpretations natural and others almost impossible.

A platform ritual—liking, flagging, prompting, remixing, posting, rating, reporting, regenerating—is a wink-like structure. Its meaning cannot be read from the click alone. The same click may indicate agreement, bookmarking, irony, obligation, visibility management, tactical engagement, harassment, flirtation, compliance, or refusal. Like Geertz’s wink, the platform gesture lives in the distance between observable motion and public significance.

This is where thick description becomes urgent for AI studies. Technical systems invite thin description because they produce logs, metrics, outputs, and reproducible operations. The temptation is to treat those traces as self-explanatory. But a trace is not an interpretation. A click is not an intention. A prompt is not a desire. A label is not a truth. An output is not its social meaning. A model behavior is not its cultural role.

The AI system does not abolish the Geertzian problem. It intensifies it.

Indeed, AI culture is full of winks mistaken for twitches and twitches mistaken for winks. A user “jailbreaks” a system: is this play, critique, abuse, research, protest, or exploit? A model refuses a request: is this safety, censorship, error, institutional speech, legal prophylaxis, or moral theater? A synthetic image circulates: is it art, evidence, misinformation, satire, memory, or commodity? A dataset category appears neutral: is it description, governance, inherited prejudice, administrative convenience, or epistemic violence?

No answer can be derived from the technical surface alone. Each requires thick description.

But thick description also disciplines critique. It prevents AI studies from drifting into grand abstractions detached from actual practice. “Automation,” “bias,” “intelligence,” “agency,” “alignment,” “creativity,” “labor,” and “control” are necessary terms, but they can become Geertzian mega-concepts: large, impressive, and increasingly empty unless tied to densely described cases. Thick description forces such terms back into contact with concrete scenes.

The question is not “What is AI doing to culture?” in the abstract. The question is: in this classroom, this workplace, this policy document, this prompt exchange, this interface, this image dispute, this dataset, this moderation event, what symbolic action is taking place? What codes are being invoked? Which actors can read them? Which institutions decide the valid reading? What forms of misunderstanding become systematic? What older arrangements continue unofficially beneath new regimes of control?

The Moroccan sheep raid has become a platform event.

The older trade pact persists as informal practice. The new authority declares it abolished. Users continue to operate under inherited codes. Institutions reinterpret those actions through official categories. Confusion becomes governance. Meaning becomes evidence. Evidence becomes punishment. The sheep are confiscated again, now as content, data, account status, model output, or policy violation.

This is not to force Geertz into digital clothing. It is to recognize that his central problem—the public interpretation of symbolic action under conditions of layered authority—has not disappeared. It has migrated.

## VIII. Against Elegant Alchemy

Geertz’s final warning is aimed at his own descendants.

A semiotic approach to culture can fail by becoming too elegant. It can turn into intuitionism, alchemy, or sociological aestheticism. It can discover symbols everywhere and responsibility nowhere. It can produce readings so beautiful that no one asks whether they clarify anything. It can become a private art of interpretive performance.

This danger is especially acute in contemporary media theory, where symbolic systems, technical systems, and political systems are densely entangled. The temptation is to build total diagrams: culture as code, platforms as ritual machines, AI as myth engine, datasets as empire, prompts as spells. Some of these metaphors are useful. Many are intoxicating. Geertz’s discipline would ask what each metaphor helps us see, where it stops working, and whether it remains tied to the hard surfaces of life.

The phrase “hard surfaces” matters. Geertz is not asking us to abandon symbolic analysis for materialist reduction. He is asking us to train symbolic analysis on political, economic, stratificatory, biological, and physical realities from the start. Meaning is not a cloud above life. It is one of the ways life becomes organized, contested, suffered, and made bearable.

A thick description of AI must therefore include more than prompts and outputs. It must include labor conditions, institutional incentives, interface design, legal risk, infrastructure, energy use, moderation regimes, classification systems, user communities, training corpora, failure cases, fantasies of automation, and the symbolic vocabularies through which all these become intelligible. It must ask not only what the system does, but what different actors believe they are doing through it.

It must also remain appraisable. The point is not to produce the most dazzling reading. The point is to produce an interpretation that helps us tell better distinctions: signal from noise, use from fantasy, agency from attribution, harm from discomfort, refusal from malfunction, evidence from theater, automation from delegation, intelligence from performance, culture from metric.

Geertz gives us a standard for interpretive rigor: not certainty, not prediction, not formal closure, but the capacity to reduce puzzlement without reducing particularity.

That is a severe standard.

It means a good account does not make the strange familiar by flattening it. It makes the strange accessible while preserving its singularity. It does not translate others into ourselves. It gives us enough footing to converse. Anthropology, for Geertz, enlarges the universe of human discourse. A contemporary thick description of AI should enlarge the universe of sociotechnical discourse: not by answering once and for all what intelligence, creativity, or agency are, but by making available the answers that different communities, institutions, users, and systems are already giving in practice.

The goal is not to solve meaning. The goal is to keep meaning consultable.

## IX. Conclusion: Turtles, Prompts, Sheep

At the bottom of thick description there is no bottom.

There are only further layers: interpretation beneath interpretation, action beneath account, account beneath theory, theory beneath another case that forces revision. This incompleteness is not a methodological embarrassment. It is the structure of the object. Culture is not a puzzle waiting for final solution. It is an ongoing public argument over what actions mean.

Geertz’s “Thick Description” remains powerful because it refuses two opposite consolations. It refuses the positivist consolation that human action can be reduced to observable behavior and explained by lawlike procedure. It also refuses the interpretive consolation that meaning is so rich, fluid, and contestable that discipline no longer matters. Against both, Geertz offers a harder path: interpretive rigor without reduction, theoretical ambition without abstractionist flight, cultural specificity without relativist surrender.

The ethnographer writes after the event, but not after meaning. Meaning continues in the inscription. The sheep raid survives as an account. The wink survives as an analytic machine. The field note survives as a site where social discourse becomes available for argument. The same will be true of our prompts, outputs, datasets, interface rituals, platform disputes, and machine-mediated gestures.

To write thickly now is to ask what these actions count as, who gets to decide, what histories make them readable, what institutions alter their force, and what forms of life they preserve or destroy.

The task is not to add context to technology. The task is to understand technology as one of the contemporary scenes in which public meaning is made, mistaken, governed, and inscribed.

Small facts still speak to large issues because they are made to. A wink can speak to epistemology. A sheep raid can speak to empire. A prompt can speak to authorship, labor, fantasy, delegation, and control. But only if we refuse to count the cats in Zanzibar and call it knowledge. Only if we stay with the dense, awkward, worldly event long enough to hear what has been said.

Culture is not the hidden program beneath action.

Culture is the contested public world in which action becomes legible at all.
