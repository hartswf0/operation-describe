# Agential Cybernetic Media

## World-Making, Apparatus, and Responsible AI After Barad, Bateson, Beer, and Flusser

### Abstract

Contemporary generative AI is too often described through three inadequate figures: the tool, the author, and the mirror. As a tool, AI appears as an instrument under human control. As an author, it appears as an autonomous source of creative intention. As a mirror, it appears as a statistical reflection of culture. Each figure captures something partial, but each also conceals the deeper operation: generative AI functions as a material-discursive cybernetic apparatus that participates in producing the worlds it seems merely to represent. Drawing together Karen Barad’s agential realism, Gregory Bateson’s ecology of mind, Stafford Beer’s viable systems theory, Heinz von Foerster’s second-order cybernetics, Vilém Flusser’s theory of technical images, Gilles Deleuze’s societies of control, James C. Scott’s critique of legibility, and recent AI architectures such as world models, generative agents, and text-to-video systems, this essay proposes the concept of **agential cybernetic media**. These are systems in which perception, prediction, representation, feedback, and action become materially entangled. Their ethical problem is not simply misuse, bias, authorship, or accuracy, but **world-making under conditions of black-box mediation**. The task, therefore, is not merely to regulate AI as a tool, but to cultivate responsible practices of cutting, modeling, relating, and responding within the worlds AI helps bring forth.

---

## 1. The Problem with Calling AI a Tool

The common claim that artificial intelligence is “just a tool” is comforting because it preserves a familiar moral geometry. Humans intend; tools execute. Humans judge; tools assist. Humans create; tools extend capacity. In this picture, responsibility remains cleanly located in the user. The hammer does not build the house; the carpenter does. The brush does not paint the image; the painter does. The model does not create the world; the prompter does.

But generative AI unsettles this geometry.

A hammer does not infer the probable structure of a house. A brush does not complete the painter’s intention by drawing on a compressed statistical memory of prior visual culture. A camera does not merely obey the photographer; as Vilém Flusser argued, it contains a program of possibilities that the photographer plays against. Generative AI intensifies this apparatus logic. It does not merely receive commands. It transforms prompts into latent trajectories, draws from learned distributions, predicts plausible continuations, compresses cultural memory, fabricates perceptual surfaces, and returns outputs that alter the user’s next intention.

The user is not outside the system. The user is coupled to it.

The prompt changes the model’s output. The output changes the user’s imagination. The user revises the prompt. The model produces a new surface. The surface becomes evidence, temptation, mood, direction, constraint. A feedback circuit forms. Meaning is not injected at the beginning and retrieved at the end. It emerges across the loop.

This is why the tool metaphor fails. Generative AI is not an inert extension of human intention. Nor is it simply an alien author. It is better understood as an **apparatus of mediated becoming**: a system that configures possibilities, enacts distinctions, modulates perception, and participates in the production of cultural reality.

The question, then, is not: *Is AI a tool or an author?*

The better question is: **What kinds of worlds do AI apparatuses help enact, and who becomes responsible for the cuts through which those worlds appear?**

## 2. Agential Realism: The Cut That Makes the World Appear

Karen Barad’s agential realism begins from a refusal of the idea that the world is composed of separate, pre-existing entities that later interact. The basic unit is not the independent object, but the **phenomenon**: an entanglement through which things become determinate. Entities do not precede relation. They emerge through **intra-action**.

This distinction matters. Interaction assumes that separate things already exist and then affect one another. Intra-action means that boundaries, agencies, and properties emerge through the event itself. A measurement apparatus does not simply reveal a pre-given object. It helps enact the conditions under which something becomes measurable as that object. The apparatus makes a cut. That cut does not merely divide reality; it produces a temporary structure of separability.

This is the **agential cut**.

An agential cut distinguishes subject from object, observer from observed, signal from noise, relevant from irrelevant, figure from ground. But this separability is not metaphysically given. It is enacted. It is local, practical, material, and consequential.

Generative AI systems perform cuts constantly.

A training dataset cuts culture into collectable examples. A tokenizer cuts language into processable fragments. An embedding system cuts meaning into vector relations. A diffusion model cuts noise into image. A content policy cuts permissible from forbidden generation. A prompt cuts possibility into a request. A ranking system cuts better from worse. A user interface cuts visible controls from hidden operations. A benchmark cuts intelligence into measurable performance. A safety layer cuts acceptable creativity from unacceptable risk.

Each cut matters because each cut helps make a world.

This is where AI ethics often remains too shallow. It asks whether the output is biased, harmful, misleading, or unauthorized. These are necessary questions. But they come too late if they only inspect the product after the apparatus has already organized the field of possibility. Barad pushes us backward into the conditions of appearance. We must ask: What distinctions made this output possible? What relations were severed? What histories were compressed? What forms of agency were attributed, denied, displaced, or obscured?

The ethical demand is not simply to judge outputs. It is to become responsible for the apparatuses through which outputs become possible.

This is an ethics of mattering.

Matter, in this frame, is not passive substance awaiting representation. Matter is active, agential, involved. Discourse is not mere language floating above reality. Discourse is material practice: a way of arranging what can be said, seen, measured, modeled, and done. AI systems are therefore not only computational artifacts. They are **material-discursive machines**. They join code, hardware, energy, labor, data, institutions, interfaces, users, and imaginaries into apparatuses that produce determinate realities.

To study AI through agential realism is to stop asking whether the machine “really understands” in the abstract and instead ask what worlds are enacted by the practices through which understanding is simulated, measured, attributed, denied, and operationalized.

## 3. Bateson: Mind Is the Circuit, Not the Skull

Gregory Bateson gives this problem a second vocabulary: mind is not located inside the individual organism. Mind is immanent in completed circuits. The unit of analysis is not “the person” but the relational system through which difference travels.

A blind person walking with a cane does not perceive the world from the skin inward. The cane, hand, nervous system, ground, and environment form a circuit. Where is the mind in this system? Not simply in the brain. Mind exists in the loop: in the pathway of difference that makes a difference.

This is Bateson’s great cybernetic displacement. The elementary unit of mind is not the isolated individual but the system of organism-plus-environment. Information travels through pathways. Patterns connect. Ideas exist within ecologies. Schismogenesis emerges through relational escalation. Learning occurs not as private possession but as transformation within circuits of communication and feedback.

Generative AI belongs inside this Batesonian ecology.

A user prompting an AI system is not a sovereign mind manipulating an external instrument. The user, interface, model, dataset, generated output, platform incentives, cultural references, and subsequent interpretations form a cognitive circuit. The system thinks, not because the model alone has a mind in the human sense, but because the circuit processes differences that alter future states.

The prompt is a difference.
The output is a difference.
The surprise is a difference.
The revision is a difference.
The generated image becomes a perceptual perturbation.
The user’s next idea is shaped by the perturbation.
The circuit learns, even if no model weights are updated.

This matters because many debates about AI cognition remain trapped in the wrong container. They ask whether the model itself possesses mind, understanding, consciousness, or intention. Bateson would ask a different question: **What is the larger circuit in which this system participates, and what differences does it transmit?**

From this perspective, “AI creativity” is not located only in the model or the user. It occurs across a circuit of human expectation, machine generation, cultural archive, interface affordance, and iterative selection. The relevant unit is not the model. It is the **human-machine-environment loop**.

This also reframes responsibility. If mind is ecological, then responsibility cannot be reduced to individual intention. The designer who builds the interface, the institution that trains the model, the platform that optimizes engagement, the user who prompts, the dataset that encodes prior worlds, and the audience that interprets the output all participate in the circuit. Responsibility is distributed, but not dissolved. The ethical task is to trace the circuit precisely enough that responsibility can be assigned, accepted, and redesigned.

Bateson’s ecology of mind thus joins Barad’s agential realism: both reject the fantasy of pre-given isolated units. Both insist that reality emerges through relation. Both force us to ask how boundaries are drawn, how information moves, and how systems become capable of self-reinforcing pathology.

The danger is not that AI becomes intelligent in isolation. The danger is that human-AI circuits become stupid, rigid, addictive, extractive, or destructive while still appearing intelligent at the surface.

## 4. Second-Order Cybernetics: The Observer Is Inside the System

First-order cybernetics studies observed systems: feedback, regulation, communication, control. Second-order cybernetics turns the frame back on the observer. The observer is not outside the system being described. The observer participates in the system of observation.

Heinz von Foerster’s ethical imperative — act always so as to increase the number of choices — offers a crucial counterpoint to the control fantasies surrounding AI. If a system reduces possibility, narrows interpretation, automates judgment, or converts ambiguity into premature closure, it may be efficient while being ethically impoverished. A good system does not merely optimize. It amplifies choice.

Stafford Beer’s viable system theory adds another principle: survival requires requisite variety. A regulator must possess enough internal variety to respond to the complexity of its environment. Systems fail when they simplify the world below the threshold required for adaptive response. This is not only a technical principle. It is political.

AI systems often promise control through simplification. They classify, rank, predict, summarize, recommend, filter, and generate. These operations are powerful because they reduce complexity. But when reduction becomes domination, the system becomes brittle. It loses contact with the richness it claims to manage.

James C. Scott’s critique of state legibility belongs here. States simplify forests into timber yields, cities into grids, people into records, land into property, and practices into administrative categories. These simplifications make society governable, but they often destroy the local knowledge — metis — on which actual life depends.

AI can become a new apparatus of legibility.

It can turn culture into training data, identity into features, desire into engagement signals, writing into embeddings, risk into scores, creativity into style transfer, and social life into optimization targets. Like high-modernist planning, it may mistake the map for the territory. Worse, it may generate new territories that conform to the map.

Second-order cybernetics interrupts this by asking: Who observes? Who models? Who benefits from the model? Who is simplified? Who is made legible? Who is excluded from the system’s categories? Who is forced to live inside its outputs?

Design, then, cannot be understood as neutral problem-solving. Design is conversation. Design is the staging of distinctions, constraints, possibilities, and responsibilities. A design process that includes the observer must also include the consequences of observation. The designer is not outside the system. The researcher is not outside the system. The AI ethicist is not outside the system. The user is not outside the system.

This is why “human-centered AI” is not enough. Human-centered design often retains a sovereign subject at the center, surrounded by tools and services. But the world described by Barad, Bateson, Beer, and von Foerster is not centered on the human individual. It is relational, recursive, ecological, and systemic.

The better aim is not human-centered mastery but **responsible participation in world-making systems**.

## 5. Flusser and the Apparatus: Playing Against the Program

Vilém Flusser’s philosophy of photography provides one of the sharpest anticipations of generative AI. For Flusser, the camera is not simply a tool held by the photographer. It is an apparatus: a programmed system that contains a universe of possibilities. The photographer does not create ex nihilo. The photographer plays against the camera program, attempting to realize improbable possibilities within a structured field.

The camera produces technical images. These images do not belong to the older world of traditional images or linear writing. They are produced by apparatuses and distributed through channels that transform social consciousness. The camera is a black box: users may operate it without understanding its internal program. The danger is that photographers become functionaries of the apparatus, endlessly realizing possibilities already contained within the program while imagining themselves free.

Generative AI radicalizes this situation.

The prompt artist, AI filmmaker, synthetic photographer, or text generator plays against a model-program. The system contains a vast possibility space shaped by datasets, architectures, optimization procedures, alignment processes, interface constraints, and platform defaults. The user explores this space through prompts, seeds, sliders, references, and iterative selection. The output feels magical because the apparatus conceals the operations that produce it.

Flusser’s question returns with new force: **Are users playing against the apparatus, or merely functioning for it?**

A generative image model does not simply produce images. It produces a **generative universe**: a space of plausible visual, textual, and cinematic forms. The user navigates this universe, but not from outside it. The apparatus suggests what is easy, what is likely, what is beautiful, what is coherent, what is safe, what is forbidden, what is recognizable, what is style.

This is why AI aesthetics so often tends toward computational mannerism. The system learns surfaces of style and recombines them with remarkable fluency. But fluency is not the same as necessity. Style can become detached from lived constraint, historical struggle, material practice, and situated meaning. The model produces the look of significance without the world that made the sign significant.

This does not mean generative AI cannot make art. It means the artistic problem changes. The task is not merely to produce beautiful outputs. The task is to understand and intervene in the apparatus: to expose its defaults, redirect its probabilities, introduce counter-histories, cultivate improbable paths, and prevent the user from becoming a functionary of latent averages.

Flusser teaches that freedom inside an apparatus is not automatic. Freedom requires playing against the program.

For AI, this means developing practices that do not merely ask the model for outputs but interrogate the possibility space itself. What does the model readily provide? What does it resist? What histories does it flatten? What forms does it overproduce? What kinds of bodies, places, gestures, and futures appear as default? What must be laboriously specified? What cannot be generated at all?

The politics of AI images lies not only in what appears, but in the structure of ease and difficulty governing appearance.

## 6. Deleuze: From Enclosure to Modulation

Deleuze’s “Postscript on the Societies of Control” describes a transition from disciplinary societies to control societies. Disciplinary societies operate through enclosures: school, factory, prison, hospital, family. They mold individuals within bounded institutions. Control societies operate through continuous modulation. They do not need fixed enclosures because control flows through access, passwords, debt, tracking, and perpetual variation.

The individual becomes the dividual: divisible into data points, scores, credentials, permissions, probabilities, and signals.

Generative AI belongs to this regime of modulation. It modulates attention, expression, search, writing, image-making, recommendation, and imagination. It does not simply discipline from the outside. It enters the creative and cognitive process as a continuous companion, assistant, filter, generator, evaluator, and accelerator.

This produces a strange form of freedom: controllable freedom.

The user can generate anything — within the possibility space.
The user can write anything — with autocomplete nearby.
The user can imagine anything — through models trained on prior imagination.
The user can explore endlessly — inside a platform architecture that records, ranks, and monetizes exploration.

Control no longer appears as prohibition. It appears as possibility.

This is why AI systems must be studied not only through bias or misinformation, but through modulation. What rhythms do they impose? What defaults do they normalize? What forms of expression do they make frictionless? What forms of refusal do they make difficult? What kinds of users do they produce?

A text generator may produce a new kind of writer: faster, more iterative, more comparative, more dependent on external suggestion. An image generator may produce a new kind of visual imagination: prompt-based, style-referential, surface-sensitive, rapidly branching. A video generator may produce a new kind of filmmaker: less bound to camera and crew, more bound to descriptive control of latent cinematic probability. A generative agent system may produce a new kind of social simulation: believable, narratively rich, but structured by memory architectures and planning loops that smuggle in assumptions about identity, action, and society.

The danger is not simply that AI controls people. The danger is that AI systems reshape the conditions under which freedom is experienced. They may expand possibility while narrowing the user’s sense of what possibility is.

## 7. World Models and Dream Environments

The “world model” lineage in AI gives technical form to a philosophical problem. A world model compresses observations into latent representations, predicts future states, and enables agents to act within simulated environments. In Ha and Schmidhuber’s formulation, the system consists of a visual component that compresses environmental observations, a memory-prediction component that anticipates future latent states, and a controller that selects actions. The agent can train inside a dream environment generated by its model and then transfer policies back into the actual environment.

This architecture is not merely technical. It is epistemological.

A world model asks: What must be retained from experience so that action remains possible? What can be compressed away? What counts as the environment? What is predictable? What is noise? What is a useful dream?

When generative AI systems are described as world simulators, the stakes become cultural. A video model that produces scenes with apparent object permanence, camera coherence, and physical continuity does not necessarily understand the world as humans do. But it has learned statistical structures sufficient to generate surfaces that humans interpret as worldly. It approximates worldliness.

That approximation matters.

If traditional media represented worlds, generative models increasingly synthesize world-like continuities. They do not merely show a scene; they can extend it, transform it, interpolate it, and simulate motion through it. They produce not isolated images but navigable surfaces of possibility.

Here Barad, Bateson, and Flusser converge. A world model is an apparatus that enacts cuts. It forms a circuit with users. It contains a program of possibilities. It produces technical images that appear as worlds. Its dreams are not private fantasies; they become public media.

The ethical question is therefore not only whether the model’s world is accurate. It is whether the model’s world becomes a substitute for the complexity it compresses.

World models are useful because they reduce. They must reduce. No model can contain the world in full. But every reduction has politics. What forms of causality are preserved? What forms of life are distorted? What relations disappear? What forms of suffering become invisible? What cultural practices become style? What histories become texture? What futures become probable?

A responsible theory of AI world models must therefore combine technical analysis with material-discursive critique. It must ask not only how models predict but how their predictions participate in shaping the world users then inhabit.

## 8. Generative Agents and the Simulation of Social Life

Generative agents extend the problem from perception to sociality. In systems such as Smallville-style agent simulations, large language models are coupled with memory streams, reflection mechanisms, planning components, observation systems, and identity descriptions. Agents remember events, retrieve relevant memories, reflect on patterns, form plans, revise actions, and interact with other agents. The result is emergent social behavior: information diffusion, coordination, relationship formation, event planning.

These systems are fascinating because they simulate not only intelligence but social plausibility.

But plausibility is dangerous when mistaken for social truth.

A generative agent architecture makes cuts into social life. It defines what counts as memory, what counts as reflection, what counts as identity, what counts as a plan, what counts as a relationship, what counts as believable behavior. These definitions are not neutral. They encode a theory of the person.

Is identity a paragraph?
Is memory a retrievable stream?
Is reflection a summary over past observations?
Is planning a hierarchical schedule?
Is social life the coordination of individual agents with goals?
Is believability the same as cultural adequacy?

These are not merely implementation choices. They are ontological commitments disguised as architecture.

Here Ubuntu philosophy and Indigenous epistemologies offer a necessary interruption. Western AI often begins from individual agents: bounded entities with goals, memories, preferences, and actions. Relational ontologies begin elsewhere. A person is constituted through relation. Being is not prior to belonging. Agency is not simply individual capacity but emerges through kinship, obligation, land, ancestry, protocol, and mutual recognition.

What would a generative agent architecture look like if it began from relation rather than individual planning? What if memory were not a private stream but a communal obligation? What if identity were not a stable prompt but a situated role within a living network? What if nonhuman entities — rivers, animals, machines, ancestors, territories — were not background objects but participants in the system of meaning?

The “Ubuntu robot” and related work in cultural robotics points toward this reframing. A social robot designed through Western individualism may model users as autonomous individuals interacting with a service device. A robot informed by Ubuntu relational ontology would instead be evaluated by its capacity to sustain relation, promote dialogue, support cultural practice, and participate appropriately in a form of life.

This is not a decorative diversity layer. It is a different ontology of intelligence.

For generative AI, the implication is stark: if we build artificial agents from impoverished theories of personhood, we will scale those theories into synthetic societies. We will produce simulations that appear socially rich while reproducing narrow assumptions about agency, memory, intention, and value.

## 9. Constructionism, Transclusion, and Objects-to-Think-With

Not every lineage in computational media leads toward control. Engelbart, Papert, Nelson, and Licklider offer another path: augmentation, construction, linkage, and symbiosis.

Engelbart’s augmentation framework treats human intellect as a system composed of artifacts, language, methodology, and training. Tools do not simply accelerate tasks. They reorganize capability. A good augmentation system changes how people think, collaborate, represent problems, and build concepts.

Papert’s constructionism similarly refuses passive instruction. Children learn powerful ideas by building with objects-to-think-with. Logo and turtle geometry make mathematics body-syntonic: the child can identify with the turtle, debug movement, and gradually bridge concrete action with abstract formalism. Error becomes epistemic. Debugging becomes self-reflective development.

Ted Nelson’s Project Xanadu adds another principle: visible connection. Transclusion, deep linkage, side-by-side comparison, and origin visibility resist the flattening of texts into isolated copies. Knowledge remains connected to its sources. Reuse remains accountable to provenance.

These traditions matter because they suggest another possible AI: not AI as black-box oracle, but AI as **constructionist apparatus**.

A responsible generative AI system would not merely produce polished answers. It would expose provenance, alternatives, uncertainty, lineage, cuts, and transformations. It would become an object-to-think-with rather than a machine for premature completion. It would help users debug their own assumptions. It would preserve visible connections between generated surfaces and underlying materials. It would support side-by-side comparison, not just frictionless output. It would amplify the user’s capacity to reason, not merely reduce the labor of expression.

This is the difference between augmentation and substitution.

Substitution says: let the machine do the work.
Augmentation says: reorganize the human-machine system so that better work becomes possible.
Constructionism says: make the system learnable, inspectable, revisable, and personally appropriable.
Transclusion says: keep origins visible so reuse remains principled.
Second-order cybernetics says: include the observer and accept responsibility.
Agential realism says: account for the cuts through which the world appears.

Together, these lineages form the basis for an ethical design program.

## 10. Against Objective Fixation

Theories of open-endedness and novelty search add one more crucial warning. Lehman and Stanley argue that ambitious objectives can deceive. Direct pursuit of a goal may prevent discovery of the stepping stones required to reach something genuinely new. Novelty search, curiosity-driven exploration, and open-ended processes can outperform objective fixation because they preserve the adjacent possible.

This insight cuts against much of contemporary AI deployment. Institutions want goals, metrics, benchmarks, leaderboards, and optimization targets. But the richer the domain, the more dangerous the objective can become. A metric for creativity produces metric-shaped creativity. A benchmark for intelligence produces benchmark-shaped intelligence. A ranking system for relevance produces relevance as defined by the system. A safety score produces safety as measurable avoidance rather than responsible relation.

Richard Sutton’s “bitter lesson” intensifies the point from another angle. Over time, scalable general methods tend to outperform systems built from hand-coded human knowledge. Search and learning, powered by computation, win. But the cultural interpretation of this lesson can become crude. It may encourage the belief that human knowledge, local context, disciplinary nuance, and cultural practice are obsolete. That is the wrong lesson.

The better lesson is double.

First, systems that can search broadly and learn from data are powerful. Second, because they are powerful, the worlds they search through, the objectives they optimize, the data they absorb, and the evaluation systems that guide them become even more ethically significant.

Open-endedness without responsibility becomes drift.
Objectives without humility become traps.
Scaling without metis becomes monoculture.
General methods without cultural grounding become apparatuses of simplification.

The task is not to reject search, learning, or scale. The task is to design systems in which open-ended exploration remains answerable to responsible practice.

## 11. Agential Cybernetic Media

We can now name the synthesis.

**Agential cybernetic media** are systems that bring worlds forth through recursive relations among apparatus, observer, model, matter, discourse, feedback, and action.

They are agential because they participate in the production of boundaries, entities, meanings, and possibilities.
They are cybernetic because they operate through feedback, regulation, communication, prediction, and adaptation.
They are media because they organize perception, representation, memory, distribution, and reception.
They are ethical because every cut excludes, includes, amplifies, suppresses, enables, and constrains.

Generative AI is one powerful instance of agential cybernetic media, but not the only one. Cameras, simulations, social platforms, maps, robots, hypertext systems, management dashboards, educational microworlds, and state databases all belong to the broader family. What distinguishes contemporary AI is the degree to which modeling, generation, interaction, and world-like synthesis converge in a single apparatus.

An agential cybernetic media analysis asks:

What cuts does the system make?
What relations does it render visible or invisible?
What feedback loops does it create?
What forms of agency does it attribute or deny?
What kinds of worlds does it make easy to imagine?
What kinds of worlds does it make difficult?
What histories does it compress into style?
What local knowledge does it flatten into data?
What possibilities does it amplify?
What forms of choice does it reduce?
What responsibilities does it displace?
What practices of response can be built into the circuit?

This framework moves beyond familiar AI debates without abandoning them. Bias, misinformation, labor exploitation, copyright, authorship, environmental cost, and safety all remain crucial. But they are reframed as symptoms of a deeper apparatus condition: AI systems are now part of the machinery through which culture perceives, predicts, narrates, and modifies itself.

The question is not whether AI represents reality correctly.

The question is how AI participates in reality’s ongoing construction.

## 12. Responsible Practice and the Ethics of Mattering

If AI systems participate in world-making, then ethics cannot be limited to rules of acceptable use. Ethics must become a practice of response within entanglement.

Barad’s “ethics of mattering” insists that responsibility is not external judgment applied after the fact. Responsibility is built into the very process by which boundaries are made. Because cuts produce consequences, one is responsible for the cuts one helps enact.

Bateson adds that pathology arises when systems lose flexibility. A system that cannot revise its categories, respond to difference, or learn from its environment becomes dangerous. Beer adds that viable systems require requisite variety. Von Foerster adds that ethical action increases choice. Scott warns that simplification destroys metis. Flusser warns that apparatuses turn users into functionaries. Deleuze warns that freedom can become modulation. Papert reminds us that systems can also become objects-to-think-with. Ubuntu and Indigenous epistemologies remind us that intelligence without relation is a damaged abstraction.

A responsible AI practice would therefore require at least seven commitments.

First, **cut visibility**: systems should expose the distinctions they make. Users should know what has been included, excluded, compressed, inferred, blocked, or transformed.

Second, **provenance and transclusion**: generated outputs should preserve visible relations to sources, inspirations, datasets, prompts, transformations, and uncertainties wherever possible.

Third, **choice amplification**: AI should increase meaningful human and collective possibility, not merely accelerate production or automate judgment.

Fourth, **relational adequacy**: systems should be evaluated by how they sustain relations among people, communities, environments, and nonhuman participants, not only by task performance.

Fifth, **metis preservation**: AI should protect contextual, local, embodied, and practice-based knowledge from being flattened into universal categories.

Sixth, **apparatus literacy**: users should learn to play against the program, not merely function within it. This requires interfaces that reveal defaults, constraints, failure modes, and alternative pathways.

Seventh, **world-making accountability**: institutions deploying AI must accept responsibility not only for outputs but for the social realities their systems help stabilize.

These are not abstract ideals. They are design principles, research methods, and political demands.

## Conclusion: Meeting the Machine Halfway

To meet the universe halfway, in Barad’s sense, is not to stand outside the world and describe it from a distance. It is to participate responsibly in the world’s becoming. To meet the machine halfway is not to surrender agency to automation, nor to pretend that human mastery remains intact. It is to recognize that human and machine agencies are now entangled in circuits of perception, prediction, memory, generation, and action.

The central problem of generative AI is not that machines may become human-like. That question is too narrow. The deeper problem is that humans, machines, institutions, images, languages, datasets, and environments are forming new apparatuses of world-production.

Some of these apparatuses will amplify freedom.
Some will intensify control.
Some will preserve relation.
Some will flatten culture into style.
Some will open new forms of thought.
Some will automate old forms of domination.
Most will do several of these at once.

The task of theory is to make these entanglements visible. The task of design is to intervene in them. The task of ethics is to accept responsibility for the cuts.

Generative AI does not merely answer prompts. It reorganizes the conditions under which prompts can be imagined. It does not merely produce images. It transforms the ecology of seeing. It does not merely simulate worlds. It participates in deciding what counts as world-like. It does not merely assist thought. It enters the circuit through which thought becomes possible.

The future of AI will therefore not be decided by intelligence alone. It will be decided by the forms of relation we build around it: the circuits, cuts, conversations, protocols, interfaces, and practices through which human and nonhuman agencies learn to matter together.

The question is no longer whether machines can think.

The question is whether our human-machine worlds can become responsible.
