# Textual Action Hermeneutics: Reading Social and Technical Deeds After the Event

## 1. The Event Vanishes; the Trace Remains

The central problem of hermeneutics is not that meaning is hidden inside another person. That is the romantic mistake: the belief that interpretation is chiefly a rescue mission directed toward an absent mind. The deeper problem is harsher and more material. Events vanish. Speech disappears. Actions pass. The human sciences inherit not the living immediacy of the event, but its remainder: the inscription, the record, the trace, the sedimented pattern, the institutional consequence. Ricoeur’s decisive move in “The Model of the Text” is to make this disappearance methodological. The object of interpretation is not simply what someone meant, nor what someone privately intended, but what becomes readable once discourse or action has detached itself from the moment of its production. 

This makes the text more than a literary object. It becomes a paradigm for the human sciences. Ricoeur asks whether the “object” of social science can be understood according to the model of the text, and whether the procedures of textual interpretation can become a general paradigm for interpreting meaningful human action. That question matters because social life is not available to us as pure presence. It reaches us as documents, institutions, controversies, consequences, archives, reputations, judgments, built environments, interfaces, routines, and inherited forms. Human action, once completed, does not remain identical to the intention of the actor. It becomes exposed. It enters history. It can be re-read by others who were not present when it occurred. 

The thesis of this essay is that Ricoeur’s model can be extended into a theory of textual action: meaningful deeds become interpretable when they undergo a form of inscription. This inscription may be literal writing, but it may also be archival, institutional, technical, procedural, or infrastructural. A law, a protest, a design decision, a platform policy, a model output, a database entry, a software commit, a moderation log, a bureaucratic denial, or an algorithmic ranking can all become textual in Ricoeur’s sense when they detach from their originating event and acquire durable meaning in a field of possible interpretation. The human scientist, media theorist, STS scholar, or cultural analyst therefore does not merely ask, “What did the actor intend?” The sharper question is: what has the action become readable as?

## 2. Discourse Is Not Language

Ricoeur begins by distinguishing discourse from language. Language, understood as system, code, schema, or competence, exists virtually. It is the condition of possible utterance. Discourse, by contrast, is event. It occurs. It is realized in time. It has a speaker, a world, and an addressee. Ricoeur draws from Saussure, Hjelmslev, Benveniste, and other linguistic traditions to separate the abstract structure of language from the actual event of saying something to someone about something. 

This distinction is crucial because only discourse can disappear. A language-system does not vanish in the same way an utterance does. A code remains available; a speech event passes. Therefore the problem of writing is not the fixation of language in general, but the fixation of discourse. Writing rescues not the whole living situation of speech, but the “said” of saying: the meaning that can be detached, repeated, re-identified, and interpreted after the event has disappeared. 

Ricoeur’s theory therefore shifts hermeneutics away from the psychology of the speaker and toward the objectification of meaning. In living speech, speaker intention and discourse meaning may overlap. To ask “What do you mean?” and “What does that mean?” can appear nearly identical. But writing breaks that overlap. Once discourse is written, the author can no longer stand beside it, gesture toward the situation, correct the misunderstanding, or rescue the speech event through tone, face, timing, and bodily presence. The text must survive by meaning alone. Interpretation begins in this wound. 

This is why writing is not merely a storage device. It is a transformation of discourse’s ontology. It makes meaning autonomous. It releases discourse from the finite horizon of its production. The text is not authorless, but the author no longer governs the circumference of its meaning. A text’s career exceeds the life, intention, and situation of its maker. Its addressee is no longer the immediate interlocutor, but “whoever knows how to read.” The text generates an audience larger than the original scene. 

## 3. What Writing Fixes

Ricoeur’s answer to the question “What does writing fix?” is subtle. Writing does not fix the event of speaking as event. It fixes the meaning of the speech event. To explain this, he turns to speech-act theory: Austin’s distinction between locutionary, illocutionary, and perlocutionary acts. A speech act includes what is said, what is done in saying it, and what is done by saying it. The propositional content of speech is most easily inscribed; illocutionary force can also be partly inscribed through grammar, mood, genre, formula, and convention; perlocutionary effect is least inscribable because it depends most directly on affective and situational force. 

The key is that meaning is not reduced to the sentence as a bare proposition. Meaning includes force, mode, recognizable social action, and codified intention. A promise, command, accusation, apology, threat, warning, or invitation is not merely a cluster of words. It is an act structured by rules that make it identifiable as such. Writing can preserve enough of this structure to permit later interpretation. The text therefore contains not the living event, but the re-identifiable noema of the event: the said, the meant-as-said, the sense-content capable of being read. 

This is the first major movement of textual action hermeneutics: the event surpasses itself in meaning. The event happens once; meaning becomes repeatable. The event is temporal; meaning can be re-identified. The event is situated; meaning can travel. The event has a speaker; meaning can survive the speaker’s absence. This does not make meaning stable in a simplistic way. It makes meaning available for argument. A text can be interpreted because it is no longer fused with the event that produced it.

The same logic applies to action. If speech can be fixed as text because the act of saying contains an exteriorizable meaning, then meaningful action can also be fixed when its sense-content detaches from the transaction in which it first occurred. A deed becomes readable when it leaves behind a pattern, consequence, mark, institution, or record that can be identified and re-identified. This is not metaphorical decoration. It is the methodological bridge from hermeneutics to the human sciences.

## 4. Action as Inscription

Ricoeur’s decisive analogy is that meaningful action can become an object of science under conditions similar to the fixation of discourse by writing. At the prescientific level, action may be understood practically: we know how to act, respond, cooperate, interpret gestures, and move through social life. But scientific interpretation requires objectification. Action must become available as something more than an ongoing transaction. It must become a delineated pattern that can be read according to its internal relations. 

Action resembles discourse because it can have propositional structure. An action can be described as doing something: teaching, promising, killing, refusing, signing, building, deleting, publishing, ranking, banning, inviting, classifying. These action predicates allow multiple complements: who acted, upon what, where, when, with what instrument, under what description, according to what rule, with what consequence. Like speech, action has a grammar. It can be identified not only as motion or behavior, but as meaningful doing. 

But action also resembles speech at the level of force. A raised hand may be a greeting, a vote, a surrender, a classroom request, a fascist salute, or a signal to stop traffic. The physical movement is insufficient. What matters is the rule-governed social sense by which an action counts as this or that. Here Searle’s notion of constitutive rules becomes crucial: certain acts are intelligible because a community recognizes the conditions under which they count as promises, declarations, authorizations, refusals, violations, or decisions. 

Ricoeur’s question then becomes: what corresponds to writing in the field of action? His answer is trace, record, mark, sedimentation. Human action leaves marks in social time. It generates durable effects. It becomes part of history, reputation, institution, archive, and memory. The deed escapes the doer. Like a text detached from its author, action becomes autonomous from its agent. It develops consequences the actor did not fully intend and cannot fully control. 

This is where textual action becomes politically sharp. Responsibility becomes difficult precisely because complex actions exceed the clean immediacy of intention. The more complex the action, the harder it becomes to assign authorship. Institutions act through distributed procedures. Platforms act through design choices, policies, models, user behaviors, and feedback loops. Bureaucracies act through forms, defaults, eligibility categories, automated triggers, and deferred decisions. The question “Who did this?” becomes less obvious, but not less necessary. Ricoeur’s model does not abolish responsibility; it shows why responsibility becomes an interpretive problem.

## 5. History as Record of Human Action

Ricoeur’s metaphor of the record is central. Human action becomes social action when it is recorded in the broad sense: not only written down by historians, but sedimented into durable patterns. History is the quasi-surface on which action leaves marks. Before official archives, there is already the social recording of action through consequences, institutions, reputations, norms, and inherited arrangements. Human deeds become documents of human action. 

The importance of this move cannot be overstated. It gives the human sciences an object that is neither raw behavior nor private consciousness. The object is meaningful behavior as socially fixed. This objectivity is not the objectivity of physics. It is the objectivity of sedimented meaning. The social world is made of actions that have become readable because they persist beyond their originating transaction. They can be interpreted, contested, reactivated, and judged.

This also means that institutions are not merely containers of action; they are action made durable. A policy is past action given future force. A form is a sedimented decision about what counts. A legal category is an interpreted world stabilized into procedure. A platform interface is a set of assumptions about users, values, incentives, visibility, and action possibilities made operational. A machine-learning model is not simply a technical artifact; it is a compressed archive of training choices, data histories, loss functions, optimization pressures, labeling regimes, deployment contexts, and institutional aims. Each becomes readable when treated as an inscription of action.

The archive is therefore not passive. It is not a warehouse of dead evidence. It is the field in which actions become available to future interpretation. The archive preserves the wound between event and meaning. It shows that actions are never exhausted by the situations that produced them. Like texts, they can open new worlds, authorize new practices, and acquire new relevance under later conditions.

## 6. From Situation to World

For Ricoeur, the text does not lose reference when it leaves the original situation. It gains a different kind of reference. Spoken discourse often refers ostensively: this person, this place, this moment, this shared situation. Written discourse cannot rely on the same immediate field of pointing. Yet that does not make it worldless. On the contrary, the text opens a world. Its reference is no longer the narrow situation of dialogue but a projected field of possible being, interpretation, and inhabitation. 

This is one of Ricoeur’s most powerful claims. The text does not merely describe a world; it discloses one. To understand a text is not first to recover another person’s mind, but to encounter a possible mode of being-in-the-world. The text enlarges the reader’s situation by inserting into it new predicates, possibilities, relations, and horizons. Interpretation is therefore not backward-looking only. It is projective. It lets a past inscription reconfigure the present field of meaning. 

The same is true of action. An important action is not merely relevant to its original situation. It exceeds that situation. It develops meanings that can be actualized elsewhere. Its importance lies in durable relevance: the capacity to be taken up, repeated, judged, contested, or transformed in later contexts. A court decision, a protest tactic, a scientific instrument, a genre convention, a design pattern, or a platform policy may begin as a local response, but later become a world-structuring reference. 

This distinction between relevance and importance is essential for cultural analysis. Many actions are relevant only within their immediate transaction. Others become important because they outlive that transaction. They become available for reenactment. They open a world of possible conduct. They generate genres, expectations, precedents, myths, fears, workflows, and counter-practices. They are no longer merely events. They are world-bearing inscriptions.

## 7. Human Action as Open Work

If action becomes text-like through fixation, autonomy, world-reference, and open address, then action also becomes an “open work.” Its meaning is not closed by its contemporaries. History can re-read it. Later actors can discover implications invisible to the original participants. New contexts can actualize dormant meanings. This does not mean interpretation is arbitrary. It means action has a future-facing hermeneutic life. 

The open work of action is especially visible in political and technical systems. Consider a design decision that seems minor at the time: ranking by engagement, preserving logs by default, making a checkbox opt-out rather than opt-in, encoding identity categories into a database, automating eligibility decisions, training a model on scraped cultural materials, or making user behavior legible to advertisers. Each decision begins somewhere. It has a local rationale. But once sedimented into infrastructure, it detaches from its designers and becomes part of a broader world of action. It can later be read as convenience, extraction, surveillance, optimization, exclusion, carelessness, innovation, or institutional violence.

This is not because later interpreters are simply projecting meanings onto neutral objects. It is because the object itself has entered social time. It has generated consequences. It has become readable through its effects. The meaning of the action is not reducible to what its makers privately intended. It lies in the structured field of consequences, rules, references, and future interpretations that the action opens.

Ricoeur therefore gives us a way to think beyond authorial innocence. “We did not mean it that way” may be relevant, but it is not final. Once action is inscribed, intention is only one part of the interpretive field. The action’s social meaning may exceed its psychological origin. Its world may be larger than its motive.

## 8. Explanation and Understanding Are Not Enemies

Ricoeur’s methodological intervention is to refuse the old dichotomy between explanation and understanding. Dilthey had distinguished the human sciences from the natural sciences by associating the former with understanding and the latter with explanation. Ricoeur does not simply collapse the distinction. He reworks it through the model of reading. Textual interpretation involves both comprehension and explanation. We first guess the whole; then we validate the guess. We move from understanding to explanation, and from explanation back to deeper understanding. 

This matters because texts are wholes, not mere strings of sentences. Their meanings must be construed. The interpreter must decide what is central and what is subordinate, what belongs to the governing structure and what is incidental. This is why interpretation always includes a moment of guessing. But guessing alone is not enough. Interpretations require validation. They must be argued, tested against the structure of the text, compared with rival readings, and shown to be more probable than alternatives. 

Ricoeur’s model is wonderfully severe here. The fact that there is more than one possible interpretation does not mean all interpretations are equal. The text is a limited field of possible constructions. Interpretation moves between dogmatism and skepticism. It cannot claim absolute verification in the natural-scientific sense, but neither does it collapse into opinion. It proceeds through conflict, probability, evidence, and relative superiority. 

For the human sciences, this is decisive. Social actions, historical events, and institutional patterns can be construed in multiple ways because their meanings are genuinely plurivocal. But this plurivocity belongs to the object itself. It is not merely a defect in the observer. Human action is interpretable because motives, rules, consequences, and descriptions form a field of possible sense. To interpret action is to argue about what the action is best understood as. 

## 9. The Courtroom of Interpretation

Ricoeur’s proximity to juridical reasoning is not accidental. Interpretation resembles legal argument because claims can be defeated. A reading must answer objections. It must withstand rival constructions. It must show why this description of the action is more compelling than that one. This is especially important when action is morally or politically charged. What counts as negligence, care, refusal, consent, coercion, discrimination, authorship, repair, or harm is rarely self-evident. These are interpretive judgments, but not therefore arbitrary judgments.

The courtroom is a model not because every interpretation should become legalistic, but because it dramatizes the polemical structure of meaning. Interpretation happens under conditions of contest. The analyst gathers traces, reconstructs rules, tests motives, weighs contexts, compares descriptions, and asks what would defeat the claim. This gives humanistic inquiry its rigor. It is not rigor through measurement alone, but rigor through disciplined defeasibility.

This is also where Ricoeur’s model becomes useful for contemporary AI and platform analysis. The meaning of a model output, ranking decision, automated denial, generated image, chatbot hallucination, or moderation action cannot be read only from the isolated artifact. It must be read through the institutional and technical chain that made it possible: training data, interface prompts, design defaults, model architecture, optimization objectives, policy layers, user context, deployment setting, and downstream uptake. Each interpretation can be challenged. Each must be validated. The goal is not to find the ghostly intention inside the machine. The goal is to interpret the action as a socially and technically inscribed event.

## 10. Technical Action as Textual Action

Ricoeur was not writing about artificial intelligence, platform governance, machine learning, or algorithmic mediation. But his model travels well because contemporary technical systems intensify the very conditions he describes. They detach action from author, distribute agency across systems, produce durable traces, and generate worlds of interpretation far beyond the moment of execution.

A model output is not a speech event in the ordinary human sense, but it is discourse-like: it appears as a saying, enters a communicative situation, refers to a world, and addresses a reader. It is also text-like: it can be fixed, copied, evaluated, circulated, detached from the conditions of generation, and interpreted by audiences unknown to its immediate user. Its meaning cannot be reduced to the intention of any single agent because there may be no single agent whose intention governs it. It emerges from a chain of human and nonhuman operations: data collection, labeling, training, reinforcement, prompting, filtering, interface design, user interpretation, and institutional deployment.

Similarly, a platform action—ranking a post, removing a video, recommending a friend, flagging a phrase, denying an account, prioritizing a search result—becomes meaningful through inscription. It leaves logs, metrics, behavioral consequences, user experiences, reputational effects, and policy precedents. It may be read as neutral optimization by one actor, censorship by another, safety enforcement by another, infrastructural bias by another, and bureaucratic opacity by yet another. The plurality of readings is not a failure of interpretation. It reflects the action’s location inside a contested social world.

Textual action hermeneutics therefore offers a method for reading technical systems without mystifying them. It refuses two bad options. The first is intentional reduction: “the system only means what its designers meant.” The second is technical fatalism: “the system is too complex to interpret.” Ricoeur gives us a third path. Treat the action as inscribed. Identify its sense-content. Reconstruct its rules. Follow its detachment from origin. Examine the world it opens. Test rival interpretations against the trace.

## 11. The Archive of Deeds

Every technical system produces an archive of deeds. Some of this archive is explicit: logs, datasets, documentation, policies, changelogs, tickets, prompts, annotations, moderation histories. Some is implicit: user adaptation, interface habits, emergent norms, workarounds, exclusions, reputational damage, cultural imitation, institutional dependency. The analyst’s task is to read across both.

This archive is not merely evidentiary. It is ontological. It is where action becomes durable enough to be interpreted. A platform’s “decision” is not only a moment of computation. It is the patterned relation among code, policy, data, institution, user, and consequence. A machine-learning model’s “answer” is not only a string of tokens. It is an event whose meaning depends on the world it opens: what it authorizes, obscures, repeats, invents, damages, accelerates, or makes plausible.

Here Ricoeur meets media theory and STS. Technologies are not just tools used by subjects; they are material-discursive arrangements that stabilize possible actions. They make some meanings easy, others difficult. They preserve some traces, erase others. They create readers, users, auditors, and publics. They produce what might be called operational texts: executable inscriptions whose meanings unfold through use.

To read such systems is not to “decode” them once and for all. It is to enter a conflict of interpretations. The same system may be read through labor, race, gender, bureaucracy, aesthetics, infrastructure, political economy, cybernetics, semiotics, or law. Ricoeur’s warning still holds: multiple readings do not imply interpretive anarchy. Each reading must show its evidence, articulate its object, name its assumptions, and face rival accounts.

## 12. Against Authorial Innocence and Interpretive Laziness

The political force of textual action hermeneutics lies in its attack on innocence. Once action enters history, the actor no longer owns its meaning. This is not a moralistic claim; it is a hermeneutic one. Meaning detaches. Consequences unfold. Records persist. Worlds open. Future readers arrive.

This matters for institutions that hide behind intention. A company may say it intended only efficiency. A government may say it intended only security. A designer may say they intended only usability. A researcher may say they intended only experimentation. A model developer may say the output was unintended. These claims belong in the interpretive field, but they do not close it. The meaning of the action must also be read in its trace: who was affected, what categories were stabilized, what behaviors were incentivized, what world was opened, what alternatives were foreclosed.

At the same time, Ricoeur’s model also disciplines critique. It prevents the critic from treating suspicion as proof. To say that an action can be read against intention does not mean it can be read however one wishes. The interpretation must be validated. It must account for the structure of the act, the archive of consequences, the rules under which it counted as meaningful, and the rival descriptions that might defeat it. The critic, too, stands before the tribunal of interpretation.

This is why textual action hermeneutics is both severe and generous. It is severe because it refuses the comfort of pure intention. It is generous because it grants that meaning is not exhausted by suspicion. It allows actions to be complex, overdetermined, internally conflicted, and historically mobile. It can read harm without flattening causality; it can read creativity without romanticizing agency; it can read systems without pretending that systems speak in one voice.

## 13. Method: How to Read an Action as a Text

A Ricoeurian method for reading action as text might proceed through six operations.

First, identify the event. What happened? Who or what acted? Under what description does the action become recognizable?

Second, identify the inscription. What trace, record, artifact, institution, interface, policy, or consequence allows the action to outlive its moment?

Third, identify the detachment. How has the meaning of the action separated from the intention of the actor, the original audience, or the immediate situation?

Fourth, identify the world. What possibilities, roles, relations, categories, or forms of life does the action open or stabilize?

Fifth, identify rival interpretations. What are the plausible readings? What does each foreground or suppress? What evidence would strengthen or defeat them?

Sixth, validate without pretending to verify absolutely. Which interpretation best accounts for the archive, the structure of the action, its rules, its consequences, and its continuing relevance?

This method is especially useful where agency is distributed. It can read software systems, institutional processes, cultural artifacts, AI outputs, public controversies, design decisions, and historical events. Its strength is that it does not require the analyst to choose between meaning and structure, intention and consequence, interpretation and explanation. It treats all of them as moments in the career of the action.

## 14. Conclusion: The World Opened by the Trace

Ricoeur’s “The Model of the Text” offers more than a theory of reading. It offers a theory of what happens when meaning survives presence. Discourse becomes text when the event of saying vanishes but the said remains. Action becomes text-like when the event of doing passes but its trace enters social time. The human sciences are hermeneutical not because they deal with vague subjective meanings, but because their objects are meaningful formations that have been fixed, detached, opened, and addressed to future readers. 

This model is newly urgent in a world of technical mediation. AI systems, platforms, bureaucracies, and computational infrastructures generate actions whose authorship is distributed and whose consequences exceed intention. They leave traces everywhere: logs, outputs, datasets, interfaces, decisions, rankings, exclusions, habits, norms, and worlds. To study them well, we need neither naïve empathy nor crude positivism. We need a disciplined hermeneutics of inscription.

The event vanishes. The trace remains. The trace opens a world. The world demands interpretation. And interpretation, if it is to deserve the name, must become answerable to the record of what has been done.
