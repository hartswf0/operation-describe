# The Machine That Paints With Words

## Operative Ekphrasis and the Collapse of the Text/Image Distinction in Multimodal AI

### Abstract

This essay argues that multimodal artificial intelligence transforms ekphrasis from a literary problem of representation into a computational problem of operation. Classical ekphrasis presumes a distinction between word and image: language describes, evokes, imitates, rivals, or translates the visual. Digital media complicate this relation because code can cause images to appear rather than merely describe them. In the sequential paradigm of classical computing, text becomes operative as executable code: it performs image production without possessing semantic meaning in the human sense. In the connectionist paradigm of machine learning, however, the relation changes again. Multimodal systems such as DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, CLIP, and related models do not merely translate text into image; they correlate text and image within a shared representational space. This produces what Hannes Bajohr calls “artificial semantics”: a weak, nonhuman, nonintentional form of meaning that is neither full understanding nor mere syntax. Through examples from visual poetry, software studies, ekphrasis theory, and Critical AI Studies, this essay proposes operative ekphrasis as a method for reading AI-generated media. The central claim is not that machines understand images and language as humans do, but that multimodal AI forces literary and media theory to confront a technical substrate in which text and image no longer remain separate media. Ekphrasis, once the art of making listeners see, becomes the machine operation through which words generate images by passing through a shared computational field.

**Keywords:** operative ekphrasis, multimodal AI, artificial semantics, Critical AI Studies, visual poetry, text-image relations, media theory

## 1. Ekphrasis After the Prompt Box

Ekphrasis once named a rhetorical miracle: words could make an absent image appear before the mind. In its ancient sense, it was not merely the literary description of artworks, but a technique of vivid presentation. The speaker transformed listeners into spectators. Later, the term narrowed. Ekphrasis became the verbal representation of visual representation: a poem describing a painting, a novel describing a statue, a literary work reaching toward an image it could never fully become.

That impossibility gave ekphrasis its charge. Word and image seemed locked in a rivalry. Language unfolded in time; painting occupied space. Lessing’s *Laocoön* made this division canonical: poetry was suited to action, painting to bodies. Later theorists such as James Heffernan and W. J. T. Mitchell recast this rivalry as a drama of hope and fear. Ekphrasis promised a crossing between media, but also threatened confusion. The image might be absorbed by language; language might fail before the image. Either way, ekphrasis depended on the border it crossed.

The prompt box changes the scene.

When a user types “an astronaut riding a horse in photorealistic style” and receives an image, language has not merely described a visual object. It has triggered an operation. The sentence is not a poem about an image, nor a caption beneath one, nor a verbal supplement to a painting. It is an instruction that enters a trained model and returns as pixels. In this context, the old question “Can words represent images?” becomes insufficient. The more urgent question is: what happens when words produce images?

This is the problem Hannes Bajohr names **operative ekphrasis**. The phrase matters because it preserves the old ekphrastic tension while relocating it inside technical systems. Ekphrasis no longer means only the verbal evocation of the visual. It becomes the computational performance of image by text. The word does not stand before the image as description. It enters a machine as input, passes through an operational substrate, and returns as visual output.

But even this formulation needs a further distinction. Not all digital operations are the same. A shell script that arranges text fragments into a visual poem and a neural network that generates an image from a natural-language prompt both involve text producing image. Yet they do so through radically different forms of digitality. The first belongs to the sequential paradigm of classical computing: explicit instructions, executable syntax, deterministic operation. The second belongs to the connectionist paradigm of machine learning: trained weights, latent spaces, statistical correlation, probabilistic generation.

The difference between these two paradigms is not merely technical. It changes what text, image, meaning, and operation are allowed to mean.

## 2. Three Poems, Three Media Regimes

Visual poetry offers a compact archive for this problem because it already places word and image under pressure. A visual poem asks to be read and seen at once. Letters become marks. Words become shapes. Syntax becomes composition. The page becomes a visual field.

Consider three kinds of works.

First, an analogue typewriter poem such as Franz Mon’s “non/tot” arranges repeated words into a visual form. The poem is made from text, but its image emerges through bodily and mechanical action: keystrokes, pressure, paper, typebar, spacing. The text does not itself perform an operation. It is arranged by the poet’s hand and machine. The visual result is a perceptual effect produced by material labor.

Second, a digitally generated visual poem such as Jasmin Meerhoff’s *They Lay* changes the relation. Here a script cuts and arranges fragments of scanned text according to formal instructions. The visible poem is produced by another text: code. There are now at least two textual layers. One is the script that acts. The other is the resulting visual object. The first text performs the second.

This is operative ekphrasis in the sequential digital mode. Code causes the image-like poem to appear. The text does not represent the image. It produces it.

Third, an AI-generated poem-like image such as Dave Orr’s “Stiny Snity Grify,” produced with DALL-E 2 from the prompt “a poem about the singularity written in a serif font,” introduces a different order. The resulting image resembles writing, perhaps even poetry, but its “text” is garbled, asemic, and unstable. It has the visual Gestalt of a poem without being fully legible as one. Here the input is meaningful natural language, but the output is not a deterministic transformation of that input by explicit rules. It is a probabilistic synthesis generated by a model trained to correlate textual descriptions and visual forms.

The three works therefore mark three media regimes:

1. **Analogue visual poetry:** text becomes image through manual and mechanical arrangement.
2. **Sequential digital poetry:** code performs image through executable syntax.
3. **Connectionist AI image generation:** prompt becomes image through learned multimodal correlation.

Each regime produces a different answer to the question: how can text become image?

The analogue answer is: through embodied arrangement.
The sequential answer is: through executable operation.
The connectionist answer is: through artificial semantics.

## 3. Sequential Digitality: Pragmatics Without Semantics

Classical computing is built on explicit instruction. A program runs through operations in sequence: read this, cut that, transform here, output there. Even when the operation becomes complex, the governing logic remains procedural. Code acts because it is executable.

This gives code a peculiar relation to language. Programming languages look like language, and programmers often speak of them as languages, but their linguistic status is unstable. Code has syntax. It may contain words recognizable to human readers. It may even carry poetic or expressive associations. But its operative force does not depend on human-like meaning. It depends on formal structure, parseability, and execution.

In this sense, sequential code has a limited pragmatics without full semantics. It does things, but it does not mean in the way natural language means. Its performativity is technical rather than communicative. A command in a script changes a file, crops an image, moves a fragment, or generates a visual pattern. But the machine does not understand the command as a situated speaker would. It executes.

This makes sequential digital visual poetry a crucial intermediate form. It is not ekphrasis in the classical sense because the code does not describe an image. Nor is it merely visual poetry in the analogue sense because the visible form is not only manually arranged. It is produced by a hidden procedural text.

The image is therefore haunted by another text. Behind the visible poem sits the operative poem: the script, the instruction set, the executable procedure. Hayles’s claim that electronic text is processual becomes central here. Digital text is not only something displayed; it is something performed. The visible object is the residue of execution.

Yet in the sequential case, the text/image distinction remains intact at an important level. The code may generate the image, and the image file may itself be encoded alphanumerically, but text and image still occupy different functional positions. Code commands. Image appears. Human viewers then interpret the visible output as text, image, or both.

The machine does not collapse word and image into meaning. It manipulates symbolic structures. The semantic fusion happens for us, at the level of perception and interpretation.

## 4. Connectionist Digitality: Semantics Without Pragmatics

Machine learning alters this arrangement. A neural network does not operate like a readable script. Its “knowledge” is not stored as explicit instructions but distributed across weights, activations, and learned patterns. One can inspect numerical values, but those values do not translate into a human-readable sequence of rules. The model’s behavior emerges from trained statistical relations rather than from commands one can simply read line by line.

This is why Bajohr’s distinction between the sequential and the connectionist is so useful. Both are digital, but they are not digital in the same conceptual sense. The sequential paradigm is procedural, explicit, and command-like. The connectionist paradigm is statistical, distributed, and model-like.

In multimodal AI, this difference becomes decisive because the model learns correspondences between different forms of data. Systems such as CLIP are trained on large numbers of image-caption pairs. They learn to place related images and texts near each other in a shared representational space. A picture of a cat and the phrase “a photo of a cat” become associated not because the model understands cats as a person does, but because it learns patterns of correlation across visual and linguistic data.

This is not semantics in the full human sense. There is no lived experience, intention, embodiment, or communicative situation. The model does not know what a cat is as a creature one might touch, feed, fear, love, or remember. But it is also not mere syntax. The model’s internal organization correlates words and images in ways that support flexible cross-modal generation. It can take a sentence and produce an image aligned with that sentence. It can treat textual and visual patterns as mutually informative.

This is the strange zone Bajohr calls **artificial semantics**: meaning that is weak, dumb, nonintentional, and technical, yet not reducible to empty formal manipulation. The model does not mean as humans mean. But it also does not operate like a classical script devoid of semantic relation. It encodes correlations that behave like meaning under specific operational conditions.

This produces a chiasmus between the two digital paradigms:

The sequential system has **pragmatics without semantics**.
It acts through command but does not mean.

The connectionist system has **semantics without pragmatics**.
It correlates meanings but does not act through explicit command-like speech.

The prompt sits at the threshold between these worlds. It looks like ordinary language. It may be poetic, descriptive, imperative, vague, atmospheric, or technical. But once entered into a multimodal model, it becomes an operational vector. It activates learned correlations. It does not describe an already existing image; it summons a probable image from a learned distribution.

The prompt is ekphrasis after execution.

## 5. The Multimodal Pictorial Third

The most radical implication of multimodal AI is not that text can generate image. Sequential code already showed that. The more radical implication is that text and image can be processed as expressions of a shared representational substrate.

Classical ekphrasis assumes two sides: word and image. Even theories that emphasize performance, response, or intermediality preserve the relation between distinct media. The word reaches toward the image. The image resists or receives the word. The drama depends on difference.

Multimodal AI changes the topology. In models trained across text and image, the relation is not simply one medium representing another. Instead, both are mapped into a shared computational space. Text and image become different entrances into a third field.

This third field is not a human imagination, not a Platonic realm of forms, not a museum of representations. It is a learned statistical space. It contains correlations, clusters, distances, probabilities, and associations. But from the standpoint of media theory, it behaves like a **multimodal pictorial third**: a substrate in which text and image are no longer primary opposites but different surface expressions of an underlying model.

This does not mean that text and image disappear culturally. Humans still encounter prompts as words and outputs as images. Interfaces still separate input boxes from generated pictures. Legal, aesthetic, political, and interpretive systems still distinguish between writing and visual art. But at the level of model operation, the distinction has been technically weakened. The system does not need to preserve Lessing’s division between temporal language and spatial image. It learns cross-modal proximity.

This is why multimodal AI does not merely remediate older media. Remediation still assumes identifiable media being refashioned through other media. But shared embedding space suggests something more unsettling: not the translation of one medium into another, but the partial suspension of medial difference inside computation.

Ekphrasis no longer means “words evoke an image.”
It means “words and images are routed through a shared artificial semantics.”

The old ekphrastic hope was that language might make us see.
The old ekphrastic fear was that such crossing violated the proper limits of media.
The multimodal condition bypasses both. It neither triumphantly fulfills ekphrasis nor simply destroys it. It changes the level at which ekphrasis operates.

The border is no longer between poem and painting.
The border is between human interpretation and model representation.

## 6. Critical AI Studies as Conceptual Stress Test

This argument also shifts how the humanities might engage AI. Much Critical AI Studies rightly focuses on power: extraction, surveillance, labor, bias, racialization, environmental cost, platform capitalism, and the ideological inflation of “intelligence.” These critiques remain essential. AI systems are not neutral instruments. They are sociotechnical arrangements built from data, capital, labor, infrastructure, institutional desire, and political asymmetry.

But there is another task alongside critique: conceptual stress testing. AI systems do not only demand ethical and political analysis. They also expose hidden assumptions inside humanistic categories. They force inherited concepts to reveal what they took for granted.

Ekphrasis is one such concept. It assumed, even when contesting it, a relation between word and image. It assumed the difference it dramatized. Multimodal AI pressures that assumption because it introduces a technical substrate in which word and image can be correlated before they are presented as distinct media to human users.

This does not mean the humanities should surrender to engineering vocabulary. Nor does it mean that technical systems get to define cultural theory. The opposite is true. The humanities become more necessary because AI systems are full of unarticulated metaphysics. They contain assumptions about meaning, representation, intelligence, perception, classification, and value. Agre’s claim that AI is “philosophy underneath” remains decisive. Machine learning systems are not only tools; they are operational theories of relation.

But if AI is philosophy underneath, then humanistic concepts must also be tested against machines. Not because machines are wiser, but because they materialize strange edge cases. They create technical situations in which old distinctions fail, mutate, or become newly visible.

Operative ekphrasis is therefore not just a term for AI image generation. It is a method. It asks:

What operation links text to image?
What substrate carries that operation?
Where does meaning enter?
Where does command disappear?
Who or what performs the transformation?
At what level does the distinction between media persist?
At what level does it collapse?

These questions allow Critical AI Studies to move beyond two insufficient responses: hype and dismissal. Hype says the machine understands. Dismissal says the machine merely manipulates symbols. Operative ekphrasis asks for a more exact account. It allows us to say: the machine does not understand as humans do, but its operations are not identical to classical syntax either. It produces a technical form of correlation that behaves aesthetically, semiotically, and politically as a weak semantics.

That weak semantics matters.

It matters because it structures what images can be generated.
It matters because it governs what prompts succeed or fail.
It matters because it encodes cultural associations into feature space.
It matters because it makes some relations feel natural and others improbable.
It matters because it turns archives of image-caption pairs into operational aesthetic worlds.

Artificial semantics is not innocent. It is trained. It is inherited. It is statistical memory with consequences.

## 7. Toward a Substrate-Aware Aesthetics

An aesthetics of AI cannot begin with the image alone. The generated picture is only the visible surface of a deeper operation. To interpret it adequately, one must read across prompt, model, dataset, interface, output, and reception. The image is not simply an image. It is the end point of a chain.

This is why visual poetry is such a useful starting point. It teaches us to see the instability of writing. Letters can become shapes. Words can become fields. Text can be seen before it is read. But AI image generation adds another layer: text can become image without remaining textually legible at all. Dave Orr’s AI-generated pseudo-poem is exemplary because it returns writing as visual residue. The prompt asks for a poem; the image supplies poem-ness without stable language. The output looks like writing while failing as writing.

This failure is not incidental. It reveals the model’s relation to text. The system has learned the visual appearance of poems, fonts, lines, pages, and typographic conventions. It can generate the image of textuality without producing coherent text. It knows writing as look before it knows writing as language.

Or more precisely: it correlates the prompt with the visual Gestalt of writing.

That distinction opens a powerful aesthetic method. Instead of asking whether the AI poem is good or bad, legible or illegible, authentic or fake, we can ask what kind of textuality the model has learned to produce. Does it produce language as semantic sequence, as graphic texture, as genre marker, as layout, as atmosphere? Does it understand “poem” as meter, as lineation, as page shape, as serif font, as cultural aura?

The answer will vary by model, training data, and prompt. But the point remains: AI-generated images should be read as artifacts of learned mediation. Their aesthetics are substrate effects.

A substrate-aware aesthetics therefore refuses to treat AI images as magical apparitions. It also refuses to treat them as mere pictures. They are operational images in a specific sense: not Farocki’s operational images of surveillance and targeting, but images produced through operations that must be interpreted as part of their meaning.

The image is evidence of a model’s learned world.

## 8. Conclusion: The End of Ekphrastic Innocence

Operative ekphrasis names a historical shift in the relation between word and image. In analogue visual poetry, text becomes image through arrangement. In sequential digital media, text produces image through code. In connectionist AI, text and image are correlated through shared artificial semantics.

This shift does not abolish ekphrasis. It makes ekphrasis stranger.

The classical ekphrastic scene involved a speaker, an audience, and an absent image summoned into vividness. The digital sequential scene involved code, execution, and output. The multimodal AI scene involves prompt, model, latent correlation, and generated image. Across these scenes, the relation between text and image becomes progressively less representational and more operational.

The key claim is not that AI has achieved human meaning. That would be the wrong conclusion. Multimodal models do not possess intention, embodiment, historical consciousness, or lived understanding. But it is equally insufficient to say that they operate only through empty syntax. Their capacity to correlate language and image within shared representation spaces forces us to recognize a weak but consequential form of artificial semantics.

This weak semantics is enough to trouble the old categories. It is enough to make ekphrasis no longer a relation between two stable media but a question of technical mediation. It is enough to make visual poetry, software studies, and Critical AI Studies part of the same archive. It is enough to show that the humanities must not only critique AI from the outside, but use AI as an occasion to revise their own conceptual instruments.

The prompt box is not just a user interface. It is a miniature theory of media.

It asks us to believe that words can become images.
It hides the machinery that makes this belief operational.
It returns a picture that seems to answer language.
It collapses description into generation.

Ekphrasis once meant making listeners see.
Now it means making machines dream in pixels.

But the dream is not private, mystical, or free. It is trained from archives, constrained by architectures, shaped by capital, and interpreted by humans who still live among words and images as cultural forms. The collapse of text and image inside multimodal AI therefore does not end interpretation. It makes interpretation more urgent.

The task now is to read the operation.
