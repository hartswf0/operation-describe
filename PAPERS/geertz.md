# Thick Programming: Culture, Control, and the Unfinished Human

## Abstract

This chapter argues that Clifford Geertz’s account of culture as a set of symbolic control mechanisms should be read as an early theory of programmable personhood. Against Enlightenment universalism and classical anthropology’s search for cultural universals, Geertz relocates the human not behind culture, beneath culture, or prior to culture, but inside the symbolic systems that organize conduct, feeling, perception, and social intelligibility. Culture is not an ornament added to a completed organism; it is the extra-genetic instruction layer through which an unfinished animal becomes a specific kind of person. This argument becomes newly urgent in the age of AI systems, prompt protocols, interface scripts, recommender systems, workflow automations, and institutional decision engines. If culture has always programmed human conduct, then contemporary computational systems do not introduce programmability into social life from the outside; they intensify, formalize, and redistribute an older anthropological condition. The method required for reading these systems is neither pure technical debugging nor traditional symbolic interpretation, but thick programming: a way of analyzing how symbolic instructions, embodied capacities, institutional scenes, and machine-mediated protocols compile into human action.

## 1. The Human Has No Backstage

The central mistake in many theories of the human is the belief that culture hides the real person. Somewhere behind the costume, behind the accent, behind the ritual, behind the etiquette, behind the interface, there is imagined to be a pure human being waiting to be recovered. This is the old backstage fantasy: strip away custom and the universal actor appears.

Geertz’s chapter destroys that fantasy. He does not merely argue that human beings are influenced by culture. That would be too weak. He argues that human beings are constituted through culture. There is no naked human subject waiting behind the curtain, no pre-cultural person lounging in street clothes after the drama of custom has ended. The person is always already performing, and performance is not superficial. It is the medium through which humanity becomes actual. Geertz’s target is therefore not only the Enlightenment picture of universal Man, but every later theory that tries to rescue the human by subtracting historical and cultural specificity. The human is not what remains after subtraction. The human is what appears through formation. 

This is why the problem of culture is not a side problem for anthropology. It is the problem of human nature itself. If what a person is cannot be separated from where they are, who they are, what they believe, how they speak, how they eat, how they gesture, how they pray, how they obey, and how they refuse, then culture is not a decorative surface laid over biological substance. Culture is the condition under which biological capacity becomes social form.

The human, in this view, is not a universal essence wearing local clothes. The human is a creature whose general capacities must be narrowed, shaped, trained, and specified by symbolic systems. We are born able to become many things and end up becoming one thing in particular. That narrowing is not a loss of humanity. It is the only way humanity becomes livable.

## 2. The Failure of Fake Universals

Geertz’s attack on cultural universals is not an attack on general thinking. It is an attack on lazy generality. The classical anthropological move was to save the concept of Man by finding practices that seemed to exist everywhere: religion, marriage, property, shelter, morality, trade. These categories promised a way out of relativism. They seemed to say: yes, cultures differ, but beneath those differences we can still identify common human institutions.

Geertz’s reply is brutal: the more universal the category becomes, the emptier it gets. “Religion” may be everywhere, but Aztec sacrifice, Sunni legalism, Hindu ritualism, Buddhist discipline, and Zuni rain practice do not mean the same thing merely because they can all be filed under a common heading. “Marriage” may be widespread, but the category explains little until one asks who can marry whom, under what obligation, with what exchange, under what kinship rule, with what gendered expectation, and inside what moral world. The fake universal does not illuminate difference. It launders difference into a label.

This matters because many modern theories of technology repeat the same mistake. They speak of “users,” “attention,” “engagement,” “content,” “interaction,” “automation,” or “intelligence” as though these categories travel cleanly across contexts. But a “user” in a welfare eligibility system, a teenager on TikTok, a radiologist reading an AI-assisted scan, a warehouse worker managed by algorithmic scheduling, and a graduate student prompting a language model are not the same kind of social actor. They are shaped by different institutions, risks, vocabularies, stakes, and scripts. To call them all “users” may be administratively convenient, but it is analytically thin.

The Geertzian lesson is clear: beware the category that explains everything by describing almost nothing. The question is not whether a practice belongs to a general class. The question is what specific symbolic machinery makes that practice meaningful, binding, repeatable, and consequential.

## 3. Against the Layer Cake Model of the Human

The second enemy in Geertz’s chapter is the stratigraphic model: the idea that the human can be understood as a stack of layers. Biology at the bottom. Psychology above it. Social organization above that. Culture on top. In this model, each discipline gets its sovereign territory. The biologist explains the body, the psychologist explains the mind, the sociologist explains institutions, the anthropologist explains meanings. Everyone gets a floor in the building.

Geertz sees the appeal. The model lets culture matter without making it everything. It allows scholars to say that culture is important while still imagining that deeper, harder, more real layers lie beneath it. But the cost is severe. Once the layers are separated, they cannot be meaningfully reassembled. One can place biological facts beside cultural facts, psychological facts beside social facts, but juxtaposition is not integration. The result is not explanation but parallel description.

Geertz’s alternative is synthetic. Biology, psychology, society, and culture must be treated as variables within unitary systems of analysis. Human action does not emerge from one layer at a time. It emerges from the interaction among embodied capacities, symbolic forms, social scenes, technical supports, institutional pressures, and learned expectations. The smile is not simply biological or cultural. The capacity may be bodily; the timing, irony, restraint, meaning, danger, and appropriateness are cultural. The human act is compiled from many sources at once.

This is where Geertz becomes unexpectedly useful for thinking about computation. A computer program is not merely a text file, a machine instruction, a user intention, an interface, a hardware substrate, or an institutional workflow. It is the operational relation among these elements. The same is true of culture. Culture is not reducible to belief, symbol, institution, habit, or rule. It is the pattern by which these elements govern the transformation of capacity into conduct.

## 4. Culture as Program

Geertz’s most explosive move is his description of culture as a set of control mechanisms: plans, recipes, rules, instructions, and programs for the governing of behavior. This is not a casual metaphor. It shifts the theory of culture from representation to operation. Culture does not merely express what people believe. It helps produce what people can do, feel, notice, desire, fear, and recognize as appropriate.

The word “program” is dangerous because it can sound reductive. It can suggest that humans are machines executing scripts. But Geertz’s argument is subtler. A cultural program is not a deterministic code that eliminates interpretation. It is a symbolic structure that makes action possible by giving form to otherwise underdetermined capacities. Human beings are not over-programmed animals. They are under-specified animals who require external symbolic ordering to act coherently at all.

This is the force of the “unfinished animal” claim. Humans do not come equipped with enough instinctive specificity to live human lives. We need recipes, calendars, myths, kinship terms, etiquette, diagrams, laws, genres, rituals, interfaces, names, maps, prayers, jokes, credentials, uniforms, dashboards, syllabi, and scripts. These are not afterthoughts. They are the means by which shapeless possibility becomes situated action.

A person does not simply “speak.” They speak English, Amharic, Javanese, academic prose, workplace politeness, bureaucratic apology, courtroom testimony, playground insult, wedding blessing, terminal command, prompt instruction. A person does not simply “eat.” They eat breakfast, fast, feast, snack, diet, host, refuse, bless, binge, ration, serve, photograph, or perform taste within a social order. A person does not simply “use technology.” They comply, hack, prompt, scroll, swipe, monitor, automate, ignore, troubleshoot, surveil, optimize, or resist within a field of symbolic and institutional expectation.

Culture is therefore not a layer of meaning draped over action. Culture is action’s grammar.

## 5. From Thick Description to Thick Programming

If culture is programmatic, then interpretation must become operational. This does not mean reducing symbols to code. It means asking how symbolic forms govern the production of conduct.

Traditional thick description asks: what does this gesture mean in this world? Thick programming asks a related but sharper question: what does this symbolic arrangement make possible, probable, forbidden, legible, desirable, punishable, or repeatable?

A wink is not merely an eyelid movement plus meaning. It is a socially executable distinction. It depends on a scene, a relation, a shared code, a risk, an audience, and a theory of recognition. In the same way, a prompt entered into an AI system is not merely a string of words. It is an operational gesture inside a trained model, an interface, a policy regime, a user fantasy, a platform economy, and a cultural scene of automation. The prompt does not simply mean. It does.

Thick programming would read such gestures as symbolic-control events. It would ask:

What capacities does this system assume?
What conduct does it invite?
What forms of personhood does it reward?
What uncertainty does it hide?
What distinctions does it force?
What does it make easy that was once hard?
What does it make hard that was once ordinary?
What cultural program is being compiled into behavior?

This method is especially important for AI because AI systems often appear to dissolve the boundary between meaning and mechanism. A prompt looks like ordinary language, but it functions as an instruction. A model output looks like expression, but it is generated through statistical procedure. A user feels like an author, but authorship is distributed across interface defaults, training data, model architecture, moderation policy, platform incentives, and inherited genres. The symbolic act has become operational without ceasing to be symbolic.

That is precisely why Geertz matters here. He gives us a way to refuse the false choice between interpretation and mechanism. Culture has always been both meaningful and operational. AI simply makes that doubleness harder to ignore.

## 6. The Interface Is a Cultural Scene

Modern interfaces are cultural programs pretending to be neutral tools. A button, menu, feed, dashboard, form, autocomplete suggestion, notification badge, ranking system, or prompt box does not merely help a user accomplish a pre-existing intention. It formats intention. It teaches the user what can be asked, what can be done, what counts as progress, what counts as error, and what kind of person the system expects on the other side.

This is where a Geertzian theory of AI must depart from both human-centered design clichés and hard technological determinism. The interface does not determine the human from above, but neither does the human simply use the interface from outside. The person and the system meet inside a programmed cultural scene. The scene has roles, permissions, defaults, taboos, rituals, and forms of face-saving. It has etiquette.

Prompting an AI system, for example, is not just command entry. It is a new etiquette of delegated cognition. Users learn how to flatter the model, constrain it, scold it, role-play with it, ask it to “think,” forbid it from rambling, demand structure, request tone, invoke expertise, simulate audiences, and repair failure. These practices are not random. They are emergent cultural forms. They are prompt etiquette, prompt ritual, prompt bureaucracy, prompt magic, prompt management.

The AI interface thus becomes a site where culture’s programmatic nature appears in miniature. The user supplies symbolic instructions to a machine trained on symbolic residues of collective life. The machine returns a plausible continuation. The user edits, accepts, rejects, reprompts, copies, publishes, or internalizes. A loop forms. In that loop, human intention is not erased, but it is reorganized. The user becomes a particular kind of human actor: part writer, part manager, part editor, part supplicant, part operator, part witness.

The question is not whether this is authentic or artificial. That opposition is too thin. The better question is: what kind of person does this symbolic-control system train into existence?

## 7. Culture, AI, and Delegated Agency

Geertz’s unfinished animal becomes newly visible under conditions of delegated agency. Human beings have always relied on external symbolic systems to stabilize conduct. But AI systems intensify delegation by allowing symbolic instructions to generate further symbolic action at scale. A prompt can produce a memo, an image, a lesson plan, a legal summary, a poem, a diagnosis draft, a marketing strategy, a software prototype, or a fake archive. The cultural program no longer merely guides human action; it can now initiate semi-autonomous chains of representation.

This does not make AI outside culture. It makes AI a dense cultural artifact: a machine for recombining inherited symbolic forms under procedural conditions. The model is not a mind in the old humanist sense, but it is also not a mere tool in the hammer-and-nail sense. It is a delegated semiotic apparatus. It operates within the gap Geertz identifies between bodily capacity and social action. It offers to fill that gap with generated language, generated images, generated plans, generated classifications, generated scripts.

That offer is powerful because humans are already beings who need symbolic completion. AI systems arrive not as alien intrusions into a self-sufficient human nature, but as new suppliers of orientation, pattern, and instruction. They promise to tell us what to write, what to say, what to notice, what to optimize, what to remember, what to forget, what to believe is likely, what to treat as normal.

The political danger follows directly. Whoever governs the symbolic programs that complete human action governs more than information. They govern possibility. They shape the narrowing by which a thousand possible lives become one lived trajectory.

This is why AI ethics cannot remain trapped in the language of bias, privacy, safety, and transparency alone, important as those are. The deeper anthropological question is formative: what kinds of humans are being produced through these systems? What habits of attention, dependence, command, suspicion, imagination, and obedience are being installed? What becomes easier to feel? What becomes harder to say? What forms of judgment decay when the interface offers completion before struggle has done its work?

## 8. Thick Programming as Method

Thick programming names a method for analyzing symbolic systems that govern behavior without reducing them to either pure code or pure meaning. It begins with Geertz’s insight that culture is made of public symbols that orient action. It adds the operational question: how do these symbols execute?

A thick programming analysis of an AI writing interface would not stop at the model output. It would examine the prompt box, placeholder text, system instructions, hidden policies, available buttons, latency, examples, subscription tiers, refusal language, editing affordances, export paths, and user lore. It would study not only what users produce, but how they learn to ask. It would treat prompting as a cultural practice with genres, taboos, hacks, prestige markers, and folk theories.

A thick programming analysis of a bureaucratic algorithm would not ask only whether the system is accurate. It would ask how categories become compulsory, how ambiguity is punished, how applicants learn to narrate themselves for machine readability, how caseworkers negotiate the gap between institutional script and lived circumstance, and how “error” is distributed across human and technical actors.

A thick programming analysis of a recommendation feed would not ask only what content is ranked. It would ask what rhythms of selfhood the feed trains: refresh, compare, react, perform, envy, confess, optimize, return. It would read the feed as ritual machinery.

This method has three commitments.

First, no symbolic system is merely symbolic. Symbols organize conduct.

Second, no technical system is merely technical. Technical systems inherit and enforce cultural distinctions.

Third, no human action is merely individual. Action is compiled through bodies, histories, interfaces, institutions, and available signs.

Thick programming is therefore not a metaphorical flourish. It is a research program for the age of programmable culture.

## 9. The Human as Compilation

The strongest version of the argument is this: the human is a compilation event.

Not compilation in the narrow software sense alone, but compilation as the transformation of distributed materials into executable form. Biological capacities, learned symbols, institutional roles, technical infrastructures, emotional repertoires, habits, prohibitions, and scenes of recognition come together to produce situated conduct. A person acts, but the action carries more than personal intention. It carries a world.

Geertz’s anthropology teaches that we do not find humanity by scraping away the world. We find humanity by studying how worlds enter conduct. A Javanese person does not become human by approximating an abstract universal. They become human through particular disciplines of speech, gesture, feeling, etiquette, and perception. The same is true, differently, for the academic, the programmer, the bureaucrat, the patient, the gamer, the influencer, the prompt engineer, the citizen, the dissident.

To be human is not to be free of programming. It is to be programmable in a special way: open-ended enough to require symbolic completion, but embodied enough that no symbolic system can fully exhaust us. Culture narrows us, but it also makes action possible. It limits, but it forms. It governs, but it gives shape. The political question is not whether we can escape cultural programming. We cannot. The question is which programs we inherit, which we obey, which we revise, which we mistake for nature, and which we allow machines to administer on our behalf.

## Conclusion: Debugging the Human Sciences

Geertz begins by rejecting simple pictures of man. He ends by forcing anthropology into detail, into the concrete, into the strange and ordinary practices where humanity actually takes form. The lesson for AI and media theory is not to abandon abstraction, but to earn it through particulars. We should distrust every theory of “the user,” “the human,” “the model,” or “intelligence” that moves too quickly past situated practice.

Thick programming offers one way forward. It treats culture as meaningful operation and technical systems as cultural scenes. It reads prompts, interfaces, rituals, classifications, and institutional scripts as programs that help convert human possibility into particular conduct. It asks not only what symbols mean, but what they make happen.

The human has no backstage. The human appears in the run-time: in the moment when body, symbol, institution, machine, memory, and scene compile into action. Geertz gave us the grammar for seeing this before computation became the dominant metaphor of social life. Now the metaphor has become infrastructure. The task is to read it thickly.
