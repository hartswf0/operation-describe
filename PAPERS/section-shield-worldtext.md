# The Shield as Worldtext

**From: "From Imagetext to Worldtext: Generative AI as Operative Ekphrasis"**
**Hartsoe & Bolter — Section Draft**

---

As classical scholars have remarked, the shield occupies a position both within and beyond the narrative of the poem. The *Iliad* is a story from the Trojan War, itself an exceptional event, and that story unfolds in the course of weeks in the tenth and final year of that war. The shield does not directly refer to those events at all; instead, it depicts the whole Homeric world: from the sky and constellations in the center to the Ocean that surrounds the world on the shield's rim. In between are scenes of the range of human activity: peace and war, agriculture, a legal dispute, wedding and festive dancing (Taplin 1980). The shield offers a picture of the variety and equilibrium of this world of gods and humans — a picture that contrasts vividly with the disorder and conflict that is the story of Achilles's withdrawal and return to battle. The shield is a detail in that story of conflict, but in describing the scenes on the shield in such detail (over 130 lines), Homer reverses macro- and microcosm: the Trojan War is a moment in the Greek epic universe, but Homer renders that universe on this shield inside that moment. The description of the shield is ekphrasis. It is imagetext. But we choose it because it also suggests the ekphrastic ambition to encompass not just an image but a world. We use the shield to gesture toward the various senses of worldtext that we propose to explore.

Homer's text does not depict a picture. It depicts a world. The shield has cosmology (earth, sky, sea, sun, moon, constellations). It has governance (a legal dispute adjudicated in a public assembly). It has ecology (plowing, harvest, vineyard, cattle herding). It has culture (a wedding procession, festive dancing). And it has a boundary condition — the great river of Ocean at the rim, the edge beyond which the world-model has no data. These are not merely scenes arranged on a surface. They are the constitutive elements of a world: the physics that govern it, the social order that organizes it, the productive cycles that sustain it, the rituals that celebrate it, and the limit that defines where it ends. The shield is not an image of a world. It is a *specification* of one.

This distinction — between depicting a scene and specifying a world — maps onto a distinction that is now emerging within generative AI research: the distinction between an **image model** and a **world model**. An image model (DALL·E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion) takes a text prompt and generates a static two-dimensional image. The prompt specifies a scene; the model renders it. The output is an imagetext in Mitchell's sense: word and image merged into a single artifact. But the output is flat. It has no physics. It has no temporal extension. Nothing in it moves, decays, or responds to further input. The imagetext is an instant — a frozen intersection of language and vision.

A world model is a fundamentally different kind of system. In the machine learning literature, the term originates with Ha and Schmidhuber (2018), who proposed that intelligent agents learn compressed spatiotemporal representations of their environments — internal simulations that allow the agent to predict, plan, and act without direct sensory access to the world at every moment. A world model is not a picture of the world. It is a *predictive engine* for the world — a learned model of how states succeed one another in time, how actions produce consequences, and how the environment responds to intervention. The concept has since been extended from reinforcement learning to generative systems: LeCun (2022) proposed world models as the foundation for a new architecture of machine intelligence, and recent work at Google DeepMind (Genie and Genie 2; Bruce et al. 2024) and at World Labs demonstrates that generative systems can now produce not just images but navigable, interactive, spatiotemporal environments from text or image prompts.

The shift from image model to world model recapitulates the shift from imagetext to worldtext. An image model generates an imagetext: a prompt produces a scene. A world model generates a worldtext: a prompt produces an *environment* — one that persists in time, responds to interaction, and is governed by learned or specified constraints. The worldtext is not a bigger imagetext. It is a qualitatively different object. The imagetext merges word and image into a single artifact. The worldtext merges word, image, space, time, interaction, and governance into a single *environment*. The prompt is no longer a description of a scene but a **constitution** of a world — it defines not what the image looks like but what the world *does*, what is possible within it, what constraints apply, what defaults govern.

Prior to the development of these generative platforms, decades of research and design in computer graphics, particularly for video games, clearly indicated a cultural desire to move beyond two dimensions. Screen-based games offered players the opportunity to explore 3D game worlds, and then the arrival of inexpensive virtual reality headsets in the 2010s seemed to fulfill the promise that the player would be able to enter into such worlds. But those worlds were created by teams of graphics experts and programmers and offered to the player to explore. Now Google DeepMind with its Genie series and World Labs are offering generative platforms through which a user can bring forth their own 3D world from a text or other prompt. The ambition is to make the worlds produced fully interactive and malleable. The player-user could potentially type or speak a new prompt and change their world in real time. The imagetext is reimagined as a worldtext. Ekphrastic hope here extends beyond the word as natural sign to something like a neoplatonic faith in the power of the word — here the prompt — to create a world.

The world model introduces two features that the image model lacks and that are essential to the concept of worldtext: **temporal extension** and **cumulative state**. An image model produces a single frame. A world model produces a sequence — a spatiotemporal unfolding that includes duration, change, causation, and consequence. Homer understood this: the scenes on the shield are not static tableaux but narratives in progress. The two armies are besieging a city. The plowmen are turning at the end of a furrow. The dancers are moving in a circle. The shield's world is not frozen; it is *in motion*. A world model captures this temporal dimension computationally. Genie 2, for example, generates interactive environments in which objects fall, characters move, and the world responds to the player's actions — not because physical laws have been explicitly coded but because the model has learned spatiotemporal regularities from vast amounts of video data. The world's physics are statistical, not symbolic: a ball falls because balls fall in videos, not because gravity has been formulated as an equation. This is the probabilistic regime of worldtext generation.

But the world model is also cumulative. Unlike the image model, which produces its output and forgets, a world model that supports interactive exploration must maintain state across time. It must remember what has happened. It must accumulate a history. And this accumulation produces its own constraints — its own inertia. A world model that has been running for some time has already committed to certain spatial configurations, certain character positions, certain narrative facts. Each new prompt or action must be consistent with this accumulated state, or the world breaks. The world model develops what we might call its own **viscosity**: a resistance to arbitrary change that grows as the world's history deepens. This is a property that imagetexts do not have. An imagetext is ephemeral — it exists for one prompt-output cycle and then vanishes. A worldtext persists, and in persisting, it acquires the burden and the richness of its own past.

Thick prompting is a key aspect of these world model generators. Even if the user or player provides only a short verbal prompt (e.g., "Make a Homeric world like the one depicted on the shield of Achilles"), the generator depends on an elaborate set of so-called system prompts that provide context to guide and constrain the model, not to mention a great deal of sophisticated procedural programming to provide the physics and interactivity. These system prompts are the constitution of the worldtext — the governing instructions that specify what the world permits, what it forbids, what it defaults to, and how it responds to user input. They are thick prompts in the Geertzian sense: densely layered specifications that situate the world within structures of meaning, convention, and constraint. The user's prompt ("make a Homeric world") is the thin surface; the system prompt stack is the thick infrastructure beneath it.

The distinction between the probabilistic and the rule-governed is critical here, and it maps onto a broader development in AI research: the neurosymbolic approach, which combines learned statistical models with explicit symbolic rules and constraints (Garcez et al. 2019). The world models now emerging are, in practice, hybrids. They generate surfaces probabilistically — textures, lighting, the visual appearance of objects — while relying on deterministic engines for physics, collision, and spatial consistency. They dream the world's appearance and legislate the world's behavior. Homer's shield already exhibits this hybrid structure: the visual richness of the scenes (golden vines, silver fish, dancers in fine linen) is poetic and evocative — probabilistic, we might say, in the sense that it activates the listener's imagination to fill in the sensory details. But the structure of the world — cosmology at center, Ocean at rim, human society organized between — is architectural and rule-governed. The shield is both dreamed and legislated. The emerging world models are both dreamed and legislated. The worldtext is their common form.

---

## Conclusion

We do not claim that these developments have matured. The world models now emerging from DeepMind, World Labs, and other research groups are early-stage systems. Their outputs are often inconsistent, their physics approximate, their interactivity limited. The genie is out of the bottle, but it is still learning to grant wishes. What we do claim is that the trajectory from image model to world model — from imagetext to worldtext — represents a conceptual shift of the first order for the theory of ekphrasis and for the broader question of how language relates to visual and spatial experience. Operative ekphrasis, as Bajohr defined it, named the moment when the word *became* the picture. Worldtext names the next moment: when the word becomes the world.

The concepts introduced in this paper — operative ekphrasis, thick prompting, imagetext, worldtext — are likely to grow and diverge as the technology develops and as scholars from different disciplines bring their own frameworks to bear. We do not expect these concepts to remain stable. We expect them to be productive — to open doors for work that we cannot yet foresee. The shield of Achilles has been read and reread for twenty-seven centuries, and each reading has found in it something that the previous reading missed. We offer our reading — the shield as worldtext, as the first specification of a world compressed into language — not as a final interpretation but as an operative one: a reading that does something, that generates new questions, and that invites others to take the next step.

Homer's text depicts a world. Generative AI is learning to build one. The theory of ekphrasis, restored to its ancient operative ambition, is the bridge between them.
