# Use Against Meaning: Ryle, Findlay, and the Hidden Life of Speech

## Abstract

This essay examines Gilbert Ryle and J. N. Findlay’s 1961 symposium “Use, Usage and Meaning” as a revealing confrontation inside ordinary-language philosophy. Ryle defends a sharp distinction between language as a learned stock of reusable linguistic instruments and speech as the situated activity of saying something with them. Findlay accepts much of Ryle’s distinction but turns against the broader Wittgensteinian slogan that philosophers should not ask for meaning but for use. The dispute exposes a deeper philosophical fault line: whether meaning can be explained by public linguistic employment, or whether use itself presupposes intentionality, connotation, denotation, and the thought behind words. I argue that the symposium does not simply oppose use to meaning. Rather, it stages a more subtle problem: use is the visible surface of meaning, but not its sufficient explanation. A philosophy of language must therefore treat speech as performed intentionality: an act in which public rules, logical grammar, context, and thought converge without being reducible to one another. 

---

## I. The Problem with the Slogan

The slogan “Don’t ask for the meaning; ask for the use” has often functioned as a disciplinary command. It tells philosophers to stop searching for hidden essences behind words and to examine the actual circumstances in which expressions are employed. Against metaphysical abstraction, it offers an apparently sober method: look at what people do with language. Meaning, on this view, is not a vaporous mental object, not a private image, not a metaphysical relation between word and world, but a pattern of use embedded in life.

Yet the slogan’s very strength is also its danger. It gains its authority by sounding practical, anti-mystical, and ordinary. “Use” seems clearer than “meaning.” It seems to replace philosophical fog with observable practice. But the Ryle-Findlay exchange reveals that this replacement is not innocent. To ask for use is not necessarily to escape theory. It may simply install a different theory, one whose assumptions are harder to see because they wear the costume of common sense.

Ryle and Findlay both write under the pressure of Wittgenstein’s later philosophy, but they respond to that pressure differently. Ryle refines the philosophical utility of use by insisting on careful distinctions: language is not speech, words are not sentences, grammatical faults are not logical faults, and knowing a language is not identical with saying something. Findlay, by contrast, worries that use has been inflated into an explanatory idol. It may help describe how words function, but it cannot replace meaning, because to explain use in any philosophically adequate way already requires some account of what expressions mean, refer to, evoke, imply, or bring to mind.

The result is not a simple quarrel between ordinary-language philosophy and its critics. It is a quarrel within the inheritance of ordinary language itself. Ryle wants to save philosophical analysis from category confusion. Findlay wants to save meaning from being dissolved into behavioral description. Together, they force a more precise question: when we examine language in use, what exactly are we examining?

## II. Ryle’s Capital and Trade

Ryle begins with a distinction borrowed and reshaped from Alan Gardiner: the distinction between language and speech. A language, for Ryle, is a stock or fund of words, constructions, idioms, intonations, and other teachable resources. Speech is the activity of using that stock to say something. His central analogy is economic: language stands to speech as capital stands to trade. Capital is not itself a transaction, but it makes transactions possible. Likewise, language is not itself the act of asserting, questioning, warning, promising, joking, or commanding. It is the durable wherewithal by which those acts may be performed.

This distinction lets Ryle correct a tempting but dangerous confusion. We often speak as though sentences were simply larger linguistic units built out of words, in the way that walls are built out of bricks. But Ryle argues that this analogy misleads. Words belong to the stock of language. Sentences, at least when considered as actual things said by someone on some occasion, belong to speech. A word can be learned, stored, misremembered, or reused. A sentence, as an act of saying, is produced. It is authored, not merely withdrawn from a linguistic bank.

This becomes clearest in Ryle’s examples of one-word sentences. Caesar’s “Vici” can be a boast; the same Latin form, uttered differently, can be a question. The word is the same piece of linguistic capital, but the speech act differs. The word is not true or false; the boast may be. The word is not arrogant; the act of saying it may be. The word belongs to Latin; the boast belongs to Caesar’s occasion of speech.

Ryle’s deeper target is the mistake of treating sentences as though they had uses in the same way words have uses. Words are instruments. Speech acts are performances with instruments. To ask for the “use” of a word is often sensible: what role does this expression play in assertions, questions, descriptions, calculations, jokes, or commands? But to ask for the “use” of a sentence can become confused if the sentence is itself already a saying, an act, an event. One does not use an execution to hang someone; the execution is the hanging. Likewise, one does not use a speech act to say something; the speech act is the saying.

This distinction lets Ryle preserve the philosophical importance of use while narrowing its proper object. The slogan “ask for the use” should not be a license to treat all linguistic phenomena alike. It should direct attention away from dormant words considered as dictionary items and toward live employment in actual discourse. But that employment must itself be categorized carefully. Otherwise, use becomes a blunt instrument.

## III. Speech-Faults and Language-Faults

Ryle’s most important refinement is his distinction between language-faults and speech-faults. A language-fault occurs when someone misuses the linguistic stock: a solecism, grammatical error, mispronunciation, malapropism, or unidiomatic construction. These are failures in mastery of the language. A French teacher may correct them.

But many things can go wrong in speech that are not failures of language. A statement may be false, tactless, irrelevant, circular, fallacious, malicious, boring, self-contradictory, or absurd while being perfectly grammatical. Cicero’s bad arguments are not bad Latin. Lewis Carroll’s impossible image of the Cheshire Cat’s grin is not bad English. A syllogistic fallacy does not become a failure of French merely because it is expressed in French.

This is where Ryle’s ordinary-language method becomes a theory of philosophical discipline. Philosophers and logicians are interested not primarily in language-faults but in speech-faults: absurdities, contradictions, category mistakes, invalid inferences, and inappropriate verification procedures. These are not errors in the linguistic instrument but in the act performed with it. The paints may be fine; the painting may still be bad.

The distinction matters because philosophers often confuse logical grammar with ordinary grammar. Ordinary grammar governs acceptable forms of linguistic construction. Logical grammar governs the possible sense of what is said. A sentence may obey the first while violating the second. Ryle therefore protects philosophy from being reduced to lexicography or grammar teaching. Philosophers do indeed “talk about talk,” but they are not merely language instructors. They examine the kinds of acts performed in speech and the kinds of failure to which those acts are vulnerable.

Ryle’s version of “use” is thus not shallow behaviorism. It is a method for inspecting live employment. To understand a philosophical expression, one must examine not only how it may be used, but how it may be misused without committing a grammatical error. Use includes the “how not to.” A philosophical investigation of use is therefore also an investigation of the limits, failures, and category-conditions of meaningful speech.

## IV. Findlay’s Reversal: Use Presupposes Meaning

Findlay agrees with Ryle’s main distinction, but he refuses the broader philosophical elevation of use. His objection is devastating because it turns the slogan against itself. To explain the use of an expression, he argues, one must often say what the expression refers to, what it connotes, what it brings to mind, what angle it gives upon its object, what formal or emotional role it plays, and how it contributes to the total meaning of an utterance. But these are already semantic notions. Use does not replace meaning; it depends on meaning.

This is the center of Findlay’s critique. The ordinary notion of use is broader than meaning, but it does not eliminate meaning. A word can be used to refer, describe, command, greet, evoke emotion, perform a social act, or contribute to a larger construction. But to say what such uses are, we must already understand reference, connotation, intention, implication, and context. We cannot explain the use of “man,” “dragon,” “red,” “remember,” or “dream” without saying something about what these words present, evoke, or help us discriminate.

Findlay therefore accuses radical use-theory of attempting to explain meaning while forbidding itself the vocabulary needed to do so. It wants to describe linguistic function only in terms of public operations and circumstances. It prefers apples in drawers, bricks in walls, color charts, signals, and observable practices. But when it encounters memory, dreams, private impressions, first-person reports, or reflexive meanings, its public apparatus strains. Human language does not operate only in the public square. It also opens into private chambers.

This is not a crude defense of private language. Findlay is not saying that meaning is sealed inside the mind and inaccessible to others. His point is subtler: public use and private understanding are entangled. First-person reports are publicly intelligible precisely because speakers understand the relation between their own first-person uses and the reported first-person uses of others. Ordinary language already contains this movement between privacy and publicity. It does not need philosophy to deny one side in order to save the other.

Findlay’s critique therefore recovers intentionality. Meaning involves directedness: thought about something, speech as presenting something under some aspect, expression as carrying an object, tone, implication, or conceptual orientation. Use may reveal this directedness, but it cannot manufacture it from public behavior alone.

## V. Rule-Following and the Obscurity of Practice

The deepest pressure point is rule-following. For a use-theory of meaning, rules are essential. To know the meaning of an expression is to know how to use it according to rules, or at least to participate in a practice where uses are accepted, corrected, extended, and stabilized. But Findlay argues that this appeal to rules becomes obscure if one refuses any prior grasp of meaning.

A rule cannot simply be a formula, because formulas require interpretation. Nor can it simply be past behavior, because any finite pattern of behavior is compatible with infinitely many rules. Nor can it be an inner mental item, if the doctrine has already forbidden appeal to private understanding. What, then, is rule-following? It risks becoming something ineffable: something shown in practice but impossible to explain.

Here Findlay detects an irony. The later Wittgensteinian attack on hidden mental meanings may reproduce the very mystery it tries to eliminate. In the *Tractatus*, meaning depended on a structural resemblance that could only be shown, not said. In radical use-theory, rule-following may likewise become something enacted but not intelligibly described. The mystery has moved from logical form into practice.

This matters because open rules are central to language. A speaker can understand “and so on indefinitely.” A child can learn to continue a numerical series. A person can grasp a rule whose applications exceed any finite performance. But no actual performance can exhaustively display that openness. If meaning is reduced to public use, the open-endedness of understanding becomes hard to explain. If, however, we allow that speakers can grasp rules, entertain meanings, and understand directions of continuation, then use becomes intelligible again—not as a replacement for meaning, but as its public unfolding.

## VI. Speech as Performed Intentionality

The productive outcome of the Ryle-Findlay exchange is not a victory of meaning over use, nor of use over meaning. The better synthesis is speech as performed intentionality.

From Ryle, we learn that language is not identical with speech. Words, constructions, and grammatical resources are not the same as the acts performed with them. We also learn that philosophical analysis must distinguish language-faults from speech-faults, grammar from logical grammar, and reusable instruments from situated actions. These distinctions remain indispensable.

From Findlay, we learn that use cannot become an imperial explanation. To describe how an expression is used often requires an account of what it means, refers to, suggests, evokes, implies, or intentionally presents. Public criteria matter, but they do not exhaust meaning. Language is not merely a social machine for coordinating visible behavior. It is also a medium through which thought takes form, remembers, imagines, dreams, reports, and reflects upon itself.

Performed intentionality names the point where these insights meet. Speech is performed because it happens in public forms, with learned instruments, under norms, in contexts, and through recognizable acts. But it is intentional because saying something is not reducible to producing sounds or marks according to rules. To say something is to direct language toward an object, possibility, memory, claim, question, demand, or imagined world. Meaning is not hidden behind use like a ghost behind machinery. But neither is it identical with the machinery. It is enacted through use while exceeding any purely external inventory of use.

This position also clarifies logical grammar. Logical grammar is not merely a set of linguistic restrictions. It is the structure of possible sense in acts of saying. It governs what can be significantly asserted, inferred, verified, doubted, or imagined. When speech fails logically, it does not necessarily fail as language. It fails as directed action. It tries to do something that its own conceptual form cannot sustain.

---

## VII. Against Slogan Philosophy

The lasting lesson of “Use, Usage and Meaning” is methodological. Philosophical slogans are useful only when they remain answerable to distinctions. “Ask for the use” is powerful when it redirects attention from abstract word-essences to living speech. It is dangerous when it becomes a prohibition against asking what use itself presupposes. Ryle’s achievement is to make the slogan more exact. Findlay’s achievement is to prevent it from becoming dogma.

A mature philosophy of language must therefore refuse both extremes. Against metaphysical reification, it should not treat meanings as occult objects floating behind words. Against reductive use-theory, it should not pretend that public practice alone explains reference, thought, memory, rule-understanding, or intentional direction. Meaning is neither a ghost nor a mere habit. It is the intelligible orientation of speech within a form of life.

This has consequences beyond mid-century analytic philosophy. In any theory of language, media, computation, or artificial intelligence, one encounters the same temptation: to replace meaning with operation. If a system produces outputs according to rules, patterns, probabilities, or public criteria, one may be tempted to say that meaning is nothing over and above use. But the Ryle-Findlay debate warns against this shortcut. Operations matter. Usage matters. Context matters. But the question of what is being said, meant, referred to, implied, or understood does not disappear simply because we can describe the machinery of production.

Use is the evidence of meaning, not always its ground. Speech is the event where language becomes action, but the act of saying is intelligible only because it carries intentional form. The philosopher’s task is not to choose between use and meaning, but to describe their hinge: the point where a learned public instrument becomes a situated act of thought.

That hinge is where language lives.
