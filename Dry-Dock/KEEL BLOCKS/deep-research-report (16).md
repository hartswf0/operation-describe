## Theoretical reconstruction

The decisive shift is not from image to text but from urn to shield. The urn, in the twentieth-century critical imaginary, became a name for the autonomous artwork: a resolved thing whose meaning inheres in its finished structure and must be read as an independent verbal object. The shield in Homer works otherwise. It is not given whole. It arrives through the action of a maker, as scene follows scene under the hammer of Hephaestus. Lessing understood this with unusual precision: Homer does not primarily show us the shield; he shows us the smith making it. The ekphrastic center of gravity thus falls not on static display but on temporal fabrication. Ancient ekphrasis already knew that description could stage process. That older insight makes prompting legible in a new way. A prompt should not be reduced to a command issued to a machine, because command language imagines a clean transmission from intention to execution. What text-to-image practice actually reveals is closer to performance: contingent, revisable, visibly social, and dependent on inherited codes. If ekphrasis once named vivid speech that brought something before the eyes of listeners, prompting names a renewed form of such staging under computational conditions. The prompter does not merely request an image. The prompter sets the terms under which a latent visual archive will answer. The image is less a completed object than the current state of a negotiation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn13view5Ņłéturn24search1Ņłéturn23view0Ņłéturn29view3Ņłéturn34view0Ņłü

Geertz helps specify what separates strong prompting from merely verbose prompting. It adds the kinds of determinations that alter what the model can plausibly return: relation instead of isolated object, medium instead of generic look, exclusion instead of passive acceptance, social frame instead of free-floating icon, and correction instead of one-shot issuance.
