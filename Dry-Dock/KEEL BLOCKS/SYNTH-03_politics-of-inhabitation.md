## Thesis

The politics of worldtext are not about what the image shows. They are about what the world allows — what it treats as ordinary, frictionless, and thinkable. When description becomes executable, critique must follow execution all the way down into governance.

---

## The Argument

Mitchell (1994) diagnosed the fantasy that still shadows generative media: ekphrastic hope, the wish that language might overcome semiotic difference and cross into visual presence. Krieger (1992) radicalized the diagnosis by centering the "natural sign" as an impossible horizon — literary theory shaped by attempts, and deceptive failures, to make language do the work of immediate visual signification. Generative AI appears to close this gap. The prompt yields an image. But the closure is simulated through vast computational mediation: learned associations, latent spaces, filtered corpora, prompt interpretation layers, and platform interfaces. The natural sign has not arrived. It has been industrialized.

The promise of presence and the promise of habitation are, as Davis (2015) argues, theological in disguise. *TechGnosis* traces how the fantasy of divine creation — speaking a world into being, naming things to make them real — migrates through Gnostic thought, Renaissance magic, cybernetic theory, and into the contemporary interface. The act of typing text and watching a world materialize feeds this fantasy. Generative platforms have made the theological gesture frictionless without making it innocent.

Bajohr (2024) names the structural consequence. Users of proprietary large models are confined to "factory settings." Prompting works only inside a given model and interface. There is "no outside-model." The generated output is an operational synthesis of inherited corpora, proprietary interfaces, hyperparameters, and defaults before it is ever a personal expression. What appears as immediacy is accumulated mediation.

Meyer's (2025) "recursive algorithmization of taste" specifies the mechanism: machines predict what narrowly modeled users will prefer, and those predictions are then used to optimize future machine outputs. The result is not a neutral average but a feedback loop in which archives, aesthetic filtering, and user-response metrics continuously narrow what appears plausible, attractive, or normal. Steyerl's (2023) "mean images" name the product: statistical renderings that converge toward social filters rather than indexical truth.

The world does not begin at the prompt box. It begins in debris fields of memory — training data, platform filters, inferential habits, aesthetic residues, economic incentives, and the uneven labor of the annotators who labeled the corpus. Simonen et al. (2025) demonstrate that default images recur across unrelated prompts. A 2024 study on data attribution for text-to-image models defines influence in terms of the specific training images whose removal would cause a model to fail to reproduce a given output. The generated environment is never empty. It arrives already sedimented.

Open-set bias detection shows that many biases cannot be reduced to a fixed checklist — new domain-specific regularities emerge from prompts that appear neutral on their surface. The sediment is social and political before it is aesthetic.

World models intensify these stakes because they relocate politics from visible content alone to permitted life-forms within an environment. If a system models how a world evolves, remembers out-of-view details, generates counterfactual trajectories, specifies how movement affects terrain, and constrains what kinds of characters or geometries it supports, then power enters not only through depiction but through affordance. Genie 2 simulates the consequences of action. Genie 3 generates photorealistic worlds in real time. Marble produces persistent, editable 3D environments. These systems are explicitly about consequences, memory, controllable interaction, and inhabitable persistence. The critical object is therefore the world's action grammar — what it allows, what it remembers, what it naturalizes, what it excludes, and what it makes difficult even to imagine.

The political question shifts accordingly. "What does the image show?" remains necessary but is no longer sufficient. In a generated world, ideology is not exhausted by iconography. It is distributed across defaults, movement rules, camera logic, object persistence, style filters, training residues, moderation boundaries, and the uneven ease with which different beings, spaces, or actions can be realized. The most important power may lie in what a world quietly treats as ordinary, frictionless, and thinkable. When description becomes executable, critique must follow execution all the way down into governance.

Homer's shield already teaches the form of this question. The shield distributes marriage, law, war, agriculture, predation, music, and dance across a single bounded cosmos and thereby stages not an image of life but a selection among forms of life. What is on the shield is a political claim about what belongs together. What is absent — slavery, disease, famine, the lives of women outside ceremony — is a political claim about what can be rendered unthinkable within the available grammar. Contemporary world models do likewise under new technical conditions: their value lies in simulated action and consequence, yet the worlds they generate remain conditioned by inherited data, hidden parameters, and commercial systems whose workings are not fully available to the outside user.

The image is the first diagnostic return. The world model is the greenhouse becoming walkable. Once the image can be entered, the old question returns in a harsher form: not what does this image show, but what kind of life has this description authorized — and what kind has it rendered unimaginable?

Human beings once asked language to preserve the world. Now they ask language to generate one. The wish has changed less than we think.

## What This Synthesis Does for the Paper

This synthesis replaces Watson's current 161-word conclusion with a ground-up build that:
1. Opens on Mitchell/Krieger as the fantasy structure, not as a historical review
2. Integrates Davis's *TechGnosis* as a sustained argument about theological residue (not a passing reference)
3. Builds the political argument through specific mechanisms: Meyer's taste algorithmization, Steyerl's mean images, Simonen's default-images research, open-set bias detection
4. Introduces "affordance" as the key political concept — shifting from visual studies (what the image shows) to STS/interaction design (what the world allows)
5. Names what the shield *excludes* (slavery, disease, famine, women outside ceremony) as the political counterpart to what it includes — a move no current version of Watson's text makes
6. Terminates on "ordinary, frictionless, and thinkable" — Report 17's sharpest sentence — as the new terminal political claim, followed by the signature closing
