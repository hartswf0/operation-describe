## From ekphrastic hope to operative ekphrasis

Mitchell’s central move remains indispensable because it names the fantasy structure that still shadows generative media. For him, ekphrastic hope is the wish that language might overcome semiotic difference and cross into a visual presence, while ekphrastic fear and indifference mark the recoil from that fantasy and the skepticism that such crossing can ever be complete. Crucially, Mitchell does not treat the verbal/visual opposition as neutral description; he insists that the familiar binaries of symbolic versus iconic, conventional versus natural signs, and temporal versus spatial media are ideological formations rather than stable scientific facts. That diagnosis matters because contemporary prompt culture still often talks as though description could pass frictionlessly into visual realization. Mitchell’s lesson is that every dream of immediacy already rests on a structure of mediation, power, and desire. citeturn9view5turn9view6

Krieger radicalizes that lesson by centering the “natural sign” as an impossible horizon. His formulation is not simply that words fail to become pictures. It is that literary theory has repeatedly been shaped by attempts, and deceptive failures, to make language do the work of immediate visual signification. This is why the ekphrastic principle remains productive even when it is untenable: it keeps exposing the resistant paradoxes of representation. In the present context, that paradox reappears whenever a generated image is treated as though the prompt had simply “become visible.” The image seems to close the gap between word and image, but the closure is simulated through vast computational mediation, learned associations, latent spaces, filtered corpora, prompt interpretation layers, and platform interfaces. The natural sign has not arrived; it has been industrialized. citeturn17search3turn17search18turn15view2

Webb’s intervention is the hinge between classical rhetoric and the present. Her account of ancient ekphrasis emphasizes theatricality, audience, and live performance. Ekphrasis, in this older sense, is not exhausted by the description of artworks and not defined primarily by a finished object; it is a mode of vivid speech whose aim is to make absent scenes present to listeners. That makes ekphrasis less like a caption and more like an event of activation. Once this is remembered, the step to procedural media becomes easier to see. A prompt, too, is not just text sitting on a page. It is text situated within an interface, addressed to an interpreter, oriented toward an effect, and judged by what it calls forth before an audience of users, platforms, and models. Webb therefore helps explain why generative prompting feels less like commentary on an image than like a staged performance of world-calling. citeturn8view11turn9view7

This is the point at which entity["people","Hannes Bajohr","media theorist and literary scholar"]’s concept of operative ekphrasis becomes decisive. Bajohr argues that multimodal AI transforms ekphrasis from a representational relation into a computational one: text does not imitate or comment upon image so much as effectively bring it about. In his formulation, multimodal AI tends toward a collapse of the text/image distinction because the technical system can process both as one type of information; accordingly, operative ekphrasis is “not as representation but as performance,” not words depicting a picture but words making a picture happen as an operation on symbolic information. This is a powerful and persuasive redescription of prompt-based image generation. Yet it also now requires extension. If the operative turn explains word-to-image generation, the current world-model turn demands a stronger claim: operative ekphrasis is mutating into world initialization. citeturn15view0turn15view1turn15view2

## The shield as worldtext

The exemplary ancient text for this shift is not the urn but the shield. In Book 18 of entity["book","The Iliad","epic poem attributed to Homer"], entity["people","Homer","ancient Greek epic poet traditionally associated with the Iliad"] does not merely list decorative motifs on the shield of entity["fictional_character","Achilles","hero of the Iliad"]. The shield begins with cosmic order—earth, sea, sky, sun, moon, constellations—and then unfolds two cities, marriage processions, legal disputation, siege and ambush, fields under cultivation, harvesting, herding, dancing, and Ocean around the rim. What appears is not simply an image but a compact world-system: institutions, labor, ritual, violence, adjudication, ecology, festivity, and cyclical time. The description is already environmental, social, and political. It specifies not only what can be seen, but what kinds of collective life exist there. citeturn10view0turn9view10turn9view11

Mitchell’s reading makes the point even more forcefully. He argues that Homer’s shield is an “imagetext” that displays its own suturing of space and time, narration and description, materiality and illusion. More than that, he notes that modern classical scholarship has often treated the shield as an emblem of the entire structure of the epic and even as an image of the Homeric world-order itself. Mitchell goes so far as to say that the shield represents more of Homer’s world than the *Iliad* does, because it includes the whole world outside the narrowed historical action of heroic war: plowing, harvest, litigation, marriage, and everyday life. That is the crucial insight. The shield is not best understood as an ornamental precursor to later ekphrastic set-pieces. It is an early model of what can now be called worldtext: a verbal artifact that does not merely image a thing but composes a world thick enough to contain social relations, temporal rhythms, and alternative forms of life. citeturn19view0turn29view1

The shield therefore belongs at the center of the genealogy, not at its ceremonial beginning. It clarifies why current generative environments are not just “bigger pictures.” The shield already exceeds depiction in the narrow sense because it organizes a world of affordances and outcomes: weddings happen there, judgments are rendered there, ambushes are prepared there, crops are cut there, dancers move there. Its content is eventful, not merely visual. In that respect, it resembles contemporary world models more than static image generators. The ancient lesson is not that ekphrasis once described art objects beautifully. It is that verbal making has long been capable of compacting a whole form of life into a procedural image-space. Worldtext is the name for that older capacity once it becomes technically executable. citeturn10view0turn19view0

## From image models to world models

The technical distinction between image model and world model is not cosmetic. Ha and Schmidhuber’s 2018 paper proposes a model that learns a compressed spatiotemporal representation of an environment and can support control, planning, and prediction across state transitions. The key term is environment, not image. Their famous claim that an agent can be trained inside its own “hallucinated dream” already marks a move from representation to inhabitable simulation. A world model is not a better picture generator. It is a machine for modeling what follows from action in a stateful space. That is why the core object of analysis changes. The decisive question is no longer, “How accurately does this output depict?” but, “What transitions, continuities, and consequences does this system make available?” citeturn10view2

The 2024 entity["product","Genie","Generative Interactive Environments model by Google DeepMind"] paper makes this distinction explicit. It presents the system as the first generative interactive environment trained in an unsupervised manner from internet video and says that it can be prompted to generate an endless variety of action-controllable virtual worlds described through text, synthetic images, photographs, and sketches. This is not a small extension of text-to-image. It is a categorical shift from depiction to controllable environnementalization. Once a system is built to yield the next observation after an action, text has ceased to function as description alone. It has become a procedure for constructing an action-space. citeturn8view7

The official materials for entity["product","Genie 2","world model by Google DeepMind"] and entity["product","Genie 3","world model by Google DeepMind"] deepen the same point. Genie 2 is said to simulate virtual worlds including the consequences of taking any action, generate different trajectories from the same starting frame, remember parts of the world that are no longer in view, and keep a world consistent for extended interaction. Meanwhile, urlWorld Labsturn4search9 describes Marble as generating persistent, navigable, controllable 3D worlds that can be edited, expanded, and combined. The accumulated effect of these claims is clear: generation now increasingly concerns continuity, consistency, consequence, and inhabitation. It establishes terrain, entities, weather, movement logic, perspective, and interaction style; subsequent renders and world sketches report back on whether those conditions have cohered. What is being authored is not a picture but a playable or navigable consistency relation. Word-to-world-model is therefore not a rhetorical flourish. It names the actual expansion of generative mediation from visual output to governed environment. citeturn30view0turn10view4turn27view0

## Thick prompting

Geertz supplies the methodological bridge for reading this new condition. In “Thick Description,” he defines culture as webs of significance and insists that its analysis is interpretive rather than law-seeking. Ethnography, on his account, is not reducible to procedures of collection; it is an intellectual effort aimed at distinguishing dense layers of meaning and rendering them intelligible. He later says that the aim of theory in such work is not to codify abstract regularities but to make thick description possible. The importance of these claims for prompting is that they relocate description from mere notation to situated interpretation. Description becomes thick when it carries the codes, contexts, and practical distinctions that make action legible. citeturn9view3turn9view4

If that methodological lesson is transplanted into generative practice, “thick prompting” becomes a useful name for an emergent mode of world-tuning. By thick prompting I do not mean simply longer prompts. I mean prompt practice understood as iterative, situated, performative labor that tries to encode not just objects but environments, moods, behaviors, action possibilities, and contextual relations. The official entity["product","Genie 3","world model by Google DeepMind"] prompt guide already operationalizes this thickness. It asks the user to separate environment from character, to specify surfaces and terrain, style, structures, movement, atmospheric behavior, object appearance, character actions, and the effects of movement on the surrounding world; it then instructs the user to modify the generated world sketch until it matches the intended world. This is not description of a finished image. It is a recursive method for tuning a world before entry. citeturn30view0

Human-computer interaction research on prompting points in the same direction. The 2023 paper *PromptPaint* argues that many text-to-image systems remain challenging to guide because prompts are insufficient for difficult concepts and because end-to-end systems badly support iterative shaping. The authors therefore build an interface in which prompts can be mixed, spatially distributed, and changed during generation. Their studies show that users steer outputs by iterating, intervening, and redistributing semantic control across different parts of the canvas and different moments of the generative process; they also show that earlier prompts tend to determine overall form while later prompts determine detail. Prompting here is no longer a single declarative act. It is a temporal workflow with intermediate revisions, local adjustments, and negotiated control. citeturn31view0

Geertz’s later distinction between inscription and diagnosis makes the parallel sharper still. In thick prompting, the prompt is the provisional inscription of a world hypothesis, while the intermediate render, world sketch, or partial generation is diagnostic feedback about how the model has interpreted that hypothesis. The user then revises the inscription in light of the diagnosis. This structure explains why prompt practice increasingly resembles rehearsal, calibration, and interpretation rather than command. It also explains why world-model prompting is structurally closer to scene direction or environment design than to verbal captioning. citeturn9view4turn30view0turn31view0

## Platform sediment and the politics of inhabitation

Once prompting is understood as world initialization, the question of memory becomes unavoidable: what does the generated world remember before any user enters it? Recent research shows that this memory is neither empty nor abstract. A 2025/2026 study on “default images” argues that text-to-image systems are designed to always yield an output, even for unknown or malformed prompts, and that in such cases they may produce closely resembling “default images” across many unrelated inputs. A 2024 paper on data attribution for text-to-image models goes further, defining influence in terms of the training images whose removal would cause a model to fail to reproduce a given output. Together, these findings show that generated outputs emerge from specific inherited priors and from traceable training-image influence. The world does not begin at the prompt box. It begins in a sedimented archive. citeturn24search1turn24search7turn25search1

That sediment is also social and political. Work on bias in text-to-image generation shows that these systems can reproduce and sometimes amplify stereotyped associations inherited from training data, while open-set bias detection demonstrates that many biases cannot be reduced to a fixed checklist because new domain-specific regularities keep emerging from prompts that appear neutral on their surface. In a widely cited media-theoretical formulation, Roland Meyer describes one part of this process as a “recursive algorithmization of taste”: machines predict what narrowly modeled users will prefer, and those predictions are then used to optimize future machine outputs. The result is not a neutral average but a feedback loop in which archives, aesthetic filtering, and user-response metrics continuously narrow what appears plausible, attractive, or normal. citeturn33search1turn23view3turn34search4

World models intensify the stakes because they relocate politics from visible content alone to permitted life-forms within an environment. If a system models how a world evolves, remembers out-of-view details, generates counterfactual trajectories, specifies how movement affects terrain, and constrains what kinds of characters or geometries it currently supports, then power enters not only through depiction but through affordance. The critical object is therefore the world’s action grammar—what it allows, what it remembers, what it naturalizes, what it excludes, and what it makes difficult even to imagine. citeturn10view3turn10view4turn27view1turn30view0

The political question accordingly shifts. “What does the image show?” remains necessary, but it is no longer sufficient. In a generated world, ideology is not exhausted by iconography. It is distributed across defaults, movement rules, camera logic, object persistence, style filters, training residues, moderation boundaries, and the uneven ease with which different beings, spaces, or actions can be realized. The most important power may lie in what a world quietly treats as ordinary, frictionless, and thinkable. When description becomes executable, critique must follow execution all the way down into governance. citeturn24search7turn25search1turn23view3turn34search4

## After the image

The strongest version of the argument, then, is not that generative AI has finally fulfilled ekphrastic hope. It is that it has displaced the site on which that hope operates. Mitchell and Krieger diagnose the fantasy that words might become immediate images; Webb restores the performative and audience-bound event of vivid description; Bajohr shows that multimodal AI turns text into operative image-production; Ha and Schmidhuber provide the technical schema for environments that model state transitions rather than static outputs; Homer’s shield demonstrates that verbal art has long been capable of specifying a compact social cosmos; and Geertz offers a method for reading description as dense, situated inscription whose value appears in the diagnoses it enables. Read together, these sources support a clear intervention: the central object is no longer the generated image but the generated world. citeturn9view5turn17search18turn8view11turn15view0turn10view2turn19view0turn9view4

Worldtext names that object. It names the point at which description ceases to be exhausted by representation and begins to function as the initialization of an inhabitable, memory-bearing, affordance-laden environment. In that situation, the prompt is not the artwork but the environmental seed; the image is not the whole work but its first diagnostic return; and politics no longer resides only in what appears but in what is renderable, traversable, and livable. The decisive critical question is therefore no longer what the image shows. It is what kind of life this description has authorized. citeturn30view0turn10view4turn27view1turn24search7turn25search1turn23view3
