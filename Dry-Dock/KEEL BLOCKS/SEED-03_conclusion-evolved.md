## EVOLVED TEXT (243 words)

The word has always wanted to make an image — Mitchell's (1994) "ekphrastic hope." The image has always wanted to become a world. Now that desire has a platform.

But the fantasy remains unstable. The prompt does not become a natural sign. Krieger (1992) showed that the natural sign is an impossible horizon; generative AI has not fulfilled that horizon — it has industrialized it. The world that emerges is not pure creation but debris fields of memory: training data, platform filters, inferential habits, aesthetic residues accumulated before any user opens the prompt box. The promise of presence and the promise of habitation are, as Davis (2015) argues, theological in disguise — the act of typing text and watching a world materialize feeds a fantasy of divine creation that generative interfaces have merely made frictionless.

Bajohr (2024) names the structural consequence: "there is no outside-model." The prompter operates inside proprietary interfaces, factory settings, and corpora whose composition is never fully disclosed. Meyer's (2025) "recursive algorithmization of taste" ensures that the outputs converge toward what the platform predicts users will accept. Thick prompting resists this convergence. It does not escape the archive; it diagnoses where the archive has already decided.

Homer's shield already teaches the form of this question. The shield distributes marriage, law, war, agriculture, and dance across a single bounded cosmos — a selection among forms of life, not a depiction of all life. Contemporary world models distribute affordances with the same structural logic under new technical conditions. The politics of worldtext will appear not in its explicit content but in what it treats as ordinary, frictionless, and thinkable.

The image is the first diagnostic return. The world model is the greenhouse becoming walkable. Once the image can be entered, the old question returns in a harsher form: not merely "what does this image show?" but what kind of life has this description authorized — and what kind has it rendered unimaginable?

Human beings once asked language to preserve the world. Now they ask language to generate one. The wish has changed less than we think.
