## TEMPLATED TEXT (730 words)

Mitchell (1994) diagnosed the fantasy that still shadows generative media: ekphrastic hope, the wish that language might overcome semiotic difference and cross into visual presence. Krieger (1992) radicalized the diagnosis: the "natural sign" is an impossible horizon — literary theory shaped by attempts, and deceptive failures, to make language do the work of immediate visual signification. Generative AI appears to close this gap. The prompt yields an image. But the closure is simulated through vast computational mediation: learned associations, latent spaces, filtered corpora, prompt interpretation layers, and platform interfaces. The natural sign has not arrived. It has been industrialized.

The promise of presence and the promise of habitation are, as Davis (2015) argues, theological in disguise. Generative interfaces have made the theological gesture frictionless without making it innocent.

Bajohr (2024) names the structural consequence. Users of proprietary large models are confined to "factory settings." Prompting works only inside a given model and interface. There is "no outside-model." The generated output is an operational synthesis of inherited corpora, proprietary interfaces, hyperparameters, and defaults before it is ever a personal expression. What appears as immediacy is accumulated mediation.

Meyer's (2025) "recursive algorithmization of taste" specifies the mechanism: machines predict what narrowly modeled users will prefer, and those predictions optimize future outputs. The result is not a neutral average but a feedback loop in which archives, aesthetic filtering, and user-response metrics continuously narrow what appears plausible, attractive, or normal. Steyerl's (2023) "mean images" name the product: statistical renderings converging toward social filters rather than indexical truth.

The world does not begin at the prompt box. It begins in debris fields of memory — training data, platform filters, inferential habits, aesthetic residues, economic incentives, and the uneven labor of the annotators who labeled the corpus. Simonen et al. (2025) demonstrate that default images recur across unrelated prompts. A 2024 data-attribution study defines influence in terms of the specific training images whose removal would cause a model to fail to reproduce a given output. Thick prompting resists this convergence. It does not escape the archive; it diagnoses where the archive has already decided.

World models intensify these stakes. They relocate politics from visible content to permitted life-forms within an environment. If a system models how a world evolves, remembers out-of-view details, generates counterfactual trajectories, specifies how movement affects terrain, and constrains what kinds of characters or geometries it supports, then power enters not through depiction but through affordance. Genie 2 simulates the consequences of action. Genie 3 generates photorealistic worlds in real time. Marble produces persistent, editable 3D environments. These systems are explicitly about consequences, memory, controllable interaction, and inhabitable persistence. The world's action grammar — what it allows, what it remembers, what it naturalizes, what it excludes — is where the politics live.

"What does the image show?" remains necessary but is no longer sufficient. In a generated world, ideology is not exhausted by iconography. It is distributed across defaults, movement rules, camera logic, object persistence, style filters, training residues, moderation boundaries, and the uneven ease with which different beings, spaces, or actions can be realized. The most important power may lie in what a world quietly treats as ordinary, frictionless, and thinkable.

Homer's shield already teaches the form of this question. The shield distributes marriage, law, war, agriculture, predation, music, and dance across a single bounded cosmos and thereby stages not an image of life but a selection among forms of life. What is on the shield is a political claim about what belongs together. What is absent — slavery, disease, famine, the lives of women outside ceremony — is a political claim about what can be rendered unthinkable within the available grammar.

The image is the first diagnostic return. The world model is the greenhouse becoming walkable. Once the image can be entered, the old question returns in a harsher form: not what does this image show, but what kind of life has this description authorized — and what kind has it rendered unimaginable?

Human beings once asked language to preserve the world. Now they ask language to generate one. The wish has changed less than we think.
