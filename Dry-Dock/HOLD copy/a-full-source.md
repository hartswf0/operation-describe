"First of all he formed a shield, both large and solid, adorning it all over; and around it he threw a shining border, triple, glittering; and from it [suspended] a silver belt. Five were the folds of the shield itself; but on it he formed many ornaments with cunning skill. On it he wrought the earth, and the heaven, and the sea, the unwearied sun, and the full moon; on it also, all the constellations with which the heaven is crowned, the Pleiades, the Hyades, and the might of Orion, and the Bear, which they also call by the appellation of the Wain, which there revolves, and watches Orion; but it alone is without a share of the baths of the ocean."
"On it also he fashioned two beautiful cities of articulate-speaking men. In the one, indeed, were marriages and feasts; and they were conducting the brides from their chambers through the city with brilliant torches, and many a bridal song was raised. The young men were dancing in a circle, and among them flutes and lyres gave forth their sounds; and the women standing, each at her portals, admired. But the people were assembled in the forum, for there a strife had arisen, and two men strove for the ransom-money of a slain man; the one affirmed that he had paid all, appealing to the people, but the other denied that he had received any thing; and both wished to find a termination [of the dispute] before a judge. The people shouted to both, supporters on either side; and the heralds reined in the people; but the elders sat upon polished stones in a sacred circle, and held in their hands the staves of the loud-shouting heralds; with these then they arose, and adjudged in turn. But there lay in the midst two talents of gold, to give to him who should pronounce a judgment the most correctly."
"But around the other city sat two armies of people, glittering in arms; and a plan was agreeable to them, either to destroy it entirely, or to divide all the wealth into two parts, which the pleasant city contained within it. But they [the besieged] did not yet obey, but were secretly arming for an ambush; their beloved wives and young children stood upon the wall guarding it, and together with them the men whom old age possessed. But they advanced; and Mars and Pallas Minerva led them, both golden, and clad in golden garments, beautiful and large with their arms, like gods, both very conspicuous; but the people were smaller.
But when they now had reached a place where it appeared fit to lay an ambush, by a river, where was a watering-place for all cattle, there they settled, wrapped in shining brass. Then apart from the people sat two spies, watching when they might perceive the sheep and curvated oxen. These, however, soon advanced, and two shepherds followed, amusing themselves with their pipes, for they did not at all foresee the fraud. But they [the liers-in-wait] having perceived them, ran up, and immediately slaughtered the herds of oxen and the fair flocks of white-wooled sheep; and slew the shepherds besides. But the besiegers, as soon as they heard the great tumult among the oxen, sitting before the councils, immediately mounting their nimble-footed horses, pursued, and soon reached them. Then, having formed a line, they fought a battle on the banks of the river, and wounded one another with their brazen spears. Among them mingled Strife and Tumult, and destructive Fate, holding one freshly-wounded, and another unwounded, and by the feet she dragged another, dead, through the affray; and the garment around her shoulders was crimsoned with the blood of men. And they were turned [to each other] like living mortals, and fought; and they drew away the slaughtered bodies of each other.")
"On it he also placed a soft fallow field, rich glebe, wide, thrice-ploughed; and in it many ploughmen drove hither and thither, turning round their teams. But when, returning, they reached the end of the field, then a man, advancing, gave into their hands a cup of very sweet wine; but they turned themselves in series, eager to reach the [other] end of the deep fallow. But it [the field] grew black behind, and was like unto ploughed [land], though being golden; which was a very great marvel.
There he also placed a field of deep corn, where reapers were reaping, having sharp sickles in their hands. Some handfuls fell thick in series upon the ground along the furrow, and others the binders of sheaves were tying with straw ropes. Three binders followed [the reapers], but behind them boys gathering the handfuls, [and] carrying them in their arms, continually supplied them; and among them the king in silence, over a furrow, was standing at the staff, rejoicing in his heart. But apostles apart, beneath an oak, were preparing a feast, and having sacrificed a huge ox, they were ministering; and women were strowing much white barley [meal] as a dinner for the reapers.
On it also he placed a vineyard heavily laden with grapes, beautiful, golden; but the clusters throughout were black; and it was supported throughout by silver poles. Around it he drew a dark-blue trench, and around a hedge of tin; and there was but one path to it, by which the gatherers went, when they collected the vintage. Young virgins and youths, of tender minds, carried the sweet fruit in woven baskets. In the midst of them a boy played pleasantly on a shrill lyre, and sung the beautiful Linus song with a slender voice; while they, beating [the ground] in accompaniment, with singing and shouting, followed, hopping with their feet."
"On it he also fashioned a herd of lofty-horned oxen; but the oxen were made of gold and tin; and lowing, they rushed from the stall to the pasture, beside a murmuring river, along the waving reed. Four golden herdsmen accompanied the oxen, and nine dog, swift of foot, followed them. But two terrible lions among the foremost oxen seized a bellowing bull, and he, dragging, roared loudly; but the dogs and the young men ran up to him. The [lions] indeed, having torn the hide of the huge ox, were devouring the entrails and the black blood; but the shepherds vainly pressed upon them, urging on the fleet dogs; for they refused to bite the lions, but stood very near, barking, and flying [them]. On it also the illustrious Vulcan formed a large pasture of white sheep, in a beautiful grove, and folds, and covered sheds and cottages."
"On it also the illustrious Vulcan embellished a dance, like unto that which in wide Gnossus Daedalus once composed for fair-haired Ariadne. There the youths and the alluring virgins danced, having their hands on each other's wrists. Of these, the [virgins] had on fine linen robes, but the [youths] were dressed in well-woven tunics, slightly shining with oil; these also had beautiful garlands, and those had golden swords suspended from silver belts. At one time they would move very lightly with skilled feet, as when a potter sitting shall make trial of a wheel fitted to his hands, whether it will run; and at another time they would move in series towards each other. A large crowd stood around the enjoyable dance, delighted; and among them two tumblers, beginning their song, spun round in the midst.
But in it he placed the vast strength of the river Oceanus, around the extreme border of the well-formed shield."
