# BULKHEAD 04 — The Shield as Performance and Process

> **Author**: Jay
> **Word count**: ~440w
> **Function**: Introduces the Shield of Achilles as the paper's classical case. Establishes Lessing (temporal fabrication), Parry/Lord (oral-formulaic = generative system), and the analogy (not equivalence) between Homer and AI.

## TEXT

Homer's description of the making of a new shield for Achilles in the Iliad (Book 18. 478–608) is often invoked as the classical example of literary ekphrasis. We propose to use Homer's text to illustrate how operative ekphrasis follows the tradition of ekphrasis and how it diverges. Interestingly for our purposes, Homer's passage prefigures a quality of operative ekphrasis mentioned above. As Lessing and later critics pointed out (Lessing 2022 [1766]; Taplin 1980), instead of describing the finished object, Homer describes how the god Hephaestus fashions the shield, scene by scene, as each is located in a different sector. That Homer described the process of making as it unfolds in time was an important point for Lessing who argued in Laocoon that poetry is a temporal medium.

The emphasis on craft and process also make it tempting to read Homer's description as an allegory for the craft of oral poetry. Just as Hephaestus is crafting a world out of bronze, tin and gold, so Homer is creating not only Hephaestus' shield but the whole of the Iliad from his materials. Homer's materials are the words and formulaic expressions of his oral tradition—expressions developed to suit the requirements of epic verse (dactylic hexameter) while at the same time capturing essential qualities of the characters and elements of that imagined world (swift-footed Achilles, Hector of the shining helmet, the wine-dark sea, and so on). Classicists still debate the question of how the Iliad was transmitted as a written text, but most agree that it arose from a long tradition of oral performance. (cf. Lord 2018 [1960]) The formulaic system enables the poet to perform the story before an audience in "real-time." The oral theory of Homeric composition, proposed by Milman Parry and elaborated by A.B. Lord (Lord 2018 [1960]), has sometimes been (mis)interpreted and critiqued as a deterministic explanation of composition. Homeric scholars today emphasize the craft of Homeric poetry was never a simple matter of dropping the right formulae into the right slot. Homeric poetry could not be fashioned by a deterministic (procedural) algorithm. On the other hand, we propose this analogy (not equivalence): that Homeric oral poetry can itself be thought of as a generative system. The system was evolved over generations by poets who fashioned formulaic epithets that suited the metrical constraints as well as larger and less constrained formulae for scenes and episodes. A poet trained in the system can then generate a story that is in the appropriate meter but also with the appropriate elements that formed the Homeric world. The process was not deterministic, but it was probabilistic. No two tellings Achilles' wrath would be line for line the same, but all of them would evoke that same Homeric world of gods and heroes, combat scenes, and formulaic epithets, such as bright-eyed Athena and rosy-fingered dawn. Homer's ekphrasis of the shield was already in this sense produced operatively.
