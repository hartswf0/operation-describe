# BULKHEAD 05 — The Shield and the Natural Sign

> **Author**: Jay
> **Word count**: ~560w
> **Function**: Connects ekphrastic hope (Mitchell) to the natural sign (Krieger). Tests the shield against actual generative AI outputs. Introduces Meyer's "platform realism" and Steyerl's "mean images." Concludes that AI shows "a nostalgia for the natural sign."

---

## TEXT

In the ancient tradition, an ekphrasis is successful if the verbal description can call forth a clear and vivid image in the listener's or reader's mind. The word aspires to become an image. As we noted above, this aspiration is what Mitchell termed "ekphrastic hope." As Krieger put it, the verbal description would become a "natural sign," as a picture was assumed to be. He added: "The narrow, literal doctrine of ekphrasis would seem to require this primitive notion of the pictorial as the naively representational." (Krieger, 12, n12) That is, the image called forth would be in a style of pictorial realism. Krieger may have characterized the view as naive or primitive, but it remains popular. The generative platforms agree. Since pictorial and photographic realism are richly represented in the data that the visual models scrape from the Internet, it is not surprising that an operative ekphrasis can often produce a realistic image of Achilles' shield or the scenes depicted on it. In fact, when prompted accordingly, the principal generative AI systems (e.g. from OpenAI, Gemini, and MidJourney) are all capable of a variety of styles, but they will default to a small range of popular styles depending on the subject matter and the prompting.

Prompting with the Homeric text does in fact typically produce images in the tradition of realism in western painting or photorealism in computer graphics. As a test, we used Google's image generator Gemini 3 Flash. Our prompt was: "Create the shield described below. Make the image as faithful and detailed as possible", and we included in the prompt the entire Homeric passage (Book 18, lines 478–608) in the original Greek. The model had no trouble with the Greek. The prompt produced a pair of images of a round bronze shield whose raised figures correspond in general terms, though not perfectly, to the scenes described in the passage. (Fig 1) The style defaulted to something between a realistic painted image and a photograph. Further text prompting could likely get closer to depicting every scene if not every detail of the verbal original. The prompts would start from the original image and call for corrections and additions. Such iteration is the most common method of thickening a prompt.

That prompt produced the shield as a whole object. A possible next step would be to pursue the logic of immersion by inviting the viewer into an individual scene on the shield. Immersion is, after all, a popular digital aesthetic in this age of videogames and computer-generated special effects in film. In this case, we focused on the vineyard scene (18.561-572) with the prompt: "Create the scene described below. Make the image as faithful and detailed as possible" followed by the excerpt in Greek. (Fig 2) The result was a representation in linear perspective and a style reminiscent of an illustrated children's book of the mid-twentieth century. It looks like an illustration of a child's version of the Bible. The generative models are trained on a vast number of images with text captions or annotations from the corpus of human production, so they tend to styles that our visual culture associates with the ancient world, in books and movies in particular.

As noted above, the various models have somewhat different qualities within the general rubric of (photo)realism. For example, OpenAI's GPT Image series is considered better at executing details of the prompt; whereas Midjourney excels at cinematic effects. Gemini's platform gives rapid and fairly accurate results. Beyond these differences, though, there is a common aesthetic that Roland Meyer (2025) and others have characterized as "platform realism." The images have a generic or "average" character (Hintze et al, 2026). They are in a literal sense averages, as they are interpolations from the numerical values assigned to the images on which the model was trained. Steyerl (2023) calls them "mean" images. Meyer further notes that the images are nostalgic in a postmodern way, expressive not of a longing for the past but for a sense of pastness. The models could have come by this postmodern aesthetic honestly in the sense that they have devoured large numbers of postmodern art as well. Generative AI produces images of images, not the natural sign. We could say that what AI shows is a nostalgia for the natural sign.
