## WATSON'S TEXT

The word has always wanted to make an image — Mitchell's (1994) "ekphrastic hope" — and the image has always wanted to become a world. Now that desire has a platform.

This paper has traced a single operation across three thresholds. The first threshold is ancient: the *progymnasmata* taught students to perform language that produces visual experience. That threshold was concealed by the modern narrowing of ekphrasis from production to representation and recovered by Bajohr's concept of operative ekphrasis. The second threshold is contemporary: generative AI materializes the ekphrastic operation computationally, collapsing word and image into the latent space of a multimodal model. The third threshold is emergent: as generative systems move from image models to world models, operative ekphrasis extends beyond the image and toward the environment.

But the fantasy remains unstable. The prompt does not become a natural sign. The world that emerges is not pure creation but sediment — platformed, shaped by the descriptions that trained it. The promise of presence and the promise of habitation are, as Davis (2015) argues, theological in disguise. The politics of a worldtext will appear not in its explicit content but in its affordances: what it allows, what it remembers, what it makes easy, what it makes impossible, what it treats as natural, and what it renders unthinkable. Homer's shield already stages this. What is present — marriage, law, agriculture, dance — is a claim about what belongs together. What is absent — slavery, disease, famine — is a claim about what the available grammar renders unimaginable.

The image is the first diagnostic return. The world model is the greenhouse becoming walkable. Once the image can be entered, the old question returns in a harsher form: what kind of life has the description authorized?

Human beings once asked language to preserve the world. Now they ask language to generate one. The wish has changed less than we think.
