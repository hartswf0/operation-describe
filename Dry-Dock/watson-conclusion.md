## T019 — PHILOSOPHICAL APHORIST: APHORISM TRIAGE

The conclusion IS a sequence of aphorisms. Each must survive the aphorist's test: is it irreducible, surprising, and honest — or is it a clever-sounding phrase?

| # | Aphorism | Verdict | Reason |
|---|---------|---------|--------|
| 1 | "The world wanted a procedure for being called forth. Now that procedure has a platform." | ⚠️ **DEMOTE.** "The world wanted" is personification — and worse, it sounds like marketing copy. Worlds don't want procedures. Engineers build them. | → Cut "wanted." The second sentence is strong alone. |
| 2 | "The resulting world is not pure creation but sediment" | ✅ **KEEP.** "Sediment" is precise — trained on prior descriptions, accumulated, layered. Does real argumentative work. |
| 3 | "theological in disguise" (Davis) | ✅ **KEEP.** Anchored to Davis. Says something the rest of the paper doesn't. |
| 4 | "what it treats as natural and what it renders unthinkable" | ✅ **KEEP.** The political affordance, irreducible. |
| 5 | "The prompt is the initialization vector. The image is the first diagnostic return. The world model is the greenhouse becoming walkable." | ⚠️ **STRESS-TEST.** These are three metaphors, not three arguments. "Initialization vector" is technical jargon used metaphorically. "Diagnostic return" same. "Greenhouse becoming walkable" — Watson's pet image. Do all three earn their place? | → Keep "diagnostic return" (precise) and "greenhouse becoming walkable" (the paper's signature image). Cut "initialization vector" — it's jargon with no argumentative payoff. |
| 6 | "what kind of life has the control signal authorized?" | ✅ **KEEP.** The final question. Irreducible. |
| 7 | "The wish has changed less than we think." | ✅ **KEEP.** Perfect closer. |

### BLACKSMITH + APHORIST TYPESCRIPT (161 words)

> The word has always wanted to make an image — Mitchell's (1994) "ekphrastic hope" — and the image has always wanted to become a world. Now that desire has a platform.
>
> But the fantasy remains unstable. The prompt does not become a natural sign. The world that emerges is not pure creation but sediment — platformed, shaped by the descriptions that trained it. The promise of presence and the promise of habitation are, as Davis (2011) argues, theological in disguise. The politics of a worldtext will appear not in its explicit content but in what it treats as natural and what it renders unthinkable.
>
> The image is the first diagnostic return. The world model is the greenhouse becoming walkable. Once the image can be entered, the old question returns in a harsher form: what kind of life has the control signal authorized?
>
> Human beings once asked language to preserve the world. Now they ask language to generate one. The difference between describing and generating — between undergoing the pressure of a world and producing its probable continuation — is the question that outlasts every platform. The wish has changed less than we think.
