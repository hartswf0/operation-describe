Worldtext: Operative Ekphrasis and the Shift from Image to World Model

The old ekphrastic question asked whether words could make readers see. Generative artificial intelligence appears to answer yes: the word becomes the picture. However, in the rapid acceleration of machine learning architectures, that answer is already obsolete. The decisive shift occurring today is not from word to image, but from word to world model. Prompting is becoming a cumulative, procedural, and increasingly spatial practice in which description no longer points at a world from the outside; instead, description helps constitute the world’s operating conditions.

Generative AI is rapidly evolving from a mere image-maker into a world-model operator. To comprehend the magnitude of this transition, it is necessary to re-examine the ancient operative definitions of ekphrasis, analyze the performative practice of "thick prompting," and recognize that the computational architecture of the modern world model is the technical actualization of an ambition first staged thousands of years ago in Homer's Shield of Achilles. The word has successfully become the picture; the urgent reality is that the word is now becoming the world.

The Generative Threshold: Operative Ekphrasis

For much of the twentieth century, literary theory and media studies treated ekphrasis as a closed representational loop. Initiated largely by formalism, the term was narrowed to mean the poetic description of a static visual work of art. The emphasis fell heavily on the rivalry between verbal and visual media—the "imagetext"—where the ekphrastic passage was treated as a finished verbal artifact representing a finished visual artifact.[3, 5]

With the advent of connectionist artificial intelligence, the ekphrastic relation became operative. As Hannes Bajohr notes, multimodal models effectively collapse the distinction between text and image because both exist as coordinates within the same latent space.[4, 13] The prompt no longer describes an image that already exists; it mathematically executes the image. Operative ekphrasis names this first threshold: the moment description becomes image-making.

Yet, this theoretical triumph is already proving insufficient. The central object of prompting is beginning to move beyond the generated image. In image models, the prompt generates a bounded visual artifact. In world models, the prompt points toward an environment: a space with continuity, affordances, physics, memory, and possible action. The word no longer aspires only to become picture. The word begins to specify the conditions under which a world can appear and remain manipulable.

Prompting as Iterative Performance

The old mistake is to imagine the prompt as a sentence that tells the machine what to draw. That is too thin. It treats generative AI like a vending machine: insert words, receive image. But anyone who has actually used these systems knows the first result is rarely the desired result. The first prompt does not finish the work. It starts the work.

Prompting is better understood as a performance than as a description. The prompt does not simply stand outside the image and explain it. The prompt enters a process. A user writes a prompt, receives an output, reads the system’s misinterpretation, revises the prompt, adds constraints, changes style, supplies reference images, removes noise, and tries again.[12]

In this cycle, the image undergoes a profound ontological shift: the image is not the end of the process. The image is feedback. It tells the user what the model heard, what the model ignored, and what the model already assumes.

This performative character links operative ekphrasis back to the older rhetorical tradition of the progymnasmata—where ekphrasis was an exercise in delivery, responsiveness, and rhetorical force, judged by its capacity to make something absent appear.[8, 10] Operative ekphrasis restores that performative condition, but it changes the audience: the prompt is now performed against a generative system, and the output answers back. The prompt becomes less like a caption and more like a score: a set of instructions that can be replayed, revised, interpreted, and varied by a system. A prompt does not evoke an image. It probes a model. It discovers a platform’s defaults. It exposes the distance between what the user says and what the system can operationalize. There is therefore no final perfect prompt; there is only the next useful version.

Thick Prompting and the Latent Archive

We suggest the term thick prompting for this increasingly layered practice. The term deliberately echoes Clifford Geertz’s "thick description," but it should not become a decorative analogy.[21] It names the practical condition of working inside generative systems.

Geertz used thickness to name the layered cultural codes that make an action meaningful. A wink is not only a physical contraction of the eyelid; it sits inside codes, habits, jokes, threats, and shared knowledge. Prompting works the same way. A prompt never touches an empty machine. It touches a model packed with cultural residue: captions, images, genres, styles, poses, lighting schemes, stereotypes, defaults, and visual habits scraped from the world.

A thin prompt assumes that the image already exists somewhere and merely needs to be requested. A thick prompt understands that the model is a greenhouse full of tangled prior growth. To prompt is to enter an archive by giving it a symptom, to disturb what it already carries. Thick prompting is therefore not verbosity. It is not simply making the prompt longer. A long prompt can still be thin if it merely piles up adjectives. Thick prompting means giving the system enough situated pressure that the result begins to emerge from a specific world rather than generic visual habit.

The thick prompt gives the model a world of constraints. It tells the system what to preserve, what to resist, what to foreground, what to suppress. It prunes the default growth. In image generation, thick prompting helps produce more determinate images. In world generation, thick prompting must do more: it must begin to specify the conditions under which an environment can persist and change.

The Shield of Achilles as Ancestral Worldtext

To understand this transition from image to environment, we must return to the foundational text of the Western ekphrastic tradition: Homer’s description of the Shield of Achilles in Book 18 of the Iliad.[24]

The shield of Achilles is not the first great ekphrasis. It is a miniature world model. Homer does not only render an object; he organizes a cosmos. The shield gathers the conditions of a whole human order: the earth, sky, sea, constellations, cities, fields, vineyards, herds, dances, weddings, lawsuits, ambushes, harvests, and the great river Ocean around the rim.[28]

The crucial point is that the Trojan War is the large narrative frame of the Iliad, but the shield reverses the scale. The war becomes one event inside a larger order. The whole world appears within the weapon of one man. The shield is therefore both artifact and model: a made thing that contains the conditions under which many other things can appear together.[26, 29] It is not merely descriptive. It is constitutive.

Homer’s description already exceeds the static image. He does not only describe what the shield looks like; he describes how its scenes unfold. Hephaestus makes the shield scene by scene, line by line. The poem does not freeze an image; it performs a process.[25, 30] The shield includes time because the shield includes action. The wedding procession moves. The dancers turn. The armies clash. The harvest advances. The shield is not a still picture pretending to be alive. It is a compressed environment.

This is where we propose the term worldtext. Worldtext does not simply mean a text about a world, nor a fictional setting. Worldtext names a textual operation through which a world becomes available as a coherent field of relations. In the shield, Homeric language composes the world as an ordered totality. The shield is the hinge between operative text and a described artifact that already behaves like a world-bearing system.

From Image-State to World-State

The shift from image model to world model changes the status of ekphrasis. The prompt no longer aims only at visual resemblance. It aims at a field of possible interaction. A generated world must maintain spatial consistency, temporal continuity, affordances, surfaces, paths, objects, atmospheres, and events. Time enters quietly; then the picture is no longer a picture, and the object is no longer finished by being seen.

A picture can lie by resemblance; a world lies by consistency. A world is not a larger image; it is an image with rules, durations, permissions, and refusals. The image says "here is what appears"; the world model says "here is what can happen next."

This aspiration is visible in contemporary generative world systems, such as Google DeepMind’s Genie or World Labs’ Marble, which point toward systems in which users can bring forth interactive, physics-bound spaces from prompts.[36, 39] The promise is that users will enter, navigate, modify, and co-inhabit generated worlds.

However, this promise should not be overstated. Current world-model platforms do not yet fulfill the fantasy of complete verbal world creation. The genie is out of the bottle, but the world is not yet stable. Generated worlds remain partial, inconsistent, platform-bound, and heavily dependent on hidden scaffolding. The apparent magic of "word to world" is supported by an immense technical and symbolic infrastructure. The prompt is not sovereign. It is one operator among many, collaborating with system prompts, training data, latent visual conventions, safety filters, physics engines, and procedural rules.

Worldtext names the hanging together of these elements. It gives us a way to study not only what the generated world looks like, but how description becomes one component in a larger world-making apparatus.

The Burden of Theory

The word becomes picture: the old dream of the natural sign. The word becomes world: the newer and more dangerous dream. Immediacy becomes inhabitation, and both promises are theological in disguise. The machine does not fulfill the ekphrastic hope; it changes the scale of the hope. Once the image can be entered, the old question returns in a harsher form: what kind of life has the description authorized?

The concepts introduced here—operative ekphrasis, imagetext, thick prompting, and worldtext—will likely diverge as the technologies develop. For now, their convergence gives us a way to name the transition underway. We cannot keep calling this image generation. The image is becoming too small a category. The prompt is moving past the still image, past the video clip, past the surface.

Humanists must stop treating generative AI as a vulgar technical novelty or a degraded image factory. They must rebuild ekphrasis around prompting, process, platform realism, and worldtext. Ekphrasis can no longer be treated as a minor literary device for describing art. Prompting can no longer be treated as a shallow user command. AI images can no longer be treated as finished pictures detached from the process that made them.

Prompting is not a caption. Prompting is not decoration. Prompting is not a cheap shortcut around craft. Prompting is a repeated act of pressure on a system that has already absorbed word and image into one unstable field. The image is an image of images. It is a statistical memory of images—a platform’s learned compromise between the prompt, the archive, the model, and the visual habits of a culture already trained to recognize itself.

Humanists must claim prompting as a serious object of theory, a serious practice of craft, and a serious continuation of ekphrasis. They must treat generative AI not as an external threat to literary and visual theory, but as the machine that forces those theories to reveal what they were already trying to say: the word has always wanted to make an image, and the image has always wanted to become a world.
