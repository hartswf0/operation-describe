# Worldtext After Ekphrasis

## Prompting, Thick Description, and the Shield of Achilles as World Model

### Abstract

This paper argues that generative AI changes the status of ekphrasis by moving description from representation toward operation. Traditional ekphrasis asked whether words could make an absent image present to a listener or reader. Image-generating AI appears to literalize this ambition: the word becomes the picture. Yet this apparent answer is already insufficient. As generative systems move from image outputs toward interactive, persistent, and spatially navigable environments, the central object of ekphrasis shifts from the image to the world model. The prompt no longer functions merely as a caption before an image; it becomes one operator among many in a larger apparatus of world generation.

To name this shift, the paper proposes the term **worldtext**. Worldtext is not simply text about a world, nor is it equivalent to a fictional setting or lore system. It names the moment when language, prompt, system prompt, image, interface, memory, model, and procedural constraint begin to constitute a world as an explorable field of relations. The paper develops this argument through three linked concepts: operative ekphrasis, thick prompting, and the Shield of Achilles. Operative ekphrasis describes the transformation of verbal description into executable media generation. Thick prompting names the iterative practice by which users negotiate with generative systems already saturated with cultural, visual, technical, and platform defaults. Homer’s Shield of Achilles provides the central pre-digital model for this transition because it is not merely an image described in words, but a world-bearing artifact: a shield that gathers war, law, agriculture, dance, labor, cosmic order, and divine making into a single structured surface. The shield clarifies the stakes of worldtext: ekphrastic hope was never only the hope that words could make pictures, but the deeper hope that words could summon a whole order of things. Generative world models make that hope technical, unstable, and newly inspectable.

**Keywords:** ekphrasis, operative ekphrasis, worldtext, thick prompting, generative AI, world models, Shield of Achilles, imagetext, platform realism

## Introduction: From Word to Image to World

The old ekphrastic question asked whether words could make readers see. Could language summon an absent image with such force that the listener or reader almost stood before it? Could a poem cross the border between saying and seeing? Could description press hard enough against the visual that the image seemed to appear?

Generative AI seems, at first, to answer yes. A user writes a prompt; the system generates an image. The word becomes the picture. The ancient wish of ekphrasis, once suspended between rhetoric, poetry, performance, and imagination, appears to have been operationalized by the machine. Language no longer merely evokes a visual object. Language produces one.

But that answer is already too small.

The decisive shift is not only from word to image. It is from word to world. Prompting is becoming a cumulative, procedural, and increasingly spatial practice in which description no longer points at a world from outside. Description begins to participate in the operating conditions by which a world appears, coheres, behaves, and changes. The prompt no longer aims only at a bounded visual artifact. It begins to condition environments: spaces with continuity, affordances, memory, physics, actions, and possible futures.

This paper calls that threshold **worldtext**.

Worldtext is not a rival term for world model. A world model, in the technical sense, refers to a system’s compressed representation of an environment that can support prediction, action, or simulation. Worldtext names a cultural and medial relation to that system. It names the moment when description becomes an interface to a world model. A worldtext is not simply a text about a world. It is not a fictional setting, a database of lore, or a game map with narrative decoration. It is a text-image-procedure assemblage through which language helps make a world available as a coherent field of relations.

Ekphrasis therefore does not disappear. It mutates. Traditional ekphrasis asks how words make images present. Operative ekphrasis asks what happens when words make images. Worldtext asks the next question: what happens when words help generate worlds?

The Shield of Achilles gives this paper its central figure because Homer’s shield already performs this movement before computation. It is not merely a famous ekphrastic object. It is a miniature world model. Homer’s description does not only render an artifact; it organizes a cosmos of war, labor, law, agriculture, dance, weather, sea, and divine making. The shield is made inside the story, for Achilles, yet it contains a world larger than Achilles. It is armor, image, system, and cosmos at once.

That is why the shield matters now. It clarifies what generative AI makes newly technical: the ancient desire for words not merely to picture a world, but to make one available.

## 1. Ekphrasis Becomes Operation

Ekphrasis is often defined as the verbal representation of visual representation. A poem describes a painting. A speaker describes an absent statue. A text brings an image before the mind’s eye. The classic problem is medial: word confronts image, speech confronts vision, temporal language confronts spatial form.

Yet this definition narrows ekphrasis too quickly. Ancient ekphrasis was not only a finished literary object preserved on a page. It belonged to rhetoric, training, delivery, and performance. It was an exercise in vividness, an act of making listeners see what was not physically present. Its force depended on timing, sequence, selection, emphasis, and the social scene of address. Ekphrasis was something done.

Generative AI returns ekphrasis to this operative condition, but with a new engine attached. The prompt is not a finished passage describing an already existing image. It is an instruction that enters a system capable of producing one. The prompt does not merely ask a reader to imagine. It asks a model to generate.

This changes the status of description. Description no longer arrives after the image as commentary, appreciation, or interpretation. It arrives before the image as condition, constraint, invitation, and control surface. The prompt is not a caption placed beneath an image. It is a lever pulled before the image exists. It participates in the image’s making.

This is the basic structure of operative ekphrasis: language becomes executable. The descriptive act enters a technical system and produces a media object. The word no longer stands outside the image and points toward it. The word is already inside the image’s conditions of emergence.

But the image is not the end of the process.

That is the mistake that still traps much discussion of AI image generation. The generated image is treated as the final artifact: an output to evaluate, admire, dismiss, critique, or compare to human art. But anyone who has worked seriously with generative systems knows that the first output is rarely the endpoint. The first image is feedback. It tells the user what the system heard, what it ignored, what it overemphasized, and what it smuggled in from its archive of learned visual habits.

The user then acts again.

The prompt is revised. A constraint is added. A style is removed. A reference image is supplied. The medium changes. The camera angle shifts. The lighting is corrected. The composition is preserved but the world is altered. The image answers; the prompt thickens. The loop continues.

The operative unit is therefore not the single prompt. The operative unit is the loop.

Prompting is a repeated act of pressure on a system that has already absorbed word and image into one unstable field. The user writes. The system makes. The user reads the mismatch. The user rewrites. The system makes again. Prompting is not command followed by obedience. It is probing, correction, cultivation, and response.

This loop relocates ekphrasis from the finished artifact to the conditions of generation. The central question is no longer simply what the image means after it appears. The central question is how the prompt, model, interface, output, and user form a repeatable circuit of production. Ekphrasis becomes procedural.

The prompt is therefore closer to a score than a caption. It can be replayed, varied, interpreted, thickened, and tested. It is not a perfect expression of intention. It is an operational gesture inside a model’s field of possibility.

## 2. Prompting as Performance

Prompting is often mistaken for description because it begins as language. That mistake keeps the analysis trapped at the wrong scale. A prompt does not simply say what an image should contain. A prompt tests what a model can make available, what a platform will default toward, what a system will resist, and what a user must restate, thicken, or redirect.

This is why prompting is better understood as performance than as description. The prompt does not stand outside the image and explain it. The prompt enters a process. It calls something forth, receives an answer, judges the answer, and acts again. The user does not merely write a description; the user performs a sequence of adjustments. Prompting happens in time.

This performative character links operative ekphrasis to the older rhetorical tradition of ekphrastic training. In that tradition, ekphrasis was not primarily a completed literary object. It was an exercise in vividness, delivery, and rhetorical force. It was meant to be performed before an audience and judged by its capacity to make something absent appear.

Twentieth-century literary criticism often emphasized the completed artwork or poem. The ekphrastic passage was treated as a verbal artifact that represented a visual artifact. The emphasis fell on the relation between word and image, or on the rivalry between verbal and visual media. Prompting shifts the emphasis back toward practice. The central question is not only what the image means after it appears. The central question is how prompt, model, user, interface, and output form a repeatable circuit of production.

As prompting has developed into a community of practice, this circuit has become increasingly elaborate. Skilled practitioners rarely rely on a single phrase. Prompts now include stylistic constraints, negative instructions, camera positions, lighting conditions, genre markers, image references, formatting rules, schemas, scripts, and metaprompts. In multimodal systems, images can prompt images, code can organize prompts, and prompts can generate further prompts. The practice has outgrown the simple formula of text-to-image.

Prompting therefore does not merely produce an artifact. It initiates a performance of description, revision, and response. The prompt is not a finished urn. It is an instruction, a performance score, a speculative command.

This matters because the generated image is not a pure translation from intention to output. It is a negotiated result. The model answers according to learned associations, platform constraints, hidden instructions, safety systems, and probabilistic interpolation. A prompt enters that field and returns transformed. The output then becomes evidence for the next act.

Prompting is thus not merely creative or participatory. Those claims are too weak. Prompting shifts ekphrasis away from the finished artifact and toward the conditions of generation. A prompt does not simply evoke an image. It probes a model. It discovers a platform’s defaults. It exposes the distance between what the user says and what the system can operationalize.

There is no final perfect prompt. A prompt can become more exact, more layered, more constrained, or more generative, but it cannot escape the system that interprets it. Prompting remains unfinished because the system remains thick.

## 3. Thick Prompting

This is why “thick prompting” matters.

The phrase deliberately echoes Clifford Geertz’s “thick description,” but the point is not to import anthropology wholesale into prompt engineering. Geertz is useful here because he names a problem of surface insufficiency. A wink is not simply an eyelid moving. It may be a signal, joke, parody, conspiracy, rehearsal, or failed imitation. The visible gesture does not carry its meaning alone. Meaning depends on codes, conventions, histories, situations, and shared forms of life.

Prompting faces a related problem. A prompt does not enter an empty machine. It enters a system already thick with captions, images, genres, metadata, interface defaults, safety constraints, platform habits, technical conventions, and cultural residues. The model does not encounter “ancient Greece,” “Tonalist landscape,” “film noir,” “mother,” “frog,” “shield,” or “world” as pure concepts. It encounters them through sedimented associations: museum photography, children’s book illustration, Hollywood costume, video game armor, academic reconstruction, stock imagery, advertising, memes, captions, and prior generated outputs.

A thin prompt pretends otherwise.

A thin prompt treats the model like a vending machine: insert phrase, receive object. “Make Achilles’ shield.” “Generate a Homeric world.” “Create an ancient battlefield.” The prompt names a subject and trusts the model’s defaults to fill the rest.

But the defaults are never neutral. The model fills silence with what it has learned to expect. It supplies generic gloss, familiar compositions, inherited clichés, dominant visual styles, and platform-friendly compromises. The thin prompt does not produce purity. It releases the default.

A thick prompt does something different. It builds an operating context. It supplies relations, constraints, exclusions, histories, materials, atmospheres, scales, actions, and forms of life. It does not merely name what should appear. It specifies what must be resisted, what must be preserved, and what kind of world would make the image intelligible.

A thin prompt says: “Make Achilles’ shield.”

A thick prompt says: “Make the shield as a worked bronze world being fabricated scene by scene, not as a fantasy emblem. Let the surface hold sky, city, field, law, dance, harvest, war, and Ocean as an ordered cosmos. Avoid heroic poster composition. Treat the shield as a world-bearing artifact whose scenes imply motion, institutions, labor, ritual, and fate.”

The difference is not length. A long prompt can still be thin if it merely piles up adjectives. Thick prompting is not verbosity. It is situated pressure. It gives the model enough organized constraint to move the result away from generic visual habit and toward a specific world of relations.

This is why prompting becomes a practice of cultivation. The prompter does not simply request an image. The prompter builds a working relation with the model. The first result becomes evidence. The user learns what the system is prone to do. The next prompt responds to that tendency. Thick prompting emerges from this back-and-forth.

In this sense, thick prompting is not a theory of perfect control. It is the opposite. It begins from the recognition that the prompt is not sovereign. The visible user prompt is only one operator among many. It acts alongside system prompts, training data, model architecture, safety filters, platform defaults, hidden instructions, interface affordances, and probabilistic tendencies. The user does not command from above. The user negotiates within a system.

Thick prompting names this practical condition: the prompt becomes a method for discovering how much description is required before a system begins to produce the kind of world the user intends.

It is provisional because there is always more to add. It is approximate because the model never gives direct access to its hidden layers. It is iterative because the first result reveals the next instruction. It is performative because meaning emerges through the act.

There is no final perfect prompt. There is only a cultivated result that has been trained far enough to carry the desired world.

## 4. The Shield of Achilles as Worldtext

The Shield of Achilles should not enter this argument as an example. It should enter as the model.

In the plot of the *Iliad*, the shield is functional. Patroclus has died. Achilles must return to battle. He needs new armor. Hephaestus makes the shield so the warrior can re-enter the war. At this level, the shield is equipment. It belongs to the immediate crisis of the story.

But Homer does not describe it as mere equipment. He expands the shield until it contains a whole world.

On the shield are the sky, the constellations, the earth, the sea, the city, the field, the quarrel, the wedding, the dance, the harvest, the vineyard, the herd, the ambush, the war, and the great river Ocean around the rim. The shield does not simply illustrate one scene from the Trojan War. It gathers the conditions of a whole human order. It holds peace and violence, labor and celebration, law and disorder, agriculture and combat, cosmic structure and social life.

The shield is a world model.

This is the crucial point. The Trojan War is the large narrative frame of the *Iliad*, but the shield reverses the scale. The war becomes one event inside a larger order. The poem pauses over an object, and the object opens into a world. Macrocosm and microcosm exchange places. The shield is small enough to be carried by one warrior, but large enough to contain the pattern of existence that gives that warrior’s violence its meaning.

That is why the shield is more than ekphrasis as vivid description. It is also more than imagetext. An imagetext binds word and image. A worldtext binds word, image, space, action, and possible life. The shield does not merely ask the listener to see a picture. It asks the listener to enter an ordered totality.

The scenes on the shield imply movement. They imply time. The wedding procession moves. The dancers turn. The farmers cut. The armies clash. The legal dispute unfolds. The harvest advances. The ocean surrounds. The shield is not a still picture pretending to be alive. It is a compressed environment.

That compression matters because it shows that ekphrastic ambition was never only about visual vividness. The desire was not merely for words to create an image. The deeper desire was for words to summon an order of things. Ekphrasis wanted the image, but the image wanted the world.

The shield makes this visible. Homer does not simply describe a finished object. He describes Hephaestus making it. Scene follows scene. Region joins region. The world accumulates through fabrication. The ekphrasis is therefore constructive as well as representational. The poem does not merely report the shield’s appearance. It performs the shield’s world-making.

This is the hinge between operative ekphrasis and worldtext. Operative ekphrasis explains how language becomes executable in image generation. Worldtext asks what happens when that execution no longer stops at the image.

A worldtext is not a text that decorates a world. It is a text-image-procedure assemblage that helps bring a world into operation.

The shield clarifies that assemblage because it already holds word, image, space, time, action, and order together. It is verbal because we receive it through Homeric description. It is visual because it asks us to imagine an artifact. It is spatial because its scenes are arranged across a surface. It is temporal because those scenes imply unfolding action. It is social because it contains institutions, labor, ritual, marriage, law, and violence. It is cosmological because Ocean bounds the whole.

The shield is not interactive in the computational sense. But structurally it is closer to a world model than to a single image. It gives not only surfaces, but relations. It tells us what kinds of actions can happen, what kinds of institutions exist, what kinds of conflicts matter, and what cosmic boundary makes the world whole.

A world is not a larger picture. A world is a picture with rules, durations, permissions, refusals, and consequences. The shield already knows this. It does not merely ask us to look. It asks us to understand how a world hangs together.

## 5. From Image Model to World Model

The movement from image model to world model changes the stakes of ekphrasis.

An image model turns a prompt into a bounded visual artifact. The user writes a phrase, and the system returns a picture. Even when that picture is complex, cinematic, or richly detailed, it remains primarily a surface. It can imply a world, but it does not yet sustain one.

A world model asks for something more demanding. It must maintain space, time, continuity, objects, affordances, memory, paths, surfaces, events, and possible action. It must not only show what appears. It must support what can happen next.

A picture can show a city.

A video can show someone walking through a city.

A world model must maintain the city as a space in which walking, turning, entering, colliding, remembering, leaving, and returning remain possible.

That is the difference between depiction and state.

This shift changes the prompt. In image generation, the prompt steers appearance: composition, style, subject, lighting, medium, mood, and reference. In world generation, the prompt must begin to steer behavior. It must specify not only what a place looks like but what it allows, what it remembers, how it changes, and what forms of action belong there.

A generated world needs more than appearance. It needs consequence. Without consequence, worldtext is only scenery with ambition.

This is why the phrase “word to image” no longer captures the scale of the problem. The old dream was that the word might become picture. The newer and more dangerous dream is that the word might become world. Immediacy becomes inhabitation. The user no longer asks only to see an image. The user asks to enter a place.

But this dream should not be overstated. Current and emerging world-generation systems remain partial, unstable, constrained, and dependent on hidden scaffolding. They may look like worlds before they behave like worlds. They may offer navigable surfaces without deep social, physical, or narrative coherence. They may provide continuity at the level of camera movement while failing to sustain consequence at the level of action.

That instability is not a reason to abandon worldtext. It is the reason the term is needed.

Worldtext should name the threshold, not the triumph. It marks the point at which prompting begins to move from image production toward environment conditioning. It names the pressure placed on language when the output is no longer only a picture but a stateful world.

Here again, the shield matters. Homer’s shield is not a world because it can be navigated computationally. It is a world because it organizes a totality of relations. It tells us that worldness is not reducible to visual extension. A world requires patterned coexistence: law beside violence, harvest beside war, dance beside labor, sky above field, Ocean around all.

A world model must do something analogous in technical form. It must hold relations together. It must make the environment behave as more than a sequence of attractive surfaces. It must keep the world from dissolving into unrelated images.

This is the passage from image to world: not more pixels, but more relation.

## 6. Hidden Prompts, Buried Laws

The visible prompt is never the whole apparatus.

A user may type, “Make a Homeric world like the Shield of Achilles,” but no system can build such a world from that sentence alone. The prompt appears simple because the machine hides the labor. Beneath the visible instruction are model priors, training distributions, system prompts, safety filters, interface conventions, procedural rules, physics engines, rendering constraints, memory structures, and platform defaults.

The user prompt is the visible petition.

The system prompt is the buried law.

Worldtext must account for both.

This matters because the fantasy of word-to-world can easily become a fantasy of magical verbal sovereignty. The user speaks, and the world appears. The prompt becomes a natural sign. Language finally escapes mediation and touches reality directly.

But generative systems do not fulfill that fantasy. They expose its infrastructure. The world that appears is not produced by language alone. It is produced by language embedded in a technical and symbolic system. The prompt acts through a model. The model acts through inherited data, architecture, filtering, optimization, and interface design.

The prompt does not create *ex nihilo*.

Neither did Homer.

This is where the analogy between Homeric composition and generative AI becomes useful, as long as it remains controlled. Homeric poetry was not computational generation, and a contemporary world model is not oral epic. But both involve inherited materials operating under constraint. Homer draws on formula, meter, mythic memory, performance tradition, narrative convention, and shared cultural knowledge. Hephaestus does not make an abstract object. He makes a world already legible within the epic imagination.

Likewise, the AI model does not create from nothing. It recombines, interpolates, and operationalizes accumulated cultural material. It acts through captions, images, styles, genres, and prior descriptions. Formula is not the enemy of invention. It is the road invention wears down by walking.

This does not make the poet and the model equivalent. The distinction matters. Homer can suffer the constraint. The model cannot. Homeric composition belongs to embodied memory, social performance, tradition, mortality, and human judgment. The model belongs to statistical patterning, technical architecture, platform governance, and computational inference. The point is not identity. The point is structural pressure.

Both reveal that world-making depends on prior worlds.

Worldtext therefore cannot be a theory of pure creation. It must be a theory of mediated constitution. Description becomes world-making only because it acts inside systems that already carry worlds in compressed form. The prompt opens the archive by giving it a symptom.

This is why platform realism enters the argument. A generated world is never only the user’s world. It is the platform’s world as well. Its defaults matter. Its hidden prompts matter. Its safety rules matter. Its aesthetic habits matter. Its training data matter. Its interface matters. The world that appears is a compromise between user intention, model tendency, platform governance, and cultural sediment.

The word becomes world only by passing through machinery.

Worldtext names that passage.

## 7. What Worldtext Lets Us Ask

Worldtext names the point where ekphrasis, prompting, and world modeling begin to diverge from the image-centered vocabulary we have inherited. It gives us a way to study not only what the generated world looks like, but how description becomes one component in a larger world-making apparatus.

This matters because AI images are often evaluated as finished surfaces. They are judged for style, realism, beauty, originality, error, or resemblance. Those questions remain important, but they do not exhaust the problem. If generative systems increasingly produce spaces, scenes, and environments rather than isolated images, then the relevant question changes.

The question is no longer only: What does the image show?

The question becomes: What kind of world has the description authorized?

A worldtext must be read not only for what appears, but for what can happen. Its politics do not appear only in explicit symbols or visible content. They appear in affordances, defaults, blocked actions, navigable paths, remembered objects, missing histories, permitted bodies, simulated physics, repeated styles, and inherited common sense.

The Shield of Achilles provides a pre-digital example of this problem. The shield does not merely present visual content. It presents a world-order. Its scenes matter because they disclose what counts as life inside the Homeric cosmos: marriage, judgment, farming, dancing, fighting, ambushing, harvesting, herding, surrounding, making. The shield’s politics are in its arrangement of possible life.

Generated worlds will have the same problem. Their politics will not appear only in explicit representation. They will appear in what the world allows, what it remembers, what it makes easy, what it makes impossible, what it treats as natural, and what it renders unthinkable.

Worldtext allows humanists to ask these questions without reducing generative AI to either novelty or threat. The task is not to celebrate the machine. The task is not to denounce the machine. The task is not to polish old theory until it looks current. The task is to rebuild the theory around a changed condition: description has become executable.

Ekphrasis can no longer be treated only as a minor literary device for describing art. Prompting can no longer be treated as a shallow user command. AI images can no longer be treated as finished pictures detached from the processes that made them.

The field has to move.

From product to process.

From image to imagetext.

From imagetext to worldtext.

From ekphrasis as description to ekphrasis as operation.

This movement does not discard the old vocabulary. It pressure-tests it. Ekphrasis remains essential because it names the ancient problem of word and image. Imagetext remains essential because it names the compound relation between verbal and visual media. Operative ekphrasis remains essential because it names the new executability of description. Thick prompting remains essential because it names the practical process by which users negotiate with layered models. Worldtext becomes necessary because the image is no longer the terminal object.

The prompt is not innocent.

The image is not final.

The world is not neutral.

---

## Conclusion: Where Ekphrasis No Longer Ends

Ekphrasis once asked whether words could make an audience see. Operative ekphrasis asks what happens when words make images. Worldtext asks the next question: what happens when words help generate worlds?

The Shield of Achilles shows that this ambition was already present at the origin of the ekphrastic tradition. Homer’s shield is not merely an image described by words. It is a world compressed into an artifact and unfolded through performance. It gathers cosmic order, social order, labor, conflict, ritual, agriculture, law, celebration, and violence onto one made surface. It is a shield, but it is also a model of the world that makes the shield meaningful.

Generative AI does not simply revive this tradition. It operationalizes one of its oldest fantasies. The word no longer only asks the listener to imagine the world. The word now begins to participate in making one.

But the fantasy remains unstable. The prompt does not transparently become the world. It enters a model trained on prior images, captions, styles, genres, defaults, and procedures. The resulting world is cumulative, platformed, constrained, and haunted by other descriptions. It is not pure creation. It is sedimented operation.

That is the value of worldtext as a concept. It marks the point where ekphrasis, prompting, and world modeling begin to diverge from the image-centered vocabulary we have inherited. Operative ekphrasis opens the first threshold: description becomes image-making. Thick prompting names the practice of working through that threshold: description becomes iterative pressure. Worldtext names what appears beyond it: not a picture, but a procedural world held together by language, image, memory, interface, model, and constraint.

The strongest ending is not that worldtext is the future. The strongest ending is that the image is no longer where ekphrasis ends.

Human beings once asked language to preserve the world.

Now they ask language to generate one.

The wish has changed less than we think.
