### I. Thesis: Word to World Model Is the Move

The old ekphrastic question—can words make readers see?—has been answered by generative artificial intelligence in the affirmative, and that answer is already obsolete. To pause at this achievement is to misread the computational horizon. The decisive theoretical intervention is not merely to celebrate the word-to-image collapse as a final destination; while Hannes Bajohr (2024) correctly diagnoses that multimodal AI makes the text/image collapse operational rather than metaphorical, image generation is only a way-station. The true conceptual rupture is the shift from word-to-image to *word-to-world-model*, a paradigm where description stops pointing at a represented world and begins constituting its operating conditions.

To grasp this reclassification, we must dismantle a specific critical history. Leo Spitzer’s (1955) and Murray Krieger’s (1992) formalist narrowing of ekphrasis into the description of static, well-wrought urns successfully concealed the form's original operativity under the illusion of the natural sign. When we recover Ruth Webb’s (1999) classical definition, we find that ancient ekphrasis was always operative: its ambition was production, not mere representation. W. J. T. Mitchell’s (1994) *imagetext* brilliantly named the compound that was the goal and nightmare of this narrowed tradition, representing the first-order computational achievement of diffusion models. Yet today, the imagetext is fundamentally exceeded. Following David Ha and Jürgen Schmidhuber’s (2018) foundational architecture, world models are not static visual arrays; they are compressed, spatiotemporal prediction engines. Therefore, *worldtext* is not a rival term for world model. Worldtext names the precise threshold when description becomes an executable interface to a world model. We are no longer reclassifying generative AI from an image-maker to a tool; we are reclassifying it as a world-model operator. The image was never the destination. The world was.

### II. Thick Prompting: Prompting as Performed Thick Description

If the generative system is moving toward an environment rather than an image canvas, user interaction must be theorized not as description, but as iterative performance. Prompting is chronically misunderstood when framed as a single act of declarative captioning. It is, instead, a cybernetic performance waged against a latent space already fatally saturated with cultural codes, default aesthetics, platform safety rails, and statistical clichés. It is here that Clifford Geertz’s (1973) "thick description" ceases to be a decorative anthropological analogy and becomes a structural necessity. The model is already thick; therefore, the prompt must thicken to negotiate its layers.

This dynamic returns ekphrasis to its ancient pedagogical roots in the *progymnasmata*. As Quintilian (*Inst. Or.* 6.2) insisted, generation must begin first in the orator's imagination—but the prompt is now performed before a generative neural network rather than a human audience. This performativity, which Renate Brosch (2018) identified as nascent in pre-AI digital ekphrasis, is now fully operationalized. As tracked in emergent promptcraft taxonomies (Liu & Chilton, 2023; Oppenlaender, 2024; Lindley & Whitham, 2025), the operative unit of generative media is the loop: write, generate, read the system's misinterpretation, revise, regenerate. A thin prompt—*“Make Achilles’ shield”*—treats the latent space as a vending machine, yielding only a generic statistical average. A thick prompt—*“Make the shield as a worked object, scene by scene, with sky, city, field, dance, war, law, harvest, and ocean as living order”*—does not succeed through mere verbosity. It succeeds by applying situated pressure, forcing the system out of generalized representation and into specific world-logic. Within this loop, the generated visual output is stripped of its status as a product. The image is not the product. The image is feedback. Thick prompting is the labor of cultivation, not command.

### III. Shield as Worldtext: Shield as World Model Not Image

To map this shift from surface depiction to state space, Homer’s Shield of Achilles (*Iliad* 18.478–608) must not enter the argument as a famous classical example. It must enter as the structural model. The shield forged by Hephaestus does not depict a frozen scene; it specifies a world. Its concentric zones legislate a complete operating environment: cosmology at the center, human governance and ecological labor in the middle rings, and the River Ocean at the rim—the absolute boundary beyond which the world-model possesses no data. As Oliver Taplin (1980) observed, the shield reverses the macro/microcosm relation: the Trojan War is merely an event inside the world of the shield, not the world inside the war. Following Albert Lord (1960), the oral-formulaic tradition was itself a generative system, producing probabilistic variants rather than identical copies. And as Lessing (1766) analyzed centuries ago, Homer avoids static description by rendering process: making unfolding in time. The shield is a constitution grown on metal.

This ancient structural analysis maps flawlessly onto Yann LeCun’s (2022) blueprint for machine intelligence and the emergent state-space architectures of platforms like DeepMind’s Genie 2 (Bruce et al., 2024) and World Labs’ Marble. The critical theoretical distinction is no longer between an image and a bigger image, but between an image and a world. Image generation asks: *what can a sentence make visible?* World generation asks: *what can a sentence make habitable?* Image models produce imagetexts; world models produce worldtexts. The shield already stages this threshold from depiction to state. Its scenes imply movement, duration, social procedure, conflict, and cosmic boundary. Yet in this transition to worldtext, the prompt is no longer sovereign. It is one operator among many, collaborating with hidden system prompts, training distributions, and procedural physics engines. The ancient shield was a world-system carried by a warrior. The computational world model is a shield the user can enter.

### IV. Conclusion: Door Opens Where Ekphrasis Exceeds Image

Ekphrasis once asked whether words could make an audience see. Operative ekphrasis asks what happens when words make images. Worldtext asks the subsequent, destabilizing question: what happens when words help generate explorable worlds? This inquiry must not resolve into a neat triumphalism of AI world-creation, nor should it claim that worldtext has arrived as a perfected, stabilized theory. Instead, it names the precise threshold at which the inherited vocabularies of visual culture and literary theory become insufficient. Operative ekphrasis opens the door, thick prompting is the practice of working through it, and worldtext names the unstable reality that begins to appear on the other side.

The shield of Achilles proves that the ambition to compress an entire functioning cosmos into an artifact and unfold it through performance was always present. Generative AI does not fulfill that ancient ambition; it simply operationalizes one of literature's oldest fantasies through brute statistical force. And as we cross this threshold, the fantasy remains radically unstable—platformed, cumulative, and assembled from the scraped sediment of billions of other human descriptions. *Worldtext* is therefore a threshold concept. It marks the point at which operative ekphrasis exhausts the surface of the image and breaches the state-space of the environment. As generative technologies advance, the concepts forced together in this moment of convergence—operative ekphrasis, thick prompting, imagetext, worldtext—will inevitably diverge. We stop at the threshold not because there is nothing left to say, but because the generative system is still unfolding. The ultimate theoretical intervention is the sober recognition that the image is simply no longer where ekphrasis ends. Human beings once asked language to preserve the world. Now they ask language to generate one. The wish has changed less than we think.
