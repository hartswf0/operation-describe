## Core Thesis

Lindhe argues that the modern (post-Heffernan) definition of ekphrasis — "verbal representation of visual representation" — amputates the body. The ancient definition emphasized **enargeia as a full-body experience**: orality, immediacy, tactility. Digital interfaces restore this bodily dimension because they require **touch** (digitalis = finger). She proposes a **"digital ekphrasis"** that returns to the ancient emphasis on multisensory engagement.

## Key Arguments

### 1. The Fingerprint is in the Name
"Digitalis" means "of the finger." The digital is etymologically tactile. Digital ekphrasis recovers the embodied, haptic dimension that print culture suppressed. Marinetti's futurist aesthetics anticipated this: "A visual sense is born in the fingertips."

### 2. Two Definitions of Ekphrasis
- **Ancient (Webb)**: A speech that brings the subject vividly before the eyes — emphasis on *effect* (enargeia), any subject, orality, live performance
- **Modern (Heffernan)**: Verbal representation of visual representation — emphasis on *referent* (artworks), written text, visual only

Lindhe argues the ancient definition is MORE relevant to digital culture than the modern one.

### 3. The Active Feedback Loop
Digital ekphrasis shifts emphasis from "representing a visual object with words" to "the user's bodily interaction with an environment." Words become a **"quasi-physical force"** acting on the user through the device interface. The reader is "inscribed in the structure through the active feedback loop."

### 4. Webb's Electrical Charge
Lindhe highlights Webb's key passage: ekphrasis assumes "live interactions between speaker and audience, with language passing like an electrical charge between them." This describes not just ancient rhetoric but also the **real-time, iterative prompt-response loop** of AI interaction.

### 5. From Measurement to Mediation
Digital culture recovers what mechanical measurement destroyed: the body as a measuring instrument. Paterson (2007) shows that pre-modern measurement was multisensory and embodied. Digital interfaces (touchscreens, gesture control) return to this bodily epistemology.

## Bridge Connections

### → AI Interaction (Bridge 18: Digital Ekphrasis = Prompt-Response Loop)
Lindhe's "active feedback loop" between user and digital interface IS the prompt-response loop of AI interaction:
- Both require **touch** (keyboard, screen, voice as physical input)
- Both are **iterative** (the user adjusts based on system response)
- Both produce **enargeia** through multisensory engagement
- Both position the user as an **active participant**, not passive receiver

### → Floridi's Distant Writing
Lindhe (2013) describes the same meta-author from the opposite direction: where Floridi sees the author becoming a designer, Lindhe sees the reader becoming a performer. Both describe the **dissolution of passive reception** into active, bodily co-creation.

### → Embodiment in World Models
The "visual sense born in the fingertips" maps onto multimodal AI systems where visual generation responds to textual touch: the prompt IS the fingertip touching the latent space.
