## Citation

Jansson, Bo G. "Ekphrasis and Digital Media." *Poetics Today* 39, no. 2 (2018): 299–318.

## Core Argument

Through the case of Swedish writer Lotta Lotass's digital tetralogy (*Redwood*, *Hemvist*, *Kraftverk*), Jansson demonstrates that digital ekphrasis breaks the simultaneous/consecutive binary that has organized intermedial theory since Varga (1989).

### The Simultaneity Problem

Classical theory posits that word and image can appear either:
- **Simultaneously** (as in emblems, comics — you see both at once)
- **Consecutively** (as in ekphrasis of an absent painting — you read the poem, then imagine the painting)

Lotass's digital work presents them **simultaneously AND consecutively**: the photograph and text appear side-by-side on the same digital page (simultaneous), but they were produced at different times and can be read in different orders (consecutive).

> "The image is present on two different ontological levels at the same time: as verbal ekphrastic re-presentation and as digitally remediated iconic/indexical sign." — Jansson 2018, p. 305

### The Yacobi Liberation

Following Yacobi (2013), Jansson argues that when the image is copresent, the poet is **freed from descriptive obligation**: "No longer constrained to specify and integrate any details of the artwork — they're all there, in the visual component — the poet can turn the image into 'a point of departure.'"

This is precisely what happens in the post-Google age (Brosch 2018): the artist is free to *think from* rather than *describe* the image.

### The Lotass Case: Remediated Postcards

Lotass's digital tetralogy uses **picture postcards** as visual sources — a doubly remediated medium (photograph → postcard → digital scan). The text responses range from factual descriptions to quotations from scientific literature to fragments of Swedish poetry. There is no single genre: the ekphrasis is a **constellation of genres**.
