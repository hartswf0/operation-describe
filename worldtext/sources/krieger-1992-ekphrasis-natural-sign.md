## Citation

Krieger, Murray. *Ekphrasis: The Illusion of the Natural Sign*. Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press, 1992.

## Core Argument

Krieger's book-length study traces the "ekphrastic principle" through Western criticism: the desire of words to achieve the spatial, visual, and immediate presence of pictures. His central claim: ekphrasis is **both miracle and mirage** — a miracle because temporal language seems to freeze into spatial vision, a mirage because the frozen picture is only an illusion words produce.

> "What is being described in ekphrasis is both a miracle and a mirage: a miracle because a sequence of actions filled with befores and afters such as language alone can trace seems frozen into an instant's vision, but a mirage because only the illusion of such an impossible picture can be suggested by the poem's words." (Foreword)

## Key Concepts

### The Ekphrastic Principle
Krieger extends ekphrasis beyond the narrow genre (description of art objects) to a **general principle**: the tendency of all literary language to aspire toward spatial form, visual presence, and the condition of the natural sign.

### Natural Sign vs. Arbitrary Sign
The core tension: words are arbitrary signs (Saussure), but poetry yearns for them to function as **natural signs** — visual substitutes for their referents. This yearning is the "illusion" of the title.

### Space vs. Time
Krieger revisits Lessing's *Laocoon* partition: spatial arts (painting, sculpture) vs. temporal arts (poetry, music). Ekphrasis is the moment when temporal art tries to become spatial — to "freeze" movement into stillness.

### The Shield as Palladium
The Shield of Achilles is both physical protection and symbolic icon. Krieger reads it as a figure for art itself: "idol of the gods and the constructed sign that summons their protection." The shield bears civilization on its face — all of Greek culture compressed into a single visual field.
