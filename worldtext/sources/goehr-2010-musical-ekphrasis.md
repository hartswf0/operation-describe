## Citation

Goehr, Lydia. "How to Do More with Words. Two Views of (Musical) Ekphrasis." *British Journal of Aesthetics* 50, no. 4 (2010): 389–410.

## Core Argument

Goehr reads ekphrasis through the myth of **Apollo and Marsyas** — the musical contest in which Marsyas's Dionysian pipe is defeated by Apollo's lyre, and Marsyas is flayed alive. Her central claim: ekphrasis is not merely a literary genre but a **system of artistic self-defense** born from the threat of censorship.

> "One art comes to another's aid in order to aid itself." (p. 392)

### Two Views of Ekphrasis

1. **Description**: The traditional view — ekphrasis describes a visual work. Goehr traces this from Wittgenstein through Hegel, showing description has its own philosophical weight.

2. **Rescue**: The hidden view — ekphrasis **rescues** silenced arts by giving them voice. Philostratus's description of the Marsyas painting lets us *hear* the pipe that Apollo silenced. The verbal art preserves the musical art from extinction.

### Musical Ekphrasis

Goehr extends ekphrasis to **music**, showing that the visual/verbal binary obscures a third art: **sound**. The Marsyas tale is about music first, painting second, words third — but ekphrasis treats it as a verbal response to a visual image of a musical event. The full intermedial chain is: music → painting → text → reader's imagination of the music.
