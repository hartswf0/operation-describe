## Citation

Webb, Ruth. "Ekphrasis ancient and modern: The invention of a genre." *Word & Image* 15, no. 1 (1999): 7–18. DOI: 10.1080/02666286.1999.10443970

## Core Argument

Webb traces the etymological and historical development of "ekphrasis" to demonstrate that the modern restricted definition ("verbal representation of visual representation") is a **mid-twentieth-century invention**, not an ancient category. The ancient rhetorical definition was radically different: ekphrasis was **any vivid speech that brings the subject before the mind's eye** — including battles, persons, places, times, and events.

## Key Claims

### 1. Ancient Ekphrasis ≠ Modern Ekphrasis
The *Progymnasmata* (Theon, Hermogenes, Aphthonios, Nikolaos) define ekphrasis as:

> "A speech which leads one around (*periegematikos*), bringing the subject matter vividly (*enargos*) before the eyes."

Subject matter included persons, places, times, **and actions** — not specifically art objects. The Shield of Achilles was classified under "manner of making" (tropos), not "art objects."

### 2. Enargeia as the Core
**Enargeia** (vividness), not subject matter, defines ancient ekphrasis. The orator transforms listeners into spectators through vivid language — a performative, audience-centered act. This connects directly to:
- Quintilian's *Institutio Oratoria* 6.2.29–32 on imagination and emotion
- Ancient memory theory: images derived from sense experience, stored and re-activated by language

### 3. The Modern "Invention"
The restriction to "description of art objects" developed through:
- **Matz 1867**: first association of ekphrasis with Philostratos's *Imagines*
- **Bertrand & Bougot 1881**: grouped ekphrasis with French *transpositions d'art* tradition
- **Spitzer 1955**: coined "the poetic description of a pictorial or sculptural work of art" — the definition most scholars now use
- **Hagstrum 1958**: established the "sister arts" tradition

> "Now, the term had been thoroughly removed from its ancient meaning and context. Instead of designating a dynamic mode of writing thought to have an immediate impact on its audience... 'ekphrasis' came to be used of a work of literature in which the movement was found only in the flow of language."

### 4. Barthes's Quiet Edit
Webb catches Barthes silently replacing the ancient category of "actions/events" (*pragmata*) with "works of art" in *L'effet du réel* — a foundational critical maneuver that masks the ancient breadth.

## Position in the Paragone

Webb does NOT take sides in the hope/fear dialectic. Instead, she historicizes the entire debate, showing that the paragone tension (word vs. image competition) is a **modern projection** onto an ancient practice that had no such anxiety.
