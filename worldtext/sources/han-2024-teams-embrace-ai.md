## Core Thesis

Han et al. (CHI '24) study what happens when **teams** (not individuals) co-prompt generative AI for creative design tasks. They find two dominant strategies: **story-first** prompting (describe the narrative, then generate visuals) and **visual-first** prompting (generate imagery, then construct narrative around it). GenAI helped teams build consensus but was perceived as an "efficient tool" rather than a true collaborator. Human partners reduced reliance on AI rather than increasing it.

## Key Arguments

### 1. Two Co-Prompting Strategies
- **Story-first**: Teams discuss narrative/theme → write descriptive prompts → generate imagery from language. This is the ekphrastic direction: verbal → visual.
- **Visual-first**: Teams generate images first → discuss what they see → build narrative from generated outputs. This inverts ekphrasis: visual → verbal.

Both strategies coexist within single sessions, often alternating.

### 2. GenAI as Consensus Tool
Remarkably, the AI served as a **shared reference point** for teams that could not otherwise agree. Generated images provided concrete artifacts around which disagreements could crystallize and resolve. The AI output was a "boundary object" (Star & Griesemer 1989) — shared enough to enable communication across different knowledge domains.

### 3. Tool, Not Collaborator
Despite co-creating with AI, participants consistently perceived the AI as an instrument, not a creative partner. The tool metaphor maps onto the ekphrastic tradition where the painting is an object to be described, not an agent contributing to the description.

### 4. Human Partners > AI Partners
Teams that had strong human collaboration actually used AI LESS. Human-human communication was more efficient than human-AI prompting for creative tasks. The AI was most useful when human expertise was insufficient — exactly the "expanded feasibility" pattern Floridi identifies.

## Bridge Connections

### → Ekphrastic Workshop (Bridge 19: Co-Prompting = Ekphrastic Performance)
Han's two strategies replicate the two directions of classical ekphrasis:
- **Story-first** = descriptive ekphrasis (Theon/Aphthonius): start with words, conjure images
- **Visual-first** = responsive ekphrasis (Philostratus's *Imagines*): start with images, generate words

The team's alternation between these modes = the paragone oscillation between word and image.

### → Webb's Live Performance
Han's finding that prompting is a **live, social performance** maps directly onto Webb's emphasis that ekphrasis was never a solitary written exercise but a rhetorical act performed before an audience. The team IS the audience.

### → Boundary Objects = Pictorial Third
The generated images that serve as "boundary objects" for team consensus = Louvel's pictorial third. Both are emergent artifacts that exist in the gap between verbal intention and visual realization. Neither the prompt alone nor the image alone produces the meaning; it arises in the interaction.
