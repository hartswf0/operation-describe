## Citation

Kennedy, David. "Ink & After: Reading between the Lines." *Poetics Today* 39, no. 2 (2018): 319–336.

## Core Argument

Kennedy surveys the landscape of intermedial typologies (Rajewsky, Wolf, Schröter) to argue that contemporary creative practice has **outrun** the critical vocabulary. Poets, novelists, and artists are producing works that exceed established medium-categories, and the typologies struggle to keep up.

### Key Frameworks Surveyed

**Rajewsky (2005)**: three categories — intermedial, intramedial, transmedial
**Wolf (2005)**: four variants — transmediality, intermedial transposition, intermedial references, plurimediality
**Schröter (2012)**: four discursive types — synthetic, formal/transmedial, transformational, ontological

### Schröter's Political Reading

Kennedy highlights Schröter's explicitly political typology:
- **"Good" intermediality** = breakdown of capitalist division of labor
- **"Bad" intermediality** = capitulation to spectacle culture, loss of artistic autonomy
- **"Ontomediality"** = the recognition (via Mitchell) that "there are no visual media" — all media are always already mixed

### The Gender Gap

> "What is missing from all these typologies is gender." — Kennedy 2018

Eva Hesse and Lygia Clark were exploring intermediality before the theorists named it. Clark's **"fantasmatic"** — "something not reducible to visibility... experiencing something without a real or identifiable referent" — anticipates Krieger's illusion of the natural sign and Bajohr's artificial semantics.

### Intersemiotic Translation

Kennedy links intermediality to Jakobson's (1959) intersemiotic translation and Aguiar/Queiroz's refinement: "a semiotic process is not a bi-lateral relation, bi-univocal, between two entities." Source and target are **multilayered systems**, and intersemiotic translation involves selecting and re-hierarchizing layers.
