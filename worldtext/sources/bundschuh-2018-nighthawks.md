## Citation

Bundschuh, Jessica. "Edward Hopper's *Nighthawks*, 1942: The Ekphrastic Poet's Collective Diner." *Poetics Today* 39, no. 2 (2018): 313–336.

## Core Argument

In the Internet age, ekphrasis must respond to a readership that already experiences image and text in **copresence** — they don't need poetry to show them a painting they can Google. So what is ekphrasis *for* now?

Bundschuh argues, through Ann Lauterbach's found-poem "Edward Hopper's Way," that contemporary ekphrasis creates a **space of intimacy within alienation**. Hopper's diner is an image of isolation; Lauterbach's poem turns that isolation into a shared experience — the reader enters the diner. Ekphrasis becomes not description but **inhabitation**.

### Key Claims

1. **Post-Google Ekphrasis**: In a network culture where images circulate freely, ekphrasis shifts from *showing* (you can't see this painting, so I'll describe it) to *dwelling* (let's inhabit this image together).
2. **Found Poetry as Ekphrastic Method**: Lauterbach's use of found materials (newspaper fragments, overheard speech) as ekphrastic material extends the genre beyond traditional description.
3. **The Diner as Commons**: Hopper's diner — the most reproduced American painting — becomes the "collective diner" where ekphrastic poets meet, disagree, and create.
