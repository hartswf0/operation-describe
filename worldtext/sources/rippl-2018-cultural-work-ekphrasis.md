## Citation

Rippl, Gabriele. "The Cultural Work of Ekphrasis in Anglophone Transcultural Fiction." *Poetics Today* 39, no. 2 (2018): 265–298.

## Core Argument

Ekphrasis in contemporary transcultural fiction (Teju Cole, Taiye Selasi, NoViolet Bulawayo) is not a formal exercise in word/image relations but a **cultural-critical practice** that negotiates identity, memory, trauma, and the colonial gaze.

### Four Expansions of Ekphrasis

Rippl identifies four ways the concept must expand:
1. **Objects**: no longer exclusively works of art (photographs, postcards, digital images, commercial objects)
2. **Representation**: replace with "re-presentation," "evocation," or "intermedial quotation" (Yacobi, Rippl)
3. **Paradigm**: move beyond the semiotic into wider cultural-critical frames (via Rajewsky, Wolf)
4. **Media**: language is no longer the only ekphrastic medium (opera, drama, film)

### Stenographic Ekphrasis

Contemporary practice often uses "relatively short, stenographic ekphrases that are mere references and nonrepresentational traces instead of fully fledged descriptions." This is **ekphrasis-by-allusion** — a brief verbal gesture toward an image that relies on the reader's already-existing knowledge (the Google condition).

### The Cultural-Critical Function

> "Ekphrasis as a social practice... 'transfers into the realm of literary art subliminated versions of our ambivalence about social others.'" — Mitchell 1992:702, via Rippl

Rippl demonstrates this through:
- **Teju Cole's *Open City***: The protagonist's flâneur walks through New York describing artworks, but the descriptions are always refracted through postcolonial consciousness
- **Taiye Selasi's *Ghana Must Go***: Ekphrasis of fabric, architecture, and domestic objects as markers of diasporic identity
- **NoViolet Bulawayo's *We Need New Names***: Visual description as a mode of bearing witness

### The Thick Description Connection

Rippl's "cultural work of ekphrasis" IS thick description in the Geertzian sense. Both:
- Attend to the **social context** of the described object
- Refuse the thin/formal reading (this painting looks like X) in favor of the thick/cultural reading (this painting means Y to these people in this situation)
- Operate at the intersection of aesthetics and politics

## Non-Obvious Connection: Stenographic Ekphrasis and Prompt Engineering

Rippl's "stenographic ekphrasis" — brief verbal gestures that trigger visual imagination through allusion — is structurally identical to **compressed prompts** in TTI systems. A museum guide who says "think of a Rothko" performs stenographic ekphrasis; a prompt engineer who writes "in the style of Rothko" performs the same operation through a machine interface.

Both depend on **shared memory** (cultural or statistical): the allusion only works if the receiver has the reference. The Four Strata model applies: stenographic ekphrasis is a Stratum III (defamiliarization) technique that anticipates Stratum IV (operative generation).
