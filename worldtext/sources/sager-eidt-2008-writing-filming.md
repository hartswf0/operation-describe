## Citation

Sager Eidt, Laura M. *Writing and Filming the Painting: Ekphrasis in Literature and Film.* Amsterdam: Brill, 2008.

## Core Argument

Film has always had a relationship with painting that mirrors the literary ekphrastic tradition, but **no one has called it ekphrasis**. Sager Eidt extends the definition: if ekphrasis is the representation of visual representation, then film depicting paintings IS filmic ekphrasis.

### The Film Extension

Literary ekphrasis: text → converts painting into words  
Filmic ekphrasis: film → converts painting into moving images

Both involve a **change of medium**: the painting enters a new representational system (literary/cinematic) that alters its reception. The camera does to painting what language does: it selects, frames, sequences, and narrates.

### Critic's "Ekphrastic Fear" in Film

Sager Eidt coins **"ekphrastic fear" applied to film**: critics reject film's ability to "do justice" to artworks, accusing cinema of fragmenting paintings through editing and camera movement. This mirrors the ancient fear that words betray images — but now the fear is that *moving* images betray *still* ones.

### The Triad

Sager Eidt's most important structural contribution: the **painting–novel–film triad**. When a painting is described in a novel, and that novel is filmed, the painting undergoes **double ekphrasis**:

1. Painting → Novel (literary ekphrasis)
2. Novel → Film (filmic adaptation)
3. Film may show the actual painting → which the audience knows through the novel → which they know through the painting

This creates a chain of medial translations: each step introduces new "perspectival montages" (Kafalenos/Sternberg).

### The Temporal Problem

Sager Eidt builds on Lessing: painting is spatial (perceived all at once), poetry is temporal (perceived sequentially). Film resolves this by being **BOTH** — spatial (each frame) AND temporal (the sequence). This is exactly what video generation models must solve: converting a static text prompt into a spatiotemporal output.
