## Citation

Brosch, Renate. "Ekphrasis in the Digital Age: Responses to Image." *Poetics Today* 39, no. 2 (2018): 225–243.

## Core Argument

Brosch addresses the **Google paradox**: Bolter (1996) and Ryan (2001) predicted ekphrasis would become redundant in the digital age. Instead, it proliferated. Why?

Because ekphrasis shifted from **representation** to **defamiliarization**:

> "Something in ekphrasis must still speak to its recipients in a way that reproductions do not." (p. 229)

### Key Claims

1. **Performative over Mimetic**: Brosch proposes the definition Bajohr later adopts: "ekphrasis is a literary response to a visual image... emphasizing the performative instead of the mimetic." This is the hinge on which Bajohr's "operative ekphrasis" turns.

2. **Embodied Cognitive Response**: Ekphrasis works through "an embodied cognitive reaction (where the 'cognitive' includes not only conscious awareness, but emotional and pre-verbal states)." This anticipates Mathieson's body thesis.

3. **Interactivity**: Digital media require the viewer/user to act. This changes ekphrasis from contemplation to participation. Gaming, VR, and interactive poetry all demand a new account of the word/image relation.

4. **Convergence Culture**: Jenkins's (2008) concept of "convergence" — producers and users interact in unpredictable ways — applies to ekphrasis too. Ekphrastic texts now circulate, morph, and recombine in network culture.

## Position in the Cosmos

Brosch is the **hinge figure** between the classical ekphrastic tradition and Bajohr's AI extension. Her performative definition is the conceptual license Bajohr uses to extend ekphrasis from "literary response to image" to "computational operation correlating text and image."
