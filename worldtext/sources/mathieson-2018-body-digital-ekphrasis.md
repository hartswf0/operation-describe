## Citation

Mathieson, Jolene. "The Body and the Possible Soul in Digital Ekphrastic Poetry." *Poetics Today* 39, no. 2 (2018): 359–378. DOI: 10.1215/03335372-4324505

## Core Argument

Digital poetry creates **immersive, embodied environments** that activate a "holistically embodied and dialogical experience" in the reader — and this embodied engagement transforms ekphrasis from a representational exercise into a **somatic event**.

### Key Claims

1. **Digital Ekphrasis as Sui Generis**: Digital poetry has a "sui generis ability to create highly particularized and immersive environments capable of activating embodied, dialogical experiences" — it is not just text about images but text-as-body-in-image-space.

2. **Beyond Remediation**: Digital ekphrasis doesn't just *remediate* art; it "critically reflects on the nature of the digital image and its relations to material ontology." The poems meditate on code, on computer-mediated poetry, on images, and on human-computer interaction.

3. **Metaphysical Tone**: Close readings of Deena Larsen's "Carving in Possibilities" (2001) and Harry Giles's "Photo of Maud Wagner" (2013) demonstrate a "willfully metaphysical" character — digital ekphrasis asks about the *being* of digital objects.

4. **Dynamic Heterarchy**: Instead of a hierarchy (text→image or image→text), digital ekphrasis creates a "dynamic heterarchy" where text, image, code, and embodied experience interpenetrate without fixed priority.
