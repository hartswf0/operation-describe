## Core Thesis

Floridi introduces **"distant writing"** as the inverse of Moretti's distant reading. Where distant reading uses computation to *analyze* large corpora, distant writing uses computation (LLMs) to *generate* new texts. The author becomes a **"meta-author"** — a designer who specifies requirements, constraints, and parameters but does not directly produce the text.

## Key Arguments

### 1. The Meta-Author as Designer
The traditional author is **both architect and executor**. Distant writing **decouples** these functions: the human designs (prompts), the LLM executes (generates). This parallels architecture (Bernini designed the Colonnade but never placed a column), fashion, industrial design, and Raphael's workshop practice.

### 2. Isotropy of Narrative Space
Distant writing reveals that narrative space is **isotropic** — equally workable in any direction, not having "natural progressions." Any starting point can reach any endpoint if coherence is maintained. This is constructivist (paths are created, not discovered).

### 3. Feasibility, Not Efficiency
Like distant reading, distant writing is about **expanded feasibility** — enabling narratives that would be impossible or impractical through close writing. Using it merely for efficiency misses its unique value.

### 4. Death of the Author → Birth of the Designer
Barthes's "death of the author" is followed by the **"birth of the author as designer"** who initiates, supervises, and is responsible for collaborative wrAIting. The meta-author is their own commissioner — no Pope Julius II needed.

### 5. Style as Prompt Parameter
Floridi analyzes how authorial style can be decomposed into specifiable parameters: vocabulary, imagery, sentence structure, pacing. These become **design constraints** in the prompt — making explicit what was implicit in craft.

## Bridge Connections

### → Ekphrasis (Bridge 17: Distant Writing = Digital Ekphrasis)
Floridi's "meta-author as designer of verbal instructions for visual generation" IS the ekphrastic poet, formalized. The ekphrastic poet describes a painting; the meta-author designs a prompt. Both:
- Specify visual/narrative requirements through verbal means
- Accept imperfect execution as constitutive (the pictorial third / the generation gap)
- Operate at a DISTANCE from the artifact they produce
- Are judged by the quality of their verbal specifications (enargeia / prompt quality)

**Critical insight**: Floridi's "distant writing" and Lindhe's "digital ekphrasis" are the same phenomenon named from opposite directions. Floridi approaches from AI/philosophy; Lindhe from literary theory. Neither cites the other.

### → World Models (Ha & Schmidhuber)
Floridi's "isotropy of narrative space" maps onto the world model's latent space: both posit a space of possibilities that can be navigated in any direction. The meta-author's prompt = the world model's conditioning vector.

### → Progymnasmata (Webb 1999)
The ancient rhetorical curriculum that taught ekphrasis as a **designable skill** (not innate talent) prefigures Floridi's meta-author. The progymnasmata student learns to specify verbal requirements for mental image generation — the original "prompt engineering curriculum."

### → Paragone (Goehr 2010)
Floridi's decoupling of designer/executor maps onto the paragone's word/image competition: the designer works in words, the executor produces in another medium. The competition is now between human design intent and AI execution fidelity.
