## Core Thesis

Webb's book-length treatment argues that the modern restriction of ekphrasis to "verbal representation of visual representation" is a radical impoverishment of the concept. In antiquity, ekphrasis was defined purely by its **effect** — bringing the subject vividly before the eyes (enargeia) — not by its referent. Any subject (battles, storms, people, places, objects) could be ekphrastic. The concept was inseparable from **live oral performance**, **phantasia** (controlled imagination), and **persuasion**.

## Key Contributions Beyond the 1999 Paper

### 1. Phantasia as Generative Mechanism
Webb's definitive account of Quintilian's phantasia: the orator generates internal **phantasiai** (mental images of absent things) through a trained, controllable process, then transmits them to the audience via enargeia. Quintilian calls someone skilled at this `euphantasiōtos` — "good at imagining."

**Critical for our cosmos**: This describes the architecture of generative AI in ancient vocabulary. The latent space IS the gallery of the mind. Phantasiai ARE latent vectors. The prompt IS the conditioning signal for controlled phantasia.

### 2. The Gallery of the Mind (Chapter 5)
Webb's analysis of memory, imagination, and spatial storage of mental images connects directly to:
- Method of loci (memory palace) = vector database
- Stored phantasiai = trained model weights
- Retrieval through cue = prompt-based generation

### 3. Enargeia Is Not Description
Webb proves that enargeia (vivid speech) is NOT the same as description. Description is a formal category (what linguists call "descriptio"). Enargeia is an **effect** — it can be achieved through narration, exposition, or any speech act. The key criterion is: does the audience feel present at the scene?

**Critical for our cosmos**: This means prompt engineering is not primarily about accurate description of the desired output. It is about achieving the EFFECT of vivid generation — which may require indirect, suggestive, or atmospheric language rather than literal specification. This explains why the best prompts are often not the most descriptive.

### 4. Persuasion, Not Representation
In antiquity, ekphrasis was a tool of PERSUASION. The orator used vivid speech to make the audience "virtual witnesses" who felt they had experienced the events themselves. The goal was not aesthetic appreciation but **behavioral change** — swaying the verdict, motivating action, shaping belief.

**Critical for our cosmos**: This maps onto AI alignment. The prompt engineer is not merely generating pretty pictures; they are using verbal specifications to STEER the model's behavior. The alignment problem = the persuasion problem: how do you ensure the audience (AI) does what you intend?

### 5. Armisen's Missing Link
Webb cites Mireille Armisen (1980): "rhetorical phantasia cancels out this intermediary [language] and takes the form of a new mental image which is the echo, in the listener's mind, of the initial image conceived by the orator." Language is CONSUMED in the generation process — it is the fuel, not the product. This describes the transformer's attention mechanism: the prompt tokens are consumed in generating the output tokens.
