Heinz von Foerster (1911–2002) was an Austrian-American physicist and philosopher who founded second-order cybernetics — the cybernetics of observing systems. His decisive contribution: the observer is inside the system they observe. There is no view from nowhere. Every description of a system is also a description of the describer.

## Key Contributions

- **Second-order cybernetics** — first-order cybernetics studies observed systems (thermostats, machines, feedback loops). Second-order cybernetics studies observing systems — it asks how the observer's own apparatus shapes what they can see.
- **"The observer enters the description"** — the act of modeling a system includes the modeler as a variable. This makes objectivity a relation rather than a position.
- **Ethical imperative** — "Act always so as to increase the number of choices." Not a rule but a principle: expand rather than contract the possibility space.
- **Non-trivial machines** — a trivial machine produces deterministic outputs from inputs. A non-trivial machine changes state through use. Humans are non-trivial machines. AI systems are trained (non-trivial in formation) but deployed as if they were trivial (expected to produce consistent outputs).

## Relevance to This Cosmos

Von Foerster's observer principle is why the worldtext cannot be a neutral description of the world. The act of building the worldtext changes the builder. The lint protocol, the atlas, the syntheses — these are not mirrors held up to a pre-existing intellectual landscape. They are instruments that shape the landscape they claim to describe. The descriptive struggle IS the observer entering the description.
