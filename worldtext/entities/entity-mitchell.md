W. J. T. Mitchell (b. 1942) is a literary and art theorist whose work reconfigured the field of ekphrasis and word-image studies.

## Key Contributions

- **Picture Theory** (1994) — Introduced the framework of ekphrastic **hope** (the desire for the verbal to make the visual present), **fear** (the anxiety that the image might overcome the word), and **indifference** (the recognition that the two media are fundamentally different). This triad replaced the simple paragone model.
- **"Ekphrasis and the Other"** (1994) — The chapter that became the single most cited text in ekphrastic theory after Heffernan's definition.
- **What Do Pictures Want?** (2005) — Extended image theory into cultural studies, treating images as agents with desires. Influential for rhetorical and cultural approaches to ekphrasis.
- The neologism "**imagetext**" — a compound word that deliberately refuses the slash between "image" and "text."

## Position in Cosmological Debates

Mitchell occupies a mediating position: he neither reduces ekphrasis to the paragone (like Heffernan) nor dissolves it into intermediality (like Clüver). His framework emphasizes the **cultural anxiety** that word-image relations generate, rather than their ontological status.
