Lygia Clark (1920–1988) was a Brazilian artist who dismantled the boundary between artwork and viewer. Her "Bichos" (Creatures, 1960s) were hinged metal sculptures that existed only through manipulation — the work was not the object but the act of handling it. Her later "Relational Objects" (1970s-80s) were stones, shells, plastic bags, and fabric used in therapeutic sessions where participants explored bodily sensation.

## Key Contributions

- **The death of spectation** — Clark eliminated the passive viewer. Her works required bodily participation to exist. A folded "Bicho" sitting untouched is not yet a work of art.
- **Art as relational proposition** — the artwork proposes a relationship between body and material. The meaning is the encounter, not the object.
- **The sensory line** — Clark's move from geometric abstraction to embodied encounter traces the same journey this cosmos maps: from representation (image as thing to look at) to operation (image as thing that happens to the body).

## Relevance to This Cosmos

Clark is the patron saint of the "operative" in operative ekphrasis. Her work proves that an artifact can be genuine only when it demands something of the person who encounters it. A Lygia Clark piece you photograph but don't touch is a dead artifact. A worldtext synthesis you read but don't struggle with is the same thing.
