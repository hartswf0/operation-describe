James A. W. Heffernan is the author of the most-cited definition in ekphrastic theory: **"the verbal representation of visual representation"** (1993).

## Key Contributions

- **Museum of Words: The Poetics of Ekphrasis from Homer to Ashbery** (1993/2004) — The first book-length study of ekphrasis. Argues that ekphrasis stages a "battle for mastery" between image and word. Traces the tradition from Homer through Virgil, Dante, Romantic poetry, and postmodern American poetry.
- The definition "verbal representation of visual representation" — became the standard reference point, even as it was increasingly contested (by Clüver, Webb, Brosch, Bajohr).

## Position in Cosmological Debates

Heffernan's definition is **narrow and paragonal**: it restricts ekphrasis to verbal media describing visual representations. This excludes:
- Non-representational painting and sculpture (Clüver's objection)
- Architecture (Clüver's objection)
- Music, performance, and digital media (Brosch's objection)
- Operative ekphrasis where description *is* generation (Bajohr's objection)

The Heffernan definition functions as the **negative reference point** around which the entire galaxy of ekphrastic worlds orbits. Every subsequent theorist defines themselves in relation to it.
