Ink & After:
Ekphrasis, Intermediality, Intersemiosis, and the
Collaborative Chains of Thomas Ingmire
David Kennedy
University of Hull
Abstract
First, I survey ideas of translation, intersemiosis, and intermediality draw-
ing on the work of European scholars. These include Roman Jakobson and his 1959
definition of intersemiosis as “an interpretation of verbal signs by means of non-verbal
sign systems” ( Jakobson 2000 [1959]: 261). I also draw on “Towards a Model of Inter-
semiotic Translation,” by Daniella Aguiar and Joao Queiroz, who suggest the concept
of “creative sign transmutation” (2009). Second, I discuss the collaborations of the
US calligraphic artist and book illuminator Thomas Ingmire. Inviting US and UK
poets to collaborate with him by writing poems in response to sheets of calligraphic
marks, Ingmire then produces highly artistic, calligraphic versions of the poems. In the
example I discuss, the poem was then produced as an artist’s book by a Japanese artist
using ancient paper-folding and bookmaking techniques. Finally, a Japanese composer
produced a musical composition in response to the poem and the artist’s book. It could
be argued that poetry written in response to calligraphic marks is at the very limits of
both mimesis and ekphrasis. However, I argue that it is precisely at such limits that
intermediality begins to happen. The article concludes with David Annwn’s proposal
of new categories of ekphrasis.
Keywords
intermediality, intersemiotic, calligraphy

1. Ekphrasis, Intermediality, and Intersemiosis: Cultural “Turns” and
Cultural Politics
Before moving on to the main focus of this article—the work of American
calligrapher Thomas Ingmire and his collaborators—I want to establish
what might be termed a context of consideration for his work and collabo-
rations. In the following pages, I outline a number of typologies of interme-
diality and a model of intersemiotic translation. It is not my intention to
suggest that the works in Ingmire’s collaborative chain can or should be
measured exactly within any or all of these theoretical frameworks. Instead,
I want to give readers the opportunity to reflect on the cultural moment in
which Ingmire’s work is taking place. There then follows a brief discussion
of a recent example of literary translation that reveals what is at stake when
a creative transfer takes place between image and word, between two
languages, and between two different poetics. I want to suggest that this
example belongs to the same cultural moment as Ingmire’s collaborations.
I bring ekphrasis into the discussion because, in the context of cultural
“turns,” it is my contention that we are living through an “ekphrastic turn” as
much as an “intermedial” one. Ekphrasis has become a “hot” topic. There

In recent years a number of wide-ranging studies have reimagined ekphrasis:
Stephen Cheeke’s Writing for Art: The Aesthetics of Ekphrasis (2008); Elizabeth
Bergman Loizeaux’s Twentieth-Century Poetry and the Visual Arts (2008); Liliane

interest? First, ekphrasis is the intersemiotic and interdisciplinary subgenre
par excellence, and therefore chimes with the wider intersemiotic and inter-
cultural moment as material moves back and forth across and between cul-
tures and languages. Second, the upsurge of interest in ekphrasis is, I would
hazard, almost nostalgic. Ekphrasis appears to confirm that word can indeed
explain and mediate image when, in fact, recent events tell us that it is often
the other way round. The images from Guantanamo Bay, Abu Ghraib, and
Wikileaks show us a “truth” or a “reality” that is not present in the official
accounts of the war in Iraq and of the so-called war on terror. Finally, a
question: is the upsurge of interest in ekphrasis symptomatic of an “after
theory” moment in literary studies because ekphrasis seems to invite and
promise relatively unproblematic, conservative, and largely descriptive read-
ings? Indeed, as I have noted elsewhere, large areas of ekphrastic criticism
seem to rely on reproducing ideas of art and representation as if modernism
and postmodernism never happened (see Kennedy 2012). Intermediality,
intersemiotics, and translation theory provide us with ways of writing about
ekphrasis that do not swerve away from its contemporary complexities.
Werner Wolf (2011: 3) has argued that we are living through an “inter-
medial turn” whose key concepts are mediality and intermediality. At the
same time, Jens Schro¨ter invokes Rosalind Kraus’s argument that we are in
a “post-medium condition,” and goes on to quote Lev Manovich on the
dilution of the concept of medium: “although the concept of medium has
become obsolete to some extent, it continues to be used from ‘sheer inertia.’”
This, he contends, is because it is difficult to replace it with new conceptual
systems, “so rather than getting rid of media typology altogether, we keep
adding more and more categories” (Manovich 2001). The very spread and
extension of the concept of medium to ever more objects, phenomena, and
ensembles, and the accompanying uncertainty of how properly to define
“medium” can be interpreted as part of the problem, which, as Manovich
makes very clear, is particularly acute for art (Schro¨ter 2010: 109).

Whether we embrace this or critique it, our response is, Schro¨ter reminds
us, necessarily political. Intermediality and/or the “post-medium condition”
is either “good” or “bad” (ibid.: 117–18). “Good” intermediality exemplifies
the breakdown of the capitalist division of labor. In contrast, nonintermedial
artforms are “monomedia” that “reproduce in their ‘specifics’ the very divi-
sion of labour that needs to be overcome in post-capitalist societies” (ibid.:
118). “Bad” intermediality represents and performs “a capitulation of art to
capitalist spectacle culture. Its ‘autonomy,’ if this refers to the area of special
competencies circumscribed by medial reflexivity and purity, i.e., ‘self-
definition,’ is lost” (ibid.: 112).
There is no doubt, however, that works of art and literature are being
produced that exceed, wholly or in part, the traditional categorizations.
Gabriele Rippl (2015: 1) notes “the sheer number of Anglophone literary
texts which participate in intermedial interfaces” (e.g., Salman Rushdie’s The
Moor’s Last Sigh [1995] and Siri Hustvedt’s What I Loved [2003]), and goes on to
argue that any discussion of art forms has to take place “against the back-
drop of their medial networks, what Bernd Herzogenrath calls their ‘arch-
intermediality’” (ibid.: 1). Such a view explains recent attempts at typologies
of intermediality. If we cannot come up with new medium names, we can at
least find new ways of talking about new works. The best known typology
has been formulated by Irina Rajewsky (2005), who proposes three cate-
gories: “intermedial” (works that have to do with crossing borders between
media); “intramedial” (works that do not involve crossing media boundaries);
and “transmedial” (the appearance of a motif or a style across a variety of
different media). Wolf (2005: 253–55) has proposed a four-part typology to
address intermediality’s principal phenomena or variants. He begins with
“transmediality,” which denotes things that are not specific to a particular
medium but appear across a range of media. Second is “intermedial trans-
position,” which refers to elements being transferred from one medium to
another, for example, Stoppard’s play about Pink Floyd’s classic album.
Third is “intermedial relations/references,” which describes what happens
when “two or more media are . . . present in a given semiotic entity,” for
example, an ekphrastic poem. Finally, there is “multi- or plurimediality,”
which denotes a combination of media, for example, a ballet or a radio
dramatization.
A different type of typology has been proposed by Schro¨ter (2012: 16) who
is less interested in defining intermediality than in describing “what ways of
talking about intermediality, in a most general sense, there are.” His typology
is also in four parts. “Synthetic intermediality” denotes a “fusion of several
media into a new medium—the intermedium—that supposedly is more
than the sum of its parts” (ibid.). He gives as examples 1960s “happenings”

What is missing from all these typologies is gender. Indeed, it is arguable
that women artists were exploring intermediality long before the theorists.
One can point to the work of Eva Hesse and the Brazilian artist Lygia Clark.
The concept of the “fantasmatic,” coined by Clark seems particularly rel-
evant to intermediality. It has been glossed by the art historian Susan Best as
“something that is not reducible to visibility . . . a term that covers the sense of
experiencing something without a real or identifiable referent” (Best 2014:
140). Clark’s conception of the work of art as a living organism or a quasi-
body, which is neither painting nor sculpture and which requires the viewer
to become a participant and handle it also seems highly pertinent (see Best
2014: 47–66).
Intermediality is closely related to intersemiosis and to intersemiotic trans-
lation. Roman Jakobson’s definition of intersemiosis in his 1959 paper “On
Linguistic Aspects of Translation” is, we might remind ourselves, quite
specific. Although he refers to the “transmutation of signs,” he defines inter-
semiosis as “an interpretation of verbal signs by means of non-verbal sign
systems” ( Jakobson 2000 [1959]: 261). This might serve in an account of, say,
a film adaptation of a novel, but it could seem limited and limiting in other
contexts. However, it is now accepted critical practice to take a more expan-
sive view of intersemiosis and develop Jakobson’s emphasis on what he terms
an “extra-linguistic horizon.” So, for example, we can sketch a chain of
intersemiotic translations involving Mallarme´’s poem “L’apre`s-midi d’un
faun” as a source, and Debussy’s music, Fokine’s choreography, and Nijin-
sky’s performance as multiple targets.
In their paper “Towards a Model of Intersemiotic Translation,” Daniella
Aguiar and Joao Queiroz (2009: 3) suggest the concept of “creative sign
transmutation.” They draw on the work of Dinda Gorle´e who argues that
intersemiosis is the “‘reconstruction’ of an artwork into a distinctive semiotic
system, creating a sophisticated collection of interconnected signs.” The
emphasis on “reconstruction” and “creation” seems to suggest that it is the

The most useful aspect of Aguiar and Queiroz’s paper is perhaps their
assertion (2009: 4) that “Emphatically, a semiotic process is not a bi-lateral
relation, bi-univocal, between two entities.” They go on to argue that in both
intersemiotics and translation, we need to reconceptualize source and target
as multilayered or multilevel systems. This means that “a translation is a
relation between multi-layered processes, and an intersemiotic translation
(IT) can be described as a multi-hierarchical process of relation between
semi-independent layers of descriptions” (ibid.).
To unpack this a little, the hierarchical arrangement of elements can
change in the translation/transition from source to target. To quote Aguiar
and Queiroz (ibid.) again, “certain layers have their relevant properties
selected and translated into new materials and processes.” They go on to
note that “for example, from literature to dance, linguistic layers (rhythmic,
prosodic, syntactic, or psychological ambience) are translated into dynamic
movement, organization of space, light design, costumes, scenography, etc.
Notably, a ‘mapping of correlations’ cannot be easily established between
layers of different nature (different semiotic systems)” (ibid.). This presents a
challenge to both the translator and the critic because, if a translation results
in very different materials and structures, “how is it possible to compare
semiotics of source and target?” (ibid.).
Changes to the hierarchical arrangement of elements seem particularly
relevant to the long-established precursor of both intermediality and inter-
semiosis that is ekphrasis. It is certainly the case that, post-Auden at least, the
impulse of ekphrastic poetry has been essentially deconstructionist, using ele-
ments of the source work against itself to produce the target poem. Or we
might say, using Aguiar and Queiroz’s terminology, that ekphrasis is an act
and process of relayering.
2. Farre´s’s Hopper, Venuti’s Farre´s: An Intermedial and Intersemiotic Example
The difficulties of comparing the semiotics of source and target can be seen in
a recent example of literary translation: Catalan poet Ernest Farre´s’s book
Hopper, translated by Lawrence Venuti and published by Carcanet in 2010.
Farre´s writes poems in response to fifty-one of Edward Hopper’s paintings,
including all the well-known ones such as Nighthawks (1942). There are, of
course, a lot of things that one could say about Hopper’s paintings—for
example, how the very few exceptional paintings derive their disturbing

This goes to the heart of Hopper’s enduring fascination: we look into the
paintings expecting narrative but what we often get are the urban spaces of
twentieth-century American capitalism, spaces that are empty to emphasize
that they are inhospitable. What we get are characters self-consciously trying
to fit themselves into public spaces where no one belongs. What we get is a
voyeurism of despair. This makes it all the more surprising that Farre´s chose
to write most of his poems in the form of monologues and, in the case of

what Venuti tells us about his translation. He says that Farre´s’s poems “com-

poetical archaisms, academic and technical jargons, foreign loan words. He
frequently includes the idiomatic expressions in which Catalan is abundant,
as well as cliche´s from elite and popular culture” (Venuti 2010: introduction,
xvii).
In one sense, that sounds exactly how we expect contemporary poetry to
be. Venuti tells us that he has tried “to match this heterogeneity.” However,
he adds that “the English of my translations goes much further.” Venuti says
he decided to translate the poems into “an American vernacular” and that he
added into them phrases taken from Hopper’s own writings and the writings
of his wife Josephine Nivison Hopper. He identifies these phrases in notes to
the poems—otherwise, we would have to know a lot about Hopper to spot
them, a job made doubly hard by the fact that Hopper didn’t say much about
the evolution or meaning of his paintings. So, throughout the translated
volume, Hopper is speaking out of his paintings and therefore about them in
ways that he never did in life.
Now, in one sense there is no problem with this if we remember that, when
pressed to explain what he was after in one late painting, Hopper famously
said “I’m after me” (in Ford 2004: 114), and if we remember that his wife
Josephine modeled for most of the women he painted. But Venuti seems
to have magnified the dubious engagement of Farre´s’s poems. The chatty
monologues simply fall short of the challenge of Hopper’s work in ways
that the poems of Auden and Ashbery do not in relation to Breughel and
Parmigianino. Indeed, Farre´s’s most successful poems seem to be those where

If we return to Farre´s and Hopper, the poems domesticate the paintings

are not. If we really could step into the world of Hopper’s paintings, it would
be terrifying—women with botched makeup that makes them look like

in this context, is utopian because it needs to imagine that art is a place where
we might be able to live—or, rather, it can barely cope with art’s unhome-

us that we must leave the museum “by closing time. You can’t live there”
(Ashbery 1987: 207). Art never stops being unhomely.

as transpositions of them. Venuti wants to give us a version of a twentieth-

identified as the creation of doppelga¨nger avatars in a range of ekphrastic
writing. Farre´s can also be said to have produced avatars to represent Hop-
per’s paintings.
3. The Collaborative Chains of Thomas Ingmire
Representations or reconstructions of semiotic and/or textual identities into
distinctive systems brings me to the main subject of this article: the collabo-
rations of Thomas Ingmire. In 2011, the American calligrapher and book
illuminator, Thomas Ingmire, invited a number of poets interested in visual
poetics to work with him on a collaborative word and image project. Ingmire,
born in 1942, originally trained as a landscape architect and has been work-
ing as a calligrapher and book illuminator for the past thirty years. He has
been involved in a number of projects, most notably the St. John’s Bible.
The poets Ingmire chose were from the United Kingdom, United States,
Philippines, and Singapore and included Alan Halsey, Jack Hirschman,

Ingmire wrote to the British artist and poet Christine Kennedy as follows:

A number of images are attached to this letter. They are spontaneous marks
made while listening to music. If it is possible, can you align one of your poems
to any of these images? I am not looking for anything particular in this; the only
connection that is important is the one that you make. Alternatively, if your time
permits and you have the interest, your response could be the writing of a new
poem. . . . The key for me is having the connection between the visual image and
the poem. My intention then will be to create a calligraphic visual/textural
response to your work. (Personal letter to Christine Kennedy, by permission
from C. Kennedy)
Two things are of immediate interest here: first, the idea of a calligrapher
making “spontaneous marks”; second, Ingmire’s use of the term “calligraphic
translation.” The Greek roots of “calligraphy” are kallo6 (beauty) and
grawή (writing), and calligraphic practice can be defined as the art of giving
form to signs in an expressive, harmonious and skilful manner. It is interesting
to note here that the contemporary Tunisian artist and calligrapher Nja
Mahdaoui considers himself both an explorer of signs and a choreographer
of letters (see his personal website). Calligraphy takes the letter and the word
and turns it into something else. What is “carried across”—to return to the
literal meaning of translation for a moment—is not meaning per se.
Calligraphy is not, then, about writing, and it is not necessarily about com-
munication of meaning. Indeed, there is an increasing body of calligraphic
art in English that is as hard for an English-speaker to read as examples of

Timothy Donaldson would be a good example of the latter (see Bilston
Gallery website). For the calligrapher or calligraphic artist, the work is, at
least in part, the record of the performative movement of the body, the
hand, and the pen. This movement is, in turn, an exploration of line and
space: both the combination of line and space that makes up letters and the
form of the letter and the space of the page. In the words of calligrapher
Brody Neuenschwander (best known for his calligraphic work in the Peter
Greenaway film The Pillow Book [1997]), “where typography seeks balance,
clarity and optimal legibility, calligraphy seeks expression and a dynamic
composition of black and white elements” and looks for “the perfect marriage
of verbal and visual content” (see Neuenschwander’s website). To gloss this a
little, the finished calligraphic work is a record of the expenditure of energy in
physical movement, and the perfect marriage of the verbal and the visual
may be a matter of taste as well as a species of possible-impossibility. In this
sense, the work of contemporary calligraphers like Ingmire, Donaldson, and
Neuenschwander is always already intermedial. Their work enacts a per-
petual flickering between signifier and signified, between language that sig-
nifies something and an image that alludes both to language and something
beyond it.
Neuenschwander, like many late twentieth and early twenty-first century
calligraphers, has been greatly influenced by the work of German calligra-
pher and theorist Hans-Joachim Burgert (1928–2009). Burgert’s theoretical
writing emphasizes the “visualization of the word,” and that “calligraphy
is concerned with the creation of a sequence and arrangement of forms.”
(see Neuenschwander’s website). What calligraphy produces is closer to
non-Western letter forms and writing traditions. For example, Chinese
and Japanese characters are made up of strokes and flicks suited to being
rendered by the brush, while Western capitals are straight and angular
because they were originally chiseled on monuments. Similarly, lowercase
letters are more flowing because they were written with quill pens. In some
aspects, calligraphy seems to be a process where Western forms are produced
using Eastern techniques. In the words of the Ukrainian calligrapher Aleksei
Chekal, “calligraphy is a step forward into the past or a stroke back into the
future!” (Calligraphy Expo website).

collaboration between Kennedy and Ingmire. As I have already indicated,

characters and, in some cases, the text produced were then processed using
Microsoft Word’s spellcheck facility.
These two processes served to interpret the calligraphic image into lan-
guage, and Kennedy ended up with many suggestions for vocabulary from
which she was able to derive a pool of English language words to work with
in composing a poem. Kennedy e-mailed Ingmire, “I think of this sort of
compositional method as a form of, or related to, a technological divination,”
and she used the word “oracular” to describe the process (e-mail Kennedy to
Ingmire, November 13, 2012). The software processes enabled Kennedy to
find, in her words, a “temperament” within the pages that Ingmire sent.
Can we decide on the nature of Ingmire’s making of marks while listening
to music? Can Kennedy’s scanning and spellchecking be called ekphrasis? To
answer the first question, we need to decide whether Ingmire’s marks are just
marks or something else. For the purposes of this discussion, we can call them
“art signs,” as in certain bodies of twentieth-century American art, where
there is a well-established practice of using calligraphy. For example, Mark
Tobey (1890–1976) overlaid white or light-colored calligraphic-type marks
and symbols onto abstract color fields. Similarly, one can point to the black
marks and signs in the work of Franz Kline (1910–62) and to the black and
white paintings, Calligraphic Studies and Octavio Paz Suite of Robert Motherwell
(1915–91). In answer to the second question, it seems clear that Kennedy
came up with a new way of making an ekphrasis. One wonders why no one
had thought before to scan an image and read it as text.
The pool of English language words Kennedy came up with was then
subjected to cut-up procedures. The text produced suggested to Kennedy the
idea of tayu, a Japanese word meaning the one who sits with the musician in
traditional Japanese theater and tells a story, but also the predecessors of the
geisha. Tayu arts included poetic composition and calligraphy as well as
dance, singing, musicianship, and tea ceremony. Once again, we can see
calligraphy pulling Western artists and writers toward the East.

The next process was the making of the lettering into a book. Ingmire

Matsunaga’s book of Ingmire’s calligraphic version of Kennedy’s poem

his poem” (e-mail to the author, August 27, 2013).

his sheets of calligraphic marks cannot perhaps be called strictly ekphrastic,
intermedial, or intersemiotic and yet it touches, at certain points, on one or all
of those categories. At first sight, each new work in the chain appears to be
discrete within its own medium, but its creation relied on a process of inter-
action that eludes easy categorization. Crucially, each new work in the chain
