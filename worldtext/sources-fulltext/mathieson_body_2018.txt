The Body and the Possible Soul in
Digital Ekphrastic Poetry
Jolene Mathieson
University of Hamburg
Abstract
New media technologies have inaugurated the most recent and important
shift in our understanding of word-image relations. While seminal studies of new media
poetry (Funkhouser 2012; Hayles 2006, 2008) and its potentially ekphrastic properties
(Lindhe´ 2013) have been published in recent years, the close relationship between
digital poetry and ekphrasis has yet to be convincingly theorized. This article focuses
on new media poetry and the digital strategies it utilizes in the ekphrastic remediation
of art images. Digital media may be unique in its ability to create highly immersive
environments that more readily activate a holistically embodied and dialogical experi-
ence for the reader in represencing images, but digital ekphrastic praxis also exploits its
multimodal, polysemiotic possibilities to critically reflect on the nature of the digital
image and its relations to material ontology. This critique, and its emphasis on both
representational and experiential reasoning, primarily takes the form of lyrical med-
itations on the material and immaterial realities of code, of computer-mediated poetry,
of images, and of human-computer interaction. The result of these poetic meditations
is a type of ekphrasis that is willfully metaphysical in tone and content, as will be
evidenced via a close reading of two digital ekphrastic poems: Deena Larsen’s “Carving
in Possibilities” (2001) and Harry Giles’s “Photo of Maud Wagner” (2013).
Keywords
digital poetry, intermediation, metaphysics, dynamic heterarchy, digital
ekphrasis

Sui Generis: Digital Poetry, Multimodality, and Image Production
Digital poetry is endowed with a generous degree of intermedial playfulness
and genericambivalence. Itssetting is dynamicand ambient. And itpossessesa
sui generis ability to create highly particularized and immersive environments
capable of activating embodied, dialogical experiences for the reader—all of
which points to digital poetry’s potential as an especially poignant and fertile
medium for assessing and remediating the infinitely reproducible and mal-
leable digital art image. The extent to which the digital regime tends to be
conceptualized, structured, and experienced in primarily visual terms has
been a focal point for crucial debates in new media studies (see Bolter and
Grusin 1999: 23; Hansen 2006; Mitchell 2015), and further signals the
importance of visual technologies as viable technogenic agents of sociobio-
logical meaning that actively shape how humans interact and coevolve with
images (see Hayles 2012). Additionally, the ubiquity of digital graphics, pic-
torial media, and online image archives and hosting platforms highlights the
necessity for a digital lyrical form capable of mediating contemporary aes-
thetic consciousness. As such, a digital poetry of ekphrasis seems not only
intuitively compelling but even axiomatic or tautological. The close relation-
ship between digital poetry and ekphrasis, however, remains theoretically
elusive despite the recent publication of seminal studies of new media poetry
(Funkhouser 2012; Hayles 2006, 2008) and its potentially ekphrastic proper-
ties (Lindhe´ 2013). There are several complex, interconnected reasons for
this, the most fundamental being a question of genre and the messy theo-
retical consequences that so often accompany definition and classification.
Digital poetry is predicated on code and computation for its creation,
displayed on electronic screens for its consumption, and, crucially, comprised
of elements from linguistic, audio, gestural, spatial, and visual semiotic modes
for its signification. Practitioners and theorists of digital poetry are careful to
foreground its literariness by pointing to its origins in concrete and visual
poetry, found poetry, automatic writing, and other experimental forms of the
late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, as well as oral and slam poetry
(see Funkhouser 2008; Wardrip-Fruin 2008). Yet born of computer exper-
imentation and genetically related to performance, computer, and interac-
tive media art (see Block et al. 2004: 13–17), digital poetry’s raison d’eˆtre is to
realize technically the more abstract premises of its literary predecessors.
A digital poem is a “poem” because its literary impetus is to poetically exploit
the disparate spaces between linguistic and audio, gestural, spatial, and visual
semiotic systems. However, by including video files, sound files, and/or gen-
erative and interactive features, it is also a multimodal, interstitial event that
can be felt, heard, seen, and navigated (see Funkhouser 2008).

This high degree of polysemy, multimodality, and sensory richness is what
renders digital poetry so uniquely seductive in the context of ekphrasis, but
also so problematic. Ekphrasis, a notoriously slippery term in it its own right,
is a mode of poetics that is also intermedial, but regardless whether it is
understood in its ancient or more contemporary senses, it invariably func-
tions via the medium of language. The single feature common to all its
competing definitions and chaotic results is its reliance on indexing verbal or
lexemic units to produce sensuous effects of representation or of image-
making within the mind of the reader. What distinguishes a digital ekphrasis
from its more traditional relatives in rhetoric and print is that it can and
does exploit a type of visuality based on generating and mapping images
external to the mind. The function of ekphrasis within the digital regime is
thus dependent on an individual poem’s operating procedures and lyrical
strategies for configuring and arbitrating its verbally produced endogenous
images and externally generated digital images. The ekphrastic quality
of a digital poem is contingent on the specific ways in which such proce-
dures, which necessarily operate on both a representational and a visceral-
experiential level, make literally and metaphorically visible images that are
not there and make absent those that are present. As I show in the following,
the complex, multilevel relations between absence and presence, between
the physical and the transcendent, between agent and actant are metaphys-
ical concepts that a digital poetry of ekphrasis will need to address at
the level of content but which will also be made manifest at the material
level.1 Acknowledging the relative obscurity and tricky conceptualization of
a digital poetry of ekphrasis, I first attempt to conceptually or theoretically
link digital poetry with ekphrasis, and in a second step, I offer a reading of
two poems, Deena Larsen’s “Carving in Possibilities” (2001) and Harry
Giles’s “Photo of Maud Wagner” (2013). These two poems are compelling
examples of a contemporary material practice that is currently exploring
and utilizing a number of polysemiotic strands to recreate, reappropriate, or
represent the visual art image in a verbalized, digital form, and can be
understood as digital ekphrasis.

Arguing that “some types of thought are better served by expressive resources

the visual, graphic, or plastic object, is never made visible or tangible except by way
of the medium of language. One might call ekphrasis a form of nesting without
touching or suturing, a kind of action-at-distance between two rigorously sepa-
rated sensory and semiotic tracks, one that requires completion in the mind of
the reader. This is why poetry remains the most subtle, agile master-medium of
the sensus communis, no matter how many spectacular multimedia inventions are
devised to assault our collective sensibilities. (Mitchell 2015: 132)
As a “multimedia invention,” digital poetry would thus be excluded from
the ekphrastic enterprise. But as an interstitial form nestled in the often
dissonant spaces between modalities, it can and frequently does distend and
augment the gaps intervening among the haptic, the aural, the seen, and the
read. The suturing ambitions or abilities of television and other multimedia
assumed by Mitchell will not be applicable in a blanket fashion to all or even
most digital poetry. Digital poetry is a highly heterogeneous amalgam of
forms, methods, and media that include but are not limited to VR poems
and other types of interactive poetry, hypertext poetry, video poetry, digital
sound poetry, kinetic and animated poetry, code poetry, poetry generators,
and other fringe and hybrid forms (Funkhouser 2008: n.p.). Further, as
Talan Memmott aptly observes, digital poetics resists taxonomy, thanks to
what he calls “the transitive aspects of its elements, and the transactive
quality of its applications” (2006: 304); thus to speak meaningfully of digital
poetry in the context of ekphrasis requires a vigilant reading of individual
case studies carefully positioned in relation to each other.
The “transitive” and “transactive” features mentioned by Memmott are
what lend the genre its vigor and hybridity. But they also indirectly reference
the digital poem’s ontological nature as a code-object, as that which is ac-
cessed via a user’s interface with an electronic screen but is not permanently
recorded or inscribed by the material artifacts presenting it. Hidden or

random but deterministic inputs and outputs from hardware, software, and

time as its own object but also as its own performative event. In Electronic
Literature: New Horizons for the Literary, N. Katherine Hayles patiently and
luminously describes how this emergent event results from what she terms
“dynamic heterarchy” or multilevel complexity comprised of “second-,
third-, and n-level emergences” that move both horizontally and vertically
to “continuously inform and mutually determine each other” (2008: 45). This
multilevel complexity is, not surprisingly, a feature of the cognizant, embod-
ied user. But it is also, crucially, a feature of the computer-mediated text. The
computer-mediated text is characterized by Hayles (drawing on Strickland
2006) as (1) multimodal; (2) “layered” through “cascading processes of inter-
pretation” on the part of language tags, programming languages, codes, and
strings, much of which is looping, often recursive, and invisible to the user
when generating the text seen on screen; and (3) a realization of “fractured
temporality,” denoting the degree to which mechanical hardware, computer
settings, and mouse function affect loading times and other temporal features
that “fracture time” in idiosyncratic, agentic ways (ibid.: 163–64).
The entangled nexus constituted by the dynamic interactions of the
embodied user and the computer-mediated text, then, forms the next level
of complexity, which Hayles labels “intermediation” (ibid.: 51–52). The con-
cept of intermediation stresses the material agency of code objects and
computer systems as well as of the human body, so that meaning-making
capabilities are equally distributed as feedback and feedforward loops.
Hayles’s notion of intermediation thus effectively mitigates Mark B. Hansen’s
subordination of technology to the body in his highly influential work on
immersion and embodiment—key concepts in much recent theorizing on
ekphrasis, especially in the field of neuroaesthetics (see Starr 2013). Hansen
censures digital technology’s privileging of ocular modes of visualization as
well as its uncanny ability to mimic or create image-objects of simulacra in
which representation and referent collapse together so completely that such
objects are no longer representable but merely haunting manifestations of
the hyperreal’s horror vacui, or what he calls “the referential void” (Hansen
2004a: 600). In light of the subsistent nature of the digital, that is its sense of
being without existing, Hansen sees a rigorously phenomenological account
of embodiment as the only possibility for rehabilitating meaning in human-
computer interaction (see Hansen 2004b; Hayles 2008: 102–13, 179–86).
Hayles’s critique of this position rightly denounces its readiness to ignore the

the mind of the listener but also taught the listener how to imaginatively and
visually engage with a text and to be visually and emotionally transported to
the scene being described. As Webb theorizes, in ekphrasis, “words do not
directly represent their subjects, but are attached to a mental representation
of that subject” (ibid.: 128), which means that rhetoricians conceived of
ekphrasis as a dialogic in which the mind of the listener is an active agent
in the process of receiving, composing, and engaging with mental images
activated via the speaker’s agentic use of rhetorical strategies, mimetic cues,
and semantic frames (ibid.: 18–22). The dialogic here, though, is a human-
to-human interaction in which both participants are agents but also actants
within the visualization process, where the visualized object is an individu-
ated, subjective, emergent realization that in each instantiation necessarily
differs. Linking the dialogical nature of ekphrasis to digital media would, it
seems to me, thus require Hayles’s concept of intermediation as that which
logically, both metaphorically and literally, corresponds to the complex,
idiosyncratic agency-actant relationship between systems and participants.
As Richard Powers so passionately argued in “Seeming and Being,” his essay
on the impossible paradox of virtual reality, “the more advanced the media,
the higher the level of mediation” (2000: n.p.). In other words, while imme-
diacy and immersion may give the impression of a reality that is directly
accessed, it is produced via mediation procedures that rely on representable
structures as well as structures of representation. The more advanced the
media, the more it relies on such structures and the more it relies on inter-
mediation. The beauty of conceptualizing a digital ekphrasis in light of
intermediation is that on various levels and to varying degrees it would
robustly accommodate rhetorical ekphrasis and its emphasis on immersion
as theorized by Webb (2009), but also ekphrasis understood as representa-
tion and meta-representation, as theorized by James Heffernan (1993) and
Mitchell (1986, 1994). Because of the dynamic heterarchy of digital poetry,
immersion via visualization and the representation of visual media are
inextricably linked. It is not that they are indistinguishable, but that they
are invariably entangled.
A poetics of digital ekphrasis would thus be a type of poetics aware of and
able to account for both the processes of visualization as well as their infinite
desire for and embodiment of the representation of visual media and vice
versa. It would be a type of poetics able to endorse both the cultural or
metaphorical power of the living image as well as the technical agency of the
enlivened digital image. It would also be a poetics deft at pursuing its mul-
timodal, polysemiotic possibilities to critically reflect on and maybe even
subvert the nature of the (de-)materialized digital image and its relation to

Deena Larsen’s now canonical “Carving in Possibilities” (2001) is a time-
based hypertext poem comprised of written text, as well as visual, audio,
animated, and kinetic elements. These elements are compressed into a
Shockwave Flash file, which means that the user cannot access the poem’s
source code and that its specific formats remain hermetic. The opening
sequence of the poem displays a blurred, pixelated, close-up image of Michel-
angelo’s colossal statue of David housed in the Academia Gallery Museum
in Florence, onto which the title “Carving in possibilities” is superimposed.
Discretely placed under the title as a full sentence are the instructions “Mouse
slowly to carve out your existence.” Located to the user’s right is a button with

The poem’s title page is accompanied by a musical theme meant to invoke
the eerie soundtracks of 1950s sci-fi films. It disappears after fourteen seconds
but remains a ghostly, vaguely visual presence in the mind of the user.
Through the progressive aspect of the present tense used in the title, the
imperative voice in the remaining text fragments, and the haunting qualities
of its soundtrack, this title page frames the user’s participation as one of active
responsibility. While the music is threatening in tone, the linguistic text
directs and firmly coaxes the user to enter its portal. Clicking on the start
button allows the user to metaphorically enter the gates of Florence and
access what is the same pixelated, close-up image of David accompanied
by the same soundtrack, but now overlayed with invisible hotspots that are
then triggered upon mouseover. Each hotspot activates three simultaneous
but different semiotic modes or responses:
1. A single color-coded line of embedded or hidden poetic text emerges
and remains as long as the pointer hovers, as seen in figures 3 and 4. In
total, it would appear that there exist upwards of fifteen different color
and typeface combinations varying in point scales that loosely denote
bundles of thematic content and/or speaker situations. It is sometimes
the case that the pointer will cover the text upon mouseover, rendering
it difficult to read.
2. A sharp, 8–bit sound connotative of both gunfire and the striking of
a hammer on marble is heard. The quality and duration of the sound
effects, in combination with the soundtrack, reference both the aes-
thetic and generic conventions of classic point-and-click adventure
games from the 1980s and 90s, especially Ron Gilbert’s Maniac Mansion
and its playful haunted-house narrative based on intertexts from horror
films in which a limited number of endings are possible depending on
user inputs (see Gilbert 1987).
3. The image of David’s face is metaphorically carved as its resolution and
color scheme become a grade sharper. While this is a time-based piece,
the poem does not end until the user has completed ca. 30–40 triggers,
at which point David’s face will become entirely focused and thus fully
“carved.” The combinations of lines are linked in physical proximity
so that thematic understanding emerges via the user’s navigation of
the mouse, but are organized in a nonlinear manner, which, in combi-

These multilevel looping circularities and degrees of interpenetration

series of idyllic and peaceful scenes organized in concentric rings that are
described by Homer “in centrifugal order, moving from the innermost circle
to the rim” (Heffernan 1993: 12). The shield, however, not only functions
as circular representation, it also has the material power of an agent. As
suggested by Heffernan, the shield “is metaphorically interchangeable with
Achilles himself ” (ibid.: 11), and even as it protects Achilles in battle, it is
instrumental in his death. Hephaestus makes the shield, yet also feels
responsible for sealing Achilles’s fate and desires to “hide him away from
death and its sorrow / at that time when his hard fate comes upon him”
(Homer: 18.466–67; quoted in Heffernan 1993: 11). Although the shield is
born of divine power, Achilles’s enemies are able to penetrate it in combat
and literal gaps in its material representation appear. These absences in
form render visible the peaceful life no longer available to Achilles upon
creation of the shield. The disturbing nature of the hole in the reality of the
shield is that it marks Achilles’s negation and makes materially manifest the
ontological wounds that representation is able to inflict. This idea finds its
poetological reflection in Larsen’s poem. The poem’s thematic hauntology
resides in its own awareness of how it functions as a digital poem—how its
Flash file format hides its image files and how its verbal powers of repre-
senting the cycles of killing and enlivening in visual media in turn materially
animate the ghost of its own represented code-object. Aware of the meta-
physical implications of dynamic heterarchy and intermediation, this poem
asks itself and us, and in turn we ask the poem: do such procedures trap
the soul in stone? Or release it? “When is the real real?” (link 10). When it is
flesh? When it is stone? When it is an immaterial body, a gap, a hole? When
it is singular? When it converges with other identities or objects across time
and space? When Achilles is the shield? When David is David? Regardless of
the answers to these questions, the metaphysical consequences are recursive.
And as the poem itself argues: “In the end . . . it is only / absence that
matters.” (link 57)

Wagner’s immaterialized body.

Roland Barthes, in Camera Lucida, famously theorized the metaphysical
realities of the photographic subject:
I call “photographic referent” not the optionally real thing to which an image or a
sign refers but the necessarily real thing which has been placed before the lens,
without which there would be no photograph. Painting can feign reality without
having seen it. Discourse combines signs which have referents, of course, but these
referents can be and are most often “chimeras.” Contrary to these imitations, in
Photography I can never deny that the thing has been there. There is a superimposition
here: of reality and of the past. (2000 [1980]: 76)
This leads Barthes to argue that photography is asymbolic, that it cannot
be reduced to cultural or political codes made miraculously accessible via the
decoding procedures of what he calls the studium (ibid.: 51); rather, as I like
to phrase it for myself, its ontology is accessible through phenomenological
reasoning, that is, through the embodied encounter with the object. This
encounter is established by what he calls the punctum, the wound that “pricks”
and “bruises” the viewer and tethers him or her in direct contact with, in
Barthes’s case, the photographic subject (ibid.: 27).
In our case, however, this poem argues that the photograph’s punctum may
be the eyes, but that its own punctum, when understood in terms of its con-
crete visual form, is the series of three question marks in line 7, and when
understood in terms of its temporality, is the repeated, dialogical act of
questioning. Because it is a poem and an enlivened meta-picture, it can
recognize and voice its own punctum, but paradoxically, it does this via writ-
ten and verbal code, in other words, through its studium. In this poem,

Concluding the Ending of the Beginning
The readings offered here of Deena Larsen’s “Carving in Possibilities” (2001)
and Harry Giles’s “Photo of Maud Wagner” (2013) suggest that a digital
poetry of ekphrasis, when considered in light of dynamic heterarchy and
intermediation, of both representational and experiential reasoning, is pos-
sible. The result is a kind of Pygmalion poetics: a poetics that uses and also
exploits the interstice between modalities, the interstice between systems of
mediation, and the interstice between “lifelikeness” and material ontology.
It is also a type of ekphrasis that is willfully metaphysical in tone and content,
capable of speculatively investigating the relations of mind and matter, time
and space, and the material and immaterial realities of code, computer-
mediated poetry, images, and human-computer interaction. It is further a
poetics of intermediation, aware of its capacity to forge what appears as a
unified image, an image that can appear to be magically entered, but is also
