Ekphrasis as Misrepresentation:
From Balzac’s Sarrasine to Corta´zar’s “Grafﬁti”
Emma Kafalenos
Washington University in St. Louis
Abstract
The proliferation of (copies of ) images in recent decades facilitates viewing
the image to which an ekphrastic text responds. Typically, I have argued previously
(Poetics Today 33, no. 1: 27 – 57), whenever readers of an ekphrasis view the visual artwork
to which the ekphrasis is a response, they become aware that the ekphrasis they would
have written would be different from the one they are reading. I do not think that this
response to an ekphrasis and the image it re-represents is new. But in addition to the
increasing availability of images, something else occurred during recent decades. The-
orists in several fields investigated the complex cognitive process of looking at a visual
artwork and responding to it verbally. The resultant findings about visual represen-
tation and cognitive responses to visual representation are new. We now know, I argue
here, that ekphrasis unavoidably misrepresents the image to which it responds —
largely as a result of the polysemy of the image, the dissimilarity of images and words,
and the perspectival montage in ekphrasis. With this knowledge, we read ekphrases
with a new sophistication. And once readers understand that ekphrasis misrepresents,
writers, in response, can institute new effects.
Keywords
ekphrasis, epistemology, image, polysemy, quotation

This new knowledge, I argue, now informs writers,’ as well as readers,’
ideas about ekphrasis. I begin this essay by looking at Honore´ de Balzac’s

primary focus of my essay—I categorize and describe what seem to me the
most pertinent findings for understanding why, and how, ekphrasis mis-
represents. Finally, I consider Julio Corta´zar’s “Graffiti” (initially published

cognitive process that produces ekphrasis.
In Sarrasine, the portrait that is described by a female character as “too
beautiful for a man” (Balzac 1974 [1830]: 232) is identified by the story’s
narrator as the model for “Girodet’s Endymion” (ibid.: 253). The French
painter Anne-Louis Girodet’s The Sleep of Endymion (painted in 1791, shown
in Paris in the Salons of 1793 and 1814, and acquired by the Louvre in 1818)
depicts a sleeping male figure lying on his back, largely in shadows but with
his upper body illuminated by moonlight. According to (the most pertinent
version of ) the Greek myth, Endymion is a mortal shepherd who sleeps by the
grace of Zeus to retain his beauty. He is beloved by Selene, goddess of the
moon, who makes nightly visits in the form of moonlight: the couple is said to
have produced fifty daughters. A title that names an element in a painting is
itself a mini-ekphrasis. By titling the painting as he did, Girodet expresses his
interpretation that the depicted figure is Endymion: a virile male, the chosen
lover of a goddess.

Arguably the first to do that is Roland Barthes, who, as early as 1964,
announced the polysemy of the image (Barthes 1977 [1964]) and, in S/Z: An
Essay, the book in which he demonstrates how to read a classic text in order

must be seen, inspected: the sexual member, shadowed in the Girodet, as it

polysemy that enables the two interpretations: the shadowed lower torso that
can be seen both as covering Endymion’s maleness and as hiding the castra-
to’s lack.
Ekphrasis, as I use the term, is a verbal re-representation of a visual rep-
resentation. In what follows I look at aspects of ekphrasis that seem to me to
make inevitable that the verbal re-representation will misrepresent the visual
representation to which it responds. I organize these aspects into three cat-
egories: the polysemy of the image, the dissimilarity of images and words, and
the perspectival montage in ekphrasis. This organization and selection is a
personal distillation of decades of reading and thinking about inter-art rela-
tions. I offer it as my understanding of the epistemological status of ekphrasis.

1. The Polysemy of the Image
In “Rhetoric of the Image,” published in 1964, Barthes tells us that “all
images are polysemous; they imply, underlying their signifiers, a ‘floating
chain’ of signifieds, the reader able to choose some and ignore others” (1977
[1964]: 39). In this statement, Barthes recognizes the correlation between the
polysemy of the object being read and the availability of multiple readings.
In the essay, he moves on to explore specific aspects of the viewed object to
which viewers’ responses can vary.1 In this early analysis he distinguishes
three types of messages an image sends and explains, for each of the three,
the knowledge an individual must have to receive the message. The three
types are a coded iconic message, a noncoded iconic message, and (where
there is one, for instance as title or caption) a linguistic message (ibid.: 36).
The linguistic message is received only by those who know the language in
which it is written. The two iconic messages are sent by the same image and
perceived simultaneously but may be read differently. The noncoded iconic
message, Barthes specifies, requires only knowing what an image is and what
the depicted objects are, whereas the coded iconic message requires the per-
ceivertodrawuponprevious cultural experiences.Becauseindividuals’ knowl-
edge and experience vary, the message they receive will also vary.
A. Kibe´di Varga concludes his analysis of image/text relations by consid-
ering the effects of polysemy. Distinguishing according to sequence what he
calls “secondary word-and-image relations,” he writes: “If the word precedes
the image, we speak of illustration. . . . If the image precedes the word, the term
used is ekphrasis” (1989: 43). In secondary relations, he writes, “What comes
first is necessarily unique; what comes after can be multiplied. One image can
be the source of many texts and one text can inspire many painters.” To
explain the multiplication that he recognizes, he tells us that each interpreter
selects and decides what to include: “illustrations and ekphrasis . . . are just
different modes of interpretation. The interpreter is never an exact translator;
he selects and judges. And this, precisely, happens whenever a poet speaks of
a painting or a painter illustrates a poem” (ibid.: 44).
Citing and expanding Kibe´di Varga’s analysis of one-to-many relations,
Tamar Yacobi distinguishes four relations between visual source and
ekphrastic response: (1) one to one, (2) one to many, (3) many to one, and

model, the specific composite exists only for the writer and only as a mental
image, which may not be detailed, and probably fluctuates over time. For
these reasons, the pictorial model has polysemous attributes that exceed those
of the individual image.
To create a pictorial model, the writer of an ekphrasis adds image to
image. To narrativize a visual object, the writer of an ekphrasis adds scenes
from other times, either prior or subsequent to the one depicted, or both.
When more than one person or narrator or character narrativizes a given
image, the things that happen in one narrativizer’s account may be different
from the things that happen in another narrativizer’s account. In Girodet’s
Endymion, as we have seen, the portrayed figure is presented by the painter as
the beloved of Selene, with whom she engenders multiple children. The same
figure is read by Balzac and his narrator as the castrato whose beauty and
beautiful singing make Sarrasine fall in love with the woman she seems to him
to be. In earlier work I have claimed that what a narrativizer chooses to add
can be interpreted like a Rorschach test and that ekphrastic narrativization,
when created by characters, is an effective strategy for conveying aspects of
characters’ personality, including changes in their mental state over time
(Kafalenos 2012: 35). Narrativized accounts that differ are strong demon-
strations of the polysemy of the image.
2. The Dissimilarity of Images and Words
The polysemy of the image enables multiple ekphrastic responses, thus any
given response is at best an incomplete re-representation of the image. But
the polysemy of the image is not the only reason that ekphrasis misrepresents
the visual object to which it responds. Michel Foucault, who writes page after
page describing Vela´zquez’s Las meninas (The Maids of Honor) in The Order of
Things, ultimately recognizes that readers of his text who have not seen the
painting cannot envision what it looks like. In his words, “the relation of
language to painting is an infinite relation. It is not that words are imperfect,
or that, when confronted by the visible, they prove insuperably inadequate.
Neither can be reduced to the other’s terms: it is in vain that we say what
we see; what we see never resides in what we say” (Foucault 1973 [1966]: 9).

This would seem the core truth of the relation between image and language:
that as much as one can say about a visual representation, one cannot con-
vey in words what one sees. The powers of the two media are different. While
both are expressive, they express different things in different ways.
The psychoanalyst Donald P. Spence, responding to the passage by Fou-
cault that I cite, concurs with Foucault that “words . . . will invariably mis-
represent the image” but makes the further claim that “the very attempt to
translate the image will destroy it because . . . the translation, no matter how
approximate, will tend to replace the original” (1982: 56). In explanation,
Spence argues that “any descriptive term will place an image into a category
to which it only partly belongs.” Then, “once a particular term is chosen . . .
this term will arouse its own network of associations and these, if they are
sufficiently compelling, will tend to supplant the image” (ibid.: 57). The loss of
the image is crucial in psychoanalysis, Spence points out, because in psycho-
analysis the patient translates into language images from an often preverbal
past and from typically nonverbal dreams. For the analyst, the images are
never available for comparison to the descriptions, and, for the patient,
Spence argues, “once [the images] are ‘swallowed up’ by a particular descrip-
tion, they are probably lost forever” (ibid.).
Spence is influenced by Roland Barthes, who argues that language is a
technique to “fix” the meaning of an image (to “fix” in the French sense, to
make immobile, to control the polysemy) (1977 [1964]: 39). In “The Photo-
graphic Message” Barthes describes the photograph “as absolutely analogi-
cal, which is to say continuous, outside of any recourse to a code” (1977 [1961]:
20). Spence notes that “patients’ memories, if true, tend to resemble photo-
graphs” (Spence 1982: 57). In doing so he is expanding Barthes’s idea of the
absolutely analogical status of the photograph to scenes or visual memories of
scenes in our world, which are similarly continuous. Developing the idea of
the photograph or viewed segments of our world as continuous, I suggest that
we consider the units from which art forms are composed. The smallest
meaningful unit in language is the word. In comparison, at least one meaning
of Barthes’s term continuous becomes clear. In photography as Barthes knew
it there is no smallest unit. Even the pixel in digital photography is not a
meaningful unit. Nor does the brush stroke in painting nor the line in drawing
carry a precise denotation. In visual representation there is no smallest mean-
ingful unit equivalent to the word in language.
Moreover, since Ferdinand de Saussure we have known that perfect trans-
lation from one language to another is never possible because words in one
language never cover exactly the same range of meaning (they differ in value,
in Saussure’s terminology) as in another language (1966 [1916]: 114–17).

3. The Perspectival Montage in Ekphrasis
In addition to the misrepresentation to which ekphrasis is subject through the
shift from medium to dissimilar medium, ekphrasis also doubles perspective
and recontextualizes. Marie-Laure Ryan, as part of her analysis of the struc-
ture of stories within stories, studies boundaries. Boundaries, she perceives,
sometimes offer “gates to get across [and sometimes merely] windows to look
through” (1990: 873). Ryan distinguishes four types of boundaries: ontologi-
cal gates and windows and illocutionary gates and windows. In the present
context our interest is in illocutionary boundaries, which, when the medium
is language, are readily differentiated. Gates, like direct discourse, are indi-
cated by quotation marks; readers can see at least a segment of a transcription
of a quotee’s speech act. In the case of windows, in contrast, according to
Ryan, “the speech act of the character is presented through the speech act
of the narrator, as in indirect discourse. We are informed of the storytelling
act of the character, but we are denied access to his actual discourse” (ibid.).
Ekphrasis is a window. Ekphrasis informs us that an image exists but does
not let us see it. An image re-represented visually, in contrast, for instance
within a novel, is a gate. Ryan’s metaphor draws attention to the difference
between the two forms of visual quotation while reminding us that both are
quotations.
In “Proteus in Quotation-Land,” Meir Sternberg analyzes the attributes
of all quotations, whether direct, indirect, or free indirect. He specifies four
“universals” that together define quotation. First, the quoted discourse is
subjective—someone’s speech or thought—not an objective piece of reality.
Second, the quoting discourse “transplant[s] and frame[s]” the quoted dis-
course “in a new environment.” Third, this “tearing a piece of discourse
from its original habitat and recontextualizing it within a new network of
relations cannot but interfere with its effect” (1982: 108). And fourth, quo-
tation involves a “perspectival montage between the original subject and the
quoting subject who cites and manipulates his discourse within its new
frame.” Sternberg continues: “Only with effort and luck, if at all, can
we . . . decompose the perspectival montage into its elements, and distribute
them among the various ‘contributing’ participants. The [quoted discourse]

Ekphrasis is a quotation. Like other quotations, ekphrasis transfers the
quoted material—the image—to a new context, thereby altering its effect.
Like other quotations, ekphrasis exhibits a perspectival montage in which the
writer’s perspective is superimposed on the image-maker’s perspective. And
as is the case with other quotations, the two perspectives—the image-maker’s
and the writer’s—cannot finally be separated. Moreover, I suggest that the
ambiguity of perspectival montage that Sternberg sees, which is inherent
when language is quoted in language, is strengthened when image is quoted
in language because of the intermedial differences that make impossible the
re-representation of an image in language. The resultant perspectival ambi-
guity is yet another reason that ekphrasis unavoidably misrepresents the
visual representation to which it responds.
Analyzing ekphrasis in narratives, Tamar Yacobi draws attention to two
further sources of ambiguity. Recognizing that creating an ekphrasis is a two-
stage process—first viewing, then verbalizing—she discerns (1) that some-
times the verbalizing takes place long enough after the viewing that the image
is no longer present to the perceiver, and (2) that sometimes in fiction the two
perspectives that create the perspectival montage (image-maker and writer)
expand to include a third perspective: image-maker, viewer, and verbalizer.
In her words, “Within the mind, the perceiver may or may not translate
images and impressions into words; whereas the teller must translate in
reporting them to readers” (2007: 167). In the latter case the ekphrasis is a
montage of three minds: the image-maker’s, the character’s, and the nar-
rator’s. Adding to the ambiguity, sometimes it is difficult to discern whether
the language in which a character’s response to an image is described is the
character’s or the narrator’s. (Free indirect discourse and narratorial sum-
mary can be difficult to distinguish in any situation, including this one.) What
to reveal, and when, and in whose words, are among the decisions writers
make that shape readers’ experience. As Yacobi recognizes, “these decisions
become powerful interpretive tools for the reader. From the selection, com-
bination, and transmission of the fictive agents’ secret life, we hypothesize the
communicator’s goals” (ibid.: 168).

Corta´zar’s story illustrates (and by exaggerating, emphasizes) all three
aspects of ekphrasis that I am claiming make verbal re-representation inevi-
tably misrepresent the visual representation to which it responds. First,
because of the perspectival montage of ekphrasis we cannot know to what
degree to ascribe the ekphrases we read to the image-maker’s hand, the man’s
perceptions, or (possibly) the narrator’s verbalization of the man’s percep-
tions. Second, because ekphrasis requires the transfer from one medium to
a dissimilar medium, what we can know about the sketches is only what the
man or the narrator can put into words—and these sketches, if we choose to
trust the descriptions, do not seem to convey the kind of meaning that words
convey. Third, the polysemy of the image is demonstrated in several ekphra-
ses but perhaps most strongly by the sketch that one person will know is a
seascape with sails and others will see as random lines.
Most contemporary readers, I am arguing, have at least a layman’s under-
standing of the complex cognitive process that produces ekphrasis. That
understanding, I am also arguing, is sufficient for readers to recognize that
two people who have never met cannot communicate as much as the man
thinks that he can read and that he can expect the woman to read from the
colored chalk sketches that are described. And in fact, at the end of the story
the first-person narrator identifies herself as the woman with whom the man
was exchanging graffiti. In the story’s last sentence she admits that she had
imagined the man who read her chalk marks as messages and came to love
her. Given this new information, we understand that the communication has
happened in the one place it could happen: within her mind alone. There is
no perspectival montage because there is only the one perspective: hers.
There is no intermedial translation because there are no images to translate
(nothing suggests that visual representations of graffiti exist anywhere, not
even as images in the woman’s mind). There is no polysemy because there are
no images to interpret. In sum, there is no ekphrasis.
But the semblance of ekphrasis that readers perceive on first reading is an
example of the effects available to writers whose readers know that ekphrastic
responses to visual representations cannot be trusted. In my reading, the
impossibility that communication through chalk marks is happening, in con-
junction with the man’s developing love for the woman, leaves me wishing
that the incipient romance could progress while recognizing that it cannot.

strengthened, becoming empathy, and that empathy extends to all men and
women in circumstances similar to hers. The story sends a strong political
message, and does so largely by relying on readers’ awareness of how readily
ekphrasis misrepresents the image to which it responds.
