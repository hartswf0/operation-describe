Edward Hopper’s Nighthawks, 1942:
The Ekphrastic Poet’s Collective Diner
Jessica Bundschuh
University of Stuttgart
Abstract
In an Internet age dominated by network structures and visuality, contem-
porary ekphrastic critics and writers have felt prompted to question anew the value
of ekphrasis to its readers. That is, it has become important to demarcate a site for
ekphrasis’s continued cultural currency for a readership well versed in relational think-
ing, who have come to expect the copresence of image and text in nearly every reading
experience. In this paper, my questioning of ekphrasis’s continued relevance proceeds
via ekphrastic responses — in particular, Ann Lauterbach’s (1995) “Edward Hopper’s
Way” — to Edward Hopper’s Nighthawks, 1942, one of the most iconic paintings in
American art. My consideration draws on Harriet Tarlo’s recognition (2009) of the
contemporary value of found poetry, here applied to advance the concerns of ekphrasis
in the process of retooling its response to a changing public sphere. Significantly,
Lauterbach’s “Edward Hopper’s Way,” a found poem marked by digression, simulates
a space of intimacy within Hopper’s pictorial elegies of alienation and lost common-
ality. Granted, “Edward Hopper’s Way,” in its oblique treatment of Hopper, does not
conform to a traditional definition of ekphrasis. Yet Lauterbach, in proposing Hopper,
the visual artist, as a lyrical guide to negotiate the tensions between public and private,
self and other, and individual and collective at play in his iconic painting, references
central concerns of ekphrasis in the digital age.
Keywords
found poetry, network, intermediality, intertextuality, collectivity, literary
hospitality

1. Ekphrasis’s Cultural Currency
Contemporary ekphrastic poets of late have recognized that, in the interest of
self-preservation, they need to redefine their genre for readers in an Internet
age dominated intrinsically by network structures and visuality. Thus, the
concern for practitioners and champions of ekphrasis is how to demarcate a
site for its continued cultural currency. Such a site is necessary to attract a
media-savvy readership, practiced in the art of relational thinking, who have
come to expect the simultaneity, or copresence, of image and text in nearly
every reading experience.
Ekphrasis may be better positioned than most literary genres to survive
this transition since it is, by design, relational and interactive. Dana Gioia
(2004: 8) contends that “critics miss the most interesting aspects of the new
popular poetry” today, namely, “the unusual mixture of radical innovation
and unorthodox traditionalism in the structure of the work itself and the
modes of performance, transmission and reception.” Indeed, to ensure the
genre’s survival, contemporary ekphrastic poems—amid a potentially disori-
enting and isolating virtual context capable of swallowing up ambiguities in
the text—need to productively construct interactive collective spaces for the
poet, the artist, and the reader/viewer alike.
I have chosen ekphrastic poems that respond to one of the most isolated
of American artists, Edward Hopper—an artist who, according to Robert
Hughes (2015: 289), has “so affected popular culture” that “his images have
become part of the very grain and texture of American experience.” While
earlier American artists painted the frontier, in Hopper, Hughes (ibid.: 289–
90) claims, “the old frontier has moved inward and now [lies] within the self,
so that a man of action, extroverted and self-naming, [is] replaced by the
solitary watcher.” Likewise, poet Mark Strand (1988: 340) links the lingering
strangeness of Hopper’s paintings to his ability to make his viewers feel like
“transients.” Hopper’s work, Strand (ibid.: 343) explains, exudes “a sense of
being in a room with a man who insists on being with us, but always with his
back turned.” We see this clearly in Hopper’s Nighthawks, 1942, an iconic
painting of American art focused on lonely individuals assembled in a col-
lective space. Over the past seventy-five years, it has become a locus of artistic
responses, from life-size tableaus through theater plays to graffiti art. Inter-
estingly, the painting continues to prompt an outpouring of contemporary
ekphrastic poems, despite, or perhaps because of, Hopper’s formidable dis-
tance and the Internet’s innumerable reproductions of his paintings. As
David Kennedy suggests in his contribution to this volume, the enduring
interest in Hopper’s work is rooted in the pleasure his viewer takes in indulg-
ing in a “voyeurism of despair” through Hopper’s “charactersself-consciously

In this article, my questioning of ekphrasis’s continued relevance, by way
of poetic responses to Edward Hopper—above all, Ann Lauterbach’s 1995
“Edward Hopper’s Way,” which employs the found poem strategy of inte-
grating and reframing existing texts—draws on Tarlo’s contention (2009)
that found poetry, the literary version of collage, is well positioned for a
contemporary readership in need of literary hospitality in an increasingly
isolated world. Found poetry has a natural affinity with ekphrasis, based on
shared characteristics: dynamicism; homage to, erasure, and remixing of
outside texts; and self-reflexive posturing about its own efficacy. Both possess
a productive ability, in an oversaturated world of images and news media,
to engage readers actively. What is most important for this investigation,
however, is the interplay between the poles of public and private, inside and
outside, and sameness and difference common to both ekphrasis and found
poetry. Indeed, “Edward Hopper’s Way,” in its oblique treatment of Hop-
per’s pictorial space, does not conform to a traditional definition of ekphrasis;
yet, Lauterbach proposes Hopper, the visual artist, as a guide (even if an
oppositional one), a “way” to negotiate those tensions on display in his iconic
painting. Ultimately, Lauterbach’s work redirects such tension—through
the strategy of found poetry—into a productive dialogue with readers. As a
result, her experimental poem becomes a hospitable place where the “home-
less” reader of the Internet age can find refuge, overcoming the alienation
expressed in Hopper’s Nighthawks, 1942.
2. The Network Structure of Found Poetry
W. B. Yeats lineated and published “Mona Lisa,” a single prose sentence
from Walter Pater’s The Renaissance (1873), as a free-verse entry in the Oxford
Book of Modern Verse, 1892–1935:
She is older than the rocks among which she sits;
Like the vampire,
She has been dead many times,
And learned the secrets of the grave . . . (Yeats 1936: 1)
Yeats’s adaptation highlights a long-standing commonality between found
poetry and ekphrasis, namely that intermediality opens up for the reader a
more profound and textured whole.
The contemporary creative writing journal, The Found Poetry Review, sep-
arates found poems into two variants: first, “untreated” poems in which the
poet’s intervention is limited to lineation or spacing, like Yeats’s “Mona

Poet Annie Dillard (1996: ix–x) asserts in the “Author’s Note” in her book
of found poems that the found-poem writer “doubles” the context of the
source text. That is, “the original meaning remains intact but it swings
between two poles,” with the result that it ties poetry’s oldest, most traditional
objective of shared experience to the innovative and contemporary strategy
of ironic foiling. The result, when wedded to ekphrasis, is an invigorated
ekphrastic poem able to bridge between its long history of mimesis and its
more recent developments, displacing, suppressing, or calling into question,
playfully or not, an original text, now transformed into yet another open-
ended artistic experience.
Without a doubt, the Internet has changed the act of reading a poem, even
if one reads it in a book, rather than from an online platform. Lauterbach
(2005: 6) worries about readers who experience primarily the passive and
isolating reality of a screen-driven, one-sided exchange, since she believes
that sincere emotional interactions are “embedded in reciprocity; and reci-
procity is contingent on receptivity.” Likewise, communications researchers
like Clifford Nass (2009) suggest that the multitasking necessitated by online
exchanges makes thinkers less able to sustain big ideas, as opposed to short
bursts of superficial inspiration. The upside, however, is that such readers are
more competent in the task of balancing multiple points of view, and thus are
better prepared for the mental acrobatics necessitated by a found poem.
Ulfried Reichardt (2015: 21) asserts that readers who leverage the pastiche-
driven “open architecture” of the digital gain an expanded and more
engaged sense of identity; that is, the interface inherent in networks redraws
the boundaries between individuals. Collective problem solving, Reichardt
claims, invigorates even those who are digitally isolated. Therefore, one pro-
found consequence for a digital-savvy reader of found poems is the celebra-
tion of decentered, multilateral, and fluid reading experiences. Aesthetic texts
wisely shaped to accommodate the needs of a new breed of readers openly
acknowledge how murky the boundaries between individuals (and texts) have

ownership and singularity “embedded in the mythical image of the lyric
poet” (ibid.: 127). Ultimately, the benefit afforded by a more flexible defi-
nition of originality is that it proposes, and even demands, greater collab-
oration on all fronts.
3. The Magnetism of Hopper’s All-Night Diner
Lisel Mueller’s poem “American Literature” proposes that Hopper’s paint-
ings are compelling to writers because of the vast expanses of emptiness they
offer, spaces she characterizes as “vacant,” “blank,” “scoured,” “bleached,”
and “bereft”:
Poets and storytellers
move into the vacancies
Edward Hopper left them.
They settle down in blank spaces
where the light has been scoured and bleached
skull-white and nothing grows except absence.
Where something is missing. (Mueller 1995: 72)
Mueller’s poem (ibid.: 72) continues: “such bereft interiors / is just what
they’ve been looking for,” “for starting fires in empty rooms.” The poet,
then, may fill the “skull-white” interiors with the heat of presence, and the
warmth of interaction.
In an article on fellow painter Charles Burchfield, Hopper (1928: 6), an
avid reader of Ralph Waldo Emerson, expresses admiration for Burchfield’s
rejection of a European aesthetic and his embrace of marginalized commu-
nal spaces in America: “the sweltering, tawdry life of the American small
town, and behind all, the sad desolation of our suburban landscape.” His
admiration of Burchfield leads Hopper to establish a connection to Emerson’s
“Self-Reliance”: “In every work of genius we recognize our own rejected
thoughts; they come back to us with a certain alienated majesty” (Emerson

Painted at a time when daily life was under threat, Nighthawks, 1942, Hop-
per’s testament to “urban emptiness,” would be “terrifying” to actually inhab-
it, as Kennedy asserts (2018: 326). Hopper’s wife, Josephine Hopper, records
in her journal that her husband painted Nighthawks, 1942 on the brink of
a public event: the United States’ entry into World War II. He began the
composition in December 1942, shortly after the attack on Pearl Harbor
on December 7:
Ed refused to take any interest in our very likely prospect of being bombed — and
we live right under glass sky-lights and a roof that leaks whenever it rains. . . . For
the black-out we have no shade over the sky light . . . but Ed can’t even be both-
ered. He’s doing a new canvas and can’t be interrupted! . . . Hitler has said that he
intends to destroy New York and Washington . . . It takes over a month for E to
finish a canvas and this one is only just begun. ( Josephine Hopper quoted in Levin
1995b: 348– 49)
Then, in late January, Josephine Hopper wrote a letter about the painting’s
completion, famously suggesting the title “Night Hawks”: “Ed has just fin-
ished a very fine picture—a lunch counter at night with three figures. Night
Hawks would be a fine name for it. E posed for the 2 men in the mirror and I
for the girl. He was about a month and a half working on it—interested all the
time, too busy to get excited over public outrages. So we stay out of fights”
(ibid.: 349). Even if one remains unaware of the backstory of the painting’s
composition that Josephine Hopper’s letter and journal provide, one has,
nonetheless, a sense of it being painted just off-stage from a communal event.
In other words, the true achievement of Nighthawks, 1942—and likely why it
has attracted so many poets—is its ability to straddle contradictory perspec-
tives: that of the collective and that of the idiosyncratic individual. Thus,
Hopper’s Nighthawks, 1942 presents a cultural topography that both cele-
brates and contests the ethics of collectivity.

the “erotic” (Wondratschek 1998: 3) pull of voyeurism, the pleasure of Hop-

mutual “spinning of wheels” is precisely that it is mutual, a joint immobility
acting as the critical first step of what will become an interplay in the public
space. Such an engagement also allows us to examine our own present
moment, in which we, as a collective, are threatened by a virtual isolation
so poignantly captured seventy-five years ago by Hopper in Nighthawks, 1942.
4. The Hospitality of the Found Poem as a Public Space
In “Edward Hopper’s Way,” Lauterbach (1995) is interested neither in
replicating the pictorial texture of Hopper’s work, nor in constructing a
temporal narrative about the individual figures in the New York City diner,
or in any other Hopper wasteland—from waiting rooms, to gas stations, to
hotel rooms, or ill-lit front porches. Instead, assuming the role of hostess,
Lauterbach greets those “guests” who arrive at the point of entry of the
“dwelling,” identified by her as a literary text, and wait for an invitation to
enter. Lauterbach’s (2011: 16) metaphor of hospitality—felt most acutely in
the inhospitable world of Hopper—transforms the literary space into a fully
three-dimensional site that opens toward “the commons”: “The idea is
to leave enough room for others, for other subjectivities, to engage and
explore, as if they were guests. We do not ask guests to strip themselves of
their stuff when they come to our dwellings—at least not yet. We are hoping
to provide a place of animated conversation, in which gaps and silences,
shifts in tone, curiosity and inquiry, might be accommodated.” That is, the
truly “accommodating” host/hostess of the poetic text lets the reader-as-
guest enter as he or she is, metaphorical baggage and all.
In today’s cultural climate, this proposition of exchange might be difficult,
Lauterbach concedes, since the “public social space [is] now all but lost in the
slippages of scroll, screen, and cell” (ibid.: 17). Such concern prompts Lau-
terbach to question if the Internet has made us feel more “abstract” and
“estranged” (ibid.) than ever before. However, her continued anxiety about

An investigation of “Edward Hopper’s Way” as an ekphrastic work does
necessarily require a loosening up of the traditional definition of ekphrasis.
Without the title’s reference to Hopper, a reader might be hard-pressed to
find an immediate connection to his work, or to painting in general. Lauter-

explains that she is “not interested in description per se.” Instead, she approa-

paintings (of which there are many) begin “as if the painting were a form of
speech that I could answer. When works of art cause me to begin to speak,
that is, to think, I am drawn toward them or into them” (ibid.). Thus, for
Lauterbach, “conversations” with paintings help her to better understand,
and enact, the intentions of her poems; in other words, such interactions
bring her and her reader “closer to the possibility of human engagement,
agency, and response” (ibid.: 209). As “Edward Hopper’s Way” demon-
strates, Lauterbach models her ekphrastic poetic exchanges on pictorial

the painter. In this poem, she initiates such an exchange with a question

Lauterbach’s “roof bound” “sheltering location”—framed by a “glassy halt”
in its parallel to a “stage” populated by figures “fixed by waiting”—refers
obliquely, surely, to Hopper’s Nighthawks, 1942. Only infrequently does Lau-
terbach provide specific details from Hopper’s individual works, since she is
more interested in the larger mood of his oeuvre. Thus, the initial focus of
“Edward Hopper’s Way” is that of the journey the artist proposes to his
viewers: “Where did you want us to go?” (ibid.: 35). In approaching the artist,
the poet finds—and guides her readers toward—connection. As Lauterbach
(2005: 6–7) asserts, this is possible when the poet “release[s] materials into
the animated middle ground between subjects,” thereby stretching subjects
beyond what is familiar (ibid.: 6). Through such an engagement, Lauterbach
participates in the “commerce of the creative spirit,” a phrase coined by
Lewis Hyde (2007 [1979]: 356), who asserts that the “primary commerce of
art is a gift exchange” that the audience/reader needs to acknowledge for
the exchange to be complete. Hyde (ibid.: 74) continues that the gift’s goal is
to create a “feeling-bond,” that is, to simply connect persons. Thus, we—as
recipients of Lauterbach’s poetic “gift,” and as part of the extended commu-
nity of recipients of Hopper’s pictorial “gift”—may actively engage the artist,
asking him, “What did you want us to see?”
Approached as offerings, the poem and the painting alike—in Hyde’s
view—become more valuable through the act of circulation, passing from
hand to hand, from computer screen to computer screen; the participation
of the artworks within a network of readers and viewers helps them, as
a result, to fulfill their mutual objective, namely, gift exchange. In this
regard, the Internet is a powerful (even if unwitting) collaborator. Likewise,
as Christine Hume (2002: 372) argues, Lauterbach’s approach to ekphrasis
is less intent on exploring Lessing’s distinction of poetry as temporal and
painting as spatial, and more committed to a return to the ancient Greek’s
conception of ekphrasis that places emphasis on process and experience,
rather than product or “the artwork’s cultural codes of connotation.”
Accordingly, Lauterbach foregrounds the ethical dimension of connectivity,
alongside the aesthetic texture of the ekphrastic text. Indeed, if process-
objectives dominate, even sites of “failure” become instructive. After all,
the desire for connectivity does not disregard or disavow the usefulness of
disconnection and rupture, as we see from Lauterbach’s (2005: 11) critique of
those who hanker unthinkingly for master narratives, at the cost of domes-
ticating the “disruptively” experimental.

5. The Fragmentary Whole
In a poem that weaves found texts from sources as various as photographer
Robert Frank, sculptor David Smith, singer Emmylou Harris, composer
Leonard Bernstein, and poets Emily Dickinson and Langston Hughes—
not to mention Jeremiah’s repurposing of the Old Testament creation story

multigeneric. Ultimately,the experience of readingthe poem mimics the real-
time text negotiation necessitated by a digital platform. And the text produc-
tively channels the resulting tension to create an explorative immediacy.

6. The Experiment of Choice
The indeterminacy and fragmentariness of the genre of found poetry offers
readers above all choice, emancipating them from a sense of helpless pas-
sivity. Found poetry’s objective of positioning readers as collaborators is
especially vital for engaging those who may be potentially disengaged, desen-
sitized by the onslaught of digital images. As Lauterbach (ibid.: 7) asserts, it is
the critical task of art to teach laypersons that they, too, “can choose to make
forms that imbue experience with experiment, and so come to know the
gladness of making ‘it’—life—anew.” That is, the poet creatively shapes an
aesthetic contingent on the choice and arrangement of the found texts. Rad-
ically, this arrangement is not one-sided: by welcoming the choices offered
by the fragmented intertextuality of found poetry, the reader recognizes not
only his or her own isolation and alienation, but learns how to become
hospitable and open to the space of the poem. Such an ability to choose is
powerful—and powerfully empowering. To interact with an artwork is, in
Lauterbach’s view, to shoulder the thrilling and yet tricky burden of agency.
In a nutshell, to choose is to invoke free will.
In “Edward Hopper’s Way,” Lauterbach’s (1995: 36) phrase, “the bliss
of solid light,” references Hopper’s most glib but revealing statement about
his art, and his predominant choice of subject matter: “I guess I’m not very
human. All I really want to do is paint light on the side of a house” (Hopper
quoted in Ferlinghetti 2001: ix). Light for a painter is no small matter;
indeed, Ferlinghetti (2001: ix), in quoting Hopper’s revealing claim, suggests

metaphoric and metonymic means, equally merged and contiguous.
Edward Hopper’s Nighthawks, 1942—an artwork, situated on the cusp of
the communal experience of war, that has compelled a rich variety of
ekphrastic responses—offers a fishbowl through which we may gaze directly
at ourselves, as recipients of his isolated urban spaces. Lauterbach’s “Edward
Hopper’s Way” testifies, as this paper has argued, to ekphrasis’s continued
commitment to hospitality and exchange, especially in the face of Hopper-
esque isolation. Granted, Lauterbach’s found poetry requires an invigorated
and more inclusive definition of ekphrasis. Yet, the bounty of such an expan-
sion is a resilient and agile ekphrasis well suited to the contemporary reader,
and able to channel complex explorations of, and interconnections between,
public and private, self and other, and original and borrowed. In essence, the
poem becomes a refuge.
