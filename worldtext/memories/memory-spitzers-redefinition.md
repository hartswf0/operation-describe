## The Trace

In 1955, Leo Spitzer wrote: *"ekphrasis, the poetic description of a pictorial or sculptural work of art."* This sentence — confident, authoritative, presented as self-evident — was a radical redefinition. Before Spitzer, "ekphrasis" meant any vivid verbal evocation (of persons, battles, places, crocodiles). After Spitzer, it meant "description of artwork." The restriction was so successful that within two decades, scholars had forgotten there was ever a broader meaning.

Webb (1999) traces the genealogy: Bertrand (1881) → Bougot (1881) → Spitzer (1955) → Hagstrum (1958) → Heffernan (1993). Each step slightly narrowed the definition. By the time Mitchell theorized "ekphrastic hope" and "ekphrastic fear," the narrowing was invisible — treated as the natural, ancient meaning of the word.

## Why It Matters

Spitzer's redefinition is a **reclassification event** in the Geertzian sense. The same word crossed from the ancient rhetorical frame (where it meant "vivid speech") to the modern academic frame (where it means "artwork description"), and its identity changed. Nobody noticed. Nobody metacommunicated about the change — a classic Batesonian double bind at the disciplinary level.

Operative ekphrasis (Bajohr 2024) is, in one sense, a return to the ancient meaning. In the ancient definition, ekphrasis was "speech that brings the subject matter before the eyes" — regardless of subject. In operative ekphrasis, the prompt "brings the subject matter into the latent space" — regardless of whether the subject is an artwork.
