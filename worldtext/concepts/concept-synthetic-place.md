## Definition

A **synthetic place** is a placed-together, metastable, worlding configuration that organizes action, meaning, memory, and power through material and interface conditions for a situated agent.

*Synthetic* does not mean fake. It means **placed together** — σύνθεσις (synthesis): assembly, composition, binding. A synthetic place is a real arrangement produced by heterogeneous parts being composed into world-like coherence.

## Seven Constitutive Elements

1. **Topology** — arrangement of relations: nearness, distance, transition, boundary
2. **Affordance** — structured field of possible action: permits, discourages, blocks
3. **Memory** — persistence of trace: return, recognition, trajectory
4. **Meaning** — binding of significance to form, value to position, narrative to experience
5. **Power** — distribution of permissions, constraints, access, exclusion, surveillance
6. **Material Support** — substrate of operation: hardware, energy, labor, cost
7. **Interface** — zone of encounter: perception, action, feedback mediation

## Core Property: Metastability

Every synthetic place is **metastable**: coherent enough to orient action, never fully closed, always conditional. Metastability is not a defect — it is what allows place to remain operative.

## Why It Matters

The concept avoids two traps:
- **Naive realism**: treating computational or generated environments as if they were "real places" in the classical sense
- **Naive constructivism**: treating them as "mere representations" with no world-making force

A synthetic place is neither. It is a **produced condition** with real effects on perception, action, memory, and power — but one whose coherence is always provisional and whose construction can always be audited.
