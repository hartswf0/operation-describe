## Definition

Meyer introduces a **three-way distinction** between camera types:

| Camera | Substrate | Relation to Reality | Optical Laws |
|--------|-----------|--------------------| ------------|
| **Material camera** | Photography | Indexical — causal bond with the photographed scene | Obeys optics (lens, aperture, focal length) |
| **Virtual camera** | 3D rendering (CGI, game engines) | Simulated — follows optical laws within a modeled world | Simulates optics (raytracing, rasterization) |
| **Synthetic camera** | AI image generation (diffusion models) | Statistical — produces "images of possible images" | No fixed location, no optical laws, only statistical patterns |

## Why This Matters

The synthetic camera has **no lens**. It does not look at anything. It does not simulate looking at anything. It produces visual outputs that *resemble* what a camera might have captured — but the resemblance is statistical, not optical. The "photographic" becomes a nameable *style* navigable in latent space, not an ontological condition of the imaging apparatus.

Meyer's closing formulation: AI images are **"distorted mirrors, not transparent windows: images from images."**

## Implications

For [[concept-seamless-vs-exposed]]: the seamless path assumes the synthetic camera can produce photographic indexicality. The synthetic camera reveals this is impossible — what it produces is the *aesthetics* of indexicality without the *substance*.

For [[concept-platform-realism]]: the synthetic camera is the imaging device of platform realism. It produces images optimized for legibility and plausibility — not for truth or reference.

For Krieger's natural sign illusion: the synthetic camera IS the illusion made technical. It produces signs that look "natural" (photographic) but are deeply "artificial" (statistical averages with no referent).
