## Definition

**Imagetext** is W. J. T. Mitchell's term for a composite, synthetic work that exceeds the word/image binary — a merged verbal-visual object that is simultaneously text and image, belonging fully to neither medium alone.

Mitchell distinguishes three forms:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **imagetext** (no space, no hyphen) | The composite synthetic work — the merged object |
| **image-text** (hyphenated) | The relation between the two media — the gap, the suture |
| **image/text** (slash) | The rupture, the problematic disjunction |

## Why It Matters for Operative Ekphrasis

The Bolter-Watson paper argues that operative ekphrasis — the prompt + the generated image together — **constitutes an imagetext in Mitchell's sense**. The prompt is not separate from the image; they form a merged verbal-visual object that simultaneously enacts:

- **Ekphrastic hope**: the word commands the image (the prompt generates)
- **Ekphrastic fear**: word and image can no longer be separated (the prompt is entangled in the output)

This means the imagetext was "always the unstated and unattainable goal" of ekphrasis — the tradition was always reaching toward the moment when word and image would merge. AI makes this possible at the technical level.

## The Semiotic Foundation

Mitchell's deeper argument (in "Ekphrasis and the Other"): there is **no essential semiotic difference** between text and image. Both are heterogeneous composites. The imagetext is not a strange hybrid but a *revelation* of what all signs already are — mixed, cross-modal, leaky.

This anticipates Bajohr's "multimodal pictorial third" by 30 years. If Mitchell is right, AI doesn't *create* the collapse of the text/image distinction — it **reveals** what was structurally true all along.

## Relationship to Worldtext

The imagetext is the 2D, static predecessor of the [[concept-worldtext]]:

```
imagetext (Mitchell 1994)
    ↓ operative ekphrasis makes it producible (Bajohr 2024)

```
