## Definition

The myth of Marsyas's musical contest with Apollo — in which the satyr's Dionysian pipe is defeated by Apollo's divine lyre at the cost of Marsyas being flayed alive — read as the **originary scene of the paragone**: the competition between arts that can end in destruction.

## The Conflict

Apollo represents divine, orderly harmony (lyre, reason, measured beauty). Marsyas represents earthly, ecstatic polyphony (pipe, body, uncontrolled sound). The contest is rigged: the god cannot lose. The punishment (flaying) is aesthetic censorship rendered bodily.

**Goehr (2010)** reads this myth as the hidden engine of ekphrasis:

> "Even if 'mere words' cannot bring actual life back, they can place what has been silenced into imagination and memory and, by this means, give it meaning and value for the future."

Philostratus's verbal description of the painting of Marsyas's punishment is an act of **rescue through description**: the words give the silenced pipe a voice. This is ekphrasis as counter-censorship.

## Historical Echoes

| Event | Apollo | Marsyas | The Description |
|-------|--------|---------|-----------------|
| Myth | Lyre (divine harmony) | Pipe (earthly body) | Philostratus gives voice to the punished |
| Lessing 1766 | Painting (space) | Poetry (time) | *Laocoon* prohibits arts from crossing |
| Clement Greenberg | "Medium purity" | "Intermedial contamination" | Modernism polices boundaries |
| Bajohr 2024 | Text | Image | CLIP dissolves the distinction |

In each case, one art form is punished (excluded, prohibited, silenced) for transgressing into another's domain. And in each case, ekphrasis is the technique that **smuggles the punished art back in** through the other art's material.

## Resolution

The conflict resolves differently across epochs:
- **Ancient**: ekphrasis as rescue (Goehr) — the verbal art protects the visual/musical art
- **Modern**: the paragone as productive tension (Mitchell's hope/fear) — competition without destruction
- **Post-AI**: the conflict dissolves — there is no Marsyas to punish because the pipe and the lyre are the same embedding

But Brosch (2018) warns that the conflict may be **permanent in human experience** even after its technical dissolution: we *feel* the difference between word and image even when the machine doesn't.
