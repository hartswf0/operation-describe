## The Scene

Ruth Webb opens by noting that the term "ekphrasis" has been redefined silently, multiple times, to suit each critic's purpose. She traces the word from its ancient meaning (any vivid verbal evocation — of persons, battles, places, animals) to its modern restriction (verbal representation of visual art). The restriction is not ancient. It dates to **Leo Spitzer, 1955** — not antiquity, not the Renaissance, but the mid-twentieth century.

**The key discovery**: The ancient rhetorical handbooks defined ekphrasis as `logos periegematikos` — "a speech that leads one around, bringing the subject matter vividly before the eyes." The key quality was **enargeia** (vividness), not subject matter. You could do an ekphrasis of a battle, a crocodile, a person, a harvest. The restriction to "artworks" is a modern invention that Webb unveils as a series of quiet redefinitions — Barthes silently replacing "actions" with "works of art" in his paraphrase of the ancient definition.

**The threshold**: Webb identifies the moment the word changed meaning. It was not a theoretical revolution but a slow, scholar-by-scholar accumulation: Bertrand (1881), Bougot (1881), Spitzer (1955), Hagstrum (1958). Each slightly narrowed the ancient definition. By the time Heffernan wrote "verbal representation of visual representation" (1993), the narrowing was complete — and invisible.

**The atmosphere**: The patient chill of historical scholarship. Webb is performing, in academic prose, exactly the operation Geertz performs on the Cohen case: showing how a term's meaning changes as it crosses from one interpretive community to another. The ancient meaning crosses the threshold of modern criticism and is reclassified.

## What This Scene Contains

- **Memory trace**: The ancient definition persists as a ghost — scholars invoke it but do not follow it
- **Governance**: The peer-review system enforced the narrow definition by citation (each scholar citing the previous narrow definition)
- **Flow**: Ancient rhetorical manuals → Byzantine compilations → 19th-century French philology → Spitzer 1955 → Modern critical theory. The word traveled through these institutional corridors, shedding meaning at each gate.
