## The Question

Bajohr (2024) argues that in multimodal AI — specifically in connectionist systems that encode text and image as the same type of information — ekphrasis "goes beyond the separation of or transition between text and image, but rather **transcends this difference**." If this is true, it means the foundational distinction of classical ekphrasis (word vs. image) has not merely been complicated or deconstructed — it has been technically annihilated.

But has it? Several sub-questions:

1. **Does a residue remain?** The human prompter still *experiences* text and image as different modes. The collapse is inside the model, not inside the user. Is operative ekphrasis truly post-paragone, or is it paragone relocated?

2. **What new tensions replace the old one?** If word/image is no longer the primary distinction, what is? Possible candidates:
   - **intent / output** (the prompt-to-image gap)
   - **control / emergence** (what the prompter wanted vs. what the model produced)
   - **training data / generated output** (the semiotic substrate vs. the surface image)
   - **human / machine authorship**

3. **Is the singularity domain-dependent?** Operative ekphrasis may hold for text-to-image systems, but does it also hold for text-to-video, text-to-3D, text-to-music? Does each modality have its own paragone?

4. **What does thick description of the singularity look like?** Can we thickly describe the moment of collapse — the exact point where a prompt becomes an image? What does that threshold *feel like* from the inside of the model?
