## Ontology

The world of classical ekphrasis is an ecology where **language struggles to make visual objects present to a reader's inner eye**. Its foundational act — describing an artwork in words — carries within it an ancient tension: the word desires the stillness and immediacy of the image, while fearing its silence and otherness. This is the world of the *paragone*, the contest between sister arts, and its inhabitants are poets, critics, and artworks locked in a centuries-long drama of representation, desire, and failure.

## Memory Traces

- Homer's Shield persists as the originary example in virtually every theoretical text.
- Lessing's *Laocoön* (1766) set the terms of the paragone debate that still organizes the field.
- Keats's "Ode on a Grecian Urn" (1819) established the Romantic ekphrastic paradigm.
- Mitchell's *Picture Theory* (1994) reconfigured the field around affect and cultural anxiety.

## Time Structure

**Epochal / Layered**: The world accumulates sediment. Each era deposits a new paradigm on top of older ones without fully replacing them. Homer's rhetoric coexists with Mitchell's cultural theory; Lessing's spatial/temporal distinction persists alongside digital kinetics.
