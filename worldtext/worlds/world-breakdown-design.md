## Ontology

A world where the hammer breaks and the design appears. Winograd and Flores's "Understanding Computers and Cognition" makes one devastating move: it traces the dominant mythology of AI (intelligence as symbol manipulation) back to a false ontology (human beings as information processors). Against this, they argue that human understanding is situated, linguistic, embodied, historical, and practical. Computers do not "understand" because understanding is not processing symbols — it is participation in a world of concern, action, breakdown, interpretation, and commitment.

The central question of this world: **does the system participate responsibly in the human world it affects, or does it merely simulate the signs of participation?**

## Places

- **The broken hammer** — Heidegger's central design scene: the tool that disappears into use until it breaks, at which point the world of dependencies becomes visible. Breakdown reveals structure.
- **The conversation for action** — Winograd and Flores's structured interaction: who asked, who committed, what conditions count as fulfillment, what remains unresolved, what breakdowns require repair
- **The commitment ledger** — where promises, obligations, and responsibilities are tracked, not as data but as social reality
- **The design wound** — the central tension: formalizing commitment can become a new bureaucracy. Not every promise wants to be rendered as a status field.

## Conflict Grammars

- **Output vs. commitment** — a generated statement becomes socially meaningful only when someone takes responsibility for its use. AI systems produce output that enters commitment structures without bearing commitment themselves.
- **Fluency vs. participation** — the old cognitivist fantasy has returned in probabilistic dress. The machine speaks more fluidly, but it does not participate. It simulates the signs of participation.
- **Alignment vs. design** — "AI alignment" is incomplete if it focuses only on model behavior. Alignment must also be a design problem about conversations, institutions, interfaces, and commitments.
- **Formalization vs. repair** — the best design does not rigidly encode speech acts. It keeps open the conditions for repair.

## The Winograd Test (for the Worldtext)

1. When the worldtext produces a synthesis, who commits to its claims? The operator? The model? The system?
2. When a synthesis contradicts a source paper, who is responsible for the repair? Is the breakdown tracked, or does it vanish?
3. The 42 broken links are breakdowns. Are they visible breakdowns (which is good — they reveal structure) or invisible ones (which is corrosion)?

Answer: They are now visible. The crosslink audit made them present-at-hand. This is exactly the Heideggerian move — the broken hammer makes the workshop's structure appear.

## Rituals

- **Breakdown logging** — treating errors, contradictions, and failures as design information
- **Commitment tracking** — every synthesis should declare who is responsible for its claims
- **Repair conversation** — when a lint report finds a problem, the repair is a structured conversation, not just a text edit

## Atmospheric Signature

Severe, humble, repair-focused. This world has no interest in demonstration or spectacle. It cares about one thing: when the tool breaks, can the world be fixed? Its atmosphere is closer to an engineering review than a seminar.
