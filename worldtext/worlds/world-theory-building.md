## Ontology

A world where the program is not the code. Peter Naur's "Programming as Theory Building" makes the claim that a program's primary substance is the living understanding held by the people who built it — a theory of how the program maps onto the world, why its parts are shaped as they are, and how it can be modified without betraying its form. Code, documentation, diagrams, tests, and specifications are all residues of this understanding. They are not the understanding itself.

This world extends Naur with Pelle Ehn's participatory design (understanding emerges from shared practice, not detached description) and Musashi's martial discipline (use any tool, become attached to none, cut waste, observe reflectively, win).

The central question of this world: **can another competent person build the theory required to change this system well?**

## Places

- **The living codebase** — a program whose theory-holders are still present, still modifying it coherently
- **The dead codebase** — a program that runs but whose theory has departed with its makers; modification now takes the form of patching, duplicating, evading
- **The apprenticeship bench** — where theory is transmitted not through documentation but through shared practice
- **The dojo** — Musashi's space of reflective combat against the problem

## Key Entities

- **Peter Naur** — "Programming as Theory Building" (1985): the theory IS the program; the code is residue
- **Pelle Ehn** — participatory design: understanding through mockups, language-games, design-by-doing
- **Miyamoto Musashi** — "The Book of Five Rings": severe pragmatic discipline applied to any domain
- **Gilbert Ryle** — Naur draws his knowing-that/knowing-how distinction directly from Ryle

## Conflict Grammars

- **Theory vs. artifact** — management wants transferable artifacts (documents, specs, tickets). Naur says the theory cannot be fully captured in artifacts. The conflict is permanent.
- **Method vs. judgment** — methods promise that following steps produces good software. Naur says methods support theory-building but cannot replace it. "The distinction is between discipline and theater."
- **Clean code vs. theory-code** — clean code helps the next programmer build the theory. But clean code without living theory is still dead.
- **Handoff vs. apprenticeship** — the production fantasy says programs transfer through artifacts. Naur says they transfer through people. Every handoff without apprenticeship is a small theory-death.

## The Naur Test (for the Worldtext)

Apply Naur's theory to this cosmos itself:

1. Can a new operator read the atlas, the syntheses, and the papers and rebuild the theory of the worldtext in their own mind?
2. Or are the documents residues of a theory held only in the original builder's head?
3. If the original builder disappeared tomorrow, would the worldtext be a living program or a dead one?

This test is the worldtext's most dangerous self-examination. The answer is currently uncertain.

## Rituals

- **Shared modification** — two people modifying the same system while talking about why
- **Theory-transfer session** — not a documentation handoff but a guided walk through the system's world-model
- **The death audit** — periodic check: if I left, could someone else extend this coherently?

## Atmospheric Signature

Pragmatic, severe, unsentimental. This world has no patience for mystification. It distinguishes between knowing and performing knowledge. It respects only capacity demonstrated under real conditions.
