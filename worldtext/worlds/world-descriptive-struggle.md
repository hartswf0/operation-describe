## Ontology

A world where language costs something. This world is built from Iris Murdoch's claim that a word in thought is not a neutral symbol — it is an event that may clarify, betray, condense, substitute, evade, or wound. Gilbert Ryle provides the disciplinary framework: thinking is not the silent recitation of already-formed propositions but the labor of bringing something into articulation before articulation has occurred. Genevieve Lloyd provides the historical depth: descriptive adequacy is not a timeless standard but a historically embedded practice with material, social, and institutional conditions.

The central question of this world: **what must be true of a system for its descriptions to be more than outputs?**

The answer this world provides: the description must have cost the describer something. It must emerge from a practice of revision, self-correction, felt inadequacy, and struggle. A description that arrives without friction — including machine-generated description — fails not because it is wrong but because it was never an event.

## Places

- **The writing desk under pressure** — where the thinker struggles to find the right word while knowing the wrong word is already available
- **The revision margin** — the space between the first draft and the adequate description
- **The confessional** — Murdoch's scene where the mother-in-law revises her description of her daughter-in-law from "vulgar" to "refreshingly spontaneous" — not because the daughter changed, but because the mother's attention deepened

## Key Entities

- **Iris Murdoch** — "The Sovereignty of Good": attention as the primary moral act; the just and loving gaze directed upon an individual reality
- **Gilbert Ryle** — "Thinking of Thoughts" and "Thinking and Inferring": thinking is not the rehearsal of finished arguments but the groping toward formulation
- **Genevieve Lloyd** — descriptive adequacy has five dimensions: inward (answering to inner pressure), metaphorical (carrying conceptual weight), factual (surviving historical depth), practical (surviving use), and ethical (accounting for what description does to the described)

## Conflict Grammars

- **Fluency vs. adequacy** — the machine produces fluent descriptions effortlessly. Murdoch says the fluent word may be the evasive word. Adequacy requires resistance.
- **Speed vs. struggle** — a fast description is always available. The question is whether it's the right one or merely the first one.
- **Cost vs. efficiency** — modern AI practice optimizes for low-cost output. This world argues that the cost IS the value. Removing it removes the thinking.
- **Word-as-token vs. word-as-event** — in the machine, a word is a probable continuation. In Murdoch's world, a word is something that happens to the thinker.

## Rituals

- **Revision under self-criticism** — the practice of reading one's own description and asking: is this adequate? Or is this evasion?
- **The blush test** — Ryle's criterion for felt inadequacy: if the describer would blush at their own description, the fiction is alive. If they feel nothing, the description is dead.
- **The five-dimensional audit** — applying Lloyd's five dimensions to any description produced in the cosmos

## Atmospheric Signature

Dense, uncomfortable, self-correcting. This world does not reward fluency. Its atmosphere is closer to a therapy session than a writing workshop — the goal is not beautiful prose but honest encounter with what resists formulation.
