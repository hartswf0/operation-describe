## Thesis

HCI is trying to study an ocean by interviewing people about a map they drew of a boat.

The field convenes workshops, conducts semi-structured interviews, constructs affinity diagrams, codes transcripts, and publishes findings about "user strategies for prompting." It studies the **astrology** of human-computer conversation — surface rituals performed with procedural confidence — while completely missing the underlying **physics** of the latent space. The generated artifact, the actual ocean in which all of this is happening, is systematically absent from the published record.

Instead of steering the co-evolution of human and machine, HCI acts as an **empirical usability janitor** for the machine learning establishment.

This world names the pathology. [[world-latent-wrestling]] names the cure.

## The Cathedral of Method

The Cathedral of Method — the CHI paper template, the affinity diagram, the IRB protocol, the standardized UX heuristic — is an apparatus of pure **Techne**. It is the knowledge of the mapmaker, assuming the latent space is a static grid that can be perfectly surveyed and universally applied. Techne is knowledge *about*. It is transferable, codifiable, teachable in a seminar room. It produces methods sections.

But the latent space is not a grid. It is the ridge in Rocky Branch, Tennessee. It is a highly volatile, pre-paradigmatic environment defined by deep uncertainty. A prompt that works perfectly on a Tuesday might collapse on a Thursday due to a silent model weight update. The embedding space drifts. The safety filters shift. The tokenizer treats a hyphen differently than a dash. None of this is documented. None of this is stable. None of this yields to survey instruments.

What the latent space demands is **Metis** — the cunning, situated, bodily intelligence that ancient Greeks attributed to the sailor, the hunter, the wrestler. Metis is not knowledge *about*; it is knowledge *with* and *through*. It is acquired not from interviews or workshops but from sustained, first-person friction with the material itself. The potter knows the clay not from a ceramics textbook but from ten thousand failures at the wheel. The prompt-crafter knows the model not from a UX study but from months of wrestling — feeling the resistance, developing intuitions, calibrating chants against the swell of a high-dimensional ocean.

HCI is trapped in a pre-paradigmatic state, trying to enforce Techne on a wilderness of Metis. They are building cathedrals of method to study the weather, without ever walking outside.

## Compressed Culture and the End of the Gallery

Traditional HCI — the HCI of the desktop metaphor, the file directory, and the graphical user interface — was built for a world of **discrete objects**. In the digital Cathedral, culture was an inventory. You could point to a file, click a button, lock a folder, litigate a boundary, and cleanly measure the exact time it took a user to retrieve an item.

Generative AI is not an inventory. It is **compressed culture**. Large language models have pulverized the operational substrate into a living field of grains. They are accretion engines where particles of human thought are stirred, recompressed, and forced to temporarily cohere. The latent space is a **sedimentary medium**.

When HCI researchers attempt to tame the AI chatbox using their traditional toolkits — creating rigid prompt taxonomies, designing drop-down menus for "tone," or writing papers on "blank canvas syndrome" — they are committing a profound **category error**. They are treating a bog like a gallery. They are trying to build clean, Euclidean sidewalks across a shifting mangrove.

You cannot govern a sedimentary medium by drawing borders or demanding explicit syntactic inputs. You can only navigate it by catching what drifts.

This is the cosmological connection to [[concept-viscosity]]: viscosity *is* the sedimentation made measurable. The model's resistance to the prompt is the historical inertia of compressed culture refusing to be commanded. High viscosity = heavy sediment = the statistical mean pulling every output toward stock photography and golden hour. The thick prompt is not a louder command; it is a hydrologic intervention — redirecting the flow of the sediment rather than trying to dam it.

## Prompt Superstition Is the New Astrology

The field's Techne-apparatus produces a specific pathology: **prompt superstition** — the theatrical performance of certainty designed to soothe the anxieties of the state or the corporation.

"Use chain-of-thought prompting for improved reasoning." "Specify the output format in the system prompt." "Negative prompts improve image quality by 23%." These findings are presented with the procedural confidence of engineering specifications. They are published in top-tier venues. They are taught in workshops. They are codified into "prompt engineering best practices."

They are astrology.

Not because they are always wrong — astrologers are sometimes right — but because they operate at the wrong level of explanation. They describe correlations between surface rituals (prompt structures) and observed outcomes (model outputs) without any mechanistic account of *why* those correlations hold, *when* they will break, or *what* the model is actually doing with the tokens. The "best practice" that worked because of a specific interaction between the tokenizer and the attention mechanism at a particular model checkpoint is reported as a universal design heuristic.

The astrologer maps the stars and prescribes action. The astronomer studies the physics. HCI's prompting research is astrology: it maps the observable surface of human-model interaction and prescribes ritual. It does not — because its methods cannot — study the latent physics underneath.

The corporations want astrology. They want heuristics, playbooks, "10 Principles for Effective Prompting." Astrology is profitable and comforting. Physics is expensive, uncertain, and prone to telling you that your entire product strategy is built on a stochastic process you do not control.

## Polanyi's Silence and the Death of the Interview

Michael Polanyi: *"We can know more than we can tell."*

This single sentence is the **executioner of the HCI user interview**.

In a volatile, shifting medium, knowledge lives in the body and in practice. It shows up as competent movement before it shows up as clean explanation. The first people who can act well inside the latent space cannot yet justify themselves in the old language.

When an HCI researcher asks a practitioner to explain their prompting strategy in a windowless workshop, the practitioner is forced to translate a complex, tacit, physical orientation (Metis) into a sterile, declarative syntax (Techne). The resulting data is worse than useless — it is an **active distortion**. The interview does not capture what the practitioner knows. It captures the practitioner's improvised rationalization of what they do, translated into the vocabulary the researcher expects to hear.

We must stop asking the wayfinder to draw a map of the ocean from memory. The ocean will not sit still. We must get in the boat and measure the physical corrections of the tiller.

This connects directly to [[world-thick-description]]: Geertz did not interview the Balinese about cockfighting. He sat in the dirt and watched. He ran from the police with them. The thick description was not extracted from the informant's self-report; it was inscribed from the ethnographer's first-person immersion. The interview is a structurally thin method applied to a constitutively thick phenomenon. Polanyi explains *why* it fails: the knowledge is tacit. It cannot be told. It can only be observed in action or learned through apprenticeship.

## The Cowardice of the Moral High Ground

There is a socially available pose in tech criticism and academia right now that feels righteous but explains nothing: standing on the moral high ground.

If the ground is shifting, moral purity offers a foothold. If the future is uncertain, refusal offers an identity. But this stance has a devastating hidden cost: it relocates the work of ethics away from contact and into posture. It turns ethical life into pure distance.

The critic who denounces the latent space while refusing to touch it inevitably slides from operational critique into symbolic performance. They are describing a swamp from a photograph. Their certainty rises only because contact would complicate their narrative. But ethics that cannot touch the medium is just a speech act.

There is a symmetric failure on the builder side. The engineers training these models are experts in compression and infrastructure, but they possess a dangerously thin relationship to the humanities. Language is not just data; it is a form of life. Technical power without linguistic and cultural literacy is brittle.

The pure critic has no Metis. The illiterate builder has no language arts. Both claim authority, and both are entirely divorced from the reality of the medium.

**The redistribution of authority in the era of AI is not optional; it is already happening.** Technical builders are losing legitimacy because they treat language merely as a compressed substrate, lacking the cultural literacy to understand the lives it shapes. But HCI is losing its leverage because it refuses to step off the hill. The discipline has a choice: descend into the medium, or become irrelevant from a distance.

## Guardrails vs. Hydrology

The Cathedral of Method tries to govern the latent space with the language of the highway: guardrails, harnesses, policy.

But a guardrail is boundary control for a low-dimensional environment. The latent space is not a highway. It is an **ecology of wetlands**.

It is a **Mangrove**: an accretion engine that catches sediment and grows land not by drawing borders, but by catching drift.  
It is a **Bog**: a memory machine of slow accumulation where sedimented patterns resist quick narratives.  
It is a **Tidal Pool**: where a tiny shift in context (a changed prompt, a modified temperature setting) flips the entire equilibrium.

You cannot put a guardrail on a mangrove. In a high-dimensional, sedimentary medium, governance is not a sign on a road. Governance is **hydrology**.

You must shape the currents. You alter what becomes rewarded, repeated, cheap, visible, and risky. You build channels. You shape incentives and liability surfaces. That is the muck-work. It is slow, local, and incredibly specific.

This connects to [[concept-platform-realism]]: Roland Meyer's insight that prompts "find" images rather than "produce" them describes the *hydrologic reality* of the latent space. The prompter navigates a flow; they do not command a machine. The guardrail paradigm assumes command. The hydrology paradigm assumes navigation. HCI needs hydrology.

## The Cosmological Implication

This world forces the cosmos to confront its own method. If OPERATION DESCRIBE is to thickly describe the latent space, it cannot rely on HCI's thin accounts. It must go to the ocean itself.

The cosmos already has the tools:

- [[world-operative-ekphrasis]] teaches that description and generation are the same act
- [[world-thick-description]] teaches that density comes from immersion, not extraction
- [[world-embodied-description]] teaches that the body is the primary instrument
- [[concept-viscosity]] provides the physics of the medium's resistance
- [[concept-platform-realism]] names the hydrologic reality: prompts navigate, they do not command

What this world adds is the **diagnosis**: HCI has committed a category error. It is treating compressed culture as an inventory. It is studying the astrology of human-computer conversation while missing the physics of the latent space. It is standing on the moral high ground while the ocean moves below.

[[world-latent-wrestling]] provides the prescription: step into the Arena. Touch the medium. Wrestle.

If HCI is to survive — if it is to reclaim the generative, steering ambition of Engelbart's Mother of All Demos — it must abandon its Cathedral of pre-cultural, formal UI evaluations. It must recognize that human intent cannot be abstracted away from the environment it operates within.

The future of human-computer interaction is not the invention of the perfect prompt template, nor is it the installation of a sturdier guardrail. The future is **mud literacy**. The discipline must learn to walk the transect, map the mangrove roots, and design instruments for wayfinding that allow human latency to dance with machine probability.

Inside, the astrologers diagram the stars on sticky notes and declare patterns. On the hilltop above, the critics denounce the ocean for being wet. Outside, the ocean moves.
