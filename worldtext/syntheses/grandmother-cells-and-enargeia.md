## The Rhyme Across 2000 Years

Bajohr (2024) reports that CLIP develops **grandmother neurons** — single artificial neurons that fire for both a *photo* of Spiderman and the *text string* "Spiderman." This is the technical basis for his claim that multimodal AI collapses the text/image distinction at the neural level.

But read alongside Webb (1999) and the *Progymnasmata*, this finding has a much deeper resonance. Ancient ekphrasis was defined by **enargeia** — the quality of language that "brings the subject matter vividly before the eyes." Quintilian explains the mechanism:

> The orator uses his own power of imagination to conjure up a scene in his mind. This exercise in visualization ensures that his language will spark a mental impression in the mind of his audience. — Quintilian, *Institutio Oratoria* 6.2.29–32 (via Webb 1999)

Webb further connects this to ancient memory theory:

> Language, particularly language which has the quality of enargeia, is produced by such images (in the mind of the speaker) and gives rise to other, comparable, images in the listener's mind. — Webb 1999, p. 12

The structural homology:

| Ancient Rhetoric | Multimodal AI |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Orator visualizes scene (speaker's imagination) | Training: CLIP learns visual patterns from images |
| Language with enargeia sparks mental image in listener | Inference: prompt text activates image-correlated neurons |
| Listener "almost sees" the subject | GLIDE generates image from activated embedding |
| Success = listener becomes spectator (Nikolaos) | Success = text produces coherent image |
| Mechanism = shared cultural memory | Mechanism = shared embedding space |

## The Non-Obvious Claim

**CLIP's grandmother neurons are the technical implementation of enargeia.** Not metaphorically — structurally. Both describe a system in which:

1. A **shared representation** exists (cultural memory / embedding space)
2. **Verbal input** activates regions of that shared representation
3. **Visual output** emerges from the activation (mental image / generated image)
4. Success depends on the **quality of the verbal input** (vivid description / good prompt)

Quintilian says the orator must first *see* the scene himself before he can make others see it. Prompt engineering manuals say the same thing: you must visualize what you want before you can write a prompt that produces it. **The prompter is the new rhetor. Enargeia is the old prompt engineering.**

## Why This Matters for the Cosmos

This connection dissolves the apparent discontinuity between the Galaxy of Ekphrastic Worlds and the Galaxy of Generative AI Worlds. They are not separate galaxies that happen to be in the same cosmos — they are **the same galaxy at different temporal frequencies.** The ancient Greeks and OpenAI are both engineering systems for converting verbal descriptions into vivid mental/digital images.

The difference (and it is a real difference, not a mere analogy) is:
- **Ancient enargeia** required a *human* receiver with *cultural memory*
- **CLIP/DALL·E** has a *machine* receiver with *statistical memory*
- Ancient enargeia could fail (the audience might not share the cultural reference)
- CLIP can fail too (the prompt might be outside the training distribution)

But the architecture is the same: **shared latent representation + verbal activation → visual generation.**

## The Dreaming Connection

Ha & Schmidhuber (2018) show that their World Models agent can **train entirely within dreams** — within hallucinated sequences generated by its own memory model. This connects to:

- Quintilian's orator who first **imagines** the scene (dreams it) before speaking it
- Webb's ancient memory theory where imagination is "a store-house of images derived from sense perception"
- Krieger's ekphrasis as "miracle and mirage" — the dream-image that language produces

The World Models paper literally calls its internal generation process "dreaming." The VAE's latent space z is the digital **store-house of images** that generates new images without new perception — exactly the mechanism of ancient imagination.
