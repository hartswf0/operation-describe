# Operational Depiction: The Model Shop Theory

> Das Bild ist ein Modell der Wirklichkeit.  
> *The picture is a model of reality.*  
> — Ludwig Wittgenstein, *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* 2.12

> It is laid against reality like a measure.  
> — *Tractatus* 2.1512

> This synthesis recovers representation from resemblance  
> and returns it to operation.

## I. The Theory

### 1.1 — The Problem

Worldtext systems face a fundamental question about representation:

**How does a worldtext *represent* the world it describes?**

The naïve answer: by resembling it. A good worldtext is one that "looks like" or "sounds like" the world it captures. A description is good when it resembles its object.

Wittgenstein, read through Susan Sterrett's "model shop" interpretation, provides a radically different answer:

**A model does not resemble its object. It *projects onto* its object through operational rules of correlation.**

The courtroom accident model — where toy cars and miniature roads are arranged on a table to test theories about what happened — *does not look like* the accident. It is smaller, simpler, made of different materials, colored differently, static where the accident was dynamic. Yet it represents the accident perfectly, because every element in the model **correlates** with an element in the reality, and the model can be **tested** — rearranged, modified, judged — to determine which *arrangement* of reality is correct.

### 1.2 — Wittgenstein's Five Features of Pictorial Representation

Read through Sterrett's anti-anachronistic lens:

| Feature | Tractatus | Model Shop Translation | Worldtext Translation |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Element Correlation** | Objects in the picture correspond to objects in reality (2.13-2.131) | The toy car corresponds to the real car; the miniature road to the real road | Each entity in the atlas corresponds to a concept in the operator's project; each distinction to a real tension |
| **Combinatorial Constraint** | The way elements are combined in the picture determines what state of affairs it represents (2.14-2.141) | The arrangement of toy cars on the table represents one possible accident sequence | The arrangement of entities, distinctions, and worlds in the atlas represents one possible theoretical architecture |
| **Projection Rule** | The "law of projection" translates between the picture and what it depicts (4.0141) | Scale, proportion, rules of correspondence between model and reality | The translation protocol between worldtext objects and the phenomena they describe |
| **Operational Mediation** | The picture is a fact (2.141) — an arrangement, a configuration, not a passive object | The model is not a photograph; it is an *arrangement* — something actively composed and testable | The worldtext is not a database; it is an *active arrangement* of concepts that can be tested, rearranged, and judged |
| **Truth/Validity Condition** | A picture agrees or disagrees with reality; true or false (2.21-2.222) | The model either explains the accident or it doesn't; the arrangement is tested against evidence | The worldtext either coheres with the project's evidence or it doesn't; the atlas arrangement is validated against the operator's experience |

### 1.3 — The Gramophone Logic

Wittgenstein's gramophone analogy (4.014) reveals the deep structure of representation:

> The gramophone record, the musical thought, the musical notation, the sound waves — all stand to one another in the same internal relation of depicting that holds between language and the world.

Four different media. None of them *resemble* each other. The groove pattern doesn't look like the notation; the notation doesn't sound like the wave; the wave doesn't feel like the thought. Yet they are all **models of each other** because they share a **logical form**: a structural correspondence that allows systematic translation.

**For worldtext**: The atlas (textual), the project (lived), the wiki (compiled), and the synthesis (theoretical) are four media that do not resemble each other but share a logical form. The worldtext system is a **projection apparatus** — a machine for translating between these media while preserving their shared structure.

## II. Sterrett's Model Shop Interpretation

### 2.1 — Historical Context

Susan Sterrett argues that Wittgenstein's picture theory has been systematically misread by the philosophical tradition, which interpreted "picture" (*Bild*) as "visual image." In fact, Wittgenstein's context was:

- **Engineering model shops**: where scale models were built and tested (wind tunnels, bridge stress tests, fluid dynamics simulations)
- **The Paris courtroom model**: where physical models of traffic accidents were assembled from miniature components to reconstruct events
- **Dimensional analysis**: the mathematical theory of how systems at different scales can share structural properties (physically similar systems)

### 2.2 — Objects as Dimensions

Sterrett's most radical move: the "objects" of the *Tractatus* are not Platonic entities or Russell's sense-data. They are **dimensions** — analogous to the dimensions (length, mass, time, temperature) used in dimensional analysis.

| Concept | Classical Reading | Model Shop Reading |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Object** | Simple, indestructible, form-giving entity | A dimension along which variation is possible |
| **State of affairs** | A combination of objects | A point in a multidimensional space |
| **Picture** | A representation that shares logical form with what it depicts | A model arranged in a (potentially different) dimensional space, connected to reality by rules of projection |
| **Logical form** | An abstract structural property shared by language and world | The law of similar systems: the invariant structure preserved across scales and media |

### 2.3 — Implications for Worldtext

If atlas objects are dimensions (not things), then:

1. **The atlas is a dimensional space.** Each entity, distinction, world, and ritual defines a dimension along which variation is measured. A specific project is a *point* in this space — a particular configuration of values along these dimensions.

2. **A worldtext model is a scale model.** It is smaller, simpler, and made of different material (language, not reality) than the world it represents. But it preserves the dimensional structure: the relations between entities, the constraints between distinctions, the tensions between worlds.

3. **Testing a worldtext means rearranging the model.** The operator does not ask "does this worldtext look like my project?" The operator asks "does this arrangement of entities, distinctions, and tensions explain what I observe? Would a different arrangement explain it better?"

## III. The Model Shop Protocol

An operational protocol for treating the worldtext atlas as a model shop.

### Step 1: Identify the Phenomenon
What real-world phenomenon is the worldtext attempting to model? Be specific.

### Step 2: Identify the Dimensions
What are the relevant dimensions of variation? What changes, what stays constant, what is in tension?

Map these to atlas objects:
- **Entities** → the players, the named things that persist
- **Distinctions** → the axes of variation, the directions in which things can differ
- **Worlds** → the ecologies where specific configurations of entities and distinctions prevail
- **Conflicts** → the tensions between incompatible configurations
- **Rituals** → the operations that transform one configuration into another

### Step 3: Build the Model
Arrange the atlas objects into a configuration that represents the phenomenon. This is the model.

### Step 4: Test the Model
- Does the arrangement explain the evidence?
- Are there aspects of the phenomenon that the model cannot represent? (These are dimensional gaps — missing entities or distinctions.)
- Would a different arrangement explain the evidence better?
- What does the model *predict* about the phenomenon that hasn't been observed yet?

### Step 5: Judge the Model
- If the model coheres with the evidence → validate, strengthen, register
- If the model fails → modify the arrangement, add dimensions, rearrange
- If no arrangement works → the dimensional space is wrong; new entities or distinctions are needed

### Step 6: Compare with Rival Models
- Can multiple models represent the same phenomenon?
- If yes: which is simpler? Which has more predictive power? Which is more productive for the project?
- Ricoeur's validation logic applies: argue for the better model, not the "true" model

## IV. The Measure Test

Derived from Tractatus 2.1512: "It is laid against reality like a measure."

A worldtext model is a **measure** — an instrument for making determinations about reality. Just as a ruler determines length by being *applied to* an object, a worldtext determines coherence by being *applied to* a project.

### Three Properties of a Good Measure

| Property | In Measurement | In Worldtext |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Calibration** | The measure must be calibrated against a known standard | The atlas must be grounded in established theory (the PAPERS corpus, the registered entities, the formalized distinctions) |
| **Contact** | The measure must be brought into contact with the measured object | The worldtext must be applied *to a specific project*, not exist in abstract |
| **Resolution** | The measure must have sufficient resolution to detect the differences that matter | The atlas must carry enough dimensions (entities, distinctions, tensions) to discriminate between different states of the project |

### The Measure Failure Modes

| Failure | Description | Worldtext Symptom |
|---------|------------|------------------|
| **Uncalibrated** | The measure has no grounding in established standards | Atlas full of ad hoc categories with no theoretical lineage |
| **Uncontacted** | The measure has never been applied to the measured object | Worldtext exists in abstract; never tested against a live project |
| **Underresolved** | The measure cannot detect the differences that matter | Atlas too coarse; critical distinctions missing; all projects look the same |
| **Overresolved** | The measure detects differences that don't matter | Atlas too fine; noise mistaken for signal; the operator drowns in irrelevant dimensions |
