## The Argument No One Has Made

There is a structural rhyme between three events separated by millennia, and no one in the cosmos has connected them:

1. **Marsyas is punished for playing the wrong instrument** (Apollonian harmony vs. Dionysian pipe)
2. **Lessing prohibits poetry from being painting** (*Laocoon*, 1766 — spatial arts forbidden from being temporal and vice versa)
3. **AI dissolves the prohibition entirely** (CLIP encodes text and image as the same data type)

Goehr (2010) reads ekphrasis through the Marsyas myth as a system of **artistic self-defense**: one art rescues another from punishment by giving it voice. Poetry *rescuing* painting by describing it is not merely aesthetic — it is political. The description is an act of *protection*, like the Shield of Achilles.

> "Even if 'mere words' cannot bring actual life back, they can place what has been silenced into imagination and memory and, by this means, give it meaning and value for the future." — Goehr 2010, p. 392

But here is the non-obvious move: **operative ekphrasis (Bajohr 2024) is the end of this rescue operation.** If multimodal AI collapses the distinction between text and image, there is nothing to rescue and nothing to punish. Marsyas and Apollo are the same neuron. The pipe and the lyre are the same embedding vector.

This means the five-millennia tradition of ekphrasis-as-protection is **obsolete** in the connectionist regime — not because images don't need words anymore, but because the very categories of "word" and "image" that made protection necessary have dissolved.

## The Counter-Evidence: Brosch's Paradox

But Brosch (2018) reports the opposite empirical fact: ekphrasis is *more* prevalent in the digital age, not less. In an era of image-saturation, writers produce *more* verbal responses to visual art, not fewer. Bolter (1996) and Ryan (2001) predicted ekphrasis would become "redundant" — they were wrong.

Why? Brosch answers: because **ekphrasis has shifted from representation to defamiliarization**. When anyone can see *Nighthawks* in two seconds on Google, the ekphrastic poem's job is no longer to show you the painting. It is to *transform* the painting — to make you see it as if for the first time.

> "Something in ekphrasis must still speak to its recipients in a way that reproductions do not." — Brosch 2018, p. 229

This means ekphrasis survives the death of its protective function by becoming a **ritual of estrangement**. It is no longer Marsyas being rescued; it is the viewer being jolted.

## The Structural Homology

| Epoch | Ekphrastic Function | Source |
|-------|---------------------|--------|
| Ancient (progymnasmata) | **Vivification** — making listeners into spectators (enargeia) | Webb 1999 |
| Renaissance–Modern | **Protection** — words sheltering images from prohibition | Goehr 2010, Krieger 1992 |
| Post-Google | **Defamiliarization** — making saturated images strange again | Brosch 2018, Bundschuh 2018 |
| Post-AI | **???** — the distinction itself dissolves | Bajohr 2024 |

The question the cosmos must answer: **what does ekphrasis become when there is nothing left to protect, nothing left to estrange, because the word and the image are already the same thing?**

## Possible Answer: Thick Description

Geertz's thick description is neither protection nor defamiliarization. It is **the weaving of significance itself**. Not: "what does this image look like?" (representation). Not: "let me save this image from silence" (protection). Not: "let me make this image strange" (defamiliarization). But: **"what does it mean that this image exists in this web of relationships?"** (significance).

If operative ekphrasis collapses text/image, then the task of the post-AI ekphrast is not to describe images or protect them or estrange them — but to *thicken* them. To place them in a web of significance so dense that their meanings multiply beyond any single encoding.

This is why the WorldText cosmos exists. It is the thick description of a collapsed distinction.
