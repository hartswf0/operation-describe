## The Discovery

Geertz's thick description is not "more detail." It is **selected context that alters the identity of the act**. The six-layer formula that makes ethnography thick is structurally identical to the six-layer architecture that makes prompts effective. A thin prompt, like a thin description, reports behavior. A thick prompt, like a thick description, maps meaning.

This synthesis makes the mapping explicit, then demonstrates it.

---

## The Formula

Geertz's working formula (derived from the Cohen case) requires six layers per descriptive unit:

| Layer | Geertz's Thick Description | The Thick Prompt |
|-------|---------------------------|------------------|
| **1. Visible act** | What physically happens? | What do you want generated? (the literal request) |
| **2. Native act** | What do the participants take themselves to be doing? | What is the *intended meaning* of the output? (genre, purpose, audience address) |
| **3. Code** | What normative order makes the act intelligible? | What *style/convention/tradition* makes this output legible? (art movement, visual grammar, cultural reference) |
| **4. Audience** | Who is watching, recognizing, doubting, or misreading? | Who is the output *for*? What do they expect? What will they recognize or miss? |
| **5. Stakes** | What is really at risk? | What is riding on this generation? (aesthetic, functional, emotional, political significance) |
| **6. Reclassification** | What does the same act become in another interpretive setting? | How will this output be *re-read* in a different context? (cross-platform migration, different audiences, future reuse) |

**That last layer is the one nobody includes in prompts.** And it is precisely the one that makes images generated by AI as durable as images born from ekphrastic tradition.

## The Proof: Thin vs. Thick Prompting

### Example 1: The Shield

**Thin prompt (Layer 1 only):**
> "A golden shield with battle scenes"

This is Geertz's "he contracted his eyelid." It reports the visible act. The model will generate a generic fantasy shield — thin, meaningless, interchangeable.

**Thick prompt (all six layers):**

> **[1. Visible act]** A circular golden shield, hammered and embossed, depicting five concentric rings of scenes.
>
> **[2. Native act]** This is NOT a weapon but a cosmological map — Hephaestus making the divine craftsman's claim that the whole world can be compressed into a single artifact.
>
> **[3. Code]** Render in the style of late geometric period Greek metalwork (8th century BCE): flat perspective, repetitive figure patterns, horror vacui, no shadows, amber-gold patina with verdigris in the recesses. The code is Homeric — each scene is a complete narrative frozen mid-action.
>
> **[4. Audience]** This image will be viewed by scholars who know the Iliad and will read every iconographic choice as an argument. The shield must look like it *could* exist archaeologically — no fantasy gloss, no video-game aesthetics.
>
> **[5. Stakes]** The shield is the locus classicus of ekphrasis. It is the first object in Western literature to be described in the act of being made. Every choice here takes a position in a 2,700-year interpretive tradition.
>
> **[6. Reclassification]** When this image appears in a presentation alongside Titian's and Rubens's painted shields, it must hold its own — not as illustration but as a *visual argument* about what computational rendering can contribute to the shield tradition.

The difference is not verbosity. The difference is **situated significance**. Every layer changes what the shield IS.

### Example 2: The Wink

**Thin prompt:**
> "A man winking"

**Thick prompt:**

> **[1. Visible act]** A man's right eye closes rapidly.
>
> **[2. Native act]** He is not blinking involuntarily. He is conspiratorially winking — signaling to a specific person that what he just said publicly was not what he meant. The wink contains a SECRET.
>
> **[3. Code]** Mid-century American bar scene: dark mahogany, dim lighting, the visual grammar of film noir. The wink is legible within a code of masculine complicity and ironic distance.
>
> **[4. Audience]** The intended recipient of the wink is in frame — a woman at the end of the bar who may or may not have caught it. A third figure (the bartender) has ALSO seen it but pretends not to have. Three different readings of the same muscular contraction coexist simultaneously.
>
> **[5. Stakes]** The image is for a philosophy textbook chapter on interpretation. It must visually demonstrate that the SAME physical event (eyelid closure) carries radically different meanings depending on context. The image is the argument.
>
> **[6. Reclassification]** When a student sees this image without the caption, they should think "film still." When they read the caption, they should realize it was generated to illustrate Ryle's thought experiment. The reclassification — from cinematic moment to philosophical demonstration — IS the pedagogical point.

## Why This Works (Cybernetically)

The six layers are not decoration. They are **context that changes the meaning of every token**.

In transformer terms:
- **Layer 1** provides the **content tokens** (what to generate)
- **Layer 2** provides the **intent embedding** (what the output is FOR)
- **Layer 3** provides the **style conditioning** (which latent subspace to sample from)
- **Layer 4** provides the **evaluation frame** (what "good" means for this generation)
- **Layer 5** provides the **weight signal** (which aspects of the output matter most — attention allocation)
- **Layer 6** provides the **transfer context** (how the output will be re-contextualized — robustness to reclassification)

Without Layer 6, the output is fragile — it works in one context but falls apart when moved to another. A shield that looks great in a fantasy game looks absurd in an academic paper. A wink that works as illustration fails as art. **Reclassification is the test of thickness.**

## The Geertzian Sentence Architecture for Prompts

Geertz's writing template:

> **[Observable action]** becomes intelligible as **[social act]** because, within **[frame/code]**, **[actors]** understand it as **[recognized obligation/right/danger]**; however, when it enters **[rival frame/institution/place]**, the same action is reclassified as **[new meaning]**, exposing **[conflict, hierarchy, misunderstanding, or jurisdictional rupture]**.

Translated into prompt architecture:

> **Generate [visible content]** that reads as **[intended meaning]** because, within the conventions of **[style/tradition/genre]**, the viewer will recognize it as **[specific visual argument]**; however, when this image appears in **[different context/platform/audience]**, it must still function as **[alternative reading]**, demonstrating **[the image's resilience to reclassification]**.

This is a **thick-prompt sentence**. It does not merely request output. It maps meaning.

## The Paragraph Architecture: Event + Reclassification

Geertz's best unit of analysis is **event-plus-reclassification**. Each paragraph follows:

1. Start with the concrete act
2. Name the local meaning of the act
3. Specify the frame that authorizes that meaning
4. Show the audience that recognizes it
5. Show the threshold where another audience reclassifies it
6. End on what that reclassification does socially

For prompt engineering, the mapping is:

1. Start with the literal generation request
2. Name the intended meaning of the output
3. Specify the style/tradition/convention that makes it legible
4. Name the audience that will evaluate it
5. Anticipate the context where it will be RE-evaluated
6. End on what that re-evaluation demands of the image

**This is the architecture of a prompt that produces durable, thick images rather than disposable, thin ones.**

## The Cohen Case = The Prompt Engineering Case

Geertz's Cohen story is, structurally, the story of **every prompt interaction that goes wrong**:

| Cohen's Journey | The Prompt Engineer's Journey |
|----------------|-------------------------------|
| Cohen has a legitimate damage claim (his intent is clear) | The user has a clear mental image (their intent is clear) |
| He translates it into the mezrag code (tribal indemnity logic) | They translate it into prompt syntax (the model's input language) |
| The translation works within its native frame (he gets 500 sheep) | The generation works within its native frame (the model produces an image) |
| But when the output crosses into another frame (the French fort), it is reclassified | But when the output crosses into another context (print, presentation, social media), it is reclassified |
| The sheep become "evidence of rebel collusion" | The generated image becomes "AI slop" or "copyright violation" or "deep fake" |
| Cohen's act, legible in one code, is criminalized in another | The prompt engineer's output, legible as creative work in one context, is dismissed or prosecuted in another |
| **The problem is not the act but the misrecognition produced by rival semiotic-legal orders** | **The problem is not the image but the misrecognition produced by rival evaluative-legal orders** |

This is why thick prompting matters. A thin prompt produces output that ONLY works in the immediate generation context. A thick prompt anticipates reclassification and builds resilience into the image by embedding multiple layers of meaning that survive context-switching.

**Cohen's mistake was thin prompting**: he specified what he wanted (indemnity) in only one code (mezrag) and did not anticipate how the output (sheep) would be reclassified by a rival frame (French colonial law). A thick-prompting Cohen would have anticipated the reclassification and built the claim in terms legible to BOTH codes simultaneously.

## The Working Formula for Thick Prompts

For any generation task, each prompt should contain six layers:

**First, the visible output.**
What should the viewer literally see?

**Second, the native meaning.**
What are you trying to SAY with this image? What act is it performing?

**Third, the code.**
Within what visual/cultural/generic tradition is this output legible? What makes it recognizable?

**Fourth, the audience.**
Who will evaluate this output? What are their expectations? What visual literacy do they bring?

**Fifth, the stakes.**
What rides on this generation? Is it illustration, argument, art, documentation, persuasion? What failure mode matters most?

**Sixth, the reclassification.**
Where else will this output appear? How will it be re-read? What must survive the context-switch?

**A prompt that contains all six layers is thick. A prompt that contains only the first is thin.** The difference is not length — you can write a concise thick prompt. The difference is whether the prompt maps meaning or merely reports desired content.
