## The Argument the Cosmos Has Suppressed

The WorldText cosmos has been built as though ekphrasis were a *formal* problem — word vs. image, space vs. time, natural sign vs. arbitrary sign. But at least three sources in the corpus insist that ekphrasis is also, irreducibly, a **political** problem.

### 1. Kennedy: The Missing Gender

Kennedy (2018) makes a piercing observation buried in his survey of intermedial typologies: **"What is missing from all these typologies is gender."** Women artists — Eva Hesse, Lygia Clark — were exploring intermediality before the theorists named it. Clark's concept of the **"fantasmatic"** — "something not reducible to visibility... a term that covers the sense of experiencing something without a real or identifiable referent" (Best 2014:140, via Kennedy) — anticipates both:

- Krieger's "illusion of the natural sign" (images produced by words that resist visual verification)
- Bajohr's "artificial semantics" (meaning produced by machines that cannot be confidently called non-meaning)

But Clark arrived there through **sculptural practice**, not theory. The cosmos has no entity page for her. No world acknowledges the gendered history of who gets to define the intermedial. **The theorists cited in every world page — Krieger, Mitchell, Lessing, Heffernan, Bajohr — are all men.** Webb, Brosch, Mathieson, Louvel, Rippl, Fjellestad, Goehr, Bundschuh are women — but their contributions have been absorbed into a theoretical framework whose grammar was set by men.

### 2. Rippl: Ekphrasis as Postcolonial Critique

Rippl (2018) demonstrates that ekphrasis in contemporary Anglophone transcultural fiction — Teju Cole, Taiye Selasi, NoViolet Bulawayo — is not about the formal relationship between word and image. It is about **cultural memory, trauma, and power**:

> "Ekphrasis as a social practice translates between self and other... 'transfers into the realm of literary art sublimated versions of our ambivalence about social others'" — Mitchell 1992:702, via Rippl 2018:269

When Cole's character in *Open City* describes a painting, the ekphrasis is not performing Krieger's "illusion of the natural sign." It is performing **the colonial gaze** — or resisting it. The question is not "can words be pictures?" but **"whose pictures do these words serve?"**

### 3. Schröter via Kennedy: "Good" and "Bad" Intermediality

Kennedy summarizes Schröter's politicized reading:
- **"Good" intermediality** = the breakdown of the capitalist division of labor. Media crossing borders = liberation.
- **"Bad" intermediality** = capitulation to capitalist spectacle. Medium-mixing = marketized fusion.

This directly maps to the AI debate:
- **"Good" operative ekphrasis** = democratized image creation, non-experts generating art, body prompting as embodied liberation
- **"Bad" operative ekphrasis** = corporate platforms monetizing prompt work, training on unconsented labor, hallucination presented as knowledge

The cosmos has been operating as though operative ekphrasis were value-neutral — a structural phenomenon. **It is not.** DALL·E is owned by OpenAI. Stable Diffusion was trained on scraped images. The prompt-craft celebrated by Lindley & Whitham takes place inside commercial products.

## The Thick Description Connection

Geertz's thick description was also, at origin, a **political act**. The anthropologist describing a Balinese cockfight was doing cultural translation — presenting a non-Western practice to a Western academic audience. The politics of that translation (who speaks for whom? who profits from the description? who is the "subject" and who is the "object"?) are precisely the politics Rippl identifies in postcolonial ekphrasis.

**The cosmos cannot be thick without being political.** A thick description that ignores power relations is thin by definition.

## Non-Obvious Structural Rhyme

| Domain | Division | Enforcer | Transgressor |
|--------|----------|----------|-------------|
| Classical arts | word ÷ image | Lessing (*Laocoon*) | Homer (Shield) |
| Gender | theorist ÷ practitioner | Male canon (Krieger, Mitchell) | Female practitioners (Clark, Hesse) |
| Colonialism | Self ÷ Other | Western gaze | Transcultural ekphrasis (Cole, Selasi) |
| Labor | creator ÷ tool | Platform owner (OpenAI) | Prompt worker / body prompter |
| AI | human ÷ machine | Copyright law | Generated images |

In each row, the **left column** describes a division that ekphrasis either enforces or transgresses, the **enforcer** is an institutional authority, and the **transgressor** is a practice that crosses the boundary. Thick description requires attending to all five rows simultaneously.

## What This Means for the Cosmos

The WorldText cosmos is currently organized around formal-structural axes: Galaxy of Ekphrastic Worlds (text/image), Galaxy of Intermedial Worlds (media crossing), Galaxy of Generative AI Worlds (computation), Galaxy of Thick Description (interpretation). **None of these galaxies has a political axis.**

This synthesis proposes that the political dimension is not a galaxy but a **gravitational field** — an invisible force that bends the trajectory of every world, entity, and distinction. Like dark matter, it shapes the cosmos without being visible in the taxonomy.

The Dark Matter audit identified 463 placeholder entries. The political audit identifies a different kind of darkness: **the structural silences about gender, colonialism, and labor that pervade the ekphrastic canon.** These silences are not gaps in the evidence — the evidence is present (Kennedy, Rippl, Schröter, Cole, Clark) — but gaps in the interpretive framework.
