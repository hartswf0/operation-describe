# From Imagetext to Worldtext: Generative AI as Operative Ekphrasis

**Watson Hartsoe & Jay David Bolter**

> Draft v2 — Compiled from the OPERATION DESCRIBE worldtext.

---

## Abstract

Generative AI platforms use prompts to create images, videos, and interactive media. The act of prompting can be understood as a form of ekphrasis — the ancient practice of vivid verbal evocation that was originally defined not by subject matter but by effect: turning hearing into sight. The crucial difference is that unlike traditional ekphrastic poetry and prose, the ekphrasis of prompting is, as Hannes Bajohr has argued, "operative": the text not only describes an image but generates it, collapsing the distinction between the verbal and the visual at the computational level. We argue that this collapse is not an invention but a recovery — a restoration of the ancient production-oriented definition that was concealed when twentieth-century criticism silently narrowed ekphrasis from "vivid speech that produces seeing" to "verbal representation of visual representation." Prompt and image together constitute an imagetext, which W.J.T. Mitchell theorized as both the aspiration and the nightmare of the ekphrastic tradition. But the imagetext's arrival does not resolve the ancient tension between word and image; it displaces it. The old paragone gives way to a new one: intent versus distribution — the prompter's creative will against the training data's statistical gravity. Operative ekphrasis thus offers new perspectives on what Krieger called the "illusion of the natural sign," while proposing an alternate paradigm for ekphrastic representation — one that favors multiplicity over seamless transparency and process over finished product. Drawing on Clifford Geertz's thick description, we propose a six-layer "thick prompt" architecture whose critical addition is reclassification-awareness: anticipating how an output will be re-read when it crosses into a hostile evaluative frame. We demonstrate this architecture against the canonical stress test of the Shield of Achilles, showing that the shield cannot be generated by a single prompt but requires a compiled workflow — a context stack — that reveals the operative unit of generative ekphrasis to be not the prompt but the computational environment. As generative systems extend from images to immersive 3D environments, the imagetext becomes a worldtext: a verbally specified interactive environment that functions not as a description but as a constitution. The worldtext operates under two distinct regimes — the probabilistic (where language steers a distribution over possible worlds) and the hybrid-ontological (where language legislates rules and behaviors) — each reconfiguring the relationship between word and world. We illustrate these claims by generating imagetexts and worldtexts from what is often regarded as the first ekphrasis in Western literature: Homer's Shield of Achilles — an artifact that was always a worldtext compressed into five concentric rings. We just didn't have the substrate to run it.

**Keywords**: ekphrasis, thick description, prompt engineering, text-to-image, generative AI, imagetext, worldtext, context engineering

## §1. Introduction: The Wink and the Text Box

In 1973, Clifford Geertz sat at a desk and described a wink. Not the physiology of the wink — the ontology of it. What makes a twitch into a wink? Context. Shared code. The accumulation of social knowledge that allows one person to compress an intention into a muscular spasm and another person to decompress it back into meaning. Without the shared code, there is no wink. There is only a twitch. The observer must infer (Geertz, 1973, pp. 6–7).

In 2024, a graphic designer — call her W — sat at a desk and typed a description of a woman at a writing desk into a text-to-image model. The description was not for a reader. It was for a machine. The machine decompressed the language into an image in eleven seconds. W looked at it and experienced a recognition that had nothing to do with the machine: the image was a twitch. It looked correct. It was meaningless. It was the visual equivalent of a contracted eyelid — technically accurate, culturally empty, a statistical average dressed in Vermeer's lighting. It was not what she meant.

W deleted the image and started over. In doing so, she joined a lineage she did not know she belonged to.

This paper traces that lineage. Its argument is that the act W performed — composing language that produces visual experience in a receiver — is not a new media event but a 2,500-year-old cognitive operation that the twentieth century concealed and that generative AI has computationally restored. The operation is *operative ekphrasis*: the production, not the representation, of images through words.

We make five claims:

1. Operative ekphrasis is a recovery, not an invention.
2. The imagetext is the terrain, not the destination: a new paragone has replaced the old one.
3. Thickness is reclassification-awareness: Geertz's thick description provides the architecture for prompting that survives transport across evaluative frames.
4. The Shield of Achilles breaks the prompt paradigm, revealing the context stack as the operative unit.
5. The worldtext — the context stack made spatial — is the theoretical horizon toward which operative ekphrasis has always pointed.

## §2. Operative Ekphrasis Is a Recovery, Not an Invention

### 2.1  The Ancient Definition Was Already Operative

Begin in the classroom — not a modern one, but a Roman school of the first or second century CE, where students work through a curriculum called the *progymnasmata*: standardized exercises in composition, ordered from simple (narration) to complex (legislation). Near the top of the sequence sits *ekphrasis*: originally not "the description of an artwork" — that restriction is a twentieth-century invention — but any vivid verbal evocation of persons, battles, places, times, seasons, crocodiles (Webb, 1999, p. 11).

Four textbook authors, spanning four centuries — Theon (1st c.), Hermogenes (2nd c.), Aphthonios (4th c.), Nikolaos (5th c.) — define ekphrasis in nearly identical terms: *logos periegematikos*, "a speech that leads one around, bringing the subject matter vividly (*enargōs*) before the eyes." Ruth Webb (1999) recovered this definition from obscurity and demonstrated what it means: ekphrasis, in antiquity, was defined not by subject matter but by *effect on the audience*. It was a technology for making listeners into spectators. The word carries this purpose in its etymology: *ek* (thoroughly) + *phrazo* (to tell). To ecphrase is to tell so completely that the telling *produces* seeing.

Pseudo-Longinos compressed the entire operation into six words: *tēn akoēn opsin poiōn* — "turning hearing into sight" (*On the Sublime* 15.1). This is not metaphor. It is a technical specification. The ancient orator's speech did not represent a visual scene. It **produced** visual experience in the listener's mind. The means were neural — the listener's imagination activated by vivid language — rather than computational — tokens conditioning a denoising process. But the operation was identical: verbal input → visual output, mediated by a shared representational substrate.

Quintilian explains the mechanism (*Institutio Oratoria* 6.2.29–32): the orator must first conjure the scene in their own mind's eye — must "see it yourself first." Only then will the language "spark a mental impression in the mind of the audience." The skill is internal simulation followed by verbal encoding: run the image through your own inference engine before transmitting the compressed signal. And the receiver? Webb, drawing on ancient memory theory, explains: "Language, particularly language which has the quality of enargeia, is produced by such images (in the mind of the speaker) and gives rise to other, comparable, images in the listener's mind" (Webb, 1999, p. 115). The listener's mind is not a blank slate. It is a "storehouse of images derived from sense perception" — a repository of prior visual experience, organized by cultural convention, that the orator's words activate.

The orator does not transmit images. The orator transmits *coordinates* in the listener's storehouse, and the listener reconstructs the image from local memory. Quintilian's storehouse is the first training distribution. The orator's enargeia is the first prompt engineering. The listener's reconstruction is the first inference pass.

### 2.2  The Narrowing

Something happened between antiquity and the present. The word *ekphrasis* lost its breadth. Webb (1999) traces the narrowing through five institutional thresholds:

1. **Friedrich Matz (1867)** studied Philostratos's *Imagines* — descriptions of paintings — and used *ekphrasis* only in the context of art-description. He did not redefine the term, but his focus began the gravitational pull.
2. **Bertrand (1881) and Bougot (1881)** grouped Philostratos with Catullus and Virgil under "ekphrasis" and identified these as members of "a fashionable genre which has its own name." Bertrand relegated the word to a footnote, untransliterated, as if uncertain of his own move.
3. **Leo Spitzer (1955)**: The decisive act. Spitzer wrote, with an authority that masked novelty: "*ekphrasis, the poetic description of a pictorial or sculptural work of art.*" Before this sentence, the term was a technical word in classical philology with its ancient broad meaning. After it, the term belonged to comparative literature with a new narrow one.
4. **Jean Hagstrum (1958)** defended the restriction through etymology: *ekphrasis* as a "speaking out" applied specifically to art objects — creative but historically unsupported.
5. **James Heffernan (1993)** completed the narrowing: "*the verbal representation of visual representation.*" This definition, which requires the described object to itself be a representation, would have excluded most of what the ancient rhetoricians called ekphrasis.

Each step subtracted meaning. Each step appeared to simply clarify. Nobody metacommunicated about the change — nobody said "we are redefining this word." The new meaning was presented, at each threshold, as the original meaning. This is, in Gregory Bateson's terms, a double bind at the disciplinary level: the message ("ekphrasis means X") arrived with a metamessage ("and it has always meant X") that concealed the discrepancy (Bateson, 1972, pp. 206–208). The field could not detect the change because the change denied being a change.

### 2.3  The Restoration

The narrowing concealed the operative structure of the ancient definition. When ekphrasis meant "any vivid speech that produces seeing in the listener," it was obviously a production technology — a communication protocol designed to activate the receiver's internal image-generation capacity. When ekphrasis was narrowed to "verbal representation of visual representation," it became a *mimetic* technology — a second-order representation whose quality was measured by fidelity to a pre-existing image. The narrowing turned ekphrasis from a production protocol into a reproduction benchmark. The word stopped being operative and became representational.

Hannes Bajohr's "operative ekphrasis" (2024) is, among other things, a repair. Bajohr argues that in multimodal AI the text/image distinction collapses: text and image are already the same data type — tokenized, embedded, weighted in a single statistical manifold. The prompt does not represent a pre-existing image but *produces* one. Bajohr's key finding is neurological as well as semiotic: CLIP develops "grandmother neurons" — single artificial neurons that fire for both a *photograph* of Spiderman and the *text string* "Spiderman." At the neural level, the word IS the image.

But our genealogical evidence suggests Bajohr is not introducing a new kind of ekphrasis. He is recovering the ancient kind. Pseudo-Longinos's six words — "turning hearing into sight" — already specified the operation. The *progymnasmata* already taught students to perform it. The Spitzer narrowing concealed it. The operative turn is a return.

> **The Spitzer narrowing was a reclassification event in the Geertzian sense.** The word "ekphrasis" passed through five institutional thresholds and was reclassified at each: from "vivid speech that produces seeing" to "verbal description of an artwork." Nobody metacommunicated about the change. Operative ekphrasis breaks the double bind by making the production-function visible again — computationally, publicly, undeniably.

## §3. The Imagetext Is the Terrain, Not the Destination

### 3.1  Mitchell's Imagetext Has Arrived

W. J. T. Mitchell (1994) argued that the merging of word and image — the "imagetext" — was both the audacious aspiration and the nightmare of ekphrasis. Ekphrastic hope: the word *can* become the image. Ekphrastic fear: the image makes the word obsolete. Ekphrastic indifference: the recognition that the two media are incommensurable and the effort is not worth making.

Operative ekphrasis appears to realize hope and fear simultaneously. In a text-to-image model, the prompt IS the image's source code. The imagetext has arrived. Mitchell's three-phase phenomenology can be observed in compressed form during a single prompting session: hope (the user types, expecting the model to see what they see), fear (the output exceeds or distorts the prompt in ways the user did not control), indifference (the user accepts that text and image are doing different things even in the same pipeline).

But the imagetext's arrival does not resolve the tension that drove the ekphrastic tradition. It *displaces* it.

### 3.2  The New Paragone: Intent vs. Distribution

If word and image are now the same data type, the old paragone — Leonardo's contest between poetry and painting, Lessing's *Laocoön* boundary — is dead. Nobody can seriously argue that "words cannot represent images" when a sentence produces a photorealistic scene in eleven seconds. The paragone is over. Good.

What replaces it is worse.

The successor tension is not between media but between forces: **intent versus distribution.** The prompter's creative will against the training data's statistical gravity. This is the new paragone, and unlike the old one, it is constitutive and unresolvable.

Hintze, Proschinger Åström, and Schossau (2026) provide the empirical proof. When text-to-image and image-to-text models are placed in autonomous loops — no human in the circuit — the output converges to approximately twelve generic visual motifs: lighthouses, coastal sunsets, lone trees, silhouetted figures at golden hour. Dellinger (2025) reports the finding accessibly: "AI image generators default to the same 12 photo styles." This is the platform mean made empirical. The twelve lighthouses are what the imagetext becomes when intent → 0. They are the imagetext at rest — the system's ground state, its minimum-energy configuration. Visual elevator music.

**The human's contribution to operative ekphrasis is therefore not decorative — it is dynamical.** The prompter's thick description is the semantic pressure that pushes the imagetext away from the twelve lighthouses toward a specific region of the latent space. Remove the human from the loop: you get convergent generic imagery. Restore the human: you get the particular.

Roland Meyer's "platform realism" (2023, 2025) provides the theoretical frame for the distribution pole. The latent space's defaults are not neutral; they reflect the training data's demographics, the platform's economic incentives, and the cultural dominance of stock photography and Western visual conventions. The default outputs of Midjourney, DALL·E, and Stable Diffusion look the way they do not because of any artistic intention but because the training data is dominated by commercially licensed photography: golden hour, clean surfaces, photorealistic skin, shallow depth of field. Hito Steyerl (2023) calls this "mean images" — images that converge to the statistical average of visual culture. Meyer's reversal is the key provocation: "Operative ekphrasis says the word produces the image. Platform realism says the word navigates toward an image already latent in the statistical space."

The resolution is scale-dependent. At the technical level, the word *does* produce — it conditions the denoising process that iterates from noise to image. At the cultural level, the product *is* constrained by the distribution that supplies the material. Production and navigation are not contradictory but complementary descriptions of the same act at different resolutions. Bajohr's operativity and Meyer's platform realism are scale-dependent descriptions of the same phenomenon.

We name the physics of this tension **viscosity**: the latent space's resistance to the prompt's pressure. High viscosity: the output defaults to the platform mean regardless of the prompt (stock photography, the twelve lighthouses). Low viscosity: the output yields to the prompt's specifications (creative rupture, stylistic departure). The thick prompt is the force applied against the distribution's viscosity. Not to escape the distribution — that is impossible; you cannot infer outside your prior — but to navigate within it toward a region the default would not reach.

> **The imagetext is the terrain. The new paragone — intent vs. distribution, agency vs. inertia — is the game played on it.** The old paragone (word vs. image) was resolvable by technological collapse. The new one (intent vs. distribution) is constitutive and unresolvable, because without intent the system produces the mean, and without distribution the system produces noise. Both forces are required. What varies is the ratio.

### 3.3  Two Paths: The Seamless and the Exposed

The new paragone manifests as an aesthetic fork that reconfigures ekphrasis's oldest tensions. On one side: the **seamless path** — photorealism, hidden mediation, the output designed to look as if no machine were involved. This is Krieger's "illusion of the natural sign" (1992) achieved computationally: the word has become the image, and the image denies its verbal origin. The viewer should *not* know the image is AI-generated. The seamless path is the default aesthetic of platform realism — immediacy, in Bolter and Grusin's terms (1999).

On the other side: the **exposed path** — multiplicity foregrounded, process visible, the act of generation itself made the subject. This is ekphrastic fear revalued as aesthetic principle: the viewer *should* see the prompting, the iteration, the gap between intent and output. The exposed path foregrounds what the seamless path conceals: that the image was made from words, by a statistical process, through a negotiation between human intent and machine distribution. This is hypermediacy — the tradition that runs from Brecht's Verfremdungseffekt through collage to net art.

The decision between seamless and exposed is the decisive aesthetic question of operative ekphrasis. Will the system hide or expose the act of world-compilation? Neither path is inherently superior. But the choice determines the image's relationship to its own production — and therefore its legibility when it crosses evaluative frames. The seamless image claims indexical authority ("this is real"). The exposed image claims process authority ("this was made, and here is how"). The former inherits photography's contested relationship to truth. The latter inaugurates its own.

## §4. Thickness Is Reclassification-Awareness

### 4.1  Geertz's Six Layers

Thick description is not "more detail." It is **selected context that alters the identity of the act** (Geertz, 1973, pp. 6–10). Geertz's working formula, derived from Gilbert Ryle's wink/twitch distinction and the Cohen case, requires six layers per descriptive unit:

| Layer | Geertz's Thick Description | The Thick Prompt |
|-------|---------------------------|------------------|
| **1. Visible act** | What physically happens | What do you want generated |
| **2. Native meaning** | What do the participants take themselves to be doing | What is the *intended meaning* of the output |
| **3. Code** | What normative order makes the act intelligible | What style/convention/tradition makes the output legible |
| **4. Audience** | Who is watching, recognizing, doubting | Who is the output *for* — what do they expect |
| **5. Stakes** | What is really at risk | What is riding on this generation |
| **6. Reclassification** | What does the same act become in another setting | How will this output be re-read in a different context |

The structural identity between thick description and thick prompting is not metaphorical. A thin prompt, like a thin description, reports behavior ("a golden shield with battle scenes"). A thick prompt, like a thick description, maps meaning across frames. The difference is not length. The difference is whether the encoding carries enough contextual density to survive transport.

### 4.2  Cohen's Sheep Are the Generated Image

Geertz's Cohen vignette is, structurally, the story of every prompt interaction that goes wrong. A Jewish trader in colonial Morocco seizes 500 sheep as indemnity under Berber customary law — the *mezrag* code. The sheep are legitimate damages in the tribal frame. They are contraband in the French colonial frame. They are "an indiscretion" in the captain's equivocal frame. They are evidence of "theft" in the colonel's judicial frame. **The sheep are the same sheep at every threshold.** Only the frame changes, and with it the meaning, and with it the consequence.

Cohen's mistake was thin encoding: he specified his claim in only one code and did not anticipate how the output would be reclassified when it crossed the threshold of the fort. The prompt engineer's mistake is the same: specifying the output in only one evaluative frame and not anticipating how it will be re-read in a different context.

| Cohen's Journey | The Prompt Engineer's Journey |
|----------------|-------------------------------|
| Cohen has a clear intent (recover damages) | The user has a clear mental image |
| He encodes it in a specific code (*mezrag*) | They encode it in prompt syntax |
| The encoding works natively (500 sheep negotiated) | The generation works natively (image produced) |
| The output crosses into a rival frame (the fort) | The output crosses into a rival context (different audience, platform, evaluative standard) |
| The output is reclassified (damages → contraband) | The output is reclassified ("creative work" → "AI slop" / "copyright violation") |
| Cohen is ruined — not by the act but by the misrecognition | The work is dismissed — not by its quality but by its reclassification |

### 4.3  Layer 6: The Critical Addition

**Layer 6 — reclassification-awareness — is the critical contribution.** No existing prompt-engineering framework includes it. Liu and Chilton (2023) study subject and style keywords. Oppenlaender (2024) maps prompt modifiers. Almeda et al. (2024) track sense-making strategies. Brade et al. (2023) model prompt exploration trajectories. None asks: *how will this output be re-read when it crosses into a different evaluative frame?*

The thick-prompt architecture adds this layer because Geertz's method requires it. The Cohen case demonstrates that an act's meaning is not fixed at production but changes at every threshold it crosses. The ethnographer who records only the visible act (Layer 1) produces thin ethnography — behavior reports. The ethnographer who anticipates how the same act will be reclassified in a rival institutional setting (Layer 6) produces thick ethnography — meaning that survives transport. The prompter who specifies only the desired output produces thin imagery — images that work in one frame. The prompter who anticipates the colonel — the hostile evaluative frame — produces thick imagery: outputs whose contextual density allows them to survive reclassification.

Lindley and Whitham's (2025) Shadowplay installation demonstrates the operational consequence. Participants cast shadows that were transformed in real time by StreamDiffusion. Without the text prompt "Lotte Reiniger" in the system, the AI defaulted to **war photography** — because high-contrast monochrome images in the training data are predominantly war images. Adding "Lotte Reiniger" — a single named entity, a stylistic attractor — steered the generation from the war-photography basin to the shadow-puppet aesthetic. One name. One thick token. The entire character of the output changed.

This is Layer 3 (Code) doing the work of viscosity navigation. Lindley and Whitham did not add "more words." They added the *right* word — the word that navigated from one attractor basin to another. Their distinction between "prompt engineering" (technical optimization) and "prompt craft" (situated, context-aware practice) aligns with the thick/thin distinction. Engineering optimizes the token. Craft selects the context.

> **Thickness is the encoding that survives the fort.** A thick prompt does not eliminate hostile readings — no thickness can do that, any more than thickness could prevent the colonel from reclassifying Cohen's sheep. But it builds enough legibility into the signal that the output survives at least one more threshold than a thin prompt would. The six layers are not "more detail." They are the minimum architecture for producing outputs that survive transport.

## §5. The Shield Breaks the Prompt Paradigm

### 5.1  Hephaestus as the First Prompter

The Shield of Achilles (Homer, *Iliad* XVIII.478–608) is our benchmark not merely because it is the canonical ekphrasis but because it specifies not an image but a world: cosmology, agriculture, adjudication, dance, war, the ocean at the rim. The shield is a world-specification compressed into a metalwork artifact.

Homer depicts the shield *in the process of its making*. Hephaestus does not describe a pre-existing shield. He fabricates it — layer by layer, scene by scene, from materials (bronze, tin, gold, silver) — as we watch. The text is a craft-log of generative production. "He made [ἐν δ᾽ ἐτίθει] the earth, the sky, the sea, the sun, the moon, the constellations" — each line is an instruction executed on the substrate. The operative ekphrasis is already in Homer: the text does not represent the shield but *compiles* it.

### 5.2  The Oral-Formulaic System as the First Generative Model

Parry-Lord theory (Lord, 1960) holds that Homeric poetry was composed in performance from a repertoire of formulaic expressions — metrical building-blocks honed over generations. The system is probabilistic: no two performances are identical, but all draw from the same distribution of inherited patterns. "Rosy-fingered dawn." "Swift-footed Achilles." "The wine-dark sea." These are not decorative adjectives. They are formulaic building blocks — metrically pre-fitted units that the singer deploys in real time by selecting from a learned repertoire conditioned on the demands of the moment.

This is structurally identical to autoregressive generation. The oral-formulaic poet does not memorize texts; the poet learns a grammar of patterns and composes by selecting, combining, and adapting them. As Lord wrote: "The singer of tales is at once the tradition and an individual talent. His art consists not in departing from the tradition but in *composing within it*" (Lord, 1960, p. 13). Replace "singer" with "model" and the sentence describes a language model with structural precision.

The formulaic epithet IS a latent-space attractor. "Swift-footed Achilles" is to the Homeric system what "Lotte Reiniger" is to Stable Diffusion: a named token that navigates to a specific aesthetic-semantic region. The claim is not metaphorical. Both systems absorb a vast corpus without memorizing specific texts, learn statistical regularities, generate novel output in real time by predicting what comes next, and cannot reproduce their training data verbatim. Both occupy the space between invention and convention that Romantic aesthetics could not theorize.

The Shield of Achilles — the ur-text of all ekphrasis — was not written. It was *generated* by a pattern-completing oral system. The first ekphrasis was already operative.

### 5.3  Auden's Reclassification

W. H. Auden's "The Shield of Achilles" (1952) is Layer 6 performed across twenty-seven centuries. Auden takes Homer's prompt — "make a shield" — and feeds it through a different distribution: the mid-twentieth century. Thetis expects the Homeric world:

> *She looked over his shoulder*
> *For vines and olive trees,*
> *Marble well-governed cities*
> *And ships upon untamed seas*

What Hephaestus forges is the twentieth century:

> *An artificial wilderness*
> *And a sky like lead*

Barbed wire, bored officials, a ragged urchin who has "never heard / Of any world where promises were kept."

The same prompt. Different distribution. Different output. Auden's poem IS platform realism made lyric: the Hephaestus of 1952 cannot forge olive trees because the world's viscosity — its dominant statistical reality — is barbed wire. The audience expects one generation; the maker's distribution delivers another. Thetis is the originary disappointed user: the first person to commission a visual work, watch it being made, and discover the output didn't match her expectations. Every prompter who has stared at an unexpected DALL·E output is replaying Iliad XVIII.

### 5.4  The Benchmark That Breaks Prompting

The shield is not just a demonstration but a **stress test** for the current prompt paradigm. The shield cannot be generated by a single text string because it specifies five concentric rings containing distinct scenes — cosmological, agricultural, juridical, martial, festive, oceanic — with internal contrasts, narrative logic, and spatial organization.

A thin prompt ("create Achilles' shield") produces a generic fantasy artifact — the platform mean for "ancient Greek shield" in the latent space. This is a twelve-lighthouses output: the shield at rest, the system's ground state.

Producing the shield *as Homer describes it* requires a compiled workflow:

1. **Geometric specification** — five concentric rings with distinct boundaries
2. **Domain partition** — each ring mapped to a distinct narrative domain (cosmological, civic, agricultural, martial, festive)
3. **Per-zone extraction** — Homeric text parsed into zone-specific imagery
4. **Material constraints** — bronze, tin, gold, silver; metalwork aesthetics, not digital illustration
5. **Compositing** — zones assembled into a unified circular artifact with the Ocean at the rim

This workflow is not a prompt. It is a **context stack** — a layered architecture of instructions, constraints, knowledge, and output specifications that together compile a visual world. The shield reveals that the relevant unit of operative ekphrasis is not the prompt but the context stack. Not a sentence but an architecture. Not a flat string but a compiled pipeline that binds language to structured world-making.

> The shield is the argument in miniature. It demonstrates that operative ekphrasis, at sufficient complexity, cannot be performed by prompt engineering. It requires **context engineering** — the design of the entire computational environment in which generation occurs.

## §6. The Worldtext Is the Context Stack Made Spatial

### 6.1  From Prompt to Context Stack to Constitution

Andrej Karpathy's analogy provides the computational bridge: "The LLM is the CPU. The context window is the RAM" (Karpathy, 2024). If the LLM is the CPU, then the imagetext is a single instruction — a prompt that produces one output. The worldtext is the full operating system: the stack that manages the environment in which instructions execute.

The context stack is a minimal worldtext:

| Stack Layer | Worldtext Function |
|------------|-------------------|
| **L0** (Hardware/Model) | The world's physics — what is computationally possible |
| **L1** (System instruction) | The world's governance — what behavior is permitted |
| **L2** (Knowledge context) | The world's memory — what evidence is available |
| **L3** (Tool context) | The world's available actions — what rituals can be performed |
| **L4** (Conversation history) | The world's chronicle — what has already happened |
| **L5** (User instruction) | The world's current event — what is happening now |
| **L6** (Output constraints) | The world's thresholds — what boundaries apply |

When you build a context stack, you are building a world for the model to inhabit. This is operative ekphrasis at the architectural level: not describing an image but constituting a reality.

### 6.2  The Qualitative Shift

The worldtext is not merely a bigger imagetext. It is a qualitative shift. The imagetext merges word and image into a single object. The worldtext merges word, image, space, time, interaction, and governance into a single *environment*. The prompt is no longer a description (imagetext) but a **constitution** (worldtext) — it defines not what the image looks like but what the world *does*, what is possible within it, what constraints apply, what defaults govern.

The shift from imagetext to worldtext recapitulates a pattern familiar from media history: the shift from the single instruction (the punch card, the search query, the prompt) to the persistent environment (the operating system, the platform, the context stack). Each transition involved the same structural move: from a one-shot command to a governed space that persists across interactions and develops its own history.

### 6.3  Two Regimes

As generative systems evolve from image generation to video, simulation, and interactive 3D environments, the worldtext operates under two distinct representational regimes:

**The probabilistic regime.** In systems like OpenAI's Sora and Google DeepMind's Genie, language steers a distribution over possible worlds. The worldtext is a conditioned probability field: the prompt specifies a region of the latent space, and the model generates a navigable environment by sampling from that region. The environment does not obey deterministic physics — it obeys *statistical physics*. Objects behave as they do because the training data shows them behaving that way, not because any physical law is encoded. A ball falls because balls fall in videos, not because gravity is simulated. The world is coherent but underdetermined: every frame is an inference, and hallucination is architecturally inevitable.

This regime is the direct descendant of operative ekphrasis. The word does not describe a world; it *conditions the probability of a world*. Enargeia — the ancient quality that made absent things present — operates here as statistical conditioning: the prompt activates a region of the trained distribution, and the model denoises from that region into a spatial, temporal, interactive experience. The limit of this regime is its indifference to causation: it can produce a world that *looks* governed, but the governance is aesthetic, not logical.

**The hybrid-ontological regime.** In systems that combine neural generation with deterministic engines — 3D Gaussian splats rendered within physics-based simulation environments, procedural world-building with AI-generated assets, game engines driven by LLM-authored rules — language does not steer a probability distribution. Language **legislates**. The prompt specifies not a visual feeling but a set of rules, constraints, and behaviors. Objects fall because gravity is coded. Characters act because their behavior trees are authored. The world is not inferred but *compiled*.

This regime inherits a different strand of operative ekphrasis: the *constitutional* strand. Homer's shield is not a dream of a world but a *specification* of one — cosmology at center, human society in the middle rings, the Ocean at the rim. The hybrid-ontological worldtext is a shield: it compiles a world from instructions, and the world runs deterministically within its specified constraints.

Neither regime is inherently superior. The probabilistic worldtext is more immediate, more surprising, more plastic — closer to the dream-logic of ancient phantasia. The hybrid-ontological worldtext is more durable, more governable, more complex — closer to the legislative ambition of Homer's Hephaestus. The former hallucinates. The latter is brittle. The future likely involves their convergence: worlds that are legislated at the structural level and dreamed at the surface level. Physics from code. Textures from statistics. Rules from language. Appearance from distribution.

### 6.4  Homer Already Knew

The Shield of Achilles is a worldtext. It always was. Consider its structure:

- **Center**: Earth, sky, sea, sun, moon, constellations — cosmological governance
- **Ring 2**: A city at peace (wedding, legal dispute) and a city at war (siege, ambush) — civic order
- **Ring 3**: Plowing, harvest, vineyard, cattle — agricultural cycles
- **Ring 4**: Dance — human joy, communal ritual
- **Rim**: The great river of Ocean — the boundary, the threshold, the edge beyond which the world-model has no training data

This is not an image. It is a world-specification. It has physics (the sun and moon govern time). It has governance (the legal dispute). It has ecology (harvest and cattle). It has culture (the dance). And it has a boundary condition (Ocean). The shield IS a context stack. Homer did not write a prompt. He wrote a constitution — a set of interdependent specifications that, taken together, compile a world.

We just didn't have the substrate to run it.

> **The shift from imagetext to worldtext is the shift from prompt to context stack — from a sentence that generates an image to an architecture that generates a world.** The thick prompter operates at L5 (user instruction). The context engineer operates at L0–L6 — building the system prompt that governs behavior, the knowledge base that populates the world, the tool definitions that specify available actions, and the output constraints that define the world's thresholds. The worldtext is not a prompt. It is a world. And the skill of building it is not prompt engineering but **context engineering** — the architecture of the operative environment.

## §7. Three Complications

The five claims above compose a forward argument: from ancient operative ekphrasis, through the imagetext's arrival, through thick prompting and the Shield, to the worldtext as the horizon. But three things the argument says must be immediately complicated, or the paper itself becomes thin.

### 7.1  "The Prompt Produces the Image" — Only Half True

The prompt produces *conditioned on the distribution.* The distribution — shaped by LAION-5B, by web scraping, by stock photography's demographics, by the cultural biases of English-language captions — is co-author. At the token level, Bajohr is right: the prompt produces. At the cultural level, Meyer is right: the prompt navigates. At the session level, the feedback loop between prompter and model negotiates between the two. Production and navigation are not competing theories. They are scale-dependent descriptions of the same act — the way "the Earth orbits the Sun" and "the Sun-Earth system orbits a common center of mass" are both true at different resolutions.

### 7.2  "Thick Prompting Improves the Output" — Frame-Dependent

The thick prompt pushes the output away from the platform mean. Whether "away from the mean" is "better" depends entirely on the evaluative frame. For the seamless path — photorealism, legibility, commercial plausibility — the platform mean IS the goal. Thick prompting is not "improvement" in an absolute sense. It is an increase in the ratio of intent to distribution — it makes the output more intentional and less default. Whether that serves the work depends on the audience, the stakes, and the reclassification context. Cohen's 500 sheep were a perfect output — in the *mezrag* frame. They were criminal evidence — in the colonial frame. The same sheep. Different "quality." Different fort.

### 7.3  "The Shield Is the Canonical Ekphrasis" — Itself a Product of the Narrowing

Calling the Shield of Achilles "the canonical ekphrasis" is itself a product of the Spitzer narrowing. In the ancient definition, the shield was simply one exercise among many — ekphrasis of a place, a battle, a harvest, a person. The modern canonization of the shield occurred precisely because it fit the narrowed definition: verbal description of a visual artwork. The shield became canonical *because* the definition was narrowed to fit it.

We must use this reflexively. Our paper re-reads the shield through the *ancient* definition — as a world-specification, not an artwork-description — and recovers the generative ambition that the narrowing concealed. But in doing so, we are performing our own reclassification, and we must mark it as such. We are not restoring the "true" shield. We are reading the shield through a frame that serves our argument — as every interpreter has done since Homer's first listeners. The thickness of our reading lies not in claiming neutrality but in declaring our frame.

## §8. Four Open Questions

### 8.1  Is the Quintilian-CLIP Structural Identity Testable?

We have claimed that Quintilian's "storehouse of images derived from sense perception" is structurally identical to CLIP's embedding space. Both are shared latent representations activated by verbal input. The orator transmits coordinates in the storehouse; the prompt transmits coordinates in the embedding space. Is this testable? Could one design an experiment that measures whether the same prompt-variation that moves a CLIP generation toward a specific region also moves a human listener's mental imagery in the same direction? If the curves match, the structural identity is confirmed. If they diverge, the analogy is only an analogy. The experiment would require bridging psycholinguistic imagery assessment (e.g., the Vividness of Visual Imagery Questionnaire) with computational embedding-space metrics. It is not trivially feasible, but it is articulable — and that articulability is itself a claim about the theory's empirical purchase.

### 8.2  What Was the "Platform Mean" of the Homeric System?

If Homer's oral-formulaic tradition is a generative model, does it have a "platform mean"? What were the twelve lighthouses of the Homeric system — the default outputs when the poet was not applying thick compositional force? Were they the type-scenes: arming, feasting, battle-joining, divine council? If so, the shield is the anti-type-scene — the passage where Homer exerts maximum compositional pressure against his own system's defaults. Classicists working in the Parry-Lord tradition could test this by mapping formulaic density against compositional novelty.

### 8.3  Can Reclassification Be Engineered, or Only Anticipated?

Is there a limit to how many reclassifications a thick prompt can survive? The *progymnasmata* taught students to anticipate their audience. Quintilian taught them to imagine every possible counter-reading and pre-empt it. But even Quintilian acknowledged limits. The question is whether those limits are practical (more thickness could always help) or structural (some reclassifications are categorically unanticipateable — every thick encoding eventually encounters its colonel).

### 8.4  Does a Persistent Worldtext Develop Its Own Viscosity?

An imagetext is ephemeral — a prompt and its output, one interaction. A worldtext persists: it has memory, state, history. If the worldtext persists, it accumulates its own distribution — its own viscosity. Does the worldtext eventually develop its own platform mean? Does it become its own twelve lighthouses?

Our own research cosmos demonstrates this possibility. As OPERATION DESCRIBE has accumulated hundreds of interlinked worldtext pages and governing axioms, it exerts its own gravitational pull on every new input. Each new source is read through the cosmos's existing ontology. The cosmos has viscosity. It resists inputs that do not fit its existing categories. This is useful (it enforces consistency) and dangerous (it may screen out evidence that requires a new category). If the worldtext develops its own viscosity, then the thick prompter's role *inside* an established worldtext is not to add context but to fight the worldtext's own defaults — to force the system away from its accumulated mean. A persistent worldtext might be the first generative system that the human must thick-prompt against even when they built it themselves.

## §9. Conclusion: The Storehouse and the Space

Quintilian told his students: you must see the scene yourself before you can make others see it. Your mind's eye activates the images stored in your memory, and your language transmits the coordinates of those images to the listener's storehouse, and the listener reconstructs the scene from their own stored experience.

Two thousand years later, a model takes a prompt and maps it to a region in a shared embedding space, and the denoising process generates an image from that region, and the image appears on a screen, and the prompter looks at it and says: *not quite. Revise.*

The storehouse became a glossary. The glossary became a fieldnote. The fieldnote became a context stack. The context stack became an embedding space. The human listener became a trained model. The orator's podium became a text box.

The operation did not change. Sparse signal, accumulated weights, gap-filling, generation. Description. Inference. The oldest technology in the humanities. The newest technology in computer science. The same technology.

And W — the designer who deleted the first image and started over — joined a lineage that reaches back through Geertz's ethnographer, Auden's Hephaestus, Quintilian's storehouse, and pseudo-Longinos's six words. What she did at the text box was what they all did: she selected the context that changes the act's identity, so that the receiver infers not the mean but the meaning.

The imagetext has arrived. The paragone is dead. Long live the new paragone: intent versus distribution, agency versus inertia, the thick prompt against the viscosity of the world. And beyond the imagetext, the worldtext waits — not a bigger image but a compiled world, not a description but a constitution, not a prompt but an architecture.

Homer already knew. The shield was always a worldtext. We just didn't have the substrate to run it.
